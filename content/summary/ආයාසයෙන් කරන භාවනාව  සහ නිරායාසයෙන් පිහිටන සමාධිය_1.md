## Main dhamma points
- This is Part 1 of a dialogue between a teacher (Speaker 1) and a student addressed as Dimutu (Speaker 2), on the talk's theme: *meditation done with effort* versus *the samādhi that is established effortlessly (nirāyāsa)*.
- The mind needs an object (*ārammaṇa*); an object can arise only where there is experiencing/feeling (*vindīma*). Where there is no experiencing, no object can be taken.
- For the one who realises nibbāna there is a state in which "mindfulness cannot be maintained," because there is no object — the mind cannot think there, and it "cannot be thought." That is precisely its value: a place unthinkable to thought.
- If any "mindfulness" arises there it is *not* the ordinary consciousness-based mindfulness we speak of in the world; it is an extraordinary awareness beyond the range of consciousness (*viññāṇa-agocara*), which is why it is called *paññā* / wisdom rather than *sati* as commonly understood.
- **Train-ticket simile:** once a ticket is booked there is nothing further "to do" and no sense in "booking again"; likewise, where there is no object there is neither a "thing to do" nor a "thing to refrain from doing." Questions like "what must I do to get there?" or "what must I not do?" both miss the point, because they assume one can reach it *via an object*.
- Both "there is feeling" and "there is no feeling" arise from the same root not-knowing (*avijjā / nodannakama*), which is the "middle." One cannot arrive at the middle from the two extremes, because the two sides are themselves built on not-knowing; only one who has seen the middle can speak of it.
- One who does not see this falls into the "death-trap" of not-knowing and takes *non-feeling* as real — that is annihilationism (*uccheda*). The wise one knows that neither the existence-extreme nor the non-existence-extreme is found, and that both extremes arise precisely from not knowing the nature in which neither is found. The teacher calls this the level of equanimity (*upekkhā*), which can never be reached by thinking.
- **Why a "good friend" is needed:** if this could be shown by a sutta, the Buddha could simply have said "avijjā is at such-and-such a place in such-and-such a sutta — study sutta number one" and no *kalyāṇa-mitta* would be needed. That he gave seventeen-thousand suttas at different levels to different listeners — each a "raft" toward this one point — shows the point itself cannot be delivered in words; putting the unknowable into a word distorts its meaning entirely. Hence the Buddha corrected the view that the good friend is "half" the holy life: the whole of the dispensation rests on the *kalyāṇa-mitta* who has *seen* and can loosen it for another.
- Vision/wisdom (*dassana, ñāṇa*) arises effortlessly (*nirāyāsa*) and cannot be extracted from a text by memorising it a hundred-thousand times. One who has seen, however, *can* recognise where in the suttas the matter is stated, and can point it out.
- The arising of right vision *is* stream-entry (*sotāpatti*); with it, *rāga* and *dosa* begin to wear away — shaken at *sakadāgāmī*, fully removed at *anāgāmī*. This vision is self-arisen and independent of another (*paccatta*, *parapratyaya-rahita*).
- **Vinaya** (discipline) likewise establishes itself *effortlessly* as the vision matures and wrong view becomes right; it is not the 228 training rules applied mechanically. The teacher sharply distinguishes (a) intellectually "matching" a proposition to an outcome — "if you see this, then rāga is removed" (like drawing lines to match exam answers) — from (b) *rāga* and *dosa* actually wearing away within oneself, which is what *sandiṭṭhika* ("seen for oneself") and true conformity with the Buddha's word mean.
- A caution against translators and writers who render profound books while admitting they have no grasp of the deep dhamma — merely choosing "suitable words." If the writer holds wrong view, the rendering carries wrong view; hence the warning not to accept a thing merely because it is written, said by a recluse, or found in the canon.
- The dialogue closes (Part 1) turning to the feeling / non-feeling pair: "feeling" just means "not-feeling is absent" (as "poor" just means "no money"); we habitually split one experience into "I didn't feel before, now I feel," placing the two on opposite sides. But where feeling itself is never actually known or found, there can be no talk of "non-feeling" either — both arise together and both are *unfound*.

## Summary

This is the first part of a recorded one-to-one dhamma dialogue between a teacher and a student called Dimutu. Its subject is the difference between meditation *undertaken with effort* and the samādhi that becomes *established effortlessly* (*nirāyāsa*) — and, more deeply, why the goal cannot be reached by any method that works through an object.

The teacher begins from the observation that the mind can take an object only where there is experiencing; remove experiencing and no object can arise. He then points to the state realised by one who sees nibbāna: there, "mindfulness cannot be maintained," because there is no object and the mind cannot think. Far from being a defect, this unthinkability is the whole point. If some awareness does arise there, it is not the ordinary consciousness-bound mindfulness of the world but an extraordinary awareness lying beyond consciousness's range — which is why the tradition calls it wisdom (*paññā*) rather than *sati*. He drives this home with the image of a booked train ticket: once it is booked there is nothing to do and no sense in "booking again." So too, where there is no object, there is neither anything "to do" nor anything "to refrain from doing"; the very questions "what should I do to get there?" and "what should I not do?" betray the assumption that one can travel there by way of an object.

From here he develops the theme of the two extremes. "There is feeling" and "there is no feeling" both spring from the same root not-knowing, which he calls the "middle." One cannot climb to the middle from either extreme, because the extremes are themselves constructed out of not-knowing; only someone who has already seen the middle can speak of it. The person who has not seen falls into the trap of not-knowing and mistakes "non-feeling" for something real — the extreme of annihilation. The one with wisdom, by contrast, knows that neither the existence-end nor the non-existence-end is ever found, and that both are thrown up precisely by ignorance of the nature in which neither appears. This, he says, is the level of equanimity, and it can never be arrived at by thought.

The centre of the talk is an argument about why a living teacher — a *kalyāṇa-mitta* — is indispensable. If the crucial point (ignorance, and the unknowable middle) could be located in a text, the Buddha would only have needed to say "study sutta number one, the matter is right there," and no good friend would ever be required. Instead he taught seventeen-thousand discourses, pitched to different listeners at different levels, each functioning like a raft or plank toward this single point — which shows that the point itself cannot be captured in words, since forcing the unknowable into language distorts it completely. This, the teacher says, is why the Buddha rejected the idea that the good friend is merely "half" the holy life and insisted the whole dispensation depends on the one who has *seen* and can unfasten the knot for another. Vision, he stresses, arises effortlessly; it cannot be squeezed out of a sutta by repetition, though one who has seen can afterwards recognise exactly where in the suttas the matter is stated. He cites the *Sañcetanā Sutta* as a place where the point is beautifully set down, and invokes the *Dāyāda Sutta*'s caution not to accept a teaching merely because it is in the canon or spoken by a recluse.

He ties this to the path itself: the arising of right vision *is* stream-entry, and with it the wearing-away of greed and aversion begins — shaken at once-return, uprooted at non-return. This vision is self-arisen and owes itself to no one else. In the same way, true *vinaya* is not the mechanical keeping of 228 rules but a discipline that settles of itself as vision matures and wrong view turns to right; and he carefully separates merely "matching" propositions to outcomes on paper from the actual, felt fading of greed and aversion within oneself, which alone is what "seen for oneself" (*sandiṭṭhika*) means. He warns against those who translate or expound profound texts without any inner grasp, choosing plausible words while admitting they do not understand; a rendering made from wrong view carries wrong view forward, which is why one should not accept a thing merely because it is written, spoken by a recluse, or preserved in the piṭaka. Part 1 ends as he turns to the pair feeling / non-feeling: "feeling," he notes, simply means "not-feeling is absent," just as "poor" means "no money"; we split a single experience into a before and an after and set them on opposite sides — but where feeling itself is never actually found, no "non-feeling" can be spoken of either, for both arise together and both are unfound.
