## Sutta referred to
- **Māluṅkyaputta** (මාලුංක්‍යපුත්ත හාමුදුරුවෝ) — named: the teacher recalls the Buddha asking Ven. Māluṅkyaputta whether lust or aversion could arise toward something not seen, heard or sensed in the past, not being experienced now, and not expected in the future — not even hoped for.
- No other sutta is named by title. The doctrinal terms *ariyakānta-sīla*, *paṭisotagāmī*, and *yoniso / ayoniso manasikāra* are used but not attributed to a named sutta. No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- Part 2 of the same teacher–student (Dimutu) dialogue, continuing the theme of effortful meditation versus the effortlessly-established. It carries on directly from the feeling / non-feeling and "non-arising" (*anutpāda*) discussion of Part 1.
- Even the statement "both extremes have *not* arisen" still takes up a not-arisen thing to make its analysis; putting the middle into words is itself *ayoniso*. One who sees the middle sorts this out by their own wisdom; one who has not seen only gets tangled repeating it.
- **The "no child born today" illustration:** to speak of "birth" you need a child that is born. If not a single child were born in the country today, you could not even speak of "birth" or "a child" today; to announce "no children were born today" would be to fabricate an empty statement. Likewise, where neither feeling nor non-feeling has arisen, even "the two have not arisen" does not apply.
- **The raft is released, not thrown away:** when one sees rightly, even the analysis (the "raft") is seen to be flawed and drops away of itself — one does not deliberately discard it; it releases when the right place is seen. This is the level at which "the Dhamma itself is let go."
- **The distortion (*vikṛti*):** because the nature cannot be brought up skilfully, a distortion is built that *covers* that nature; and it is *inside* this artificial distortion that the student's "clinging and colliding" (*ælīma / gæṭīma*) during meditation actually occur — not in the underlying nature, which remains as it is.
- Where feeling itself cannot be known, and hearing itself cannot be known, no craving or aversion can arise toward felt or heard things. For one in whom wisdom has arisen, no *vitakka* (thought-fabrication) about heard things even occurs; hence such a person does not "even by force" drift toward the four woeful states — the current runs upstream. The *ariyakānta-sīla* (the virtue dear to the noble ones) is thereby preserved unbroken, with no knot-maker in between.
- For one who has seen contact (*phassa*) as the middle, "killing beings" and "not killing beings" are both stories tied onto a being that is not there; "killing" is a mere action-story in which no actual being is found. So both keeping the precept and breaking it exist only within the distortion; one who has seen has no talk of either keeping or breaking — which is why the *ariyakānta-sīla* is called unbreakable, and why the Buddha can guarantee such a one will not fall to the woeful states: no such *saṅkhāra* forms. This is transcending action (*kriyāva ikmavā*).
- **Where the knot is struck, the striking gradually lessens** — and the reduction of this knot-striking at contact *is* the wearing away of *rāga* and *dosa*.
- **Breath-meditation analysis:** frustration in meditation ("a bird cried, a vehicle passed — my meditation broke") shows that we cling to *catching the object* as pleasure and collide with *failing to catch it* as pain. But the clinging and colliding are to a breath onto which we have tied a knot by *thinking about* "feeling" — not to feeling itself, which was never actually connected to the breath. Invoking Māluṅkyaputta: since feeling can never be tied to breath, one who does not strike the knot cannot cling to or collide over breath.
- **Walking analysis:** in walking, the bending, lifting and placing of the leg are never actually cognized; they are all tied to the concept "going/walking." Yet even "going" is not itself understood while walking. The teacher's instruction to "keep the mind on the action" is not repeating "going, going"; but because "going" is not cognizable, the mind cannot in fact be maintained on it. Where no object is comprehensible, if any awareness arises there it cannot be within the range of consciousness (*viññāṇa-gocara*).
- **Chair / stepped-on-object illustration:** while walking, a chair bumps the leg or something is stepped on, and *that* is where clinging or collision occurs — with the chair or the thing underfoot, never with "going," which was never found. We tie the bump and the step onto "going," but the friction belongs to the particulars, not to the (non-existent) act.
- Applied to breath: "breath" is irrelevant to feeling, so "maintaining mindfulness on the breath" is maintaining it on a fabricated breath unconnected to feeling — and likewise "cutting vegetables" fixes the mind on knife and vegetables while "cutting" itself is never cognized. All such "maintaining of mindfulness" is *maññanā* (conceiving); worldly mindfulness is a bundle of *maññanā*.
- **Redefinition of "inattention" (*asihiya*):** it does not mean "I failed to keep mindfulness." It means *behaving heedlessly, without knowing.* The whole world lives precisely in that *asihiya* — including what it proudly calls "mindfulness."
- The wisdom that sees this worldly "mindfulness" itself as *asihiya* is like turning a covered vessel upright, opening a closed pot, or light coming to one in the dark — this is *paṭisotagāmī*, going against the stream. Such a one sees that the very object grasped in order to "be mindful" was grasped in ignorance; everything the world calls *yoniso* is seen to be *ayoniso*.
- Crucially: one cannot cultivate *yoniso-manasikāra* directly. By watching the *ayoniso* continuously, one sees *how the ayoniso arises* — and the very wisdom that sees the *ayoniso as ayoniso* is itself *yoniso*.
- Dhamma discussion and dhamma listening (*kalyāṇa-mitta sevana*) are like giving a tree fertiliser and water: the giving is something we do, but the tree's growing — the arising of vision — happens of itself, by nature, and cannot be forced.

## Summary

This second part continues the intimate dialogue between the teacher and his student Dimutu, picking up exactly where Part 1 left off, on the nature of "non-arising" and on what meditation actually is. The teacher first tightens the earlier point about the two extremes: even to say "both extremes have not arisen" is still to take up a not-arisen thing as the subject of analysis, so that formulation too is *ayoniso*. He makes this vivid with the image of birth: to talk about "birth" at all you need a child that has been born; if not one child had been born in the whole country today, you could not even meaningfully speak of "birth" or "a child" — and to declare "no children were born today" would just be to manufacture an empty sentence. In the same way, where neither feeling nor non-feeling has arisen, even "the two did not arise" fails to apply. When this is seen, he says, even the analysis — the raft — is recognised as flawed and falls away of its own accord; it is not thrown away deliberately but released once the right place is seen.

He then names the trouble directly: because that underlying nature cannot be brought up skilfully, a *distortion* is constructed that covers it, and it is inside this artificial distortion that the student's experience of "clinging" and "colliding" during meditation takes place. The nature itself is untouched. From here he argues that where feeling itself cannot be known, no craving or aversion can arise toward felt things at all; for one in whom wisdom has arisen, not even a thought-fabrication about heard things occurs, so such a person cannot be dragged even by force toward the woeful states — the current turns upstream, and the "virtue dear to the noble ones" (*ariyakānta-sīla*) is kept unbroken because there is no knot-maker left in between. For one who has seen contact as the middle, "killing" and "not killing" are alike stories tied onto a being that was never there; keeping and breaking a precept both belong only to the distortion, which is why this virtue is called unbreakable and why the Buddha can guarantee that such a one will not fall — no corresponding formation is built. This is what it means to transcend action; and as the striking of the knot at contact gradually lessens, that lessening simply *is* the wearing away of greed and aversion.

The heart of Part 2 is a sustained dismantling of ordinary meditation practice through concrete examples. In breath meditation, the frustration we feel — "a bird cried, a car passed, my meditation broke" — reveals that we treat catching the object as a pleasure to cling to and losing it as a pain to resist; but, the teacher shows, the clinging and colliding are directed at a breath onto which we have tied a knot by *thinking about* feeling, not at feeling itself, which was never actually joined to the breath. He invokes the Buddha's question to Māluṅkyaputta — whether lust or aversion could arise toward something never seen, heard or sensed in past, present or future, not even hoped for — to make the point that, feeling never being tie-able to breath, one who does not strike the knot cannot cling to or quarrel with the breath. He then turns to walking: the bending, lifting and setting-down of the leg are never really cognized; they are all bundled under the concept "going," and yet "going" itself is not understood even as one walks. His instruction to "keep the mind on the action" is not a matter of repeating "going, going"; and because "going" cannot be grasped, the mind cannot in fact be fixed on it — so if any awareness settles where no object is comprehensible, it cannot lie within the range of consciousness. The chair that bumps the leg, or the object stepped on underfoot, is where clinging or collision truly occurs — with those particulars, never with the (non-existent) act of going, onto which we merely tie them.

Applying all this, the teacher and student conclude that "maintaining mindfulness on the breath" is really maintaining it on a fabricated breath unrelated to feeling, just as "cutting vegetables mindfully" fixes attention on knife and vegetables while "cutting" itself is never cognized. All such "keeping of mindfulness" is conceiving (*maññanā*); worldly mindfulness is a bundle of *maññanā*. He redefines "inattention" (*asihiya*) accordingly: it does not mean failing to keep mindfulness, but behaving heedlessly without knowing — and the whole world, including in what it calls its "mindfulness," lives precisely there. The wisdom that recognises this worldly mindfulness itself as *asihiya* is likened to a covered vessel being turned upright, a closed pot opened, light reaching one in the dark: this is *paṭisotagāmī*, going against the stream. Such a one sees that the very object grasped in order to be mindful was grasped in ignorance, so that everything the world calls "wise attention" (*yoniso*) is seen to be *ayoniso* — and the very seeing of the *ayoniso as ayoniso* is itself *yoniso*, since *yoniso-manasikāra* cannot be cultivated directly but only revealed by watching how the *ayoniso* arises. The teacher closes the segment with the image of the tree: dhamma discussion and listening in the company of a good friend are like giving a tree fertiliser and water — the giving is ours to do, but the growing of the tree, the arising of vision, happens of itself and cannot be forced.
