## Sutta referred to
- No sutta is named by title.
- One canonical formula is directly quoted, unattributed: *manasikāra-sambhavā sabbe dhammā* ("all dhammas arise from attention"). It is used as a doctrinal phrase, not introduced as belonging to any named sutta. No sutta should be inferred.
- The doctrinal terms *anidassana* (non-manifestive), *anusotagāmī / paṭisotagāmī* (going with / against the stream), and *yoniso / ayoniso manasikāra* are used.

## Main dhamma points
- This is Part 3 — the short concluding segment of the three-part teacher–student dialogue on effortful meditation versus effortlessly-established samādhi.
- Taking up *manasikāra-sambhavā sabbe dhammā* ("all dhammas arise from attention"), the teacher argues that the Buddha would therefore *not* say "do attention (*manasikāra*) in order to see the truth": to see the true nature is precisely where the mind loses its opening to arise — the very *condition* for doing attention falls away.
- At that point mind / consciousness becomes *anidassana* (non-manifestive) and cannot establish itself there. Seeing this again and again, one sees that the whole business of "maintaining mindfulness" is pure *asihiya* (heedless not-knowing): what we attempt as *yoniso* is in fact being done as *ayoniso*.
- "Mindfulness" has been maintained on a *knotted-up* thing. "Going" (the act of walking) was never actually found; what we have really been attending to are the bumpings and steppings of the leg — "my leg hit this, this got trodden on." That backward habit is itself the stream we are caught in.
- We are used to travelling in the direction of grasping an existing thing and maintaining it; once this is seen, the very grasping-and-maintaining-of-mindfulness shows itself to be *asihiya*.
- Because the whole thing rests on not-knowing, seeing *how ayoniso-manasikāra arises* is likened to lighting a lamp for someone in the dark, turning upright what was upside-down, light reaching one in darkness — a *complete turning-over*. Like a river: it is easy to drift downstream (*anusotagāmī*) and hard to swim up (*paṭisotagāmī*); this seeing is the upstream direction.
- Grasping an object in order to maintain mindfulness is exactly *feeding fertiliser to consciousness* so that it can arise — providing it an occasion and an object. But is consciousness sustained on an object what the Buddha calls the way toward death, or the way to the deathless? Once the *ayoniso* is recognised as *ayoniso* and everything is seen as *ayoniso*, those constructions cease to arise — and one inevitably drops into the "not-understood" place.
- When the knot-striking is let go, where does one fall? Into the true nature itself — the place that is not understood. While walking, eating, bathing — at each of these, without the knot-striking, "the middle" establishes itself. This "middle" is not really a middle: since there is nothing there to name, it is simply a state that does not fall to either side, an unknown nature.
- This is why the one who sees transcends the two extremes: where this is seen, there is no cause for two extremes to form at all.

## Summary

This brief final segment brings the three-part dialogue to its point. The teacher takes up the canonical statement that "all dhammas arise from attention" (*manasikāra-sambhavā sabbe dhammā*) and draws a sharp conclusion from it: precisely because everything mental arises from attention, the Buddha would not instruct someone to "do attention in order to see the truth." To see the true nature is the very place where the mind loses its opening to arise; the condition for exercising attention drops away, and consciousness there becomes non-manifestive (*anidassana*), unable to establish itself. Seen repeatedly, this exposes the whole enterprise of "maintaining mindfulness" as itself heedless not-knowing (*asihiya*): what we try to perform as wise attention (*yoniso*) is in fact carried out as *ayoniso*, mindfulness fixed all along on a knotted-up object.

He returns to the walking example to seal the argument. The act of "going" was never actually found; what we have really been attending to are the incidental bumpings and steppings of the leg — "my leg struck this, this got trodden on" — which is exactly what we remember afterwards. That habitual movement toward grasping an existing thing and sustaining it is the current we are carried in; and once this is seen, the grasping-and-maintaining that we called mindfulness reveals itself as inattention. Because the whole structure rests on not-knowing, to see *how* unwise attention arises is like a lamp being lit for someone in the dark, a vessel turned upright, light reaching the blind — a complete turning-over. As with a river, it is easy to drift downstream and hard to swim against it; this seeing is the upstream movement, *paṭisotagāmī*.

Finally he names the mechanism plainly: to grasp an object so as to maintain mindfulness is to feed fertiliser to consciousness, giving it both occasion and object to arise — and he asks whether consciousness sustained on an object is what the Buddha called the road toward death or the road to the deathless. Once the *ayoniso* is recognised as *ayoniso*, and everything is seen in that light, these constructions stop arising and one inevitably falls into the "not-understood" place. Letting go of the knot-striking, one falls into the true nature itself; and in walking, eating or bathing alike, without that knot-striking, "the middle" establishes itself — a "middle" that is not truly a middle, but simply a state that leans to neither side, an unknown nature. This, the teacher concludes, is how the one who sees transcends the two extremes: where this is seen, there is no longer any cause for two extremes to form.
