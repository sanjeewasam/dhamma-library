## Sutta referred to
- **Kaccānagotta Sutta** (කච්චානගොත්ත සූත්‍රය) — named: cited for the Buddha's statement to Kaccānagotta that most people are caught in the eternalist and annihilationist views (*sassata / uccheda*).
- **Nalakapāna Sutta** (නළකපාන සූත්‍රය) — named: the speaker recalls the Buddha asking about a form *adiṭṭhā adiṭṭhapubbā, na ca passati, na ca taṁ hoti passeyyan-ti* (never seen, never seen before, not now seen, and not thought "may I see it in future") — whether *chando vā rāgo vā* (desire or lust) could arise toward it.
- Directly quoted Pāli phrases, not attributed to a named sutta: *diṭṭhe diṭṭhamattaṁ* (in the seen, only the seen); *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* (the meeting of the three is contact); *kāyañca paṭicca phoṭṭhabbe ca uppajjati kāyaviññāṇaṁ* (dependent on body and tangibles, body-consciousness arises).
- The exchange in which a wanderer asks the Buddha "Is there a self? / Is there no self?" and the Buddha stays silent, later explained to Ānanda, is recounted as an example of the "unanswerable/burning" (*avyākata / tapanīya*) questions — but the speaker explicitly says he *cannot recall which sutta* and that it was "not Vacchagotta." No sutta title is claimed for it, so none is recorded here.
- Ālāra Kālāma and Uddaka Rāmaputta are named as persons (abiding in *nevasaññā-nāsaññā*), not as suttas.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker dialogue (the speakers address each other as elder/younger brother, *aiya / malli*) examining the two extremes of **existence and non-existence** (*ata / näta*, i.e. *bhava / vibhava*, *sassata / uccheda*).
- **Non-existence can never actually be experienced.** What we ever meet is only *the non-existence of an existing thing* — "no illness," "no money," "the shop I know is not there." True non-existence is never found; out of ignorance we imagine it is something that can be experienced, and this imagined "non-existence" is what people mistake for annihilation (*uccheda*).
- Existence over-run becomes non-existence: *bhava* pushed to its limit yields *vibhava*. **Craving for non-existence** (*vibhava-taṇhā*) is wanting an existing thing to not-be — and it operates unnoticed, so we never recognise it as craving. Both cravings — for existence and for non-existence — are *dukkha*.
- Everyday illustrations: we are content when the neighbour gives no trouble, when illness is absent, when there is no sound during meditation — these are all cravings for non-existence, unfelt as craving precisely because we are "at ease" in them.
- **Meditation and samādhi:** samādhi cultivated without wisdom is a craving for the *absence* of objects; one who lacks the vision takes samādhi to be *nirodha* (cessation). The one with vision knows samādhi is itself a subtle craving — a pleasant abiding won by suppressing objects — which is exactly why anger flares the instant a sound or a mosquito breaks it. Meditators suppress objects so no anger arises while sitting, but rage returns the moment they rise, because the craving for the quiet was never seen.
- **Ālāra Kālāma and Uddaka Rāmaputta** rested in "neither-perception-nor-non-perception" (*nevasaññā-nāsaññā*), taking "there is no perception" (the removal of an existing perception) as liberation. But that is still the existence/non-existence pair, with "I" merely hidden; the view was not abandoned, so they reach only the Brahma worlds and inevitably return. One who sees that *uccheda* is never actually found does not go that way.
- **The arahant "does not exist after death"** is likewise not true annihilation but the "non-existence of an existing thing" — the arahant who was found, now taken as not-found. Since *uccheda* can never be found by an "I," this too is the world's non-existence-extreme.
- **The unanswered (avyākata) questions:** to "Is there a self?" and "Is there no self?" the Buddha stayed silent, because both rest on the questioner already holding a self — "yes" would confirm his imagined self, "no" would make him think his existing self is destroyed. One who has seen the cessation of contact (*phassa-nirodha*) does not answer existence/non-existence questions framed around a thing, because rightly seen no "thing" is found there. Hence to all four alternatives (is / is not / both / neither) the Buddha declares the question itself wrong.
- **Nalakapāna:** toward a form never seen, never previously seen, not now seen, and not even hoped to be seen — no desire or lust can arise, because such a thing is not in the mind at all. This shows that where "seeing" itself is not cognized, seeing is empty of "seeing" — *diṭṭhe diṭṭhamattaṁ*.
- **The play / dream illustration:** watching a drama we weep and laugh genuinely; the chair, table and house in the play are recognised with the very same perception-marks (*saññā*) as real ones, with no difference at all. Only afterwards do we "separate" — this was a play, this is real life. That separating is exactly how the two extremes (real/unreal, existing/non-existing) are *built together*. In truth "seeing is just seeing," common to play, dream and waking alike; no reality-status is inherent — we impose it. In a dream a dog chases and "I" run; the running "I" is manufactured by perception. All three are one: *saññā* deluded by *saññā*.
- **Perception is a mirage.** The Buddha said *saññā* should be seen as a mirage (*marīci*). "Nose and breath," "chair and body" are only *recognitions* (*saññā-mātra / paññatti-mātra*) — mere designations. Like a small child who cannot pick out a chair from the word alone, recognition itself can never actually grasp a thing. Because two such recognitions are knotted together in ignorance, they become "things"; feeling then becomes a thing; and the "I" who feels becomes real "a hundred percent."
- **How contact (*tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*) is fabricated:** while actually sitting, "chair" and "body-touching-the-chair" are not cognized at all (the un-understood, equanimity level). But the moment one *thinks* (*manasikāra*), two conditions get knotted — "my body" and "the chair it touches" — and that knotting *is* contact; consciousness then establishes itself on the two (*kāyañca paṭicca phoṭṭhabbe ca uppajjati kāyaviññāṇaṁ*). The knotting of feeling-and-perception is how the five clung-to aggregates and the whole "I and the world" arise from a mere act of interpretation. The speaker notes the striking point that this interpretation conjures a whole human being — head, two arms, two legs — where none was found.
- **The Dhamma is itself a "poison."** Ignorance is a poison; the Dhamma the Buddha gave is a stronger counter-poison — also a poison — to be *used to escape* the first, but not held onto, for if kept it turns to poison and kills. *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* was given to show *where* the person-view (*sakkāya-diṭṭhi*) arises — like warning "there is a pit on the road, don't fall in," not "sit in the pit." People took it as a doctrine to hold rather than a means of escape.
- **The "understanding" itself is the suffering.** The very arising of "it is understood" (*tērīma*) is where dukkha is; try to understand *that* as dukkha, and again an "understander" is created. The arahant is described as one who does not even receive "understanding" — understanding has ended and wisdom has become strong. If a person-view is rebuilt even from "I understood the Dhamma," the understanding never ends; even to describe an arahant from our own "understanding" recreates the "I." This is why the Dhamma, if made into a real thing, becomes Māra.

## Summary

This is an intimate two-person dhamma dialogue on the two extremes of existence and non-existence, the speakers addressing each other as elder and younger brother. Its governing insight is stated early and returned to repeatedly: non-existence can never actually be experienced. Whatever we meet is only *the non-existence of an existing thing* — no illness, no money, the familiar shop not there — and genuine non-existence is never found. Out of ignorance we imagine that non-existence is itself something experienceable, and this imagined absence is what gets mistaken for annihilation. From this they unfold the pair of cravings: craving for existence (the eternalist pull) and craving for non-existence (*vibhava-taṇhā*), the latter operating so quietly — in our contentment when the neighbour is silent, when illness is gone, when meditation is undisturbed — that we never recognise it as craving at all. Both, they agree, are suffering.

They apply this directly to meditation. Samādhi pursued without wisdom is really a craving for the *absence* of objects, and one who lacks the vision takes such samādhi to be cessation itself. The one who sees knows it is a subtle craving: a pleasant abiding purchased by suppressing objects, which is exactly why anger erupts the instant a sound or a mosquito breaks the calm, and why the meditator who was serene on the cushion flares up the moment he rises. This leads them to Ālāra Kālāma and Uddaka Rāmaputta, who rested in "neither-perception-nor-non-perception," treating the removal of perception as liberation — but that is still the existence/non-existence pair with the "I" merely concealed, the view unabandoned, so they attain only the Brahma worlds and must return. The younger speaker names the *Kaccānagotta Sutta* here: the world is caught in the annihilation-extreme it relies on, yet the Buddha shows that this very "non-existence" is itself never found; the real annihilation is never met, and not knowing this, people think it is.

The middle section works through the "unanswered questions." A wanderer asks whether a self exists, and the Buddha is silent; whether no self exists, and again silence; Ānanda later asks why. Because, they explain, both questions rest on the questioner already grasping a self: to say "yes" would confirm his imagined self, to say "no" would make him think his existing self is destroyed. One who has seen the cessation of contact will not answer existence/non-existence questions built around a thing, since rightly seen no thing is found there — which is why the Buddha rejects all four alternatives as wrongly put. The elder speaker then recalls the *Nalakapāna Sutta*: toward a form never seen, never previously seen, not now seen and not even hoped for, no desire can arise, because it is not in the mind at all — showing that where "seeing" itself is not cognized, seeing is empty of seeing, *diṭṭhe diṭṭhamattaṁ*.

The dialogue's most sustained teaching comes through the images of the play and the dream. Watching a drama we weep and laugh in earnest, and the chair, table and house on stage are recognised with exactly the same perception-marks as the ones at home — no difference whatever. Only afterwards do we separate them: that was a play, this is real. That act of separating, they show, is precisely how the two extremes are built together, for in truth "seeing is just seeing," identical across play, dream and waking, with no reality-status of its own; we are the ones who impose it. In a dream a dog chases and "I" run — the running "I" fabricated by perception — and all three states are one and the same: perception deluded by perception. From here they turn to how contact is manufactured. While one actually sits, "chair" and "body touching chair" are never cognized; but the moment thought arises, two conditions are knotted — "my body" and "the chair it touches" — and that knotting *is* contact, upon which consciousness establishes itself, *kāyañca paṭicca phoṭṭhabbe ca uppajjati kāyaviññāṇaṁ*. "Chair" and "body" are only recognitions, mere designations, like words a small child cannot match to any object; recognition can never actually seize a thing, and perception itself should be seen as a mirage. Yet because two recognitions are knotted in ignorance they become things, feeling becomes a thing, and the one who feels becomes real "a hundred percent" — a whole human being with head, arms and legs conjured where none was found.

They close on two sharp points. First, the Dhamma itself is a poison: ignorance is poison, and the teaching is a stronger counter-poison meant to be *used to escape* and then let go, never held — for if kept it turns to poison and kills. *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* was given to show *where* the person-view arises, like a warning "there is a pit, don't fall in," yet people take it as a doctrine to sit in rather than a way out. Second, and most subtly, the very "understanding" is itself the suffering: the arising of "it is understood" is where dukkha lies, and to try to understand *that* only manufactures an "understander" again. The arahant is one who does not even receive such understanding — understanding has ended and wisdom grown strong — so that even to describe an arahant from within our own understanding rebuilds the "I." This, they conclude, is why the Dhamma, once made into a real thing to be grasped, itself becomes Māra; the path opens only where one is caught nowhere, seeing even this seeing as a flaw. The recording ends as they wrap up.
