## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker dialogue conducted as a live experiment on the theme of the title: **how the establishing of one sense-base is itself what causes the (apparent) establishing of the other sense-bases**. The teacher tests this directly on the student rather than expounding it abstractly.
- The teacher has the student, who has been gazing at and "thinking about" a light/bulb, suddenly switch: "now think about a river." At that instant the fixation on the light "breaks" at the point of view (*diṭṭhi*) and drops into "thinking about the river."
- We never notice that "the light" was only a *thought/conception* (*kalpanā*); we take it that there was a light "as actually seen." This failure to see that the understanding is only an understanding breeds a **distortion** (*vikṛti*).
- On the mental plane there was no real "watching" going on at all — only conceiving *about* a bulb; no actual sense-base was operating. When told "think about the river," the student focuses under the view "I am now thinking about a river," and it is from that very inner feeling — "I am thinking about a river now" — that the notion of **a separate mind that thinks** is generated. So "a mind that thinks" and "the thought" get cleanly split off from each other.
- **The margin/dividing-line:** the moment one side is separated out, the other side is instantly formed. There is no independent basis for the split — a single thought is divided in two *by another thought*, and one can never actually grasp "a thought." Before "thinking about the river," one was simply "not-thinking," resting in one of seeing / hearing / feeling; it was all one heap of conceiving.
- Therefore **the sense-faculty (*indriya*) is born right there**, in the very act of thinking "river" — a condition is fabricated on that side. It is *not* that a mind pre-existed and then reached out to take the object "river"; the whole "faculty" story arises in the act itself.
- **Building a beginning:** we start with "here is where the mind was born." But "the mind was born now" implies "before, it was not born — it was off," just as "the light came on now" implies it was off before. Yet an unlit, never-arisen thing cannot "come on." From this the sense of permanence and self (*nitya / ātma*) forms, and then hardens into "continuity" (existence, *bhava*): the mind acquires a duration, "river-thought" becomes a found thing, and the tidy "mind-and-thought" story ripens.
- **But no "off-point" is ever found.** Wherever one looks there is only *receiving/gaining* (*läbīma*) — never an off-state. For something to be seen as "arisen," there must be a prior "off"; only at such an off-point could one fearlessly say "here it was off, no mind — and here it arose." Since there is only gaining upon gaining, one cannot even call it "gaining," and the story "a mind existed, and an existing mind arose" cannot proceed. One can never name where, when, or how the mind arose.
- Hence **"birth/arising befalls no one"**: because arising can never actually be found (there being no off-state), there is no moment at which anything "arose." "Arising" is only ever a thing we grasp after the fact and name.
- **The person is built from that very "it arose":** "a mind arose" manufactures "the thinking I"; "I was in seeing" manufactures "the seeing I"; "I was in hearing," "the hearing I." All of this rests in a nature of conceiving (*maññanā*), a mere thought-heap (*hitaluwa*) — thoughts reflected upon while thinking.
- When this is seen, **both the seer and the seen wear away together** — a wisdom in which neither "the seeing I" nor "the seen thing" applies; one can say only that neither is found. The distortion built on the thought-heap is exactly what makes us imagine a separate "seeing I, hearing I, feeling I, knowing I."

## Summary

This recording is a short, closely-worked dialogue in which a teacher demonstrates the talk's title-thesis — that the standing-up of one sense-base is itself the cause of the apparent standing-up of the others — not by explaining it but by running a live experiment on his student. The student has been gazing at and "thinking about" a light. The teacher abruptly says, "now think about a river," and draws the student's attention to what just happened: the fixation on the light broke at the point of view and instantly reformed as "thinking about the river." From this the teacher extracts his first point — that we never notice "the light" was only a conception; we assume there was a light "as actually seen." This unnoticed slippage, mistaking an understanding for a found reality, is what builds a distortion.

He then shows that on the mental plane there was never any real "watching": only conceiving *about* a bulb, with no sense-base actually operating. When the student turns to "the river," he does so under the view "I am now thinking about a river," and it is precisely from that inner sense — "I am thinking about a river now" — that the idea of a separate mind that thinks is produced, split cleanly away from "the thought" itself. The crucial move is the dividing-line: the instant one side is separated out, the other side is manufactured with it, and there is no independent ground for the division at all — a single stream of conceiving is simply cut in two by yet another thought, and "a thought" can never actually be seized. So the sense-faculty is born right there, in the act of thinking "river"; it is never the case that a mind pre-existed and then took up an object. The whole apparatus of "faculties" arises within the act.

The dialogue then turns to how a beginning is fabricated. We say "here is where the mind was born," but "born now" smuggles in "before, it was off," exactly as "the light came on now" implies it was previously off — and an unlit, never-arisen thing cannot come on. From this the sense of permanence and self forms and then sets into "continuity," so that the mind seems to have a duration and "the mind and its thought" becomes a satisfying, complete story. Yet, the teacher insists, no off-point is ever actually found: wherever one looks there is only receiving upon receiving, never a gap of non-being. For anything to be seen as "arisen" there would have to be a prior off-state, and since there is none, one cannot even properly call it "gaining," and the story "a mind existed and an existing mind arose" collapses. One can never name where, when, or how the mind arose — which is why, he concludes, birth or arising befalls no one at all; "arising" is only ever a thing we grasp and name after the fact.

Finally he shows how the person is assembled out of this very "it arose": "a mind arose" fabricates "the thinking I," "I was in seeing" fabricates "the seeing I," and so on for hearing, feeling and knowing — all of it resting in a nature of mere conceiving, a heap of thoughts thought about while thinking. When this is seen, the seer and the seen wear away together, leaving a wisdom in which neither "the seeing I" nor "the seen thing" applies; one can say only that neither is found. The distortion built upon the thought-heap is exactly what had made us imagine a separate seeing-I, hearing-I, feeling-I and knowing-I. The teacher closes by noting that this is a fruitful point that opens up further when explored at length.
