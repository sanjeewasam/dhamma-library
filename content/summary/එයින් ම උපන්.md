## Sutta referred to

- Not explicitly named, though the teaching on "na ca so na ca añño" (neither the same one nor a different one — regarding death and rebirth) is referenced, and the Pāli phrase "avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā" from the paṭiccasamuppāda formula is cited.

## Main dhamma points

- The Pāli term **tajjo samannāhāro** means "born from that itself" — it points to the fact that phenomena arise right there, not from somewhere else, and not from something that was already existing.
- Using the analogy of combustion (jvalana/flame): the flame does not come from the oil, nor was it stored in the oil. Yet combustion arises. Its origin cannot be pointed to — it is tajjo samannāhāro.
- If something has no traceable origin (mūla), it cannot have a traceable end either. Where there is no beginning, there can be no ending.
- The Buddha's statement that "the one who died is not the same one reborn, nor is it a different one" dismantles both sides: you cannot show a being who was previously existing, nor can you show a different being who arrived. Both claims are blocked.
- This is why saṃsāra is called **anavarāgga** (without discoverable beginning) — not as a poetic statement, but because there is genuinely no root and no end to be found.
- The same principle applies to hearing (savana): within hearing itself, there is no traceable origin of hearing. The arising of hearing is tajjo samannāhāro.
- Because this rootlessness is not seen, saññā (perception/recognition) fills the gap — e.g., "it was a bird" or "it was a vehicle." This is **yaṃ vedeti taṃ sañjānāti** — what is felt is what gets labelled/recognised.
- The label (saññā) gives the illusion that something was understood, but nothing within the label actually conveys real content. This is the true meaning of **marīci** (mirage).
- **Avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā** — because this rootlessness is not known (avijjā), fabrications (saṅkhāra) arise, constructing an origin where none exists.
- The teacher warns against trying to match these observations to pre-existing conclusions or targets (e.g., "is this the same as that concept?"). The realisation (sākkhāt) must arise on its own through the inquiry, not by fitting an idea to a framework.
- This understanding requires a composed, settled mind (samādhi). It cannot be grasped in a single hearing or through rushed thinking.

## Summary

This discussion centres on the Pāli term tajjo samannāhāro, which the teacher translates as "born from that itself." He develops the meaning through the analogy of combustion: when oil is heated, a flame arises, yet the flame was not stored inside the oil, nor did it travel from some external source. The combustion arose right there — and yet one cannot point to an origin within combustion itself that explains its arising. This is what the Buddha asks practitioners to see.

The teacher connects this to the question of rebirth. The traditional narrative says a being who lived in a past life died and was reborn here — as though an existing entity transferred from one location to another. But the Buddha said: "It is not the same one who died, nor is it a different one." Both directions are blocked. If you cannot show that the same being continued, you cannot claim a being existed who then came. If you cannot show a different being arrived, you cannot claim an external origin either. The question of origin is itself dismantled.

This, the teacher explains, is the real depth of the statement that saṃsāra is anavarāgga — without discoverable beginning. It is not merely that the beginning is very far away. It is that there is no root (mūla) to be found. And where there is no root, there can be no end (aga) either. The arising and ceasing that we perceive — birth and death, the arising and passing of mental states — happens within this rootless space, and it is the failure to see this rootlessness that generates the entire chain of becoming.

The teacher then applies the same analysis to hearing. When a mind-moment of hearing arises, can one find within that moment where hearing came from? The hearing is tajjo samannāhāro — born right there. Its origin is untraceable within itself. Because this is not seen, the mind fills the gap with saññā — "it was the sound of a bird," "it was a vehicle." The Pāli formula yaṃ vedeti taṃ sañjānāti describes this: what is felt is what gets labelled. But the label itself, when examined closely, contains no actual content. The sound-label "bird" does not deliver understanding of hearing — it delivers a fabricated origin. This is the true meaning of marīci (mirage): recognition of something where nothing was actually understood. And this is why avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā — not knowing the rootlessness is what drives the fabrication.

The teacher emphasises that this inquiry must not be rushed or forced into a pre-existing framework. He explicitly warns against trying to match the emerging understanding to a target conclusion ("is this the same as that concept?"). The realisation must arise through sustained, composed contemplation. He likens it to planting a seed: provide the right conditions and it will grow; do not dig it up to check whether it matches your expectations.
