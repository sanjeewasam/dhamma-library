## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- The Buddha's **adze-handle simile** is referred to (the grooves worn into an adze/tool handle by the grip, which one cannot see happening from day to day, yet the wear occurs) — attributed to the Buddha but with no sutta title given.
- The terms *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, *sīlabbata-parāmāsa*, *viññāṇa* and *paññā* are used, but no sutta is named and no continuous Pāli passage is quoted. No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The first in the "*Kaḷaṇa Mitu* — good-friend dhamma discussion" series; a two-speaker dialogue. Its theme: **actions we are certain "I did" were never actually done by an "I" at all.**
- There are not two things — "it went right for me" and "it went wrong for me"; the pair cannot both apply. What is understood is only "the not-having-occurred" of the act.
- Everyday illustrations dominate: re-checking whether the laptop was put in the car, re-opening the door to confirm; driving off and then checking the side mirror to see whether the garage door was really closed; re-confirming, minutes later, something one has just done. The point: **if "I" had actually done it, there would be nothing to re-check.** The very habit of re-checking shows the deed was never really "mine"; the action is never caught in awareness at all.
- Just as with the Buddha's adze-handle, the wearing happens without being seen or caught. Here too, "success" requires that there be *no doer* — there is no doer in this.
- This is why the strengthening of *this* vision moves toward the cessation of the self-view (*sakkāya-nirodha*); the speaker links it to the Buddha calling *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* and *sīlabbata-parāmāsa* "already-finished matters."
- When wisdom is established, the feeling "I did it" ceases to arise. But this must first be grasped intellectually, at a reflective level, as a matter to be understood; only after being understood again and again does an awareness settle *within the not-understood nature itself* — an **extraordinary awareness** that is wisdom/vision (*paññā / dassana*), not thinking. This awareness is not "seeing with a mind"; where the act cannot be caught by a mind, no mind can see it.
- Even so, while such awareness is established, small errors still occur, because full arahantship has not been reached and consciousness (*viññāṇa*) still surfaces from below and leads at times. The Buddha's instruction "stand up, establish mindfulness" is carried out in that uncaught place by that awareness — yet small slips still happen there because *viññāṇa* still predominates.
- **The change-giving example** (parallel to the trishaw example elsewhere): handed Rs 500 for a fare mistakenly thought to be 340 (really 480), the person tries to give back 160 when only 20 is due. The analysis: "480" is not actually cognized in the moment; the thought and the deed are never connected; each is a mere coincidence (*ahambuva*). We feel something is "off" only afterwards, through "I made a mistake." Were there really a doer, he would realise mid-act, "I must take 480 — this is wrong"; he does not.
- **The red-light example:** cars in all five lanes stop at a red light, none running it — how, if there is no doer and each act is coincidence? Wouldn't half go and half stop? The teacher answers that it is done without any great effort, ungraspable exactly at the point where it "comes out right"; this is the very "knot" the Buddha said cannot be grasped. And the counter-instances — people who *do* run red lights — show that the whole thing is built precisely to *support the continuation of saṁsāra*: it is constructed so that it can always be confirmed as "done by a person."
- **The blindfold pot-breaking game (*kaṇāmuṭṭi*):** of ten players the seventh happens to strike the pot — do we say he did it by deliberation? It is struck by chance; a run of good hits looks like skill. All of it is done for sport, by coincidence.
- The heart of the talk: this whole process is fashioned so that it can be confirmed as "done by a someone" — and *that* is its illusion, its danger, its deception (*māyā*). What the Dhamma must reveal is that it is **not done by a person at all**. That seeing is the understanding, the wisdom, and the purpose of these discussions.

## Summary

This opening "good-friend" discussion is a two-way conversation built almost entirely from ordinary examples, all pressing a single point: the actions we are sure "I performed" were never actually performed by any "I." The speakers begin from the observation that "it went right for me" and "it went wrong for me" cannot both be true, because there is no doer to whom either could belong; what is really seen is only that the act never occurred as we suppose. To make this concrete, one speaker offers a string of familiar habits — re-checking whether the laptop went into the car, re-opening the door to be sure; driving off and then glancing in the side mirror to confirm the garage door closed; confirming, minutes later, something just done. The force of these examples is simple: if "I" had genuinely done the thing, there would be nothing left to verify. The compulsion to re-check betrays that the deed was never truly caught as "mine" — the action is never registered in awareness at all, much as, in the Buddha's adze-handle simile, the handle wears away under the grip without the wearing ever being seen.

From this they draw the structural conclusion that "success" in seeing this requires that there be no doer, and that the deepening of this very insight is what moves toward the cessation of the self-view (*sakkāya-diṭṭhi*). But the teacher is careful about how the insight matures. At first it must be grasped intellectually, at a reflective level, as a matter to be understood; only after it has been understood repeatedly does an awareness come to rest *within the not-understood nature itself* — an extraordinary awareness that is wisdom or vision rather than thought, and that cannot be described as "seeing with a mind," since where the act cannot be caught by a mind, no mind can see it. He adds an important qualification: even while such awareness is established, small errors still occur, because full liberation has not been reached and consciousness still rises from below and takes the lead at times. The Buddha's instruction to "stand and establish mindfulness" is carried out in that uncaught place, yet slips still happen there.

The examples then sharpen the paradox. In the change-giving case — handed 500 for a fare wrongly thought to be 340, the man tries to return 160 when only 20 is owed — the speakers show that the figure "480" is never actually present in the moment; the thought and the deed are never joined; each is a coincidence, and we sense something wrong only afterwards through "I made a mistake." Were a doer really present, he would catch the error mid-act; he never does. The red-light example seems to resist this: all five lanes stop, none runs the light, which looks impossible without a deliberate doer. The teacher answers that it happens without any effort, ungraspable precisely where it comes out right — the very "knot" the Buddha said cannot be seized — and that the people who *do* run red lights reveal the deeper design: the whole apparatus is built to *support the continuation of saṁsāra*, constructed so that it can always be confirmed as "done by a person." The blindfold pot-breaking game makes the same point playfully: the seventh player who happens to strike the pot did not do so by deliberation; chance hits look like skill. This, they conclude, is the illusion, the danger, the deception at the core of the whole thing — that it can always be read as "done by a someone" — and the entire purpose of the Dhamma, and of these discussions, is to reveal that it is not done by a person at all.
