## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- One canonical formula is directly quoted, unattributed: *cakkhuñca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhuviññāṇaṁ; sotañca paṭicca sadde ca …* ("dependent on eye and forms eye-consciousness arises; dependent on ear and sounds …").
- The terms *bhava-taṇhā* / *vibhava-taṇhā*, *rāga / dosa*, *upekkhā*, *phassa* (*passa*), *diṭṭha-suta-muta* and the path-stages (*sotāpanna, sakadāgāmī, anāgāmī*) are used, but no sutta is named. No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The tenth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation opening on **anger and the danger of "fitting" the Dhamma to a pre-formed conclusion.**
- A consultant's work is to point out others' faults. If one overlays the supramundane Dhamma onto this ("I must have no aversion, only compassion"), one still gets angry, and then suffers a disappointment — "I've heard so much Dhamma yet still get angry." The error is that the Dhamma has been used to reach a conclusion decided in advance ("I cannot be one who gets angry").
- What should instead be seen: the person is one thing, what he does is another, and between the two there is a mismatch — and in that gap lies **craving for non-existence** (*vibhava-taṇhā*): I want things as I like them and dislike the *absence* of what I want. "Absence" is something never actually obtained, so a collision arises against it. Merely to *recognise* this craving is enough; living in the world one may still act to correct others.
- For one on the path (*sotāpanna*, *sakadāgāmī*) anger still arises but is *recognised* differently; that recognition suffices. For an *anāgāmī*, anger does not arise at all, both *bhava-* and *vibhava-taṇhā* being abandoned. At the equanimity (*upekkhā*) level one can neither suffer nor be specially delighted; wisdom is strong and the mind becomes still — not by adding happiness, but by the wearing-away of what disturbs.
- **The absence-happiness illustrations.** We feel no special joy at being healthy; our contentment is really in the *absence of illness* that we hold underneath as "I am a well person." Likewise "no dog barking at me now," "no tiger here now" — we are fearless *through the non-existence* of the feared thing, and take that non-existence for happiness. Every mind has these two sides. We need not even think of the tiger now, because trouble comes only from *existing* things; and it is precisely this not-thinking that makes the underlying craving-for-absence invisible to us. Only when the absence flips to presence — "I fell ill" — are we shaken; the health that was present all along was never felt *as* health, because "well" needs the cause (illness) to be present, and that cause had not arisen.
- **The two-tile / gap (*hiḍasa*) analogy — central.** Between two tiles a line appears, and we assume the line exists "because of the two tiles." But the gap is a gap precisely because the tiles are *absent* there — the line is present exactly where tiles are not. So the line cannot really be made to *depend on* the two tiles; yet at first glance we see only "causes joined together." This applies to every sense-door — eye-and-form, ear-and-sound, all *diṭṭha-suta-muta* — and "because of these" (*nisā*) is exactly *saṁsāra*: a "because" that links things which in fact have no connection (*cakkhuñca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhuviññāṇaṁ*).
- **The second extreme.** When one sees "the gap was not in the two tiles," consciousness immediately supplies the opposite error: "then it can stand *apart from* the two — without the tiles." (As with "there is no chair where I sit" implying a chair exists somewhere else.) So one either gets caught *by* contact within the arising-side, or takes contact to exist *independent* of its conditions. Both are wrong. Contact neither stands in the two tiles nor apart from them — it is a *not-standing*; not knowing that not-standing, we build the two-sided story. The teacher pointedly refuses the student's premature conclusion "so contact cannot be obtained/cannot stand," warning that this is the realisation that comes *after* seeing contact, not a conclusion to be grasped in advance.
- **"Not-seeing."** Eye and form are present, yet "I didn't see it." We take up "seeing" through its very absence, so that "not-seeing" becomes real; but if eye-and-form did not truly arise, "seeing" itself never arose — we say "I didn't see" only because we do not grasp contact as contact. (Parallel to the earlier "shop I didn't notice" and "breath I didn't feel" examples: the cause never arose, so the "un-arisen" story can never come within consciousness's range.)
- **Method — see it everywhere, and do not try to "understand" your way out.** This must be established at every sense-door and moment, not confined to one simile; seen in one place only, it lasts only for that moment, while the knot is being struck unnoticed all day long. The knot was struck in *ignorance*, throughout saṁsāra — not deliberately ("I want to become a fool") — so it cannot be undone by deliberate understanding: "you climb out of the well by the very well you fell into," coming out through not-knowing, not through intellectual grasp. Understanding reached through consciousness ("I've clearly grasped this point," even "I saw contact") is just another delusion consciousness displays; true vision is not that.
- **Closing instruction:** watch, in every daily situation, both craving-for-existence and craving-for-non-existence — seeing the clinging and the colliding *together* — for that combined seeing, exercised in the living use of the world, is the skill of one in whom wisdom is maturing.

## Summary

The tenth good-friend discussion begins from a practical worry — anger — and quickly exposes a subtle trap in how the Dhamma is used. A consultant's job is to point out others' faults; but if one lays the supramundane teaching over this and resolves "I must feel no aversion, only compassion," one still becomes angry and is then left with a disappointment: so much Dhamma heard, and still the anger comes. The teacher identifies the mistake as using the Dhamma to reach a conclusion fixed in advance ("I cannot be someone who gets angry"). What ought to be seen instead is that the person is one thing and his conduct another, and that in the mismatch between them lies craving for non-existence: I want the situation as I like it and recoil from the *absence* of what I want. Because "absence" is never actually obtained, a collision forms against it — and simply recognising this craving is enough, for one may still act in the world to correct things. He distinguishes the stages here: on the path anger still arises but is recognised, and that recognition suffices; for the non-returner it no longer arises at all, both craving for existence and craving for non-existence having fallen away, leaving an equanimity in which one can neither suffer nor be specially pleased, the mind grown still not by any added happiness but by the wearing-away of what disturbed it.

He then illustrates the hidden craving-for-absence with a series of everyday cases. We feel no particular joy at being healthy; our contentment really lies in the *absence of illness*, which we carry underneath as the assumption "I am a well person." So too we are fearless because no dog is barking at us now, because no tiger is here now — fearless *through* the non-existence of the feared thing, which we quietly take for happiness. Every state of mind, he says, has these two sides, present and absent, though we never notice; and it is precisely because trouble comes only from *existing* things that we never need to think of the tiger, and so the underlying craving for its absence stays invisible. Only when the absence flips into presence — "I have fallen ill" — are we shaken, and only then is illness "well understood," while the health that was there all along was never felt as health, because "well" requires the cause, illness, to be present, and that cause had not yet arisen.

The heart of the talk is the two-tile analogy. Between two tiles a line appears, and we assume the line exists *because of* the tiles; but the gap is a gap precisely where the tiles are *absent*, so the line cannot truly be made to depend on them — yet at a glance we see only "causes joined." This, he stresses, holds at every sense-door — eye-and-form, ear-and-sound, all that is seen, heard and sensed — and the little word "because" is exactly saṁsāra: a link asserted between things that in fact have no connection, as in "dependent on eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises." But the moment one sees "the gap was not in the two tiles," consciousness rushes in with the opposite error — "then it can stand apart from them, without the tiles at all" — just as "there is no chair where I sit" makes us suppose a chair exists somewhere else. So one is caught either *by* contact within the arising, or by taking contact to exist independent of its conditions; both are wrong, for contact neither stands in the two tiles nor apart from them but is a *not-standing*, and it is our ignorance of that not-standing that builds the whole two-sided story. Here the teacher deliberately refuses the student's premature conclusion that "contact therefore cannot be obtained," warning that this is a realisation which comes only *after* contact is seen, not a formula to be adopted in advance.

He completes the picture with "not-seeing": eye and form are present, yet we say "I didn't see it," taking up "seeing" through its very absence so that not-seeing becomes real — though if eye-and-form never truly arose, seeing itself never arose, and we speak of "not seeing" only because we fail to grasp contact as contact. Finally he turns to method. This must be established at every door and in every moment, not confined to a single simile, for seen in one place only it lasts just that moment while the knot goes on being struck unnoticed all day. And crucially, the knot was tied in ignorance across the whole of saṁsāra, not by deliberate choice, so it cannot be untied by deliberate understanding: one climbs out of the well by the very well one fell into, emerging through not-knowing rather than through intellectual grasp. Any understanding reached through consciousness — "I've clearly seen this," even "I saw contact" — is only one more delusion consciousness displays, and true vision is not that. He closes by directing the student to watch, in every situation of daily life, both craving for existence and craving for non-existence, seeing the clinging and the colliding together, since that combined seeing, exercised in the living use of the world, is the skill of one in whom wisdom is ripening.
