## Sutta referred to
- No sutta is named by title. Several canonical phrases are directly quoted, unattributed:
  - *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ, sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ* ("whatever is of a nature to arise, all that is of a nature to cease") — attributed to **Koṇḍañña's** first realisation (named as a person, not a sutta).
  - *avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā* ("with ignorance as condition, formations") and the counterpart that with the ending of ignorance there is no formation — and that even "there is no formation" cannot be said.
  - the four factors of stream-entry: *kalyāṇa-mitta sevana, saddhamma savaṇa, yoniso manasikāra, dhammānudhamma paṭipadā*.
  - *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā* ("all formations are impermanent").
  - *na ca so na ca añño* ("neither the same one nor another"), applied to the lamp-flame.
- The teacher's own repeated refrain is *tatta tatta vipassati* — used to mean "moment by moment, there and there, it arises and ceases." It is not presented as a sutta title.
- The Buddha's teaching to **Ānanda** that of those who take up "the hindrances" some become bound for good destinations (*sugatigāmī*) and the rest for woeful ones (*dugatigāmī*) is recalled, and *pañca nīvaraṇa* (the five hindrances) is mentioned, but no sutta is named.

## Main dhamma points
- The eleventh "good-friend" dialogue; the two speakers develop, through many everyday examples, the single point that **no prior moment is ever actually the condition for a present one** — each moment arises from its own conditions and immediately ceases, so the "cause-becoming-condition" we assume never really occurs.
- **The looking-and-writing analysis:** when you look at a number, a perception ("two") knots with the act of looking; when you write, a fresh mind arises with its own perception. If the two perceptions happen to coincide we say "I looked and wrote it correctly" — but that is just another knot. The looking-mind died the instant it arose, so it can never be the condition for the writing-mind, and no comparison between them is ever actually possible in life.
- Hence, if "five" arises while writing though "two" was seen, one writes "five" and never catches the error in the act, because "two" (the dead looking-mind) cannot be present in the moment "five" is written. So work "going right" or "going wrong" alike happens **by chance** (*kaṇā* — a blind shot): right by chance, wrong by chance, like the blindfold pot-breaking game where one player hits and another misses, both equally blind.
- The teacher's refrain **"*tatta tatta vipassati*"** marks each of these: the money lent (the knot "he will repay" is dead once formed; his not-repaying later is a new, unconnected knot); the shopping list of four vegetables from which only three arise at the shop so only three are bought; the vitamin tablet placed for the grandmother though it was already given in the morning — in every case the later "problem" is a fresh knot, not connected to any earlier one, and "thinking/planning" is simply the manufacturing of a problem by knotting unconnected things.
- **The coconut-oil lamp:** the flame burning fifteen minutes on is not the flame first lit, but is produced by the oil-drop arriving at that very moment; each moment's flame comes from that moment's fuel, with no connection to the first. When the lamp goes out, what goes out is neither the originally-lit flame, nor born of it, nor apart from it — *na ca so na ca añño*, neither the same nor another; and so it is with the aggregates.
- Because the prior "cause" is always already dead (*nūpadicca* — un-arisen at the present moment), "the present is built by taking a dead past as condition" is something that can never actually happen. This is why one who sees this places no faith in plans or in "I'll do such-and-such in future": the future is un-arisen, the past is dead, and only a fabricated *saṅkhāra* bridges them.
- **Reinterpreting Koṇḍañña's insight:** *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ, sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ* is taken to mean that *both* the arising-cause and its effect are un-arisen — so *nirodha-dhamma* does **not** mean "it ends right there." An "ending" would require a "beginning"; where there is no starting-point there is no ending either. To assert annihilation (*natthi-anta*) is to have slipped straight into the eternalist extreme; the true sense is that the un-arisen has neither beginning nor end.
- **Mindfulness / inattention as unconnected knots:** when work goes wrong we cover it by saying "my mindfulness slipped — that was inattention," slapping the label "inattention" on it via consciousness. But the "mindfulness" we invoke as the missing cause is itself an *un-arisen* mindfulness — never actually found — so neither a real "mindfulness" nor a real "inattention" is ever obtained. The wisdom that sees this recognises that the very "mindfulness" we maintain is itself inattention — mindfulness seen as mindfulness (*sihiya sihiyen dækīma*).
- Living in the world by taking all this as real means the Four Noble Truths are never met and this *saṅkhāra-dukkha* is never seen — it "cannot be grasped," because what is ever really found is only *avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā*: the *absence* of ignorance is never found, formation-fabrication is never caught (it always registers as "right"), and even "there is no formation" is itself a formation.
- Because of all this, the teaching **cannot be delivered by a book**; it must be known through a *kalyāṇa-mitta*, which is why the Buddha placed *kalyāṇa-mitta sevana* first among the four factors of stream-entry, and why one is told not to take it merely from the canon.
- **On a monk's claim and on merit:** a monk had suggested that "75% could attain stream-entry" by the current style of practice; the speaker judges this not to fit the Dhamma, recalling that the Buddha did not say such a thing to Ānanda but rather that of those who take up "the hindrances," some are bound for good destinations and the rest for woeful ones — the point being that the seen-heard-sensed-cognized are four *maññanā* (conceivings) that must be seen *in practice*, not merely named. The speakers also touch on offering *dāna* at the temple, noting that a meritorious deed should still be done even while these subtleties are understood.

## Summary

This eleventh good-friend conversation is built almost entirely from homely examples, all pressing one relentless point: that no earlier moment is ever truly the condition for a later one. The speakers begin with the act of copying a number. When you look, a perception — say "two" — knots with the looking; when you write, an entirely fresh mind arises with its own perception, and only if the two happen to coincide do we congratulate ourselves that "I looked and wrote it correctly." But that coincidence is just one more knot, for the looking-mind died the moment it arose and can never be present to serve as the condition for the writing-mind. So when "five" arises at the moment of writing though "two" was seen, one writes five and never catches it, because the dead "two" cannot stand beside the living "five" to be compared. From this they draw the startling conclusion that success and failure alike happen by sheer chance — a "blind shot," like the blindfolded players at the pot-breaking game, one of whom hits and one of whom misses, both equally sightless.

The teacher marks each instance with his refrain, "*tatta tatta vipassati*" — moment by moment, there and there, it arises and ceases. Money lent on the promise "I'll repay" forms a knot that is finished the instant it is made; the later failure to repay is a wholly new knot, unconnected to the first, so there is no ground for resentment. A shopping list of four vegetables yields only three at the shop because only three perceptions arise there, and the fourth is "missed" not through any failed prior condition but simply because it never arose. A vitamin tablet is set out for the grandmother though it was already given that morning, and the knots — the morning dose, the tablet placed in the paper, the sudden "why did I put this?" — are each disconnected from the others, so that "thinking" and "planning" turn out to be nothing but the manufacture of problems by tying unconnected things together. The coconut-oil lamp seals the image: the flame burning after fifteen minutes is not the flame first lit but is produced by the oil-drop arriving just then, each moment's flame from that moment's fuel; and when the lamp finally dies, what dies is neither the original flame, nor something born of it, nor something wholly apart from it — neither the same nor another, exactly as with the aggregates.

From these examples the teacher builds the doctrinal turn. Because the "cause" is always already dead by the present moment, the very idea that "the present is fashioned by taking the dead past as its condition" can never actually occur; one who sees this places no trust in plans or in "I shall do such-and-such later," since the future is un-arisen and the past is finished, and only a fabricated formation pretends to bridge them. He rereads Koṇḍañña's insight — "whatever is of a nature to arise, all that is of a nature to cease" — to mean that both the arising cause and its effect are un-arisen, so that "cessation" does not signify "it ends right here"; an ending would demand a beginning, and where there is no starting-point there is no terminus, so to declare annihilation is already to have fallen into eternalism. He then exposes how we cover our errors: when something goes wrong we say "my mindfulness slipped — that was inattention," but the very "mindfulness" we invoke as the missing cause is an un-arisen mindfulness, never actually found, so that neither a real mindfulness nor a real inattention is ever obtained, and the wisdom that sees this recognises that the mindfulness we so carefully maintain is itself inattention.

He closes the teaching by insisting that to live taking all this as real is precisely why the Four Noble Truths are never met and this formation-suffering is never seen: what is ever really found is only "with ignorance as condition, formations," for the absence of ignorance is never found, the fabricating is never caught because it always registers as "right," and even "there is no formation" is itself a formation. This, he says, is why the teaching cannot be gotten from a book but only through a good friend — which is exactly why the Buddha set the association with a *kalyāṇa-mitta* first among the four factors of stream-entry, and why one is warned not to accept it merely because it stands in the canon. The talk ends with a critical aside about a monk's claim that most could attain stream-entry through the current style of practice — which the speaker finds unfitting, recalling that the Buddha told Ānanda instead that some who take up the hindrances go to good destinations and the rest to woeful ones, since the seen, heard, sensed and cognized are four conceivings that must be seen in practice, not merely named — and with a brief, warm exchange about offering alms at the temple before the customary salutations.
