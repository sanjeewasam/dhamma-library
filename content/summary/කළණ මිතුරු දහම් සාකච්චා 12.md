## Sutta referred to
- No sutta is named by title.
- The **Aṅgulimāla** episode is referred to: the Buddha's words "I have stopped/stilled (*ṭhito ahaṁ*); you stop" — recounted as teaching, with Aṅgulimāla named as a person, but no sutta title given.
- The technical pair *rūpa-kāya adhivacana-samphassa* (designation-contact of the form-body) and *nāma-kāya paṭigha-samphassa* (impact-contact of the name-body) is quoted as doctrinal terminology.
- Terms used include *ānāpānasati*, *upekkhā*, *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, *mūla moha / mūla avijjā*, *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*, *uccheda*, *asaññā* (non-percipient realms), and *nirāyāsa* (effortless). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The twelfth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation centred on **meditation, the feeling of "lightness/relief," equanimity (*upekkhā*), and the true "stilling."**
- **The heavy-bag analogy:** carrying a heavy bag with effort and then setting it down, one exclaims "what relief!" — but that "lightness" is simply the state one was in *before* picking up the bag; setting it down only returns one to where one already was. No new thing arrived.
- Applied to meditation: refining the object (breath) finer and finer until it subsides is likewise just a *return* to the objectless state one was in all along. So "I meditated and grew subtle" is like lifting a bag only to set it down and call it relief — one was already in that subtle, objectless state; nothing extra was gained.
- Hence the objectless, non-thinking state *is* meditation, and we are always in it; the meditator merely, with great effort, suppresses all other perceptions by clinging to a single perception (*ānāpānasati*) — exactly like carrying the bag. The real purpose of meditation is to steady the mind so as to *see the true nature* and arouse wisdom (*paññā*).
- **If wisdom is not aroused,** reaching the objectless state is only *upekkhā* — but not seen *as* equanimity; instead it is the root delusion (*mūla moha*), pure not-knowing. One is then deceived into taking it for cessation (*nirodha*): "lights appear," "I float up." These are the seen-heard-sensed becoming real again — a "one who feels" reappears through self-view (*sakkāya-diṭṭhi*), like a flower blooming: lightness is felt and a feeler of lightness exists. So the whole thing falls straight back into *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* and cannot remove the self-view. The self-view is not removed by "removing"; it dissolves only when it is seen that seeing-and-not-seeing, at the level of not-knowing, is never grasped as not-known.
- **Why suppression is not cessation:** in meditation feeling is made real (through the breath), so no fault is seen in feeling; when the breath grows subtle and is no longer felt, one slips into "not-feeling" (*uccheda*) — caught there without realising it, for lack of wisdom. Those caught in this annihilation-extreme reach the Brahma-worlds and the non-percipient realms (*asaññā*) — the fruit of suppressing perception. Feeling cannot be denied because the breath is felt, and the breath cannot be denied because feeling is real on this side; this is the "magician at the four-way junction" — once formed, it cannot be denied from within.
- Whatever the practice, when one grasps a knife one knows "a knife" — grasped *as* a knife, not "knife seen as knife" (perception seen as mere perception); attention rests on feeling. It is grasped without knowing *rūpa-kāya adhivacana-samphassa* and *nāma-kāya paṭigha-samphassa* — that a knife has merely been knotted onto feeling. Even one who has reached root-equanimity still grasps "a knife," but now with the wisdom that a knife was tied onto feeling.
- **The door-locking example (revisited):** you re-check whether you locked the door, having "forgotten" — yet you did lock it, because you were in the effortless (*nirāyāsa*) state; the locking happened by feeling (you touched the door), but since feeling is not cognized, "a felt door" is not grasped either. We cover this by saying "my mindfulness lapsed, I was in another awareness," and so bury the truly precious place — for the real mindfulness *was* there; that effortless state is our true nature and *is* nibbāna. "Dwelling in nibbāna" means exactly this. We fail to catch it as nibbāna only because we file it under "I was inattentive."
- **The planet-walking analogy:** on some planet people walk from birth; a visitor from elsewhere asks "who can stop? is there such a thing as stopping?" and everyone anxiously searches how to stop — go left? right? — precisely because they do not know they are always walking. Had they known they only ever walk, they would not seek its opposite. Just so, being always in "meditation" is not-knowing, and we suppose we must *do* something to stop or to still.
- **Aṅgulimāla:** the Buddha said "I have stilled; you stop" — meaning not that he had stopped *walking*, but that even while going he was stilled, for the Buddha had neither a foot nor a great earth "engaged" in the act of walking. So if, while walking, "walking" is not cognized, and while hearing, "hearing" is not cognized, then nowhere is anything really felt or understood — one is stilled anyway. This is action never being caught: wisdom is that action does not establish and contact does not establish, so one is *already* still. Like the planet-dwellers who, seeing that walking is all there is, have nothing to seek in "stopping," one who sees likewise is always already stilled and no longer goes round hunting for methods to stop.

## Summary

This twelfth good-friend conversation turns on meditation and the seductive feeling of "lightness," and it opens with a deceptively simple image. When you carry a heavy bag with effort and then set it down, you sigh, "what relief!" — but the teacher points out that this lightness is nothing new: it is exactly the state you were in *before* you lifted the bag, and setting it down only returns you to where you already were. He maps this straight onto meditation. Refining the breath finer and finer until the object subsides is likewise only a return to the objectless state in which you were resting all along; so "I meditated and became subtle" is like hoisting a bag just to put it down and call the result relief. The objectless, non-thinking state, in other words, simply *is* meditation, and we are always in it; the meditator merely labours to suppress every other perception by clutching a single one — the breath — which is the very act of carrying the bag. The true aim of meditation, he insists, is not this suppression but the steadying of the mind so that the real nature can be seen and wisdom aroused.

The danger, then, is doing all this without wisdom. Reaching the objectless state without insight is only equanimity — but equanimity not recognised as such, and therefore experienced as the root delusion itself, pure not-knowing. Here one is easily deceived into mistaking it for cessation: lights appear, one seems to float, and through the self-view a "feeler" reappears like a flower opening, so that lightness is felt and someone exists who feels it. The whole attainment thus collapses back into the very self-view it was meant to dissolve, and cannot touch it — because self-view is never removed by an act of removal, but only dissolves when one sees that, at the level of not-knowing, seeing and not-seeing are never grasped as not-known. The speaker makes the mechanism explicit: in meditation the breath makes feeling real, so no fault is seen in feeling; when the breath grows too subtle to be felt, one slides into "not-feeling," the annihilation-extreme, caught there unawares for want of wisdom — and this, he notes, is how those who suppress perception reach the Brahma-worlds and the non-percipient realms. Feeling cannot be denied while the breath is felt, and the breath cannot be denied while feeling is real; this is the magician at the crossroads, undeniable once formed. Whatever one's practice, grasping a knife one knows "a knife" — grasped as a knife, not as mere perception — attention fixed on feeling, without knowing that a knife has simply been knotted onto feeling through designation-contact and impact-contact; even one who has reached root-equanimity still grasps "a knife," but now with the wisdom of how it was tied on.

From here the teacher returns to the familiar door-locking example to locate the true state. You re-check whether you locked the door, sure you have "forgotten" — yet you did lock it, because you were in the effortless state, the locking done by feeling as your hand met the door; but since feeling itself is not cognized, no "felt door" is grasped either. We paper over this by declaring "my mindfulness slipped, I was in some other awareness," and in doing so we bury the most precious place of all, for the real mindfulness was present there: that effortless nature is our true nature and is itself nibbāna, and "dwelling in nibbāna" means precisely this — we simply fail to recognise it, having filed it under inattention. He closes with two images that seal the point. On some planet, people walk from birth, and when a visitor asks whether anyone can "stop," they all rush about searching for the way to stop — only because they do not know they are always walking; had they known walking is all there is, they would never seek its opposite. And so the Buddha told Aṅgulimāla, "I have stilled; you stop" — not that he had ceased walking, but that even in going he was stilled, having neither a foot nor a great earth engaged in the act of walking. If, while walking, "walking" is never cognized, and while hearing, "hearing" is never cognized, then nothing anywhere is truly felt, and one is stilled already: action never establishes, contact never establishes, and one who sees this — like the planet-dwellers who find nothing to seek in "stopping" — is always, already still, and stops running round in search of a method to stop.
