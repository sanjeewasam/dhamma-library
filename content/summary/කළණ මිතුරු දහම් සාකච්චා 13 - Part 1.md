## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- One canonical line is directly quoted, unattributed: *paccuppannañ ca yo dhammaṁ tattha tattha vipassati* ("and whatever dhamma is present, one sees it right there") — rendered by the speaker as "*pacchuppanna yo dhammaṁ tatta tatta vipassati*." Presented as a doctrinal phrase, not tied to a named sutta.
- The four *āsavas* — *avijjā-āsava, bhavāsava, kāmāsava, diṭṭhāsava* — are used as terms, along with *saṅkhāra*, *saññā / vedanā*, and *nitya-saññā*. No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- Part 1 of the thirteenth "good-friend" dialogue, continuing directly the "throwing away the tea" example from the previous discussion. A two-speaker conversation.
- A mind cannot arise without a condition, and "throwing away" cannot occur without a condition: in ordinary life something happens (rightly or wrongly) only when a perception (*saññā*) knots with an action. The cold tea is thrown into the sink only because the perception "cold tea can't be drunk" knots with the act of carrying the cup.
- The knot is struck **without anyone striking it** — we do not even know how it was struck. Yet we build a "relationship" out of it — "I did this work" — which gives the sense that the action is under my control. When the outcome matches, "my work / my mindfulness is good"; every result thus supports the view "this is correct," which is only "*I* am correct." When it goes wrong we take it as "my fault, my mindfulness lapsed."
- So the knot can never be seen, because "I" am standing there to answer for *both* sides: as long as ignorance persists, "I" persist, so a right result is credited to me and a wrong one is charged to me. Either way the self is confirmed.
- **The four āsavas:** the matching-and-throwing happened through an *āsava*, and *āsavas* arise from not-knowing (*avijjā*) — *āsava* and *avijjā* condition each other, and the *saṅkhāra* that forms while ignorance persists is what is separately named *āsava*. The four cannot be taken apart; they arise as one lump in a single moment and are separated only for analysis, all four present in each: *diṭṭhāsava* is the "I / mine did it" sense, *bhavāsava* is "these exist and persist," together with *kāmāsava* and *avijjā-āsava*.
- **Two eternalised perceptions in one event:** the perception "cold tea is thrown away" (which knots with the cup and sends the tea to the sink) works alongside the perception "a cup is not-to-be-thrown" (eternalised through *absence* — the cup simply remains). Neither the throwing of the tea nor the keeping of the cup is done by thinking. To someone with a microwave the "empty cup" case never arises, because their eternalised perception is "a tea-bearing cup, to be reheated" — so which way the act turns depends entirely on the perception that knots.
- The cup is like "a black stone on the road" or "the ground underfoot" — 100% eternalised, never questioned, requiring no thought; whereas the leftover tea is not eternalised at all (throw / don't throw is undecided). "Not throwing the cup anywhere else in the house" is likewise sure, holding all the way to the sink.
- An action requires a cause–effect relation to be constructed, and the knotting is exactly what we take as the "cause": "Why throw the tea? — it's cold, can't be drunk." The act (throwing) and the form (the tea, its colour/shape/*samudaya*) gain value only by becoming *conditions* for each other, and the moment they mutually condition, "I" arise along with them — "I threw it without mindfulness."
- **Action is empty of action.** "Throwing" is always joined only with the perception "tea"; without "tea," "throwing" has no meaning — as "hitting" requires a mosquito or a dog, else "hit" said in the air is a dud with no object. The thing done is never found *within* the action, so the action is empty of action; "killing" is a thing only because a mosquito or dog belongs to it, yet that mosquito or dog is found only within the killing-concept, never really — so "killing" too is empty of killing. All three — doer, doing, and thing-done — are fabricated by the *āsavas*, which do not come from elsewhere but spring up within the very event, and once sprung contain all four.
- **The culminating point:** at the sink, whatever "plan" was in the mind, the event is created by the perception that knots *there*, not by the plan — it cannot be planned, so it is not done by a person. Invoking *paccuppannañ ca yo dhammaṁ tattha tattha vipassati*: whatever arises now by conditions, see it "there and there" (*tattha tattha*); doing so, wisdom comes that this can never be a person's plan — proven because his plan was one thing and what happened was another, and so the self-view breaks.
- When the event happens to come out right we never even name a "knot" or feel a doubt — "I thought it, I did it," confirmed, nothing more to seek. But the real value lies in seeing "there and there": the perception "reheat + tea" at the moment of grasping versus "throw + tea" at the sink are two minds that can never co-arise, so no comparison or measuring of right-and-wrong is ever actually possible — yet we constantly "compare," doing the impossible. To see this is to see the non-occurrence (*novīma*): what is here is only a non-occurrence, an impossibility (*abhavya*).

## Summary

This first part of the thirteenth good-friend discussion picks up the "throwing away the tea" example from the previous conversation and works it into a close analysis of how actions, and the "I" who seems to perform them, are fabricated. The speakers begin from the principle that no mind and no act can arise without a condition: in ordinary life something happens — rightly or wrongly — only when a perception knots with an action. The cold tea goes into the sink only because the perception "cold tea can't be drunk" knots with the act of carrying the cup. The crucial observation is that this knot strikes without anyone striking it; we do not even know how it was struck. Yet out of it we manufacture a "relationship," the sense that "I did this," and with it the conviction that the action lies under my control. When the result matches our expectation we praise "my work, my good mindfulness," so that every outcome quietly confirms "this is correct" — which is only "I am correct" — and when it goes wrong we file it under "my fault, my mindfulness slipped." Either way the self is upheld, and precisely for that reason the knot itself can never be seen: as long as ignorance persists, an "I" stands ready to take credit for success and blame for failure alike.

From here the teacher brings in the language of the *āsavas* (taints/influxes). The matching and throwing occurred through an *āsava*, and *āsavas* spring from not-knowing, each conditioning the other, so that the formation arising while ignorance persists is just what is separately named *āsava*. The four — of ignorance, of existence, of sensuality, of views — cannot be prised apart; they arise as a single lump in one moment and are distinguished only for analysis, every one of them present in each: the view-taint as the "I / mine did it," the existence-taint as "these things are and persist," bundled together with the taints of sensuality and ignorance. He then shows two eternalised perceptions operating in the very same event. The perception "cold tea is thrown away" knots with the cup and sends the tea to the sink, while the perception "a cup is not to be thrown" is eternalised through sheer absence — the cup simply stays in hand. Neither the discarding of the tea nor the keeping of the cup is done by thinking; and to a person with a microwave the "empty cup to discard" case never even arises, because their eternalised perception is "a tea-bearing cup to be reheated." Which way the act turns is thus decided entirely by the perception that happens to knot. The cup, he notes, is like a stone on the road or the ground underfoot — a hundred percent eternalised, never questioned, requiring no thought at all — whereas the leftover tea is not eternalised in the least, its fate undecided.

The teacher then presses the point that an action can only exist once a cause-and-effect relation has been constructed, and that the knotting is exactly what we seize as the "cause": "Why throw the tea? — it's cold." The act of throwing and the form of the tea acquire value only by becoming conditions for one another, and in the very moment they mutually condition, "I" arise together with them, complete with "I threw it without mindfulness." This leads to his sharpest formulation: action is empty of action. "Throwing" is always joined only to the perception "tea"; strip away "tea" and "throwing" means nothing, just as "hitting" requires a mosquito or a dog, without which "I hit" spoken into the air is a hollow word with no discoverable object. The thing done is never found within the action itself, so the action is empty of action; "killing" seems a real thing only because a mosquito or dog belongs to it, yet that creature is found only inside the killing-concept and never in fact — so killing too is empty of killing. Doer, doing and thing-done are all fabricated together by the taints, which arrive from nowhere but well up within the event and, once arisen, contain all four.

The discussion culminates at the sink. Whatever plan was in the mind, the event is created by the perception that knots there and not by the plan; it cannot be planned, and therefore is not done by any person. Invoking the line "and whatever dhamma is present, one sees it right there," the teacher urges that whatever arises now by conditions be seen "there and there," for in that seeing comes the wisdom that this can never be a person's doing — proven by the fact that the plan was one thing while what happened was another, so that the self-view itself breaks. When the event happens to come out right we never notice any knot or feel any doubt; we simply certify "I thought it, I did it," and look no further. But the whole value lies in seeing the "there and there": the perception "reheat the tea" at the moment of grasping and the perception "throw the tea" at the sink are two minds that can never arise together, so no comparison or measuring of right against wrong is ever actually possible — and yet we compare ceaselessly, forever doing the impossible. To see this clearly is to see the non-occurrence: what is present here is only a non-occurrence, an impossibility.
