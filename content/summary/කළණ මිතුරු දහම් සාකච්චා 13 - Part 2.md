## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Several canonical phrases/verses are directly quoted, unattributed:
  - *aññā-eva saññā uppajjati, aññā-eva saññā nirujjhati* ("one perception arises, another perception ceases").
  - *na ca so na ca añño* ("neither the same one nor another").
  - the verse *na-y-idaṁ attakataṁ bimbaṁ, na-y-idaṁ parakataṁ aghaṁ; hetuṁ paṭicca sambhūtaṁ, hetu-bhaṅgā nirujjhati* ("this image is not self-made, nor made by another; arisen dependent on a cause, with the breakup of the cause it ceases").
  - *diṭṭhe diṭṭhamattaṁ bhavissati* ("in the seen there will be merely the seen").
- These are used as doctrinal phrases; no sutta title is given for any, so none should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- Part 2 of the thirteenth "good-friend" dialogue, continuing the tea/lamp analysis. A two-speaker conversation.
- **The manufactured continuity:** "the same person who took the tea came to the sink, and that same person threw it" — from this we build the puzzle "why did he throw what he took to reheat?" and thereby fabricate a persisting self: the one who started is the one who finished. But the one who started can never actually be the one who finishes.
- **The lamp-flame** seals it: the flame first lit is not the flame that goes out; at each moment the arriving drop of oil is the condition for that moment's flame, so no connection can be built between the flame lit and the flame extinguished. *Aññā-eva saññā uppajjati, aññā-eva saññā nirujjhati* — one perception arises, another ceases; the very one that arose is never experienced dying, and the one that dies is never experienced arising. Hence *na ca so na ca añño* — one cannot say "another lamp went out," nor "the same lamp."
- The task is doubly hard: one must *see* the relation (this cause → this effect) and, *within that very seeing*, see that it is not a relation — without pasting any "separate thing" onto it. One must see arising (*samudaya*), and within seeing arising, see the non-occurrence.
- **The "birth" story unravels at contact (*phassa / passa*).** The whole talk of a birth is spoken only because a cause-and-effect (oil-drop → flame) was constructed; but contact is always the nature of *an un-arisen cause*, so where "flame exists / flame doesn't exist" cannot even be said, "flame arose / flame ceased" cannot be reached either, the oil-condition itself being un-arisen.
- **The verse "not self-made, not made by another."** Against "it happens spontaneously," the Buddha's verse is quoted: this image is neither self-made nor made by another; it arises dependent on a cause and ceases with the cause's breakup. So the flame is not made by me, not by another, not spontaneous — it arose dependent on the oil-drop; to make a flame one cannot simply make it, for cause, effect, and the burning (contact) must set together for the event to form.
- Once the event is built, the two extremes are argued — "this is, so that is; no oil, no flame" — but turning this over is merely worldly (*lokīya*); it is *phassa* that manufactures the "because of this, this / without this, not this." The verse's line *hetu-bhaṅgā nirujjhati* is examined: *nirujjhati* means *ceasing* (non-arising, *anuppāda*), not annihilation, and *bhaṅga* does not mean the cause is *destroyed*. The very same words carry a worldly sense on the surface and a supramundane sense when drawn out beyond the words: read with vision (*dassana*) they point to the deathless, read without it they give only the world's meaning. This, again, is why one must "not take it from the piṭaka" — a sutta-expounding teacher without vision delivers a distorted analysis.
- **"I can do."** As long as the feeling "I can do" persists, one's erring cannot stop; this "I can do" is claimed even for what one never did, and it arises only from the duality of do/not-do. Seen as it really is, the whole thing drops away. If contact is not seen in its true form, feeling and perception inevitably knot, and from that knotting proliferation (*papañca*) is built; seen rightly, there is no going to feeling-and-perception and no proliferation.
- **The extended probe.** The teacher presses: in every action, see the condition (*pratyaya*) as un-arisen *now*. Sitting, one does not even cognize "chair, body, the two touching" — why? Because no attention (*manasikāra*) occurred, no condition was met. When the student keeps "jumping ahead" to the next stage, the teacher pulls him back to the bare moment in which nothing is understood, insisting that the capacity to *see* right there must be developed.
- He drives toward the root: a feeling arose, and from it "no-feeling" is taken, yet even "it is not felt" is not understood — and *why* is it not understood? Not because there is merely "no cause for a consciousness to arise" (which the teacher grants), but because the very *condition* has not been read out. Saying "it is all not-knowing," parrot-fashion, never reveals the conditioned relation. Seeing the condition directly is precisely how one realises that *the condition is never actually found*: "seeing arising, arising is abandoned" is the realisation of cessation, and cessation is not realised only because we never truly see the condition.
- **Feeling and not-feeling as the two ends of the knot.** Contact is two things knotting, and we see only one end of the knot; the other end is never caught — that never-caught side is what he is asking about. *Udaya-vaya* (rise-and-fall) is existence and non-existence together; seeing both at once, one realises that both are *not found*, and only then is contact's cessation (*passa-nirodha*) realised. Not-feeling can never be understood because feeling itself is never found; if feeling is a mere view, "not-feeling" cannot be spoken; and one who never recognises "feeling" cannot recognise "not-feeling."
- **The born-blind (*jātyandha*) analogy** makes it sharpest: one blind from birth never knew seeing, so cannot understand "not-seeing," has no notion that a form was ever there, and none that a form was removed — he simply does not know seeing at all. We are exactly like him at every moment, which is why this is not cognized by consciousness. Such a thing lies *outside consciousness's range* (*agocara*), and realising that out-of-range nature *is* the equanimity level (*upekkhā*), the very place we stand — where wisdom must come, to see in a single moment that both "it is felt" and "it is not felt" are not understood.
- **The action-side caution.** "Feeling" seems obtained because of a felt thing, yet feeling is never actually obtained; but if one concludes "because of a felt thing, no feeling is obtained," one slides into the view "feeling exists but I never get it" — the nihilist extreme, searching outside. What is meant instead is seeing that feeling *and* non-feeling are both not understood — this is *diṭṭhe diṭṭhamattaṁ bhavissati*, "in the seen, merely the seen," where nothing is found. So all dhamma-statements — "seeing did not arise, seeing is not found, seeing arises from ignorance" — are just talk about the un-found, easily parroted without seeing contact; they are stories built from *not* seeing contact. To hold "I can understand" is itself the distortion — like bolting the stable after the horse has fled.

## Summary

This second part of the thirteenth good-friend discussion carries the tea and lamp examples into a rigorous examination of continuity, causation and contact. The speakers first expose how a persisting self is manufactured: because "the same person who took the tea came to the sink and threw it," we frame the puzzle "why throw what he meant to reheat?" and so fabricate a continuous doer, the one who began being the one who ended — though, the teacher insists, the one who began can never in fact be the one who ends. The lamp-flame proves it: the flame first lit is not the flame that finally goes out, since at every instant the arriving oil-drop is the condition for that instant's flame, and no thread can be run between the lighting and the extinguishing. "One perception arises, another perception ceases"; the very perception that arose is never caught in dying, nor the dying one in arising — so it is "neither the same nor another." The genuine task, he stresses, is doubly delicate: to see the relation of cause to effect and, in that same seeing, to see that it is not a relation, without smuggling in any separate thing — to see arising and, within arising, its non-occurrence.

The whole talk of a "birth," he shows, is spoken only because a cause-and-effect was first constructed, and it unravels precisely at contact, which is always the nature of an un-arisen cause: where one cannot even say "the flame exists or doesn't," one cannot reach "the flame arose or ceased," the oil-condition itself being un-arisen. Against the lazy notion that things "just happen by themselves," he quotes the verse that this image is neither self-made nor made by another, but arises dependent on a cause and ceases with the cause's breakup — not by me, not by another, not spontaneously. Once the event is assembled, the two extremes get argued back and forth — "this is, so that is; no oil, no flame" — but this, he says, is merely worldly turning-over; it is contact that fabricates the very "because of this, this." Examining the verse's phrase "with the breakup of the cause it ceases," he is careful that "ceases" means non-arising, not annihilation, and that "breakup" does not mean the cause is destroyed. The same words, he notes, bear a worldly meaning on the surface and a supramundane one when drawn out beyond the words — read with vision they point to the deathless, read without it they give only the world's sense — which is exactly why the teaching cannot be taken straight from the canon, since a learned expounder lacking vision delivers a distorted reading.

The heart of Part 2 is a patient, probing dialogue in which the teacher refuses the student's tendency to leap ahead. He keeps returning him to the bare moment in which, sitting, one does not even cognize "chair" or "body touching chair," because no attention has occurred and no condition has been met — and then presses beneath that to why even the attention failed to form, insisting the answer lies in never having seen contact as contact. He drives toward the root: a feeling arises, "no-feeling" is taken from it, yet even "it is not felt" goes unrecognised, and this is not merely because "there is no cause for consciousness" but because the condition itself has never been read out. To chant "it is all not-knowing" like a parrot, he warns, never reveals the conditioned relation; only seeing the condition directly discloses that the condition is never actually found, which is what "seeing arising, arising is abandoned" — the realisation of cessation — really means. Contact, he explains, is two things knotting, and we ever see only one end of the knot while the other is never caught; that uncaught end is what he is asking after, for rise-and-fall are existence and non-existence together, and only seeing both at once reveals that both are not found. Not-feeling can never be understood because feeling itself is never found; one who never recognises feeling cannot recognise its absence — exactly as one born blind, never having known sight, can form no notion either of "not seeing" or of a form once present and now removed. We are, he says, precisely like the born-blind at every moment, which is why this lies outside the range of consciousness; and to realise that out-of-range nature is the equanimity level itself, the very ground on which we stand, where wisdom must finally arise to see in one instant that "it is felt" and "it is not felt" are alike not understood. He closes by guarding the action-side against a false turn: feeling seems obtained through a felt thing yet is never really obtained, but to conclude "so no feeling is ever obtained" is to fall into the nihilist view of searching outside for a feeling one supposedly lacks. What is meant is only to see that feeling and non-feeling are both un-understood — "in the seen, merely the seen," where nothing is found — so that all the pious statements about seeing "not arising" or "arising from ignorance" are recognised as mere talk about the un-found, parroted without ever seeing contact, and the conviction "I can understand" as the distortion it is: bolting the stable long after the horse has gone.
