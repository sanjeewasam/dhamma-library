## Sutta referred to
- **Kālakārāma Sutta** (කාලකාරාම / කාලතාරාම සූත්‍රය) — named: cited for the Buddha's teaching that nothing and no one can be obtained through not-knowing (*nodänīma*).
- The phrase *diṭṭhi-nijjhāna-kkhanti* ("acquiescing to a view after pondering it") is quoted as a doctrinal term, but no sutta is named for it.
- Terms used include *manasikāra*, *anusaya*, *bhava*, *udayabbaya-ñāṇa*, *passa-samudaya*, *pañcupādānakkhandha*, *paṭiccasamuppāda*, *sammā-diṭṭhi*, *saddhamma-savana*, and *kalyāṇa-mitta āsraya*. No further sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- Part 1 of the fourteenth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation continuing the breath and "manufactured continuity" themes.
- **How the breath fabricates a past and a self:** the moment one thinks of the breath — "I've been breathing since morning without noticing; if I hadn't breathed I'd be dead, so I must have persisted" — the attention (*manasikāra*) arising *now* constructs a past: a breath un-noticed since morning, a nose that persisted, an "I" that persisted, remembered only now. This gives a false persistence (*bhava*) and an "I" spanning past, present and future.
- Invoking the *Kālakārāma Sutta*, the teacher notes the Buddha taught that nothing can be obtained through not-knowing; so "I persisted through a not-knowing present since morning" is impossible — pure view (*diṭṭhi*). No breath was ever obtained or arose in past, present or future; there is only a thought, a feeling, a mere attention. Even "the breath to be obtained" that we discuss is only *anusaya-manasikāra* fabricated by consciousness.
- **Caution against turning insight into analysis:** when one sees through this, the very knotting/engaging (*yedīma*) must *stop*; if one keeps applying the analysis "I cannot obtain a self / a thing," one merely re-inherits it. The Buddha said that, once established, existence and non-existence are not even in his vocabulary — such statements do not arise. So one should not even say "feeling can't be obtained, non-feeling can't be obtained"; more simply, "no difference can be grasped even by force."
- When teaching others one must use words, but the real work is on one's own side: to notice the shift the instant one *sees* (as against merely hearing) — a settled awareness in which no consciousness-forming occurs, a place where nothing is understood.
- **Even "empty" cannot be said of it.** If that nature is understood as "void/empty," that is still another consciousness understanding it; since the nature can take no object, "empty of what?" When *samudaya* occurs, "there is a thing"; when "there is nothing" is understood, that is another *samudaya* — the "contact-arising extreme." Even *udayabbaya-ñāṇa* is qualified: at the true nature there is no arising at all, and if something "arose," it arose by view. So the "empty" understanding is itself one more conceiving (*maññanā*).
- **We speak from within delusion.** At the true state "it never was, cannot be, will not be" — this cannot be said while established there; but from here, still deluded, one can say "we are deluded now." The scarecrow analogy: seeing a scarecrow as a man, then on approach recognising "not a man"; yet even recognising "delusion" is only relative to a prior delusion, and seen by right view one cannot even say "I *was* deluded" (that would be an un-formed thing) — so there is no consolation "I was previously deluded."
- **Comparison itself cannot really be done.** To compare the "deep-thinking state" that arose during the discussion with the present state requires "a former" and "an empty now," and to say "I was deluded before, cannot be deluded now" is itself a consciousness-level. To see a view *as* a view is *sammā-diṭṭhi*; but if "before was X, now is Y" is understood, that is only a consciousness level.
- Everything one says is merely a "quality/attribute" (*guṇa*) — outer, distorted perceptions laid over the nature; one must see that even "this is such-and-such" is wrong, that *any* pattern of understanding is flawed.
- **On teaching nibbāna:** to explain it, one says "you understood, didn't you?" and the listener seems to grasp it as true — and *that very "understanding"* must then be shown to be wrong. We seize everything comparatively (*diṭṭhi-nijjhāna-kkhanti*), which is a consciousness level; yet without hearing (*saddhamma-savana*, association with a *kalyāṇa-mitta*) it cannot be understood either. So when telling someone "such a nature exists," one must add "do not take even 'such a nature' — that too is a view," including "my saying this is itself a view, since wisdom is not established" — so the listener does not grasp but keeps seeing the flaw.
- **The whole "I existed" fabrication** — past, present and future, "I was, I am, I will be" — is assembled at this constructing point: the five clung-to aggregates gathered, dependent origination built from a cause-effect dhamma, a persisting nature, a world, an inner and outer — the entire lump sits right there.
- **Nibbāna is within saṁsāra:** "we made it neither saṁsāra nor apart from saṁsāra." We understand "saṁsāra" as a thing; within that very understanding, one must see the non-understanding — that is cessation within arising. The world sees "going," but for one who has seen, going happens while within the going stands a wisdom that never catches "him," set where action is out of range (*kriyāva agocara*) — for every action. He understands "there is an action; it is not that I existed before it and now do not" — the nature is simply so, only its un-understandable-ness remaining. To understand "empty" there is one more conceiving done in ignorance — seeking the non-void within the void.
- **A warning on speaking:** whatever appears, one gains the ability to speak without clinging; but while speaking the mind unknowingly sinks into the words, and later "I didn't mean it that way," though at that moment the point was upheld as maximally true — that clinging must stop, and one should speak as if waving a toy to distract a child, with no feeling attached. With the wearing-away, the reactivity, arguing and inner burning toward whatever anyone says gradually subside as "it is a conceiving" is directly seen — and *thinking too is an action*.
- **"Bring the cup / bring the rice."** The word "bring" carries no content — bring what? a chair, phone, slippers, pen, book? — so "bring" binds to no object; the act "bringing" never occurred, and the thing (water/tea/rice) is never obtained either; the two never knot. Yet we tie a thing behind every action, and that tie is the feeling-perception knot; when it loosens it can establish neither on the five aggregates, nor on the plate, nor separately on "bringing" — establishing stops.
- **The five aggregates assembled:** the intention "to bring" is a *saṅkhāra*, and the *saṅkhāra* just *is* the five clung-to aggregates in brief. Form is what eye/hand/nose/tongue meets; by that feeling one recognises (*saññā*) "an amberella / an incense stick / rough — a black stone"; feeling being recognised is "discerning perception." The *saṅkhāra* is seeing one construction from five facets, like photographing one stupa from five sides — one stupa, five views.
- **The child and the sweet (*kævum*):** a child forms the aggregate "kævum" (a feeling-perception knot); seeing, asks "what is this, mother?", and the mother knots a perception, "a kævum, son," carrying shape, colour and story; later, a different variety prompts "that is *koṇḍa kævum*," "*nāraṁ kævum*" — after the feeling-perception knot, *saṅkhāra* elaborates endlessly, and once built, all four (form, feeling, perception, formation) are known by consciousness, completing the five clung-to aggregates.
- Feeling comes through the action; where such a thing cannot be assembled, arriving at that point, within this very construction lie the five aggregates and dependent origination — some enter through the aggregates, some through dependent origination, to see the emptiness — but wherever one enters, **contact (*phassa*) must be seen, for everything rests in contact-arising (*passa-samudaya*).**

## Summary

This first part of the fourteenth good-friend discussion opens on the breath and shows, with great care, how both a past and a persisting self are fabricated in the present moment. The instant one thinks of the breath — "I've been breathing since morning without ever noticing; had I not breathed I would be dead, so I must have been here all along" — the attention arising now constructs an entire past: an un-noticed breath, a persisting nose, a persisting "I," conveniently "remembered" only now. This manufactures a false continuity and an "I" stretched across the three times. Invoking the *Kālakārāma Sutta*, the teacher recalls that the Buddha taught nothing can be obtained through not-knowing, so "I persisted through the not-knowing that was present since morning" is simply impossible — a pure view. No breath was ever obtained or arose in past, present or future; there is only a thought, a feeling, a mere attention, and even the "breath to be obtained" that the two are discussing is itself only a latent attention fabricated by consciousness.

From here the second speaker repeatedly guards against a subtle error: turning insight into a new analysis. When one truly sees through this, the very knotting must stop; but if one keeps re-applying the formula "I cannot obtain a self or a thing," one merely inherits it again. He recalls that the Buddha said existence and non-existence were not even in his vocabulary once he was established, so one should not even declare "feeling can't be obtained, non-feeling can't be obtained" — it is enough to say "no difference can be grasped even by force." Teaching others requires words, but the real work is on one's own side, in noticing the shift the moment one *sees* rather than merely hears — a settled awareness in which no consciousness forms, a place where nothing at all is understood. And crucially, even "empty" cannot be said of that nature: to understand it as "void" is still another consciousness understanding it, and since the nature can take no object, "empty of what?" When something is grasped there is "a thing"; when "there is nothing" is understood, that is one more arising — the contact-arising extreme — so that even the knowledge of rise-and-fall is qualified, for at the true nature there is no arising whatever, and anything that "arises" arose by view.

The teacher stresses that they are speaking from within delusion. At the true state "it never was, cannot be, will not be," which cannot be said while one is established there, though from here, still deluded, one can say "we are deluded now." He uses the scarecrow taken for a man and then recognised on approach as not a man, only to point out that even recognising "delusion" is relative to a prior delusion, and that seen by right view one cannot even say "I was deluded" — so there is no comfort to be had in "I was deluded before." Comparison itself, he adds, cannot really be performed: it would require a "former" and an "empty now," and to say "I was deluded before but cannot be now" is already a consciousness-level; to see a view as a view is right view, whereas to understand "before was this, now is that" is only consciousness at work. Everything one manages to say is thus merely an attribute laid over the nature, and one must see that even "this is such-and-such" is wrong — that every pattern of understanding is flawed. This bears directly on teaching nibbāna: when the listener seems to grasp the explanation "as true," that very grasp must be exposed as wrong, since we seize everything comparatively, acquiescing to views after pondering them; yet without hearing the true Dhamma in the company of a good friend it cannot be understood at all. So one must say "such a nature exists" and immediately add that "such a nature," and even one's own saying of it, is itself only a view — so the listener never settles into a grasp but keeps seeing the flaw.

The discussion then widens to how the whole "I existed" is assembled — the five clung-to aggregates gathered, dependent origination built from a cause-and-effect dhamma, a persisting world with an inner and outer, the entire bundle sitting at this one constructing point — and insists that nibbāna lies within saṁsāra itself: we understand "saṁsāra" as a thing, and within that very understanding the non-understanding must be seen, which is cessation within arising. The world sees "going," but for one who has seen, going still happens while within it stands a wisdom that never catches the "goer," set where action itself is out of range — and this holds for every action. The teacher then dismantles action through the everyday "bring the cup, bring the rice": the word "bring" carries no content, binds to no object, and the act of bringing never actually occurs, nor is the thing brought ever really obtained, so the two never knot — yet we tie a thing behind every action, and that tie is the feeling-perception knot which, once loosened, can establish neither on the aggregates, nor on the plate, nor on "bringing." Finally he lays out how the aggregates are built: the intention "to bring" is a formation, and the formation just is the five aggregates in brief; form is what the senses meet, feeling is recognised as "this is an amberella, an incense stick, a rough black stone," and the formation is one construction seen from five facets, like one stupa photographed from five sides. The child learning "kævum," then "koṇḍa kævum" and "nāraṁ kævum," shows the feeling-perception knot elaborated endlessly by formation until all four are known by consciousness and the five clung-to aggregates are complete. Wherever one enters this — through the aggregates or through dependent origination — the teacher ends by insisting that contact must be seen, for everything rests in contact-arising.
