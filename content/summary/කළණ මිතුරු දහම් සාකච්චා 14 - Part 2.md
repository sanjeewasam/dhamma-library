- Terms used include *pañcupādānakkhandha*, *paṭiccasamuppāda*, *nitya-saññā*, *mūla moha*, *avijjā*, *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*, *jivhā-viññāṇa*, *kāya-sparśa*, *sammā-vācā*, *anāgāmī* and *arahat*. No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- Part 2 of the fourteenth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation continuing the breath/self analysis and turning to the root nature and the newborn.
- It opens by reaffirming that, however one enters, **contact must be seen — everything rests in contact-arising (*passa-samudaya*).** The teacher notes that very few suttas treat the existence/non-existence extremes directly; most teach the aggregates, form and dependent origination, given to suit each person's development. The two speakers' focus on the existence/non-existence pair does not suit everyone, and some who hear these discussions rightly feel "this approach doesn't work for me" and stop — because it is not their way (*pariyāya*).
- **The hidden "capacity" beneath the breath.** The perception "I could have known the breath since morning" is the eternal perception (*nitya-saññā*): with attention it asserts "a capacity to experience existed since morning," and holding that capacity we conclude "I existed since morning but failed to notice a breath." So one silently assumes "I am one who can know breath," and this assumed, hidden, ungraspable capacity is exactly what a newborn holds — seeing, hearing, sensing, thinking (*diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*). Thinking of the breath now fabricates the past upon this hidden, unknowable base.
- **The newborn as the "mould" of root delusion.** On this base the "I" and "I exist" are seated, and the newborn is precisely "the nature established on that base." A baby does not know who scolds or praises it, has no agenda, only smiles and watches — yet it *receives* hearing (sounds without words), taste (sweet/bitter by tongue-consciousness), and touch ("something touched"); contact is received. So although the accumulated perception-heaps from outside are absent (that part is empty), the root delusion (*mūla moha*) is present at its fullest, and the "I" is established. The newborn is like the die/mould of root delusion — and that root delusion *is* saṁsāra.
- Referring to the sutta on the newborn, the teacher rejects the naïve inference that, lacking developed perceptions, a baby dying young would be a non-returner or arahant: on the contrary, the baby bears the most severe nature — ignorance itself.
- **Animals carry the same base.** A struck dog runs, so it too has "I must escape"; it does not know whether it was hit by a stone or a man, but "it hurts when struck" is enough for saṁsāra. Dogs and cats travel saṁsāra without accumulating perception-heaps as we do — they simply go along. One might then think an animal should have it *easier*: a dog has no perceptions, no taints, no words, speaks no wrong word — so a dog's taints are "number one" extinguished, its "right speech" fulfilled. But no: without vision (*dassana*), the forming of formations never stops; the view remains just as it is, and the animal bears it too. Even if the eye does not see and the ear does not hear, the other two (sensing and thinking) remain, and two of the four suffice for saṁsāra to roll on.
- **So the "only-if-we-accumulate" idea breaks.** Saṁsāra is not built merely from these accumulated formations, such that not accumulating them would end it; rather, on the base — the eternal perception hidden within seeing-hearing-sensing-thinking together with "I" — saṁsāra is built from birth to death, and the "I" hides right there.
- **The true nature versus where we dwell.** The true nature (the "empty place / space," the un-arisen) is where there is no arising at all — the word does not apply, it cannot be known; that un-existing nature is the real nature (*yathā-svabhāva*). But we do not dwell there; we dwell here in saṁsāra, seated in the root-nature's eternal perception, forming karma and the unwholesome and wandering on. One established in the not-known place has no cause for karma to form, can seize no point to build upon — the un-formed place — and to be established there is nibbāna, where no point can be grasped even by force.
- **The recurring caution against re-analysing.** To the student's question whether it is good to "think this way," the teacher repeats: do not go into thinking like "I cannot obtain a self." Seeing the nature is fine; from the seeing one should notice the *fault* — that "I" arises from a feeling never obtained and a hearing never heard — but to *think* "no self can be obtained" is like re-contemplating the very view "I must arise," and rebuilds the two extremes (a "someone who cannot obtain," a second end). So one should limit such investigation; it need not be pursued.
- **"Look right at the root."** The root question is: how, within a not-knowing nature, can any recognition arise at all — how, while dwelling in the "seeing exists" mode with seeing itself unknown, can one think about a seen thing? If we are always in "feeling," how could a breath-feeling ever be knowable? It is a nature un-knowable by its very nature, outside consciousness's range; seeing that, one realises "if this is the nature, then my sitting-and-thinking is a distortion of it." Just that much is enough — one need not expand into "I can't obtain, it was never obtained," which only keeps re-surfacing the duality.
- **On wearing away (*gevīma*).** What needs to wear away re-surfaces whenever it is re-contemplated as a "topic"; the wearing-away happens through *use/practice* that reduces it — like a tool that rusts away when unused but, used repeatedly, stays free of rust and remains serviceable.

## Summary

This second part of the fourteenth good-friend discussion begins by reaffirming that, by whatever route one approaches, contact must be seen, since everything rests in contact-arising. The teacher observes that the canon says very little directly about the existence/non-existence extremes and mostly teaches the aggregates, form and dependent origination, each given to fit a listener's maturity — so the two speakers' own preoccupation with the existence/non-existence pair will not suit everyone, and some who hear these talks rightly conclude "this is not my way" and stop. He then returns to the breath to expose the hidden assumption beneath it: the perception "I could have known the breath since morning" is the eternal perception, which under attention asserts a "capacity to experience" reaching back to the morning, so that we conclude "I existed all along but simply failed to notice." In other words we quietly take ourselves to be "one who can know breath," and that assumed, ungraspable, unknowable capacity is exactly what a newborn holds — the bare seeing, hearing, sensing and thinking — upon which, thinking of the breath now, the whole past is fabricated.

The heart of Part 2 is the figure of the newborn as the very mould of root delusion. A baby knows nothing of who scolds or praises it, carries no agenda, only smiles and looks about — yet it still receives hearing, taste and touch; contact is received. So although the outer, accumulated perception-heaps are entirely absent — that part is genuinely empty — the root delusion is present at its fullest and the "I" is already established; the infant is the die that stamps out root delusion, and that delusion simply is saṁsāra. Referring to the sutta in which the Buddha asks about the newborn, the teacher rejects the tempting inference that a baby, lacking developed perceptions, would be liberated if it died young: on the contrary it bears the gravest nature of all, ignorance itself. He extends the point to animals: a struck dog runs, so it too holds "I must escape," and though it cannot tell whether a stone or a man struck it, the bare "it hurts when struck" is enough to carry it through saṁsāra. Dogs and cats travel on without accumulating perceptions as humans do, which might suggest they have it easier — no perceptions, no taints, no words, no wrong speech, as if their taints were foremost extinguished and their right speech fulfilled. But without vision the forming of formations never stops; the view persists unchanged, the animal bears it too, and even where the eye does not see and the ear does not hear, the remaining two of the four — sensing and thinking — suffice to keep saṁsāra rolling.

This dismantles the idea that saṁsāra is built merely from our accumulated formations, such that ceasing to accumulate them would end it. Rather, saṁsāra is built upon the base — the eternal perception hidden within seeing, hearing, sensing and thinking, together with "I" — from birth to death, and it is precisely there that the "I" conceals itself. Over against this stands the true nature: the empty, un-arisen place where nothing arises at all, to which no word applies and which cannot be known — the real nature — where we do not in fact dwell, since we live here in saṁsāra, seated in the eternal perception of the root nature, forming karma and wandering. One established in the not-known place finds no cause for karma to form and can seize no point on which to build, and to be established there, where nothing can be grasped even by force, is nibbāna. The teacher closes by twice repeating his central caution: seeing this nature is good, but turning it into the thought "I cannot obtain a self" merely re-contemplates the view "I must arise" and rebuilds the two extremes; one should instead simply see the fault — that "I" arises from a feeling never obtained and a hearing never heard — and look no further, right at the root, asking how any recognition could arise within a not-knowing nature at all. And on the wearing-away of defilement he offers a homely image: what must wear away keeps re-surfacing whenever it is made into a topic to ponder, and it thins only through use, like a tool that rusts when idle but, kept in use, stays serviceable.
