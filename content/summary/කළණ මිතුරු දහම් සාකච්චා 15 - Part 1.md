## Sutta referred to
- **Mūlapariyāya Sutta** (මූලපරියාය සූත්‍රය) — named: cited for *pathaviṁ pathavito maññati* ("one conceives earth as earth"), and for the worldling's conceiving that a self exists in / falls away through the earth element.
- Directly quoted phrase (from that sutta): *pathaviṁ pathavito maññati*.
- The **Aṅgulimāla** episode is referred to — the Buddha's "I have stopped; you stop" — with Aṅgulimāla named as a person, no sutta title given.
- Terms used include the four elements (*pathavī, āpo, tejo, vāyo*), *saññā / vedanā*, *saññā-nānatta*, *nitya-saññā*, *anicca*, *adhivacana*, *diṭṭha-suta-muta*, *manasikāra*, and *kāya / phoṭṭhabba*. No further sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- Part 1 of the fifteenth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation examining the elements, perception, and feeling.
- **Comfort is only reduced pain.** Sitting in the most comfortable posture, the same posture soon becomes tiresome and one shifts — showing that what we hold as "pleasure" is merely a reduction of pain. And the "hardness" felt on the seat: does the hardness come *from* a chair, or do we conceive "a chair" *because of* the hardness-sensation? It is the latter — the sensation makes us posit a chair, and it is on the strength of such sensations that we declare everything to "exist."
- **The ice-cube.** Hold ice and one feels "hard" (*pathavī*); it melts and one feels flowing (*āpo*); the hand warms as the ice cools and one feels heat (*tejo*); left in the sun it evaporates (*vāyo*) — four elements felt at different moments from one thing. Yet one cannot say the thing in hand *was* earth, water, fire or air; each is only a *recognition* (*saññā*), not a thing that existed. "Hard" was never anywhere; there is only recognition, and thinking about the recognition, we name it "ice"; later, recognising "flowing," we name it "water." The difference between ice and water is just these two recognitions.
- Invoking the *Mūlapariyāya Sutta*'s *pathaviṁ pathavito maññati*: the worldling conceives earth as earth, imagines a self persisting in it and a thing that existed and is lost. But if earth is seen *as* earth, no conceiving follows. "Hard" cannot be mapped onto chair, table or bed — one sensation cannot distinguish them — just as "flour" cannot tell bun from jam-bread from sponge, and "petrol" cannot yield "fire." So seen rightly, one can go nowhere from *pathavī*; yet in fact we conceive endlessly, "conceiving earth as earth."
- **The flour analogy.** Fish-bun, jam-bread, "kiṁbulā," vegetable roti — all are flour, and from "flour" one cannot tell which is which; there is only a *diversity of perception* (*saññā-nānatta*). "Jam-bread" is not in the flour, "bun" is not an idea within flour. So a single recognition (of hardness) is made into "a thing" — a mere *saññā-mātra*, *vedanā-mātra*; and seen *as* perception, comprehension stops there.
- **If it were real it could not vanish.** Where is the ice now? Melted. One can say neither "it was" nor "it ceased," because it is a mere perception, not a thing that exists and perishes. Yet we do find "a thing" — and the crucial point is that what we get is "a thing *within pathavī*": conceiving a non-existent thing in earth is *pathavī-maññanā*, and the un-arisen is nowhere. Were a real thing there, it could not be erased; something would remain. Where is "hardness"? We locate it in stones and chairs, yet a doorframe has none; a "hard wind" fells trees, but is that hardness anywhere?
- **Pain belongs to no place.** In a dream a dog bites and it hurts, though there are no teeth and no leg — the pain arises with the leg-and-teeth conditions *in the mind*, not from real teeth. So feeling belongs to no location: pain from a mosquito bite makes us talk of the mosquito, but within "pain" no mosquito and no place it came from is found. Not knowing this, when pain arises we assign it "from the leg / head / stomach / back." Within the pain a leg (*kāya*) and a touched-thing (*phoṭṭhabba*) are displayed — two perceptions knotted (a leg exists, a chair the leg struck exists), with a pain on the other side; feeling is the experienced pain, and to it perceptions are knotted — the *vedanā-saññā* knot. Seeing that pain is only pain, a mere feeling-thought, no perception knots to it and no "leg-struck-chair" story forms.
- **Seeing the knot as knot.** There is no separate "knotless place" to seek; the very nature is understood *as* a knot — that is the comprehension, and nothing else happens. Seeing saṁsāra *as* saṁsāra is itself release/cessation. Everyone tries to see "impermanent" by watching the "permanent" become impermanent; but here one sees that within the pain there was never a leg, dog or chair — so there is nothing to "make cease," it was never there.
- **Impermanence within permanence.** The view "impermanent" arises only when the "permanence" is rightly seen by wisdom — seeing the fault of having made permanent what was never there. We have fixed the seen-heard-sensed as permanent at a place never found; seeing "it was never there" is seeing *anicca* within the very permanence-perception, for the *nitya-saññā* is itself never actually cognizable, and to see that realises the impermanent characteristic right there.
- **The fish in water.** Ask a fish what water is like and it cannot say, being born, living and dying in water with nothing to compare. To describe water one contrasts it with "hard/dry"; a fish that has only ever known water cannot recognise water *as* water without knowing non-water. Recognition requires a contrast — which is why we never register the "hardness" or "heat" we feel constantly: we are *in* feeling like a fish in water, and attention (*manasikāra*) is done precisely to manufacture the comparison. Hence "I am breathing" is registered only when attention brings a comparison. The rotating earth makes the same point: we believe it rotates because we were *told*, not because we felt it — having become the rotation, no "rotating" registers.
- **The walking-planet and Aṅgulimāla.** People born walking search for how to "stop" only because they never knew they are always walking; likewise we hunt for nibbāna because we do not know we are already "stopped." The Buddha's "I have stopped; you stop" means stopping *within action itself*, not stopping from walking — a wisdom that sees action as never obtained, which is not a separate faculty but the very not-obtainable nature, lying outside consciousness's range. So no consciousness can arise there, and things cannot be knotted to action — eye-and-form cannot be knotted to seeing, ear-and-sound to hearing.
- **The account-number example.** Copying a bank account number from a phone, and having once suffered from a wrong number, one now writes with full care and double-checks digit by digit — yet the cashier finds two digits wrong (the first two written as different digits). One had checked twice and both times it "seemed right"; two came wrong, eight right. We conclude "the eight came right because I looked and wrote correctly" — but *that* is exactly the ungraspable "knot" the Buddha likened to the crossroads magician: where is the "knot" when it goes right? It went right, no problem — and yet a life-long ungraspable knot sits right there.

## Summary

This first part of the fifteenth good-friend discussion takes up the four elements, perception and feeling to show that what we take for solid, existing things is only recognition. It opens with a homely observation: we sit in the most comfortable posture we can find, yet the very same posture soon becomes unbearable and we shift — proof that "comfort" is merely a reduction of pain. And the hardness we feel on the seat, the teacher asks, does it come *from* a chair, or do we conceive "a chair" *because of* the hardness? Plainly the latter: the sensation prompts us to posit the chair, and it is on such sensations that we base our conviction that anything at all "exists." The ice-cube makes this vivid. Held in the hand it feels hard — the earth element; it melts into flowing water; the hand warms as the ice cools, giving heat; left in the sun it evaporates into air. Four elements are felt from one thing at different moments, yet one cannot say the thing was earth, water, fire or air; each is only a recognition, never a thing, and "hard" was never located anywhere. We simply think about the recognition and name it "ice," then, recognising flowing, name it "water" — the whole difference between ice and water being just these two recognitions.

Drawing on the *Mūlapariyāya Sutta*'s phrase "one conceives earth as earth," the teacher shows that the worldling takes earth to be earth, imagines a self persisting in it, and supposes a thing that once existed and is now lost — whereas if earth is simply seen *as* earth, no conceiving follows. "Hard," after all, cannot be mapped onto a chair or a table or a bed, since one sensation cannot tell them apart, exactly as "flour" cannot distinguish a bun from a jam-bread or a sponge, and "petrol" can never yield "fire." From the bare element one can go nowhere — yet in fact we go everywhere, conceiving without end. The flour analogy is pressed home: every kind of bread is only flour, and from flour one cannot say which is which; there is merely a diversity of perception, "jam-bread" being no idea contained in flour. So a single recognition of hardness is inflated into "a thing," a mere perception treated as real, and once perception is seen *as* perception, comprehension halts there. Were the thing real, it could never simply vanish — where is the ice now? — and one can say neither that it "was" nor that it "ceased," because it was only a perception. And yet, crucially, we always do find "a thing *within* earth": to conceive a non-existent thing in the element is precisely conceiving-earth-as-earth, and the un-arisen is nowhere to be found. We place hardness in stones and chairs, though a doorframe has none, and speak of a "hard wind" that fells trees, though no hardness is anywhere to be located.

The teacher then turns to feeling, and shows that it too belongs to no place. In a dream a dog bites and it genuinely hurts, though there are no teeth and no leg present; the pain arises with the leg-and-teeth conditions in the mind, not from any real teeth — so feeling is tied to no location at all. A mosquito bite makes us talk of the mosquito, but within the pain itself no mosquito, and no place a mosquito came from, is ever found; not knowing this, we assign each pain to a leg, a head, a stomach, a back. Within the pain, he explains, a leg and a struck object are displayed — two perceptions knotted together, with a pain on the other side — and this is the feeling-perception knot; but seen for what it is, pain is only pain, a bare feeling-thought to which no perception need attach and around which no "leg-struck-chair" story forms. From this he draws his central move: there is no separate "knotless place" to seek, for the very nature is understood *as* a knot, and that understanding is the whole of the comprehension — seeing saṁsāra as saṁsāra is itself release. We all try to witness impermanence by watching something permanent decay, but here one sees instead that within the pain there was never a leg or a dog or a chair to begin with, so there is nothing to "make cease." The view "impermanent" arises only when the supposed permanence is rightly seen — seeing the fault of having fixed as permanent what was never there — so that impermanence is found within the very permanence-perception, which is itself never truly cognizable.

He closes with a cluster of analogies for why all this stays hidden. A fish cannot say what water is like, having known nothing but water; recognition requires a contrast, and we never register the hardness or heat we feel unceasingly precisely because we are immersed in feeling as a fish is in water — which is why attention has to be exercised at all, to manufacture the comparison, so that "I am breathing" registers only when attention supplies one. We believe the earth rotates only because we were told, never having felt it, having become the rotation itself. People born walking hunt for a way to "stop" only because they never knew they always walk, and just so we hunt for nibbāna, not knowing we are already stopped; the Buddha's word to Aṅgulimāla, "I have stopped; you stop," means a stopping *within* action itself — a wisdom that sees action as never obtained, lying beyond consciousness's range, where nothing can be knotted to action, not even eye-and-form to seeing. And the account-number example seals it: a man who copies a bank number with the utmost care, double-checking each digit because he was once burned, still hands over a slip with two digits wrong; he had checked twice and both times it "seemed right," and though eight digits came right we credit "I looked and wrote correctly" — which is exactly the crossroads magician's knot the Buddha said cannot be grasped, sitting undetected right where the thing "went right."
