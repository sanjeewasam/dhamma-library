## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- The verse fragment *na-y-idaṁ attakataṁ, na-y-idaṁ parakataṁ* ("this is not self-made, this is not made by another") is quoted again, together with "nor is it spontaneous."
- The **Māluṅkyaputta** teaching is referred to — that a thing not experienced in past, present or future can occasion no lust — with Māluṅkyaputta named as a person, no sutta title given.
- Terms used include *kamma-abhisaṅkhāra*, *cetanā*, *saññā*, *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, *anicca*. No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- Part 2 of the fifteenth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation continuing the account-number "knot," the tea example, and adding a bank-card story.
- **The knot hides in what goes *right*.** When the account number comes out right, no "knot" can be caught — the crossroads magician; one cannot call a right action a "knot." The "I looked at the phone and wrote it" connection cannot be broken (remove the phone and one cannot write, so "I wrote by looking" seems proven) — yet a terrible ungraspable knot sits exactly there. The teacher notes that seeing this makes one begin to doubt not the *wrong* outcome but the *right* one — "how does the right one come right?" — which we normally never question, since there is nothing to investigate when work succeeds. But it is very hard to see the successful outcome itself *as* a knot.
- **Right and wrong are both blind shots.** We take the "I lost mindfulness" label only for the wrong ones (my fault) and confirm "my mindfulness is good" for the right ones; but if both are seen as "blind shots" (*kaṇā*), one would neither rejoice at success nor grieve at failure. As in the blindfold pot-breaking game — some strike the pot, some swing and miss, and even the striker never saw — the whole thing is blind hits.
- **Why "I did it" collapses.** Looking at the phone, "two" is recognised; but the mind that arises while *writing* is not that mind — it is a fresh constructing (*kamma-abhisaṅkhāra*), and the intention may be re-fabricated or something not-there written. If the two perceptions ("2" and "2") happen to match, we "hit the knot" and credit "I wrote what I saw — my skill, my mindfulness." Yet if I truly did it, why do exactly two digits go wrong while I look and write with full care? Because the looking-mind and the writing-mind are two, either of which may hit or miss — so even the right ones are blind hits. Hence *na-y-idaṁ attakataṁ, na-y-idaṁ parakataṁ*, nor spontaneous: not by me, not by another, not by itself.
- **The exam parallel.** The mind studying is not the mind writing the exam; were they the same, nothing could go wrong — yet easy questions go unanswered and hard ones answered, both blind. Studying once should establish it, with no need to study again; the very effort of repeated study shows how hard we strike to hit this ungraspable knot. "Studying" as an action is not actually engaged; two un-engaged things are taken as "a knot struck," when in fact no knot was struck — only "it seemed struck." To see the knot *as* a knot is to see that no knot was ever struck there.
- **The tea and the person-view again.** The mind at taking the tea (to reheat) is not the mind at throwing it; "the same person who took it came to the sink and threw it" is our knot, and "why did I throw what I meant to reheat?" ties the reheat-story to the throw-story — which is exactly "the world's problem." Invoking Māluṅkyaputta: the act "throwing" was not found before the moment, at the moment, or after it — one cannot think about a never-found act once it has passed — so across all three times it is not found. Self-view (*sakkāya-diṭṭhi*) is carried to both extremes as "an I exists," a single continuous mind: "I have one and the same mind" is permanence, but at the level where a formation arises there is no place for it, and seen rightly there is none.
- **The bank-card story — a waking dream.** He went to the bank to deposit cash, put his card into the machine, and the card jammed; security said it could not be retrieved (a Saturday, the machine's fault) and to come Monday. At home he puzzled, "why on earth did I put a card into a cash-deposit machine, which needs no card — you just enter cash by account number?" On Sunday, taking out his wallet, he found the card *in the wallet* — so "the card stuck in the machine," believed a hundred percent real, had never happened, exactly like a dream in which a dog bites though there is no dog and no teeth. If consciousness supposes it did something, it fully "did" it, without a second thought; other cases are blind shots *when done*, but this shows that even a *not-done* thing is taken as a blind shot. His mind was a hundred percent certain "I put the card in," yet it never occurred — an eyes-open dream; no card ever went from wallet to machine and jammed.
- **See it within oneself.** Such incidents are not to be brushed aside but seen within oneself: what "arises and engages" is realised to be un-arisen, and seeing this, one wears toward "my not-being-here." Those in whom this is well developed see precisely these things — increasingly, the errors reveal themselves.

## Summary

This second part of the fifteenth good-friend discussion drives home, through vivid everyday failures, that neither success nor failure is ever the doing of an "I." It opens on the account number copied from a phone: when it comes out right, no "knot" can be caught, for one cannot call a correct action a knot — the crossroads magician again. The connection "I looked at the phone and wrote it" seems unbreakable, since removing the phone would make writing impossible, and yet a terrible, ungraspable knot sits precisely there. What is striking, the teacher observes, is that seeing this makes one begin to doubt not the mistake but the *success* — "how does the right one come out right?" — a thing we never normally question, because a job that works leaves nothing to investigate; and it is very hard indeed to see the successful outcome itself as a knot. He then exposes our double standard: we reserve the label "I lost mindfulness" for the failures, taking them as our fault, while crediting the successes to "my good mindfulness"; but if both were seen as blind shots, we would neither rejoice at the one nor grieve at the other, exactly as in the blindfolded pot-breaking game where the one who strikes the pot saw no better than the one who missed.

The mechanism is laid bare. Looking at the phone, "two" is recognised; but the mind that arises while writing is a different mind, a fresh act of construction, and the intention may be re-fabricated or something absent written down. If the two perceptions happen to coincide, we "hit the knot" and congratulate ourselves that we wrote what we saw; but if we truly did it, why do exactly two digits go wrong while we look and write with full care? Because the looking-mind and the writing-mind are two, either of which may hit or miss — so even the correct digits are blind hits, and the verse holds: this is not self-made, not made by another, not spontaneous. The exam makes the same point, since the mind that studied is not the mind that writes, and were they one, nothing could go wrong — yet easy questions go unanswered and hard ones answered, both blind; studying once should suffice, and the very compulsion to study again betrays how hard we strike to hit this ungraspable knot. "Studying," as an action, is never actually engaged; two un-engaged things are taken as "a knot struck," when no knot was struck at all — only it seemed so — and to see the knot as a knot is to see that no knot was ever struck there.

He returns to the tea to expose the person-view: the mind that took the tea to reheat is not the mind that threw it, and "the same person who took it threw it" is our fabricated knot, so that "why did I throw what I meant to reheat?" ties the reheat-story to the throw-story and becomes exactly "the world's problem." Invoking the teaching to Māluṅkyaputta, he shows that the act of throwing was found neither before, at, nor after the moment, and one cannot think about a never-found act once it has passed — so across all three times it is simply not found, while self-view is carried to both extremes as "an I exists," a single continuous mind, which is permanence, though at the level where a formation arises there is no place for any such thing. Finally he tells the bank-card story as a waking dream: going to deposit cash, he put his card into the machine, the card jammed, security told him it could not be retrieved and to return Monday — and at home he puzzled over why he had put a card into a machine that needs none, only to find the card in his wallet the next day. The card "stuck in the machine," believed a hundred percent real, had never happened, precisely like a dream in which a dog bites though there is neither dog nor teeth; consciousness, supposing it did something, "did" it without question, so that even a thing never done is taken as a blind shot. Such incidents, the teacher urges, are not to be waved away but seen within oneself, for what seems to arise and engage is realised to be un-arisen, and seeing this one wears steadily toward one's own not-being-here — which is exactly what those in whom this is well matured come to see, the errors revealing themselves more and more.
