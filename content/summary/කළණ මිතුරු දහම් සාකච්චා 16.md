## Main dhamma points
- The sixteenth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation on the "permanence-perception" (*nitya-saññā*), the elements, and non-occurrence.
- **Neither percipient nor non-percipient.** The Buddha describes a state that is "neither percipient nor non-percipient" — not asleep, not perception-absent — caught by none of these; asked "did you hear that thunderclap?" he answers "I did not hear it." Even in "hearing," the thunder struck him as no knot, though it strikes us.
- **The perception no one grasps.** Most get stuck at the *nitya-saññā*. Break a chair to pieces and one says "no chair — just pieces," then is caught by the new perception "pieces," or "a chair made of the four great elements" — one perception simply replaces another, and the sense of "existence" shifts. Lightning unheard here is "obtained through absence" — "there is lightning we don't hear" — and one is caught in that too. A newborn dwells in the bare "something seen, something heard," the "I" hidden in the base.
- **The milk-tea (parallel to the lamp).** Having the milk tea already, we say "tea-leaves and hot water make milk tea" — fitting causes to an effect we already possess. This is the *nitya-saññā*: were the milk tea not already known, one would have to watch how it forms from leaves, water and sugar; instead we know the effect first and back-fit the causes, fabricating "these causes → this effect" across the three times.
- **The Ruvanvæli stupa and the two "graspables."** Near the stupa, eyes open, one sees it; eyes closed, one "sees" it in the mind — two different things. If it were purely mind-made, one could grasp the mind-image exactly, wall and all, as one grasps the real; but the mind-image cannot be grasped. This yields two knots: the eye-seen (one knot) and the mind-seen-and-compared (two knots), and the difference (*nānatva*) between them is itself the deception. The worldling finds the stupa "graspable / alive / three-dimensional," while the mind-image is not — and what is "lacking" in the mind-image is only *existence* (*ati-bava*). In truth both are taken *through existence*: the eye-seen becomes real *through seeing*, the mind-seen becomes real *through cognizing*, both being *nitya-saññā*, and both appearing side by side at the level of the seen-heard-sensed-cognized.
- **The elements are not recognised as conceiving.** People take the four great elements to be "a thing that really exists in the world." But *avijjā-samudayā rūpa-samudayo*: form arises with the arising of ignorance, so the "form-dhamma" is itself a mental (*nāma*) dhamma, and even "solidity" taken as solid is a *nāma* nature. To insist "there is a real thing — the four elements exist" cannot lead to the supramundane; it is being stuck in the *nitya-saññā*. And "the four elements" is only "me": who sees them? — me; who thinks "four elements"? — me; so self-view (*sakkāya-diṭṭhi*) only hardens.
- **Ignorance is not a thing.** In *udayabbaya-ñāṇa*, "rise-and-fall" is not caught by time — past, future, present, internal, external, gross, subtle, far, near — and by wisdom it points to *non-arising*: what appears to arise never truly arose. Breaking the chair into "form arises from ignorance-kamma-craving" treats ignorance as an existing thing that combines to make form; but ignorance is only *not knowing arising* (*samudaya*), not a thing at all, and to make ignorance into a thing is a still greater folly.
- **The frog in the milk tea.** A foreigner and a Sri Lankan are each served milk tea with a frog in it; both say "there is a frog in the milk tea," and the statement is true for both. But underneath, the Sri Lankan runs "a frog *cannot* be in milk tea" — a non-occurrence, an impossibility — whereas the foreigner runs "perhaps here they make milk tea with frogs," eternalising the frog as a real, possible thing to be accepted. This illustrates *avijjā-samudayā rūpa-samudayo*: the same "thing" is taken as real-and-possible or seen as never-occurring, depending on whether the impossibility is seen.
- **"Purifying the mind."** *Sacitta-pariyodapanaṁ etaṁ buddhāna sāsanaṁ* — but if there is a mind "to purify," a mind is presupposed as existing; that, the teacher notes, is not the point.
- **Non-occurrence is the wisdom.** Sitting, no feeling registers, yet one cannot even say "I am absorbed in it," because it is not understood — and one should not re-think it as a post-mortem. Because that settled awareness is present, the "thinking" does not run on into *vitakka*, and the tendency to run into *vitakka* gradually lessens (though beginners must at first do the *vitakka*). The **Sāriputta** incident — struck on the head from behind and not even feeling it — shows that where the action is not found there is always wisdom, an extraordinary awareness; and such a one does not re-contemplate "this happened, I did this." Thoughts of the kind may still arise, but they pass like lines drawn on water, noted nowhere.
- **The trousers example.** At the moment of removing them nothing is understood; only afterwards "I took off the trousers here." With wisdom, even at the moment of removing there is the wisdom "it is not understood," so one never turns aside to contemplate; and though afterward "I did this, put it there" may arise, wisdom knows all such are never-occurred events. This is not "recalled" but *fabricated* in the moment ("I took off the trousers, there's a pile, another bag on it") and never seen for the distortion it is. Every action, all day, is at that level: thinking is only a thought, action only a thought — one need not even *think* "it is a thought," so no *vitakka* proliferates and no "I did this, I put this here" arises. To wave a toy car for a child is just a word, a mere idea, with nothing to sink into; whereas one who sinks into "I did this, at this moment I did it just so" can be seen to be deeply sunk in.

## Summary

This sixteenth good-friend discussion works over the "permanence-perception" — the *nitya-saññā* — through which we take the world to be solidly existent, and shows how it hides even from careful practitioners. It opens with the Buddha's description of a state "neither percipient nor non-percipient," neither asleep nor devoid of perception, in which, asked whether he heard a thunderclap, he answers that he did not: even within "hearing," the thunder was no knot for him, though for us it always is. The trouble, the speakers agree, is that no one grasps this permanence-perception. Break a chair into pieces and one declares "no chair, only pieces," but is immediately caught by the fresh perception "pieces," or "a chair built of the four great elements" — one perception merely replacing another while the sense of "a thing existing" quietly relocates. Lightning we cannot hear is likewise "obtained through its absence," and we are caught in "there is lightning we don't hear." A newborn already dwells in the bare "something seen, something heard," with the "I" concealed in that base.

The milk-tea example, parallel to the lamp, exposes how causation is back-fitted: because we already have and know "milk tea," we say tea-leaves and hot water produce it — but if we did not already know the effect, we would have to watch it form, whereas in fact we grasp the effect first and only then fit causes to it, fabricating "these causes make this effect" across past, present and future. The Ruvanvæli stupa sharpens the analysis of "existence." Standing near it with eyes open, one sees the stupa; with eyes closed, one "sees" it in the mind — and these are two different things, for the mind-image cannot be grasped as the real stupa can, wall and all. From this arise two knots — the single knot of eye-seeing and the double knot of mind-seeing-and-comparing — and the very difference between them is the deception. The worldling finds the real stupa "graspable, alive, three-dimensional" and the mind-image not, and what the mind-image "lacks" is only *existence*; yet in truth both are taken through existence, the eye-seen becoming real through seeing and the mind-seen through cognizing, both alike being the permanence-perception, both appearing together at the level of the seen, heard, sensed and cognized.

From here the teacher turns to the elements and to ignorance. People take the four great elements to be a real thing in the world, but "with the arising of ignorance, the arising of form" means the form-dhamma is itself a mental dhamma, and even "solidity," taken as solid, is a mental nature; to insist that the elements really exist cannot reach the supramundane but only keeps one stuck in the permanence-perception — and "the four elements," after all, is only "me," since it is I who see them and I who think "four elements," so that self-view merely hardens. In the knowledge of rise-and-fall, he adds, "rise-and-fall" is caught by no time — past, future, present, inner, outer, gross, subtle, far, near — and by wisdom it points to non-arising: what seems to arise never truly arose. To break the chair down into "form arises from ignorance, kamma and craving" is to treat ignorance as an existing thing that combines to produce form, whereas ignorance is only *not knowing arising*, no thing at all, and to make it into a thing is a still greater folly. The frog-in-the-milk-tea makes this vivid: a foreigner and a local both truly say "there is a frog in the tea," yet the local runs underneath "a frog *cannot* be in milk tea" — an impossibility — while the foreigner supposes the tea may be made with frogs, eternalising the frog as a real possibility; the same "thing" is thus either accepted as real or seen as never-occurring, exactly as "form arises with ignorance." And when the Buddha says "the purifying of one's own mind is the teaching of the Buddhas," the teacher notes that to speak of a mind "to purify" presupposes a mind that exists — which is not the point.

The discussion closes on non-occurrence as the wisdom itself. Sitting, no feeling registers, and one cannot even claim "I am absorbed," because nothing is understood — and it must not be turned into a post-mortem. Because that settled awareness is present, thinking does not run on into proliferation, and the tendency to do so gradually thins, though beginners must at first exercise the deliberate thinking. The story of Ven. Sāriputta, struck on the head from behind and not even feeling it, shows that where the action is not found there is always wisdom, an extraordinary awareness that does not re-contemplate "this happened, I did this"; such thoughts may still arise, but they pass like lines drawn on water, recorded nowhere. So too with taking off one's trousers: at the moment nothing is understood, and only afterwards does "I took them off here" appear, fabricated on the spot and never seen for the distortion it is — for every action, all day long, is at that level, thinking being only a thought and action only a thought, so that one need not even *think* "it is a thought," and no proliferation of "I did this, I put this here" arises. Waving a toy car for a child is just a word with nothing to sink into; and one who instead sinks into "I did this, just so, at this very moment" can be seen to be deeply sunk in.
