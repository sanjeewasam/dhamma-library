## Sutta referred to
- **Nandakovāda Sutta** (නන්දකෝවාද සූත්‍රය) — named: cited as containing the oil-lamp simile, through which (the speakers recall) five hundred arahants / an assembly of nuns attained. *(The speaker is uncertain which elder — "Sāriputta or who" — gives the simile.)*
- Directly quoted formula, unattributed: *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti; yaṁ sañjānāti taṁ vitakketi; yaṁ vitakketi taṁ papañceti* ("what one feels, one perceives; what one perceives, one thinks about; what one thinks about, one proliferates").
- The phrase *tajjo samannāhāro* ("the appropriate coming-together") is used as a term.
- Other terms: *sota- / ghāna- / jivhā- / kāya-viññāṇa*, *anidassana*, *saññā-vipallāsa*, *papañca*, *vedanā / saññā*, *pañcupādānakkhandha*, the "eleven modes" (*ekādasa*: past/future/present, internal/external, gross/subtle, inferior/superior, far/near). No sutta title should be inferred beyond Nandakovāda.

## Main dhamma points
- The seventeenth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation on how a sound becomes "a crow," how the person is assembled, and non-occurrence.
- **The three stages of a sound.** When a sound is heard, the original mind has no connection at all to "bird / crow / TV." At the moment of hearing, no such thing arose, "hearing" was not in the mind, and "bird / crow / dog" was not in the mind; only at the third stage, by thought-fabrication (*vitakka*), is "a crow" produced. This is the proliferation (*papañca*) stage.
- Invoking *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti; yaṁ sañjānāti taṁ vitakketi; yaṁ vitakketi taṁ papañceti*: what is felt is recognised as "a sound" (*saññā*), what is recognised is thought about (*vitakka*), and in the thinking it is known as "a crow, a dog" — proliferated together with a form (*rūpa*). Once knotted ("this sound is the bird's, this a vehicle's"), it is later untied as false — yet since the sound has a matching thing and the heard thing seems real, "how can hearing be false?" It is false only when the knot is rightly seen.
- **Consciousness finds no foothold.** If the perception is rightly recognised, eye and ear become irrelevant; unable to plant a foot on any of the five (*sota-, ghāna-, jivhā-, kāya-viññāṇa*), consciousness becomes *anidassana* (non-manifestive), and no consciousness manifests beyond.
- The knotting cannot be stopped "at form"; it happens *at the moment of recognition and thinking*. A taste is recognised, then thought into "apple / orange"; a smell into "what is it"; a touch on the body into "mattress / cushion / mosquito / ant" — and that recognised thing *is* the proliferation, which *is* rāga or dosa: a mosquito → collide, a pleasant thing → cling. So greed and aversion form beyond the proliferation, and there is nothing further to ask.
- **What are rāga and dosa for?** When one sees there is no place for consciousness to establish, what occasions greed and aversion? — the attempt to *escape* such a thing makes the proliferation real; we make proliferation real, and then, everything being real, the mantra runs on. For one who sees, there is no need to "remove" greed and aversion; as the path clears, what must happen happens, with no need to think of the goal.
- **The person assembled.** "Feeling" (*vindīma*) here means only the *vedanā* of the seen-heard-sensed-cognized — not eye, ear or nose, which are *determined by* the felt things. The felt thing is recognised (*saññā*) as sound / taste / form; the *vedanā-saññā* knot then produces the sense-base ("this came by eye, this by ear"), and that knot builds the five clung-to aggregates — the two principal ones, feeling and perception, knotting *is* the aggregates forming, which *is* the person forming.
- We have heard "feeling, perception" endlessly, but only seeing the knot *forming* reveals the non-occurrence. Chanting "the five clung-to aggregates are suffering" as a mantra does not help — it is not understood, and reciting it to one's death only builds another formation to carry onward.
- **Perception can recognise nothing.** Seeing the sound-mind, one cannot go beyond it — a sound-mind cannot produce a form ("when I say 'a sound,' can you produce from that word what the sound is?"). So this perception recognises nothing; what we take to be recognised is nothing recognised at all — the mirage — a never-existing thing "recognised." This is *saññā-vipallāsa* (perversion of perception): one perception is knotted onto another, a lone perception filled out into a thing not in it. The fault is not seeing perception *as* perception; seen through, there is no more room for "it came from there to here." With this the "eleven modes" (near/far, inferior/superior, internal/external, and the rest) end in their very name.
- **The oil-lamp (a witness).** Lighting a lamp, no light or flame actually "travels" from the matchstick to the wick — it is not a flame leaping across. Held close for two seconds it may not light; yet a fine thread at the wick-tip ignites instantly, because there the temperature/state for combustion is met — when the wick is thin it heats to ignition in a moment. Today it lit the instant it was brought near because a thread stuck out — a fine "witness" to *there and there it arises*.
- The **Nandakovāda Sutta**'s oil-lamp simile is recalled, through which an assembly of five hundred realised — showing that for one with the capacity, hearing even this much, rightly seen, suffices for realisation, "a hundred to two hundred percent"; and the purification is one in which nothing at all remains.
- **Why it stays hidden.** Focusing on "recognition as mere recognition" is not easy: for aeons the notion "it comes from outside" persists — a sound heard, a vibration, a wave arising — and these (scientific) accounts of vibration and wave distort it, making the mirage-simile impossible if the heard thing "really exists out there," proven by hearing and packaged as "a wave."
- **The defilement-sleep.** In a dream a dog barks and is also seen, and there is never any doubt — which is exactly why a dream cannot be told from waking, there being no difference at all. What then distinguishes sleeping from waking? Nothing — which shows the being sleeps in a "defilement-sleep" (*keles-nidda*), always covered by defilements. "Waking up" as we mean it — rising and thinking mindfully again — is not real waking; real waking is waking by wisdom from that defilement-sleep.

## Summary

This seventeenth good-friend discussion traces, step by step, how a bare sound becomes "a crow," how the person is thereby assembled, and why the whole process is a non-occurrence. The speakers begin by noting that when a sound is heard the original mind bears no relation to "bird, crow, or TV": at the moment of hearing no such thing has arisen, "hearing" is not present in the mind, and "crow" or "dog" is not present either; only at a third stage, through thought-fabrication, is "a crow" produced — the stage of proliferation. They lay this over the canonical sequence "what one feels, one perceives; what one perceives, one thinks about; what one thinks about, one proliferates": the felt sound is recognised as "a sound," then thought about, then known as a crow or a dog and proliferated together with a form. Once this knot is tied — this sound is the bird's, that a vehicle's — it can be untied as false, and yet, because the sound has its matching object and the heard thing seems real, one protests "how can hearing be false?" It is false, they answer, only when the knot itself is rightly seen; and when it is, eye and ear become irrelevant, consciousness can plant its foot nowhere among the five sense-consciousnesses, and so becomes non-manifestive, with nothing manifesting beyond.

The knotting, the teacher stresses, cannot be halted "at form" but happens at the very moment of recognising and thinking — a taste thought into apple or orange, a smell into "what is it," a touch into mattress, cushion, mosquito or ant — and that recognised thing simply *is* the proliferation, which simply *is* greed or aversion, colliding with the mosquito, clinging to the pleasant. So there is nothing further to ask about how greed and aversion arise: the attempt to escape such a thing makes the proliferation real, and once everything is real the mantra runs on by itself, while for one who sees there is no need to "remove" greed and aversion, since as the path clears what must happen simply happens. He then shows how the person is assembled: "feeling" here means only the *vedanā* of the seen-heard-sensed-cognized, not the eye or ear, which are themselves determined by what is felt; the felt thing is recognised as sound or taste or form, the feeling-and-perception knot produces the sense-base ("this came by eye, this by ear"), and that knot builds the five clung-to aggregates — the knotting of feeling and perception being nothing other than the aggregates forming, which is the person forming. We have heard the words "feeling, perception" countless times, he observes, but only seeing the knot actually form reveals the non-occurrence, whereas chanting "the aggregates are suffering" as a mantra never helps, since it is not understood and only builds one more formation to carry onward.

From here the discussion turns to why perception can recognise nothing at all. Seeing the sound-mind, one cannot get beyond it, for a sound-mind can produce no form — "when I say 'a sound,' can you produce from that word what the sound is?" So this perception recognises nothing, and what we take to be recognised is nothing recognised, exactly as in the mirage: a never-existing thing is "recognised." This is the perversion of perception, one perception knotted onto another and a lone perception filled out into a thing not contained in it; the fault is simply not seeing perception as perception, and once it is seen through there is no more room for "it came from there to here," so that the eleven modes of contemplation end in their very name. The teacher illustrates arising with the oil-lamp, recounting his own observation that no light or flame ever travels from the matchstick to the wick; held close for two seconds the lamp may not light, yet a fine thread at the wick-tip ignites at once, because just there the state for combustion is met — a fine witness to "there and there it arises." He recalls the oil-lamp simile of the *Nandakovāda Sutta*, through which an assembly of five hundred realised, taking this to show that one with the capacity needs only to hear this much, rightly seen, for realisation, and that the resulting purification leaves nothing whatever remaining.

Finally they consider why all this stays hidden. To focus on recognition as mere recognition is not easy, for the conviction that "it comes from outside" has held for aeons, reinforced by accounts of a sound as a vibration or a wave — accounts that distort it and make the mirage-simile impossible, since the heard thing seems to "really exist out there," proven by hearing and packaged as a wave. And in a dream a dog barks and is seen with never a doubt, which is precisely why a dream cannot be distinguished from waking: there is no difference at all. What then separates sleeping from waking? Nothing — which reveals that the being sleeps in a "defilement-sleep," perpetually covered by defilements, so that "waking up" in the ordinary sense, rising and thinking mindfully once more, is no real waking, and real waking is only the waking by wisdom from that sleep of defilements.
