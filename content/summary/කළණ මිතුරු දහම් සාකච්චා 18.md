## Main dhamma points
- The eighteenth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation on coincidence, the impossibility of "maintaining mindfulness," and the wishless (*appaṇihita*).
- **Nothing to rejoice or grieve over.** There is nothing to be glad of in gaining or sorry for in losing, for no one plans and gains, and no one plans and fails to gain; only one who sees *tatta tatta vipassati* understands the Buddha's word. On a highway, everyone drives with full attention knowing a crash would be ruinous, none intending to crash — yet the one who crashes crashes, not "the one meant to." One cuts vegetables praying "let me not cut my hand," yet cuts it; walks praying "let me not slip," yet slips and falls. Everything is coincidence (*ahambu*) and cannot be planned.
- **Where people get stuck.** When similar perceptions happen to arise on two occasions, one concludes "my plan worked out" — and *that* is the magician's own turf: he displays "the condition is being met," i.e. that the outcome is under our control. The worldling mind only ever sees "the condition is met" — I can carry out what I planned before, plan for next month, do it next month.
- **The wishless is everywhere.** The very aim "don't slip" ends in slipping, "don't cut" ends in cutting — so the *appaṇihita* (undirected) nature is present in every daily incident, the aspiration (*paṇidhi*) never determining the outcome.
- **The self-arisen knowledge and the good friend.** With whom did the Buddha discuss this? — it arose by *svayambhū-ñāṇa*, self-arisen knowledge, which should evoke great faith. Even now, though it is explained to one, it could never be thought out alone; we would push such incidents aside as mere aberrations ("I slipped up, a lapse") instead of taking them to insight. These errors are "a blessing in disguise" — the very incidents under discussion are all the *erring* ones, which we habitually shove away rather than use, forever trying instead to fix the work so it will not err.
- **"Maintaining mindfulness" is impossible.** Is what we try to maintain even a mindfulness — and is it mindfulness, or inattention, that we cultivate? The whole notion of "a mindfulness that can be maintained" is mistaken: even the Abhidhamma says a mind lasts but seventeen mind-moments, so is there any maintainable mind at all? To "maintain mindfulness" would require it to pass from mind to mind, and if it could be maintained there would be a someone and a persisting mindfulness — in which case error could *never* occur; and if error never occurred, "right" could never be found either, since unbroken rightness has no meaning. Hence consciousness is always "right," never catching an error, merely "rectifying" from somewhere. And plainly "I, a person, sitting here, maintain this" cannot be done.
- **When the impossibility is realised, the journey stops.** Like a tethered dog that runs in circles all day and finally, realising the running is useless, lies down at that very spot, so watching these incidents the sense of "it cannot be done" grows in the mind bit by bit toward a hundred-and-more percent. Only "it cannot be done" registers; hearing and hearing, it moves to forty, to fifty percent — and once the "it can't be done / it's a knot / there is only error" side passes fifty percent, the mind can no longer be forced to prove "here it is right." Realisation then settles on the far side of fifty, and from there it *descends* — wisdom establishing not on the growing side but on the wearing-away side.
- **Coincidence is our very nature.** Once one example is caught, more get caught, the same example returning again and again; we catch it only "now and then" because we have a little skill, but the nature itself is just that — and if it caught *every* time, one could not even think about the "right" side. So our very being is coincidence; and seeing everything as coincidence, the "I plan and do" story becomes impossible, whereupon wisdom — not formation, not thought-fabrication — establishes itself there.
- **Mindfulness and inattention are maintaining the blind.** The "mindfulness" we maintained is coincidence; we are deceived by a blind shot, taking the hits for "mindfulness" and the misses for "inattention," so that mindfulness-and-inattention is simply the tending of the blind (*kaṇā*). Realising this, the very "mindfulness" we cultivated is seen to be inattention, and the arising of wisdom is, in the end, just this "impossibility."
- **The wishless samādhi.** Falling into this samādhi *is* the realisation of impossibility (*appaṇihita-samādhi*): "one cannot aspire." The realisation that comes is precisely that no aspiration (*paṇidhāna*) can be made — one cannot bring about "may it be thus / may it not be thus," cannot do it by force. It ends there.

## Summary

This eighteenth good-friend discussion turns on coincidence and on the impossibility of "maintaining mindfulness." The speakers begin from the point that there is nothing to be glad of in gaining or sorry for in losing, since no one plans and gains and no one plans and fails, so that only one who sees "there and there it arises" understands the Buddha's word. They pile up ordinary illustrations: on the highway everyone drives with full attention, knowing a crash would be catastrophic, none intending it, yet the one who crashes crashes — never conveniently "the one meant to"; one cuts vegetables while praying not to cut one's hand, and cuts it; one walks praying not to slip, and slips. Everything is coincidence and cannot be planned. And here, they note, is exactly where people get caught: when similar perceptions happen to recur, one concludes "my plan worked out," which is precisely the crossroads magician's turf, for he displays "the condition is being met," the comforting sense that outcomes lie under our control — so that the worldling mind only ever registers "the condition is met," believing it can execute what it planned before and plan again for later. But the wishless nature, they insist, is present in every incident: the aim "don't slip" ends in slipping, and the aspiration never decides the result.

From here the conversation asks with whom the Buddha could have discussed such things, and answers: it arose by self-arisen knowledge — which should awaken deep faith. Even when it is explained, it could never be reasoned out alone; we would file such incidents away as mere aberrations rather than carry them to insight, whereas in truth these errors are a blessing in disguise, for the very incidents under discussion are all the *erring* ones that we habitually push aside instead of using, forever busy fixing our work so it will not slip. The teacher then dismantles "maintaining mindfulness" itself: is what we try to maintain even a mindfulness, and is it mindfulness or inattention that we cultivate? The notion of a maintainable mindfulness is incoherent, since even the Abhidhamma grants a mind only seventeen mind-moments; to maintain it would require it to pass from mind to mind, and if it could be maintained there would be a self and an abiding mindfulness, in which case error could never occur — and if error never occurred, "right" could never be found either, unbroken rightness being meaningless. This is why consciousness is always "right," never catching its own error, merely rectifying from somewhere; and plainly "I, a person, sitting here, maintain this" simply cannot be done.

When that impossibility is genuinely realised, they say, the journey stops — like the tethered dog that runs in circles all day and at last, seeing the running is useless, lies down where it stands. Watching these incidents, the sense of "it cannot be done" grows bit by bit, moving to forty and then fifty percent, and once the side of "it can't be done, it's a knot, there is only error" passes the halfway mark, the mind can no longer be forced to prove "here it is right"; realisation settles beyond that point and from there descends, wisdom establishing not on the swelling side but on the side that wears away. We catch such an example only now and then, having a little skill, but the nature itself is exactly that, and were it caught every time one could not even entertain the "right" side — so coincidence is our very being, and once everything is seen as coincidence the "I plan and do" story becomes impossible and wisdom, not formation and not thought, takes its place. The "mindfulness" we so carefully maintained is thus itself coincidence: deceived by a blind shot, we call the hits "mindfulness" and the misses "inattention," so that the whole business is merely the tending of the blind, and the very mindfulness we cultivated is seen at last to be inattention. The discussion closes on the wishless samādhi: to fall into it is nothing other than the realisation of impossibility, the seeing that no aspiration can be made and that "may it be thus, may it not be thus" can never be brought about by force.
