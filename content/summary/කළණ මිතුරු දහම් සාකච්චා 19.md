## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Several canonical passages are directly quoted, unattributed:
  - *cakkhusmiṁ sati, rūpe sati, cakkhu-viññāṇe sati, phassa-paññattiyā paññāpessatī-ti ṭhānam-etaṁ vijjati* ("when the eye is, when form is, when eye-consciousness is, that a designation 'contact' be made manifest — this is possible") and its negative counterpart *cakkhusmiṁ asati, rūpe asati, cakkhu-viññāṇe asati … n'etaṁ ṭhānaṁ vijjati* ("… this is not possible").
  - *cakkhuṁ paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu-viññāṇaṁ; tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* ("dependent on eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises; the meeting of the three is contact").
  - *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti; yaṁ sañjānāti taṁ vitakketi; yaṁ vitakketi taṁ papañceti* (feeling → perceiving → thinking → proliferating).
- A monk's "*nandiṭṭha* discourse" is referred to (a teacher, not a sutta). No sutta title is given for any of these; none should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The nineteenth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation giving the series' clearest reading of the contact-designation (*phassa-paññatti*) passage.
- **Seeing is the middle; eye and form are the two sides.** Seeing arises from not-knowing — seeing itself can never be known — and it is because we do not know that un-knowability that we connect "eye" and "form" and think about a "seeing," or speak of a "middle" whose cause is un-arisen.
- **The conditional "sati."** This is *cakkhusmiṁ sati, rūpe sati, cakkhu-viññāṇe sati, phassa-paññattiyā paññāpessatī-ti ṭhānam-etaṁ vijjati*: only *when* eye, form and eye-consciousness are taken as present is a designation "contact" made manifest — "this is possible / this is found." Crucially, *sati* ("when it is") is **not** "it exists": as with "*when* it rains, the river fills," which neither asserts that it rains, nor that the river filled, nor that it does not rain. The particle is purely conditional.
- **All four assertions are wrong.** It is wrong to say "because eye and form exist, contact arises"; wrong to say "because contact exists, eye and form were formed"; wrong to say "because contact exists, the causes knot"; and wrong to say "because the causes exist, contact arises." None of the four holds.
- **The shop and the tree.** A shop stands beside a tree. Passing by, one clearly remembers the shop and everyone in it, but has no memory whatever of the tree — though both the tree and the eye were present. Did a contact (a seeing of the tree) then arise? No. So one cannot say "because the causes (tree + eye) exist, contact forms," for it did not form. And conversely, if "seeing/contact" were a thing stably existing, then eye and form would *have* to knot every time and the tree would *have* to be seen — but it was not. So one can go neither to "seeing exists continuously" nor to "eye and form necessarily produce seeing"; both are wrong, which is exactly why the text uses the conditional "*sati*."
- **How the world becomes manifest.** When the three are taken as present, the designation "contact" arises, and there *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* is fulfilled — "dependent on eye and forms eye-consciousness arises; the meeting of the three is contact" becomes manifest as "the world." From contact then unrolls *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti; yaṁ sañjānāti taṁ vitakketi; yaṁ vitakketi taṁ papañceti* — the felt is recognised (as, say, "a chair"), the recognised is thought about, and the thought becomes proliferation.
- **The negative side.** *cakkhusmiṁ asati, rūpe asati, cakkhu-viññāṇe asati … n'etaṁ ṭhānaṁ vijjati*: where eye, form and eye-consciousness are *not* taken as present — as with the un-noticed tree — no designation "contact" is made manifest; "this is not possible." There no contact is found, and one cannot even say "there is no contact"; one can say neither "the form was there" nor "it was not" — a place beyond both.
- **Wisdom sees both sides.** Seeing this not-knowing, one can go neither to "seen" nor to "not-seen"; the fault of both is visible only to one who has seen the not-knowing. That wisdom works on both sides at once — seeing that when the three are taken as present a "contact" becomes manifest, and that when they are not, no contact and no manifestation stands. That is *paññā*.
- **Even "wisdom" is only a manner of speaking.** Where contact does not stand, there is not even a cause to call it "wisdom"; only where contact is seen is there "wisdom" — and even this is said only as a topic, for no "wisdom-thing" is needed. Wisdom has an end where *paññā* arises, but one cannot say "it exists," since to say so is to grasp a thing again; this is why the arahant is called *paññā-vimutta* (liberated by wisdom) without importing any "wisdom-thing."
- **Cessation precedes the knowledge of rise-and-fall.** Only one who has seen the unconstructed (*asaṅkhata*) gains *udayabbaya-ñāṇa*; as a monk's discourse puts it, that knowledge does not *produce* a cessation — rather, only one who has *seen* cessation gains the knowledge of rise-and-fall. Because not-knowing is not known, contact is formed; seen as not-knowing, contact is not formed. So **from the very same one place comes saṁsāra, and from the very same one place, nibbāna.**

## Summary

This nineteenth good-friend discussion is the series' most careful reading of the contact-designation passage, worked out almost entirely through a single scriptural formula and one homely example. The speakers begin by placing "seeing" as the middle, with eye and form as its two sides, and observe that seeing arises from not-knowing — that seeing itself can never be known — so that it is precisely our ignorance of this un-knowability that leads us to connect "eye" and "form" and to conjure a "seeing," or to speak of a middle whose cause has never arisen. They then anchor the whole analysis in the phrase "when the eye is, when form is, when eye-consciousness is, a designation 'contact' is made manifest — this is possible," insisting that the little word *sati*, "when it is," is not at all the same as "it exists." Their illustration is rain: "when it rains, the river fills" asserts neither that it rains, nor that the river has filled, nor that it does not rain — it is purely conditional. From this they draw the sharp conclusion that all four ways of stating the matter are wrong: not "because eye and form exist, contact arises," not "because contact exists, eye and form were formed," not "because contact exists, the causes knot," and not "because the causes exist, contact arises."

The shop-and-tree example makes this concrete and is the heart of the talk. A shop stands beside a tree, and passing by we vividly remember the shop and everyone in it, yet retain no memory whatever of the tree — although the tree and the eye were both plainly present. Did a contact, a seeing of the tree, then arise? It did not. So one cannot say "because the causes exist, contact forms," since here it did not form; and conversely, if seeing were a thing stably existing, then eye and form would have to knot every single time and the tree would have to be seen — yet it was not. Neither "seeing exists continuously" nor "eye and form necessarily produce seeing" can therefore be maintained, and this, they show, is exactly why the text speaks conditionally. When the three *are* taken as present, the designation "contact" is made manifest, and there "dependent on eye and forms eye-consciousness arises; the meeting of the three is contact" is fulfilled as "the world," from which unrolls the familiar sequence — the felt is perceived, the perceived is thought about, and the thought becomes proliferation. But the negative form of the passage completes the picture: where eye, form and eye-consciousness are not taken as present, as with the un-noticed tree, no designation "contact" is made manifest, "this is not possible" — and there one can say neither that contact exists nor that it does not, neither that the form was there nor that it was not, a place lying beyond both.

From here the teacher draws the doctrinal conclusions. Seeing this not-knowing, one can go neither to "seen" nor to "not-seen," for the fault of both is visible only to one who has seen the not-knowing itself; and that wisdom operates on both sides at once, seeing both that a "contact" becomes manifest when the three are taken as present and that no contact stands when they are not — and this is what *paññā* is. Yet even "wisdom," he cautions, is only a manner of speaking: where contact does not stand there is not even a cause to call it wisdom, and where contact is seen "wisdom" is said only as a topic, for no wisdom-thing is required; wisdom reaches an end where *paññā* arises, but one cannot say "it exists," since to say so is to grasp a thing again — which is why the arahant is called liberated by wisdom without any "wisdom-thing" being brought in. He closes by inverting the usual order of insight-knowledge: only one who has seen the unconstructed gains the knowledge of rise-and-fall, and, as a monk's discourse has it, that knowledge does not produce a cessation but arises only in one who has already seen cessation. Because not-knowing is not known, contact is formed; seen as not-knowing, contact is not formed — so that, in the end, saṁsāra and nibbāna are found in the very same one place.
