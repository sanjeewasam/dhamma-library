## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Two canonical phrases are directly quoted, unattributed:
  - *imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti* ("when this is, that is") — the general formula of conditionality.
  - *(yo) paṭiccasamuppādaṁ passati so dhammaṁ passati* ("who sees dependent origination sees the Dhamma").
- Doctrinal terms used: *paccayā* (condition), *phassa / passa*, *paññatti* (with *vacī-* and *rūpa-paññatti*), *saññā* as *marīci* (mirage), *aññamañña*, *āyatana-nirodha*, *bhava-nirodha*, *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, *aṭaloka-dhamma* (the eight worldly conditions), *upekkhā*. No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The second "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation drawing together earlier threads (the un-noticed breath, the tile-gap, the jasmine fragrance) into a single account of contact, designation and the cessation of the sense-bases.
- **The danger of turning the teaching into a formula.** Of the un-knowable, unobtainable nature one can say in words "it cannot be known / cannot be obtained," but if that phrase is then applied to *everything* — "how is a perception obtained where nothing can be obtained? how a feeling?" — as though solving an equation, one only imagines one has understood; this is not insight. At the true nature (where no "I" stands) one cannot even say "cannot be obtained," since that presupposes a person who cannot obtain; the words are fine for teaching, but what must be *seen* is that very place.
- **Why watch conditionality.** One watches "arising-by-condition" not because the world is "wrong," but to break free of the deceived nature we live in daily. Seeing the condition, one also sees the condition is *never found*, and from the un-found place sees how the condition seems to arise — not to destroy anything, but to expose the fabrication (the *vikṛti*), which becomes "real" only because we do not know how the condition arises.
- **The tile-gap (central).** Between two tiles a "line" appears, seemingly "because of the two tiles." Does the gap belong to the tiles? To neither. But "unrelated to the tiles" is equally wrong, since without the tiles no line appears — so both "related" and "unrelated" are wrong. Seen from the line's own side, the tiles are not found *in* the line; hence "the line is because of the tiles" is wrong and "the line has no relation to the tiles" is wrong, because we take up *un-found* tiles even to speak of the line. Seen as gap, the line is isolated, a dhamma of its own, the tiles irrelevant to it.
- **This is contact (*phassa*).** The gap/line is contact; eye and form are the two tiles; seeing is the line. So all four are wrong: "because eye and form, seeing"; "seeing exists, so eye and form"; "contact exists, so the causes knot"; "the causes exist, so contact." Contact does not *stand* (*na pihiṭīma*) — it is "empty"; but one must not understand it as "empty" either, for then "empty" becomes a thing ("empty of what? — of tiles, of eye, of form"), which is one more conceiving. To see contact by contact (*passa passayen*) is *passa-nirodha* — contact never stands, and no consciousness or wisdom ever finds such a standing; where it is not found *is* wisdom, and the extraordinary awareness that settles there sees all the "ways of standing," i.e. all views.
- **The two-fold realisation, and the same base.** The realisation is two-fold: (1) that not-knowing (*avijjā*) lies outside consciousness's range — the earlier discussion; and (2) that, not knowing that un-cognizable not-knowing, the knot forms with "condition" (*pacchayā*) as its root — today's discussion. These are not the same place but rest on the *same base*, ignorance: the earlier talk was the ignorance-nature itself (the "off," knot-free state), and today's is the contact *built upon* that base by not knowing it — like a building on its foundation.
- **Links to the breath and the jasmine.** The un-noticed breath "since morning" was a non-obtaining, a non-standing that was taken as real; likewise "a feeling of breath at the nose" is a non-standing given reality — so the earlier non-obtaining and this contact-level are one. The jasmine (*pichcha mal*) fragrance makes the same point: "jasmine fragrance" is only the intention/perception "jasmine," and jasmine was never in the intention; perception is a *mirage* (*saññā = marīci*). The fragrance was never actually experienced; only "a fragrance" was thought, and the very intention that thinks "I recognised a fragrance" is itself the mirage. Even seeing the jasmine basket at the stupa (eye and nose together), neither the intention that "saw" nor the one that "smelt" contains any jasmine.
- **How the knot supports itself.** Feeling (*vedanā*) is the "value/signal" of the recognised; we recognise "a fragrance," yet a fragrance was never actually known, and not knowing that non-knowing we slide into the conceiving "it is smelt." So perception ("fragrance") and feeling (the felt value) are conditions that knot — and that knotting *is* the knot: feeling confirms the fragrance and the fragrance confirms the feeling (*aññamañña*, mutual support), the crossroads magic that cannot be grasped. Thus "fragrance" is a thought-construct and "smelling" is a thought-construct — two constructs, mere thinking; the jasmine was never in the world, the "dog" never there (dog and seeing being two sides of one construct), and even in a dream "dog" is a perception while "barking" is "hearing" fabricated, sound and hearing arising together and mutually.
- **How designation (*paññatti*) is built.** From the non-standing contact — a mere thought-construct — the designations "mother," "sound," "crow," "world" are built (*vacī-paññatti, rūpa-paññatti*). The teacher corrects a slip: contact is not itself the *paññatti* but the *root* from which it forms — at the non-standing place a name is given to contact ("seeing"), then "mother appears because of seeing" is knotted on, and so the designation arises.
- **Cessation of the sense-bases and of "I".** An eye is obtained only if a form is seen, a nose only if a fragrance is obtained; if jasmine-fragrance was never obtained, there is no nose — so the sense-base ceases, the *āyatana* being thought-constructs. Because seeing arises, the "seer I" stands; because feeling, the felt thing stands; "the meeting of the three stands" *is* "I stand." Where this standing does not occur, no "I" and no being arises — *bhava-nirodha*, *āyatana-nirodha*, the crushing of self-view (*sakkāya-diṭṭhi*). Hence *paṭiccasamuppādaṁ passati so dhammaṁ passati*: the "Dhamma" taught is exactly this non-obtained nature, which is why it is called "only a raft."
- **Method and fruit.** Watching the knot form — with sound, with smell — consciousness's knotting gradually lessens and heavy attachment thins, so that awareness settles toward the "floor" (*patula*): the equanimity level where "did I breathe since morning, was it felt or not" are both absent. The meditative samādhi (*samatha*) draws one near that floor, and the one near the floor receives this wisdom — samādhi supporting *paññā* — so that virtue is kept, concentration forms and wisdom arises. One is thereby unshaken by the eight worldly conditions (*aṭaloka-dhamma*), whose gains and losses come only at the consciousness-level (the knot); seeing both sides of the knot, pleasure and pain are not split apart but seen as equal — and equanimity is not "removing an existing pleasure and pain to reach a middle" but seeing that there is no cause for pleasure or pain to stand at all, pleasure and pain, greed and aversion, cold and heat being like the two tiles, and the line where no tile is.

## Summary

This second good-friend discussion gathers several strands from earlier talks — the un-noticed breath, the tile-gap, the jasmine fragrance — into one careful account of contact, designation and the cessation of the sense-bases. The speakers begin with a warning against turning the teaching into a formula: of the un-knowable, unobtainable nature one can indeed say "it cannot be obtained," but to apply that phrase mechanically to everything, asking "how is a perception obtained where nothing can be obtained?", is merely to imagine understanding, not to see. At the true nature, where no "I" stands, one cannot even say "cannot be obtained," since that already assumes a person who fails to obtain; the words serve to teach another, but what must actually be seen is that very place. And the point of watching conditionality, they stress, is not that the world is wrong but that we live in a deceived nature and must break free of it — for seeing the condition, one sees that the condition is never found, and from the un-found place one sees how it seems to arise, exposing the fabrication that becomes "real" only through our ignorance of how it arises.

The heart of the talk is the tile-gap. Between two tiles a line appears, apparently because of the tiles; yet the gap belongs to neither tile, while "unrelated to the tiles" is equally false, since without the tiles no line shows — so both "related" and "unrelated" collapse. Seen from the line's own side the tiles are simply not found in it, so neither "the line is because of the tiles" nor "the line is unrelated to the tiles" holds, because we take up un-found tiles even to speak of the line at all. This, they show, is contact: the gap is contact, eye and form the two tiles, seeing the line — and all four statements about it are wrong. Contact does not stand; it is "empty," yet one must not rest in "empty," for then "empty" becomes a thing, empty "of tiles, of eye, of form," which is one more conceiving. To see contact by contact is the cessation of contact, for contact never stands and no consciousness or wisdom ever finds such a standing; where it is not found is wisdom itself, and the awareness that settles there sees every "way of standing," which is to say every view. The realisation, they add, is two-fold and rests on a single base: the earlier discussion had shown that ignorance lies beyond consciousness's range, the knot-free "off" state itself; today's shows how, not knowing that un-cognizable ignorance, the knot forms with "condition" as its root — a building raised on the same foundation of ignorance.

They then bind this to the concrete examples. The un-noticed breath "since morning" was a non-obtaining taken as real, and "a feeling of breath at the nose" is likewise a non-standing given reality, so the earlier non-obtaining and this contact are one. The jasmine fragrance drives it home: "jasmine fragrance" is only the perception "jasmine," in which no jasmine is present, for perception is a mirage; the fragrance was never actually experienced, only thought, and the very intention that thinks "I recognised a fragrance" is itself the mirage — even at the stupa, with eye and nose both engaged, neither the intention that saw nor the one that smelt contains any jasmine. Feeling is merely the value of the recognised, and since a fragrance was never really known, not knowing that non-knowing we slide into "it is smelt"; so perception and feeling knot together, feeling confirming the fragrance and the fragrance the feeling in mutual support — the ungraspable crossroads magic — leaving "fragrance" and "smelling" as two thought-constructs and nothing more. From such non-standing contact, they explain, the designations "mother," "sound," "crow," "world" are built, contact being not the designation itself but the root from which, once named "seeing," the construct "mother appears because of seeing" is knotted on. And so the sense-bases themselves are seen to cease: an eye only where a form is seen, a nose only where a fragrance is obtained, so that where jasmine-fragrance was never obtained there is no nose — the sense-bases being thought-constructs — and where the "meeting of the three" does not stand, no "I" and no being arises, which is the cessation of existence and of the sense-bases and the crushing of self-view. This, they conclude, is what "who sees dependent origination sees the Dhamma" means, the Dhamma being just this non-obtained nature, and therefore only a raft. Finally they describe the fruit: as one watches the knot form, consciousness's knotting thins and awareness settles toward the "floor," the equanimity level where the questions "did I breathe, was it felt" fall away; meditative calm draws one to that floor and there wisdom arises, so that virtue, concentration and wisdom unfold together, and one stands unshaken by the eight worldly conditions — seeing pleasure and pain not as a middle reached by removing them but as having no ground to stand on at all, like the two tiles and the line where no tile is.
