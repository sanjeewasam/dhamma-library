## Sutta referred to
- **Cūḷa-suññata Sutta** (චූල ශුන්‍යත සූත්‍රය) — named: cited for the graded emptiness beginning with forest-perception and village-perception, ascending through neither-perception-nor-non-perception, transcending even the sense-bases, to "supreme unsurpassed emptiness" (*paramānuttara-suññatā*).
- Directly quoted phrases, unattributed:
  - *māyā-upamañca viññāṇaṁ* ("consciousness is like an illusion / a magic-show").
  - *manasikāra-sambhavā sabbe dhammā* ("all dhammas originate from attention").
  - *phassa-mūlaka sabbe dhammā* ("all dhammas are rooted in contact").
- The **Māluṅkyaputta** teaching (a thing not experienced in past/present/future can occasion no lust) is referred to, with Māluṅkyaputta named as a person. The Buddha's calling the eye/ear "old kamma" (*purāṇa-kamma*) is referred to without a sutta title. No sutta should be inferred beyond Cūḷa-suññata.

## Main dhamma points
- The twentieth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation on annihilation (*uccheda*), the fabricated "mental process," and the assembly of the continuous "I."
- **The world's "annihilation" is another eternalism.** True *uccheda* is the un-known nature, of which nothing is known. But what meditators reach by cultivating calm (*samatha*) to a state where "nothing is understood" is an *uccheda-eternalism* they mistake for nibbāna — precisely because they do not recognise it.
- **The sleep analogy.** In sleep one can say neither "seeing exists" nor "seeing does not exist"; sleep is never actually experienced, and neither "there is sleep" nor "sleep is absent" is known. So "sleep" is a non-obtaining — and even "non-obtaining" cannot be said, for to say "annihilation" turns it into eternalism. This un-knowable nature can be seen only by wisdom, never being within consciousness's range.
- **The breath and the "body."** The breath is never experienced; "I breathe" registers only when *recalled* (by attention). What then arises is the mind-base — mind and thought — not a "body"; the "body" is knotted on by the mind, no body having been experienced before the recollection. Before the question, neither "there is a body/breath" nor "there is not" was present, so the bodily experience never actually existed; only in thinking do "a body, a breath" appear, and that felt experience is the mind's (*mano-dhamma*), a mere intention (*cetanā*), not a bodily experience. Invoking Māluṅkyaputta: the body can never be experienced by the body — only thought "experiences" it — so it is mind-intention, not body; and a never-experienced thing thought about is a conceiving, an illusion, like a projected image from a lens where there is no projector: *māyā-upamañca viññāṇaṁ*, consciousness the magician showing "a body and a breath" as two real things whenever one thinks.
- **The tree (photosynthesis) analogy.** A tree takes in carbon dioxide and releases oxygen with no awareness that it does so; a process occurs, but it is not "relevant" to the tree. We, however, have built a *mental process* for ourselves that is likewise not relevant to the body — and it is this fabricated mental process (the awareness/consciousness) that binds us to the world and builds saṁsāra. A dying tree has no "I go to the woeful states"; only because we have built this mental process do we knot "saṁsāra" — "I existed in a past life, and that same I was born here." When that process is released (in the arahant), the tree and this body are one, merging with the un-obtained nature and freed even from "there is such a nature" — becoming tree-like.
- **Kamma and result.** "My leg broke" contains, in the breaking, no "leg" and no past-life story; but we knot on "leg," and then "the leg broke because in a past life I broke an animal's leg." When "the leg broke" is made real in two instances — past and present — the two are knotted into the result-story; yet seen rightly, "breaking" holds no idea of "leg," and a "leg" is never actually obtained in past or present. So kamma too is a piece of the delusion, and *vipāka* is "real" only within it — real because the action was made real, the two instances then knotting into a cause-and-result tale.
- **Attention and contact as root.** *Manasikāra-sambhavā sabbe dhammā* and *phassa-mūlaka sabbe dhammā*: all dhammas originate from attention and are rooted in contact — a dhamma (a leg, a breath) arises only with the arising of attention. And, as the crossroads magician, at the moment of arising it cannot be grasped or denied from any side: "there is no breath" cannot be argued while a breath is felt, the felt breath being taken as *the* breath, so it can never be called false — exactly as a dream, seen as real while dreaming, cannot be recognised as a dream from within it, its recognition requiring wisdom beyond consciousness.
- **The continuous "I."** From morning, hearing, seeing and sensing accumulate into "a self who persisted and did things all day" — memory weaving a lovely story of "I, my son, the world, a fine day." But seeing the knot forming, no world and no "I" is built: "the world being built" is both world and "I" built together — seeing form builds a "seer," sound a "hearer" — so that a hearer-seer-taster-knower seems to persist continuously. The five sense-consciousnesses are like six lights in one bulb showing different colours in turn: eye-and-form makes the form-world, ear-and-sound the sound-world, and so on, and knotting all five yields one continuous "I" who sees, hears, tastes and knows.
- **Supreme emptiness, and even that released.** In the *Cūḷa-suññata Sutta*'s ascent, one passes forest- and village-perception, up through neither-perception-nor-non-perception, transcending the sense-bases, to supreme unsurpassed emptiness — the place where not a drop is obtained. Yet even "empty" is one more obtaining (as "no sleep" is an obtaining within sleep), so this supreme emptiness *is* the nature-dhamma (*sabhāva-dharma*), tree-like — and even "empty" must be released.
- **The eye as "old kamma."** The Buddha calls the eye and ear "old kamma": through the raft, this means that not seeing the action (*kamma / kriyā*) rightly is exactly why an eye is obtained now — because in past lives the action was not seen through, an eye, ear and nose are obtained again, which is saṁsāra. For one who sees through, no kamma forms and no rebirth follows; self-view is crushed.

## Summary

This twentieth good-friend discussion opens on annihilation and quickly distinguishes true *uccheda* — the un-known nature of which nothing is known — from the "annihilation" the world imagines. What meditators reach by driving calm to a state where "nothing is understood," the speakers say, is really an annihilation-eternalism mistaken for nibbāna, precisely because it is not recognised for what it is. They illustrate with sleep: in sleep one can say neither that seeing exists nor that it does not, and sleep itself is never experienced, so "sleep" is a non-obtaining that cannot even be called a "non-obtaining," since to name it "annihilation" already turns it into an eternalism; this un-knowable nature can be seen only by wisdom, lying forever outside consciousness's range. The breath makes the same point in a more intimate way. The breath is never actually experienced; "I breathe" registers only when it is recalled, and what arises on recollection is the mind-base — mind and thought — not a body, the "body" being knotted on by the mind. Before the question was even asked, neither "there is a body and breath" nor "there is not" was present, so the bodily experience never existed at all; only in thinking do a body and a breath appear, and that is the mind's own intention, not a bodily experience. Recalling the teaching to Māluṅkyaputta, the teacher stresses that the body can never be experienced by the body, only thought about, so that a never-experienced thing entertained by thought is a conceiving, an illusion — like an image thrown from a lens where there is no projector at all — and consciousness is the magician who, whenever one thinks, displays "a body and a breath" as two real things.

The tree becomes the governing image. A tree draws in carbon dioxide and gives off oxygen without the least awareness of doing so; a process occurs, but it is not "relevant" to the tree. We, by contrast, have built for ourselves a mental process that is equally irrelevant to the body — and it is exactly this fabricated mental process, the awareness, that binds us to the world and constructs saṁsāra. A dying tree entertains no "I shall go to the woeful states"; only because we have raised this mental process do we knot together a saṁsāra in which "I existed in a past life and that same I was born here." When the process is released, as in the arahant, the tree and this body are one, merged into the un-obtained nature and freed even of "there is such a nature," so that one becomes tree-like. The same analysis dissolves kamma and result: "my leg broke" contains, in the bare breaking, no "leg" and no past life, but we knot on a "leg" and then a story that the leg broke because in a former life we broke an animal's leg; when "the leg broke" is made real across two instances, past and present, they are tied into a result — yet seen rightly "breaking" holds no idea of "leg," and no leg is ever really obtained, so that kamma too is a strand of the delusion and *vipāka* is real only within it, real only because the action was first made real.

From here the teacher draws the roots together — all dhammas originate from attention and are rooted in contact, a leg or a breath arising only as attention arises — and repeats that at the moment of arising the thing can be neither grasped nor denied from any side, the felt breath being taken as the breath so that it can never be called false, exactly as a dream cannot be known as a dream from within. He then shows how the continuous "I" is assembled: from morning onward, hearings and seeings and sensings are gathered by memory into a lovely tale of a self who persisted and acted all day, of "I, my son, the world, a fine day"; but once the knot is seen forming, neither world nor "I" is built, for the building of the world is the building of both together — seeing form raises a seer, sound a hearer — so that a single hearer-seer-taster-knower seems to run on continuously, the five sense-consciousnesses being like six coloured lights in one bulb, eye-and-form making the form-world, ear-and-sound the sound-world, and all five knotted into one persisting "I." Finally he turns to the *Cūḷa-suññata Sutta*'s ascent through ever-subtler emptiness up to supreme unsurpassed emptiness, the place where not a drop is obtained, only to warn that even "empty" is one more obtaining — as "no sleep" is an obtaining within sleep — so that this supreme emptiness simply *is* the nature-dhamma, tree-like, and even "empty" must be let go; and he closes on the Buddha's calling the eye and ear "old kamma," which, taken as a raft, means that an eye is obtained now precisely because the action was never seen through in past lives, so that for one who does see through, no kamma forms, no rebirth follows, and self-view is crushed.
