## Sutta referred to
- **Alagaddūpama Sutta** (අලගද්දූපම සූත්‍රය) — named: the speakers associate with it the "does-not-conceive" (*na maññati*) passage discussed at length, note that an assembly of five hundred realised in connection with it, and recall its raft simile. They challenge anyone who argues to take a single line of it and explain it.
- The **fourfold "does-not-conceive" passage** is quoted/paraphrased in Sinhala and applied to each sense: the Tathāgata does not conceive a to-be-seen thing apart from seeing, does not conceive an "unseen," does not conceive a "to-be-seen," does not conceive a "seer" — and likewise for the heard, sensed and cognized.
- The **unanswered (avyākata) questions** — a brahmin asking whether it exists / does not exist / both / neither after death, and the Buddha declaring each question wrong — are referred to without a sutta title.
- The phrase *pubbe ananussutesu dhammesu* ("a dhamma not heard before") is used. No sutta should be inferred beyond Alagaddūpama.

## Main dhamma points
- The twenty-first "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation built around the fourfold "does-not-conceive" formula and the nature of the un-known.
- **The Tathāgata conceives no seen, unseen, to-be-seen, or seer.** (1) We hold that things exist *apart from* seeing — the town shop or post office "exist" though unseen now; the Buddha forms no such notion of "things existing apart from seeing." (2) We form "oh, this was there and I didn't see it" (the salad that was present but unnoticed); the Buddha forms no "unseen thing existed." (3) Because we hold that unseen things exist — Somāwatiya, Nāgadīpa, unseen yet believed real — we expect "there are things to be seen if I go"; the Buddha has no such expectation of a "to-be-seen." (4) And there is for him no sense of a separate "seer." The same fourfold applies to hearing, and to the rest.
- If all five sense-fields are seen rightly one is safe, but any *analysis* lands one in one of these four views; only wisdom settled at the un-known nature escapes all four. This is *pubbe ananussutesu dhammesu*, a dhamma not heard before — not a subject or lesson but an extraordinary vision transcending consciousness, an extraordinary awareness beyond description, whose wonder dies the moment it is made into a "matter."
- **Going further is only the reduction of suffering.** In the most comfortable posture, weight and pain arise within minutes and the posture wears out — so what we hold as "pleasure" is only a reduction of pain, and arising itself is suffering, "birth like the beginning of death."
- **Birth is the obtaining of a death.** For us "someone was born" means "born, then dies"; but a birth today is a death added to today's death-list. To the arahant a birth appears *as* a death — birth and death alike being "obtainings" (*läbīma*), not a "born-then-perishing." Birth itself is the obtaining of death.
- **The seen is "I".** Seeing itself is "I" — not "I existed and then saw," but with seeing, "I." Seen as a mere reflection of seeing, the notions "a seer" and "a to-be-seen" cannot form; the "I" is separated out only because seeing was made real, and no "person obtains" the seeing, since the person is just the seeing done.
- **The heavy-bag and "lightness."** Setting down a heavy bag, one exclaims "what relief!" — but this "lightness" is not a felt thing; one simply returns to the state one was in before lifting the bag, and one never feels "lightness" while merely sitting. So "lightness" is neither known-as-present nor known-as-absent — outside consciousness's range; "light" is really just "the weight gone," the *not-feeling*, which we misname "lightness." The whole affair is the pre-existing, un-noticed state to which one returns — and both "the lightness is felt" and "not felt" fall outside what consciousness can register. This is exactly the un-known nature that the "does-not-conceive" passage points to, falling into none of the four.
- **The Alagaddūpama Sutta and the raft.** Through this sutta an assembly of five hundred realised; the teacher offers to fall silent if any learned disputant can write out and explain a single line of its "does-not-conceive" formula. And the raft simile is recalled: the Dhamma is a means, not a thing to be held.
- **The unanswered questions.** To a brahmin's "does it exist after death? / does it not? / both? / neither?", the Buddha declares each question wrong, because this cannot be grasped by consciousness or reached by thinking.
- **Nibbāna conceived as separate is itself conceiving.** People take saṁsāra as one thing and the supramundane / nibbāna as a separate thing to escape into — and that is itself *maññanā*, conceiving nibbāna. The real fault is not knowing convention (*sammuti*) rightly; to see convention *as* convention *is* the supramundane, and to see through is not to see another thing. Nibbāna is the absolute (*nirapekṣa*), the world the relative (*sāpekṣa*); seeking an absolute nibbāna, one only rebuilds a relative pair of "saṁsāra versus nibbāna."
- **The magic-show.** A trick is "magic" only while one does not know how it is done; grasp the system and "magic" dissolves — there is no "magic exists / does not exist." Saṁsāra is the magic: grasp its workings and both "saṁsāra exists" and "saṁsāra does not exist" fall away, and not that a separate "non-existence" then exists — for once the mechanism is seen, "is there magic?" and "is there no magic?" are alike wrong. The arahant is a *muni*; and such a one can then dwell subtly and skilfully in practical life.

## Summary

This twenty-first good-friend discussion is organised around the Buddha's fourfold "does-not-conceive" formula, which the speakers read out and unfold sense by sense. The Tathāgata, they note, does not conceive a to-be-seen thing standing apart from seeing — whereas we firmly hold that things exist independently of our seeing, the town shop or post office "being there" though unseen at this moment. He conceives no "unseen thing that existed," while we readily form "oh, that was there and I simply didn't see it," as with a salad on the table one failed to notice. He conceives no "to-be-seen," whereas we, holding that unseen places like Somāwatiya or Nāgadīpa nonetheless exist, carry the expectation that "there are things to be seen if I go." And for him there is no separate "seer" at all. The same fourfold, they add, runs through hearing and the other senses; and while all five fields seen rightly leave one safe, any attempt to *analyse* drops one into one of the four views, so that only wisdom settled at the un-known nature slips past them all. This, they stress, is a dhamma never heard before — not another subject to be studied but an extraordinary vision beyond consciousness, whose very wonder evaporates the instant it is turned into a "matter" to be explained.

They pause to characterise what "going further" amounts to: only the reduction of suffering. Even the most comfortable posture grows heavy and painful within minutes and wears itself out, so that what we call pleasure is merely a lessening of pain, and arising itself is suffering — birth being like the beginning of death. This yields a striking reframing: for us "someone was born" means born and then, later, dying; but a birth today is really a death entered on today's list, and to the arahant a birth appears *as* a death, birth and death alike being obtainings rather than a sequence of born-then-perished, since birth itself is simply the obtaining of a death. From here they turn to the "I": seeing itself is "I," not an "I" that pre-existed and then saw, and seen as a bare reflection of seeing, neither a "seer" nor a "to-be-seen" can form — the "I" being carved out only because seeing was made real, with no person left over to "obtain" the seeing, the person being nothing but the seeing done.

The heart of the talk is the heavy-bag illustration. Setting down a load, one sighs "what relief," yet this "lightness" is not a felt thing at all: one has merely returned to the state one occupied before lifting the bag, and while simply sitting one never feels any "lightness." So "lightness" is neither known as present nor known as absent — it lies outside consciousness's range; "light" is only "the weight gone," the not-feeling that we misname, and the whole episode is just the pre-existing, unnoticed state to which one returns. Both "the lightness is felt" and "it is not felt" thus fall outside what consciousness can register — which is precisely the un-known nature the "does-not-conceive" passage points to, escaping all four alternatives. The speakers tie this to the Alagaddūpama Sutta, through which five hundred realised, and the teacher offers to keep silent if any learned disputant can take a single line of its formula and explain it, while recalling its raft simile — the Dhamma a means, not a possession. They invoke, too, the unanswered questions, where the Buddha rejects "exists / does not / both / neither" after death as wrongly put, since the matter is beyond consciousness and beyond thought. Finally they expose the subtlest trap: taking nibbāna as a separate thing to escape into is itself the conceiving of nibbāna, the real fault being that convention is not seen as convention — for to see convention as convention *is* the supramundane, and to see through is not to glimpse some other thing. Nibbāna is the absolute and the world the relative, and one who hunts an absolute nibbāna merely manufactures a fresh relative pair of "saṁsāra and nibbāna." The magic-show seals it: a trick is "magic" only while its workings are unknown, and once the system is grasped the very word dissolves, so that "is there magic?" and "is there none?" are equally mistaken — as with saṁsāra, whose mechanism once seen leaves neither "it exists" nor "it does not," and no separate non-existence standing in its place; the arahant is thereby a *muni*, able to move subtly and skilfully through ordinary life.
