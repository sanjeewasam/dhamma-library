## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- The Buddha's **horse-hair / arrow simile** is referred to — that catching "feeling" by feeling itself is as hard as splitting a horse-hair and hitting the split with an arrow — but no sutta title is given.
- Terms used: *viññāṇa*, *paññā*, *avijjā*, *rāga / dosa / moha*, *sihiya / asihiya* (mindfulness / inattention), *samasta-dænīma* and *samasta-sihiya* ("total knowing" / "total mindfulness"). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The twenty-second "good-friend" dialogue; a short two-speaker conversation clarifying the phrase "I do not feel it" and the meaning of "total knowing."
- **"I do not feel it" is not the ordinary sense.** It does not mean "I fail to feel"; it points to a special condition — as if standing *outside* and shining a torch on the whole process, watching seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting and touching operate. It is a wakefulness (*avadimat bava*), but not a consciousness-based one.
- That wakefulness does not arise from consciousness. At the un-known nature one cannot split "mindful" from "inattentive" (*sihiya / asihiya*) as we do all day — it cannot be done, and this is not "refraining" but a transcending of both doing and not-doing. If feeling is caught *by feeling itself*, it is as fine as splitting a horse-hair and striking the split with an arrow; for one who so catches feeling, "feeling" no longer applies.
- **The danger in "total knowing."** The term *samasta-dænīma* is acceptable, but if it is taken to mean "catching every nameable knowing," it is wrong. The instruction "attend to the whole field, then concentrate on a single object" makes consciousness arise at that one object — so what does "total knowing" really mean?
- **The television analogy (central).** The "ON" state of a TV is not any channel, and one cannot define "ON" by summing up all the channels. "Total knowing" is the ON / the screen; the individual knowings are the channels. However many channels there are, and however they are added together, they are never the ON — the ON is ON whatever channel plays. So "total knowing" (*paññā*) has *no relation* to the individual knowings (the channels); it is not "five channels gathered into one channel" (which would still be consciousness concentrating on one object), but the un-related whole.
- Hence *samasta-dænīma* is wisdom (*paññā*); to focus on one from it is "consciousness arising"; and the instruction to watch the doings without greed, aversion or delusion is carried out by wisdom, a wakefulness that is not object-based.
- **Why no greed or aversion.** These arise only through not-knowing as condition; to concentrate on a single object is to concentrate on ignorance itself, on the deception, on the "I." At "total knowing" one cannot even make "feeling" manifest — just as, immersed in the rotating earth, one never experiences "rotating" (one only hears and believes it), so, immersed in feeling, one cannot experience "feeling." Catching feeling by feeling is that horse-hair-and-arrow subtlety.
- **Mindfulness and inattention governed from one place.** From that single place both are governed, and the governing itself becomes manifest: the inattentive moments and the mindful moments both appear *without being split apart*. Where the splitting does not apply, a "total mindfulness" (*samasta-sihiya*) is present — not a feeling, not an experience — and because our inattentive and our mindful moments alike are "touched/focused" there, "inattention" no longer applies, everything being seen from one place. We still keep "inattention" merely as a word only because inattention has not yet been caught.
- The other speaker draws it together: *sihiya / asihiya* apply only because of "I" — it is the arisen "I" that is mindful or inattentive; where wisdom settles and no "I" stands, neither "mindful" nor "inattentive" applies, there is no word for it, and no one who is mindful or inattentive.

## Summary

This short twenty-second good-friend discussion clarifies two things that had proved slippery: the phrase "I do not feel it," and the meaning of "total knowing." The teacher first insists that "I do not feel it" carries no ordinary sense of failing to feel; it names a peculiar condition, as though one stood outside and shone a torch over the whole process, watching seeing, hearing, smelling, tasting and touching operate — a wakefulness, but not one built on consciousness. At the un-known nature, he explains, one simply cannot perform the everyday split between "mindful" and "inattentive"; this is not a matter of refraining but of transcending both doing and not-doing, and if feeling is ever caught by feeling itself it is as fine as splitting a horse-hair and hitting the split with an arrow — so fine that, for one who catches it, "feeling" no longer even applies.

The heart of the talk is a correction about "total knowing." The term is allowable, the teacher grants, but it becomes an error the moment it is taken to mean "catching every nameable knowing," for the very instruction to survey the whole field and then concentrate on one object is what makes consciousness arise at that object. He clarifies with a television: the "ON" state is no channel at all, and cannot be arrived at by adding up all the channels; "total knowing" is the ON, the screen, while the individual knowings are the channels, and however many channels there are they are never the ON, which remains ON whatever plays. So "total knowing" — which is wisdom — bears no relation to the individual knowings, and is not "five channels gathered into one" (that would still be consciousness fixed on a single object) but the un-related whole. To focus on one knowing out of it is precisely "consciousness arising," whereas the instruction to watch one's doings without greed, aversion or delusion is carried out by this wisdom, an object-less wakefulness.

He then explains why greed and aversion do not arise there: they come only through not-knowing as their condition, so that to concentrate on a single object is to concentrate on ignorance, on the deception, on the "I." At the level of total knowing one cannot even make "feeling" appear — just as, carried along by the rotating earth, one never experiences "rotating" but only hears of it and believes it, so, immersed in feeling, one cannot experience "feeling" — and to catch feeling by feeling is that horse-hair subtlety. From this one place both mindfulness and inattention are governed, and that governing itself becomes manifest, the inattentive and the mindful moments alike appearing without ever being split apart; where the splitting does not apply there is a "total mindfulness," not a feeling and not an experience, and since our inattentive and mindful moments are equally lit from that one place, "inattention" no longer applies — we retain the word only because inattention has not yet been caught. The other speaker closes the circle: mindfulness and inattention have purchase only because of an "I," for it is the arisen "I" that is mindful or inattentive, and where wisdom settles and no "I" stands, neither term applies, there is no word for it, and there is no one who is either mindful or inattentive.
