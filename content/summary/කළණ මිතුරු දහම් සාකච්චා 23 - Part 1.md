## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- The **leaf/value simile** is attributed to the Buddha — a scrap of paper bearing figures, whose worth is grasped differently at each level of maturity (a kindergartener, a grade-5/6 child, an O-Level student, an A-Level graduate) — but no sutta title is given.
- Terms used: *abhisaṅkhāra* (with *abhisaṅkhāretaṁ*), *ahambu* (coincidence), *pratyaya* (condition), *kaṇā* (the "blind"). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- Part 1 of the twenty-third "good-friend" dialogue; a short two-speaker conversation continuing the "account-number" and blind-pot-game analysis of coincidence and the impossibility of "I can do."
- **"Don't let it go wrong" is identical to "let it come right."** There are not two things here — right and wrong; "let nothing go wrong" is simply "let all come right," so there is no genuine two-fold story. Seen rightly, one can neither wish "may it be so" nor "may it go wrong," can neither deliberately err nor deliberately succeed; and there is then nothing to cling to or collide with — no clinging even by force, no rejoicing at success, no grief at failure, "nothing to do in this work at all."
- **The blindfold pot-game (*kaṇāmuṭṭi*).** Winner and loser stand in the very same place: the one who struck the pot struck with eyes closed, and the one who missed also had eyes closed — both are "blind." So who won and who lost? We assign winning and losing to the blind. No special skill made the hit; it hit by chance and missed by chance, so the striker has nothing to be glad of and nothing to grieve — coincidence at every moment. Seen thus, one cannot even lay the word "clinging" or "colliding" on it; and life itself is revealed as a game, the day's doing as play.
- **Yet without the key seeing, "I can do" cannot end.** Until one sees that two consciousnesses can never be compared, the feeling "I can do" cannot end and life never gets past this knot. The teacher notes the point of his own repeated instruction — "watch the condition; everything makes *this* the condition for *that*" — is precisely to discover that the prior never actually *became* a condition: "did it become a condition for me? — it did not."
- **Fabrication (*abhisaṅkhāra*).** "*Abhisaṅkhāretaṁ*" is not idle — it is fabricated by thinking. Earlier, looking, one saw "two"; but if one could only *think* the seen "two," would one think "three"? No. So when "three" is written, it exposes *abhisaṅkhāra* — one thought it up. We believe "the three I wrote are what I saw," but the past is never obtained; "the past" is not a thing found, only now thought to have been found — so the nature itself is a *non-finding*.
- Because of our poor comprehension we suppose "I maintained something and can look back and write it down"; but there is no way to grasp, as knots, where the "right" ones even are.
- **These are gifts of nature.** Such points come from the nature of things (*svabhāva-dharma*), not previously met even by the speaker — a point never caught in all this time.
- **The leaf and its value.** A child picks up a scrap of paper marked with figures. To a kindergartener it means nothing (better given toys). A grade-five or -six child recognises "these are digits" — a little worth. An O-Level student sees "this is an A-Level problem I cannot yet solve" — recognising its value without being able to work it. An A-Level graduate finds it highly valuable, a fine problem from a good source, worth much if learned. The same paper thus carries a value exactly proportioned to each one's level of comprehension — and so, at the listener's present level, this deep teaching, even if he cannot yet "solve" it, is at least recognised as a great value.

## Summary

This first part of the twenty-third good-friend discussion is a brief, pointed continuation of the analysis of coincidence and the impossibility of a doer, worked mainly through the blindfold pot-game. The speakers begin by collapsing the apparent duality of right and wrong: "don't let it go wrong" is exactly the same wish as "let it come right," so there are not two things at all, and once this is seen one can neither wish an outcome nor its opposite, neither deliberately fail nor deliberately succeed, and there is nothing left to cling to or push against — no forced detachment, no delight in success, no sorrow in failure, "nothing to be done in this work." The pot-game makes the point vivid: the player who smashes the pot and the player who swings and misses both stand in the same place, both blindfolded, both "blind," so that to speak of a winner and a loser is only to award victory and defeat to the blind. The hit came by chance and the miss by chance, and the striker has therefore nothing to celebrate and nothing to mourn; seen this way one cannot even attach the words "clinging" or "colliding" to it, and the whole of daily life shows itself to be a game, a round of blind pot-breaking.

But the teacher is careful to note what must first be seen for this to land: until one sees that two consciousnesses can never be compared, the conviction "I can do" cannot end, and life never clears this knot. His own repeated instruction to "watch the condition" — to see how everything makes this the condition for that — is aimed precisely at the discovery that the prior never actually functioned as a condition at all: "did it become a condition for me? it did not." He ties this to fabrication: the term "constructed" is no idle word, for what is constructed is thought up. Looking, one saw "two"; but could one merely *think* the seen "two," one would never think "three" — so the writing of "three" exposes the construction, the thing thought up, even as we insist "the three I wrote are just what I saw." The past, however, is never obtained; "the past" is not a found thing but only something now supposed to have been found, so the nature itself is a non-finding, and our belief that we "maintained something and can look back and write it" has no ground, there being no way even to locate, as knots, where the "right" outcomes are. These realisations, he adds, are gifts of the nature of things, points he himself had never met in all this time. He closes with the Buddha's image of the leaf: a scrap of paper bearing figures is worthless to a small child, a set of mere digits to a middle-schooler, an unsolved higher problem to an O-Level student, and a treasure to an A-Level graduate — the same paper valued exactly according to each one's comprehension — so that this deep teaching, even where the listener cannot yet work it out, is at least recognised at his level as a thing of great worth.
