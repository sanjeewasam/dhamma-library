## Sutta referred to

- Directly quoted Pāli phrases, unattributed:

  - *cakkhuṁ paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu-viññāṇaṁ* ("dependent on eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises").
  - *navaṁ natthi sambhavaṁ* ("there is no new becoming").
- The **Jātaka tales** (the "550 births") are referred to, and a *sati / vimutti / paṭisaraṇa* formula ("mindfulness's refuge is release; release's refuge is nibbāna") is alluded to — neither with a sutta title. No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The twenty-fourth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation, one of the fullest, on ignorance as the "permanence-perception," how "mother and eye" and the self are fabricated by thinking, and what *samudaya* really means.
- **Seeing/hearing/sensing/thinking arise from attention (*manasikāra*) as condition; from that, the world and "I."** The root delusion (*mūla moha / mūla avijjā*), which the speakers equate with equanimity (*upekkhā*), is the un-known nature: since morning one does not know whether a breath was taken or expelled — both unknown — and, not knowing this, one takes it that "there was hearing, there was feeling," so that the seen-heard-sensed-cognized (*diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*) become *permanent* (*nitya*).
- **Ignorance is the permanence-perception (*nitya-saññā*).** The un-known nature is not itself "permanent"; rather, *not knowing* the not-knowing produces a false permanence — one must not take not-knowing as permanent, for it is the *base* on which the four become permanent. And to *see* that the four were made permanent on the basis of not-knowing is itself the impermanence-perception (*anicca-saññā*): not a separate impermanence set against permanence, but the very seeing that the permanence was a deception, that such a permanent thing never existed.
- **Ignorance is the root tangle** that never surfaces; those who merely chant "*avijjā*" without seeing it give it no weight. For one who sees the root there are no arising-and-ceasing stories, no birth and no death, because nothing occurs there — only not-knowing, with no tale of arising or ceasing — which is what "transcending birth and death" means.
- **How "mother and eye" and the self are built.** An eye "sees a form," "mother" appears, and both "I" and "mother" arise from attention — so *avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā* is realised exactly there: "mother" and "eye" alike are seen to arise from ignorance as condition. Not knowing that "seeing" itself was never obtained, seen things become permanent, and on a mother "seen" where seeing was never obtained the folly of "a mother and an eye that saw her" proceeds. The subtlety: "mother" and "eye" are not themselves the illusion; the *sense that a mother and an eye exist* is the illusion, mother-and-eye becoming real only through *thinking* (*hitena kama*). One is to see impermanence *within the thinking* — that they appear through thinking, not through existing — but must never slide to "mother is false, eye is false," which is fixed wrong view; at the level where thinking itself is not obtained, "mother, eye" do not even arise.
- **The standard account is only a view.** *Cakkhuṁ paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati* the Buddha taught elsewhere for the external (per the raft), but to take it as the real nature — "this is true" — is the root-lie. The whole explanation (light strikes the eye, eye-sensitivity, contact, feeling-and-perception, recognising the mother from old memory) is only a set of views, stories made in ignorance to make the world real; the Dhamma-statements too are views, to be recognised and released, not held — things to escape from, not to grasp.
- **Anicca, dukkha, anatta.** The things arising from the un-known are false, not permanent; we take the seen-heard-sensed-cognized as a self and as permanent. The very "sense that a mother and an eye exist" *is* suffering — "eye, mother, suffering" — for the "understanding" that "there is, it exists" is where dukkha is fabricated. Again the warning: do not go to "mother false, eye false," for until the true nature is reached "this is false" is only another arising.
- **What is really obtained?** The world presents mother, eye and the rest; but even the "sense that these are obtained" is not itself obtained — the *thinking* that they are obtained is un-obtained; and if "thinking" were experienced as thinking, "thinking" itself would vanish, an un-thinkable place.
- **"Becoming the state."** As a tree releases oxygen and absorbs carbon dioxide with no "action" found and no idea "I breathe," so where "seeing" is not even thought there is no mother and no eye; seeing is real only through the thought of seeing. If wisdom settles at that source (*yoni*), then when "the sense of a mother" arises, the "thinking" is not found there — it cannot even be thought.
- **Association only, no fabrication.** Like the lotus rooted in mud yet untouched by a single drop, wisdom *recognises* (a mother, and so on) while no "I recognised" is built inside; recognising happens, but neither a recognizer nor a recognised is fabricated, and one can say neither "recognised" nor "not." There is only association. As long as existence (*bhava*) is really obtained the problem is not solved; here existence is not really obtained — one can speak to the mother, care for her, do one's duties, standing on the earth and associating with all the world associates with, while inside the "fabrication" is a non-fabrication. Hence formations already formed wear away (*purāṇa*, old kamma spent), and *navaṁ natthi sambhavaṁ* — no new becoming is formed: that is the nature of wisdom.
- **Old kamma and result.** "Old kamma" is not an "off-state," for no off is ever obtained; and since no off is obtained, neither "I did before" nor "I didn't" is formed, so old kamma can bear no fruit. If a leg breaks now and one takes "it broke because I broke an animal's leg before," both the past and present breakings are found only as formations, so under *avijjā paccayā* one does not fit them into a kamma-result, and the doctrine of kamma-and-result is released. Is that doctrine then false? To wisdom, no kamma-result can be pinned — yet the Buddha taught the Jātakas within convention, after his awakening, to give people an idea; the events of the "constructed period" can be recounted, "worn away" meaning only that those formations have no seed-power to sprout again, so that one may recite all five-hundred-and-fifty births while none of them can yield a new result.
- **What *samudaya* really is.** It is *not* "eye-and-form gives consciousness" — that is obvious, needs no wisdom, is the world's own account and our very folly. Real *samudaya* is standing in the not-known place and, thinking "I saw," conceiving a "seeing" — obtaining a seeing where seeing cannot be obtained; that obtaining is ignorance. So *samudaya* is the not-obtainable being "obtained," and to see that it cannot be obtained is wisdom; the arising itself is suffering; arising happens through not-knowing, and seeing not-knowing rightly, no arising can occur and only cessation is realised. Thus seeing and hearing are *not* the arising (that is after-the-fact talk); the arising is the very source (*yoni*) where hearing and seeing never arose — one step lower.
- **The base cannot be called false.** The pre-arising nature of hearing and non-hearing is the not-knowing, the root delusion; seeing it, arising shows itself as non-arising, as never having arisen, and no tale of "a hearing obtained or not obtained" can be told. The base itself is un-met, and one cannot call it false, for where nothing is known neither "false" nor "true" applies — we build "true and false" only by grasping the obtained thing. We know nothing, not a particle of saṁsāra; true knowledge (*vidyā*) and wisdom (*paññā*) *are* the not-known place, and there wisdom finds nothing to get and nothing to remove.
- **Even mindfulness is released.** "Establish mindfulness" was said, and also "be freed even from mindfulness" — for one rightly established, mindfulness too is released: mindfulness's refuge is release, and release's refuge is nibbāna.

## Summary

This twenty-fourth good-friend discussion is among the fullest in the series, drawing the whole analysis together around ignorance as the "permanence-perception," the fabrication of "mother, eye and self" through thinking, and the true meaning of arising. The speakers begin from the point that seeing, hearing, sensing and thinking arise from attention as their condition, and that world and "I" are built from that. The root delusion — which they equate with equanimity — is simply the un-known nature: one does not know whether a breath was taken or let go since morning, both being unknown, and precisely because one does not know this, one assumes "there was hearing, there was feeling," so that the seen, heard, sensed and cognized harden into permanence. Ignorance, then, *is* the permanence-perception; the un-known nature is not itself permanent, but not knowing the not-knowing manufactures a false permanence, and one must not mistake not-knowing for something permanent, since it is only the base on which the four are made permanent. To see that the four were made permanent on the basis of not-knowing is itself the impermanence-perception — not a separate impermanence but the very recognition that the supposed permanence was a deception that never existed. Ignorance is thus the root tangle that never surfaces, lightly named by those who have not seen it; and for one who has, there are no stories of arising or ceasing, no birth and no death, because nothing occurs there at all.

From this the teacher shows how "mother and eye" and the self are constructed. An eye "sees a form," "mother" appears, and both "I" and "mother" arise from attention, so that "with ignorance as condition, formations" is realised exactly here — mother and eye alike springing from ignorance. Not knowing that seeing itself was never obtained, seen things become permanent, and upon a mother "seen" where seeing was never obtained the folly of "a mother and the eye that saw her" runs on. The crucial subtlety, repeated with care, is that mother and eye are not the illusion; the illusion is the *sense that a mother and an eye exist*, they becoming real only through thinking — so impermanence is to be seen *within the thinking*, in that they appear through thought rather than through existing, while one must never fall into "mother is false, eye is false," which is fixed wrong view, since at the level where thinking itself is not obtained neither mother nor eye even arises. The familiar formula "dependent on eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises," he notes, was taught for the external as a raft, and to take it as the real nature is the root-lie; the entire textbook account — light on the eye, sensitivity, contact, feeling and perception, recognition from memory — is only a set of views, stories fashioned in ignorance to make the world real, and even the Dhamma-statements are views to be recognised and released, not held.

He then reads this through impermanence, suffering and non-self: the things arising from the un-known are false and impermanent, yet we take the seen-heard-sensed-cognized as a self and as permanent, and the very sense that a mother and an eye exist *is* suffering, since the "understanding" that they exist is where dukkha is built — with, again, the warning not to slip into "mother false, eye false." Even the "obtainedness" of things, he adds, is not itself obtained: the thinking that they are obtained is un-obtained, and were thinking experienced as thinking it would vanish into an un-thinkable place. He offers the tree once more — releasing oxygen with no action found and no idea "I breathe" — to show that where seeing is not even thought, no mother and no eye arise, seeing being real only through the thought of it. Where wisdom settles at that source, the arising of "a sense of mother" cannot locate any "thinking" behind it. This is "association only, no fabrication": like the lotus rooted in mud yet untouched by it, wisdom recognises without building any "I recognised," so that neither recognizer nor recognised is fabricated, and while existence is not really obtained one may still speak to the mother, care for her and do one's duties, standing with both feet on the earth and associating with all the world associates with, while inside the "fabrication" is a non-fabrication — so that formations already made wear away and "there is no new becoming."

The discussion closes on kamma and on the meaning of arising. "Old kamma" is not an off-state, for no off is ever obtained, and since none is, neither "I did before" nor "I didn't" is formed, so old kamma can bear no fruit; a present broken leg, taken as the ripening of a past deed, is found only as two formations, so under "ignorance as condition" the whole doctrine of kamma-and-result is released — and though to wisdom no result can be pinned, the Buddha still recounted the Jātakas within convention to give people an idea, "worn away" meaning only that such formations have lost the seed-power to sprout, so that one might recite all five-hundred-and-fifty births with none able to yield a new result. Finally the teacher redefines *samudaya*: it is not "eye-and-form gives consciousness," which is obvious, needs no wisdom, and is our very folly, but the standing in the not-known place and, thinking "I saw," conceiving a seeing where seeing cannot be obtained — that obtaining being ignorance itself. So arising is the not-obtainable "obtained," to see which as unobtainable is wisdom; the arising is itself suffering, happening through not-knowing, and seen rightly no arising can occur and only cessation is realised. Seeing and hearing, therefore, are not the arising but after-the-fact talk; the arising is the very source where hearing and seeing never arose, one step lower — a base that cannot even be called false, since where nothing is known neither "false" nor "true" applies, and we build both only by grasping the obtained thing. We know nothing, not a particle of saṁsāra; true knowledge and wisdom simply *are* the not-known place, where there is nothing to get and nothing to remove — and there even mindfulness, once rightly established, is released, its refuge being release, whose own refuge is nibbāna.
