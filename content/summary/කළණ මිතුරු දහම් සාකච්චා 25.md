## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly quoted phrases, unattributed: *sabbe dhammā manasikāra-sambhavā* ("all dhammas originate from attention") and *sabbe dhammā phassa-sambhavā* ("all dhammas originate from contact").
- The episode of the Buddha bringing **Sāriputta** to arahantship by "continually stroking" a single point (teaching him one thing repeatedly) is referred to, with Sāriputta named as a person, no sutta title given.
- The terms *animitta* (signless) and *suññata* (empty) — "the great monk's" liberation-categories — and *ānāpānasati* are used. No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The twenty-fifth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation in which one speaker describes, in the first person, a matured/settled seeing, while the other probes it.
- **Even a lifted hand is never cognized.** Told to "establish mindfulness" and watch, one finds that "lifting a hand" is not actually cognized at all; the "action" is fixed as permanent, and it is the same in sleep as in waking — there being no difference between dreaming and being awake. At night a leg bends, an arm extends or folds, and we do not know it; that not-known level is now visible, and an "action" of the kind we imagine never actually operates, nor can it even be known.
- **No bodily process is ever obtained.** We suppose there is a process going on in the body, but such a process was never actually obtained by us — hard even to conceive. When one thinks "lift the hand," it appears *as attention itself* (*manasikāra*), not as "a hand and its form."
- Invoking *sabbe dhammā manasikāra-sambhavā / phassa-sambhavā*: "lifting a hand" is an attention arisen from contact — not a hand, not a body, but appearing as attention. When attention is seen *as* attention, no "hands and legs" story is found; and one must not think twice "this is attention," for in seeing attention as attention even the "it is attention" understanding drops away, insight maturing until even "attention" no longer applies. There is then no thinker and no thought — a *me-less attention*, with no person inside or outside, personhood found on neither side.
- **The samādhi here.** It is not the object-fixing concentration; it is a samādhi established in a nature where attention itself is severed — a place where one cannot think, cannot hold the mind by thinking. It is signless (*animitta*), "chosen from the signless"; and *animitta / suññatā* admit no analysis, since to describe the signless one would have to bring back the very notion of "sign."
- **What can and cannot be done.** One cannot do breath-meditation (*ānāpānasati*), for no "breath" becomes manifest, and cannot do deliberate calm-practice; but loving-kindness (*mettā*) — "may all beings be well" — is possible. Does the mind get disturbed or angry? No: a felt object may still strike, but not forcibly and not for hours as before, because it is not "made real" — the wisdom-nature deflects it, and the object is not taken up into existence.
- **A remaining trace, and how it is seen.** Even "not liking" may arise — but "not liking" is itself a liking, a leaning of the mind; liking and disliking are both one's own wanting, "internal" (*ajjhatta*) — though "internal" too is wrong, since it implies an external. One neither clings nor collides; delusion (*moha*) is recognised and equanimity seen; with ignorance gone there is no fabrication, and one watches it as a tree watches its leaves fall, with no bias toward whatever drops — a hand lifted or lowered showing the wisdom no change, no "now it is lifted."
- **Method: look at the one same place.** No new examples are needed. The Buddha brought Sāriputta to arahantship by a single continuous "stroking," teaching one thing repeatedly; so one should keep looking at the very same point, since changing examples scatters the mind — even hearing a discourse that switches images makes the mind wander. Where thinking must be cut, more looking at the one thing lets wisdom arise and attention be severed; so one "polishes" the same point seen the day before. There is no "method" to hand over — nothing to do, nothing to refrain from — one can only keep examining the same point, as the Buddha did when people asked the same thing four times over; and to give a "do this / don't do this" instruction toward the effortless would be entirely wrong.

## Summary

This twenty-fifth good-friend discussion is distinctive in that one speaker describes, from within, a matured and settled seeing while the other draws it out with questions. It opens on the lifting of a hand: even when one is told to establish mindfulness and watch, "lifting a hand" is never actually cognized, the supposed "action" standing fixed and permanent, and — strikingly — the same in sleep as in waking, since there is no difference between dreaming and being awake. At night a leg bends and an arm folds without our knowing, and that not-known level, the speaker says, is now plainly visible; an "action" of the kind we imagine never operates and cannot even be known. We suppose a process runs on in the body, yet such a process was never actually obtained and is scarcely conceivable; and when one thinks "lift the hand," what appears is attention itself, not a hand with its form. Bringing in "all dhammas originate from attention, from contact," he explains that "lifting a hand" is an attention arisen from contact — not a hand, not a body — appearing as attention; and when attention is seen as attention, no story of hands and legs is found. One must not even think twice "this is attention," for in that seeing the very understanding "it is attention" drops away, and insight ripens until "attention" too no longer applies — leaving no thinker and no thought, a me-less attention with no person inside or outside, personhood found on neither side.

The other speaker probes the concentration and conduct of such a state. It is not, the first replies, the object-fixing concentration but a samādhi established where attention itself is severed, a place where one cannot think or hold the mind by thinking — signless, chosen from the signless, admitting no analysis, since to describe the signless would require the very notion of a sign. He cannot do breath-meditation, for no breath becomes manifest, nor deliberate calm-practice; but loving-kindness remains open to him. Asked whether the mind is disturbed or angered, he says no: a felt object may still strike, but not forcibly and no longer for hours as before, because it is never made real — the wisdom-nature turns it aside and it is not drawn up into existence. Yet a trace remains: even "not liking" can arise, though "not liking" is itself a liking, a leaning of the mind, and both liking and disliking are one's own wanting, "internal" — while even "internal" is wrong, implying an external. He neither clings nor collides; delusion is recognised, equanimity seen, and with ignorance gone nothing is fabricated, so that he watches it all as a tree watches its leaves fall, indifferent to whatever drops, a hand raised or lowered registering in the wisdom no change at all.

Finally the two turn to method, and the first speaker insists there is none to teach. No new examples are wanted: the Buddha brought Sāriputta to arahantship by a single continuous stroking, teaching one thing over and over, and so one must keep looking at the very same point, for changing the examples only scatters the mind — even a discourse that switches images sets the mind wandering. Where thinking must be cut, it is more looking at the one thing that lets wisdom arise and attention be severed, so one simply polishes the same point seen the day before. There is nothing to do and nothing to refrain from, no technique to be received, only the continued examining of the same point — exactly as the Buddha allowed people to ask the same question four times over — and to offer a "do this, don't do that" instruction pointed at the effortless would be wholly mistaken.
