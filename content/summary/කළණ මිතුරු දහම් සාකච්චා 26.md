## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- The **deva-son's question "how did you cross the flood?"** and the Buddha's answer *appatiṭṭhaṁ anāyūhaṁ* ("not halting, not straining, I crossed") are referred to, but no sutta title is given.
- Directly quoted phrase, unattributed: *māyā-upamañca viññāṇaṁ* ("consciousness is like an illusion").
- The phrase *imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti* ("when this is, that is") is used. Terms: *nirāyāsa*, *phassa / sparśa*, *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, *aja-amara* (unborn-undying), *jāti-jarā-maraṇa*, *sotāpanna / sakadāgāmī*, *kalyāṇa-mitta sevana*, *saddhamma-savana*. No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The twenty-sixth "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation on birth (*ipadīma*), the matchbox-and-fire analysis, and the transcending of birth, aging and death.
- **"I didn't feel it before, now I do."** Asked whether one feels the hospital (an object) now, one recalls the prior moment as "I didn't feel it before" — yet before, no notion of "not-feeling" ever arose. So the "not-feeling" now spoken of never existed as an idea. Any "mindfulness" placed there is thought *by consciousness*: only once a world, a dependent-arising and a consciousness have formed does one think "mindfulness," and only then does the split of mindful / inattentive appear ("I was inattentive, now I am mindful").
- **The place beyond both cannot be reached by fixing the mind.** Where both mindfulness and inattention are absent cannot be discussed through consciousness; that state (wisdom) transcends both. One cannot even "place the mind" there, for the instant one tries to fix the mind, a "thing" appears and consciousness intervenes — fixing the mind is itself a consciousness-level.
- **Hence effortless (*nirāyāsa*).** Not by establishing, not by striving: the deva-son asks the Buddha how he crossed the flood, and the answer is "not halting, not straining, I crossed." So no "do this / don't do this," and no "think this / don't think that," can be given — both apply nowhere.
- **Not "prior merit" either.** Asked whether it arises by past merit or pāramī, the teacher answers that the Buddha describes it as a *Path to be cultivated*, not "luck by chance," taught over seven days, seven months and so on (with the "soil on a fingernail" simile for how few realise it); and one cannot even call it "prior merit," since that would leave one waiting for merit to ripen — it is rather a crossing beyond consciousness, a seeing-through of consciousness, a transcending of the duality, which appears only with the arising of wisdom.
- **Why the Dhamma must still be heard.** To the student's confusion — "you say hearing the Dhamma is useless" — the teacher corrects: not useless. We hold a "grasped Dhamma" (a dependent-arising, a set of aggregates, a duality — the two extremes of existence and non-existence); taking *that* as real and investigating with it, we conclude "the world is a delusion, it is false." But this is understood only *relative to* the held Dhamma, so "the world is false" is a comparative understanding — and then the two extremes re-form: "it is *true for me* that the world is a delusion," so that the delusion-fact becomes real and self-view rebuilds, a "someone who realised the world is false" standing there, with the grasped "truth" as one extreme and the "falsehood" as the other. This is exactly holding "the Dhamma is thus, the Buddha said so, therefore it is true."
- Seen by wisdom (beyond "I"), no "I" is found who understood, and seeing by wisdom is not a doable act — hence "not striving, not halting." A stream-enterer can recognise a stream-enterer, a once-returner a once-returner; the stages can be discerned — but *not through talk*, for wisdom is not seen in talk; talk only displays the rapid fabricating-nature (the "junk"). Between two who have seen, the "junk" does not come out — the "junk" being talk like "this is false," "it arises and ceases," "it is dependently arisen," "it arises from prior non-existence and ceases without remainder," "it never arose," "it is a delusion" — a whole set of phrases which one who has seen recognises (as views to be released) and which no longer issue from him as held truths.
- **The matchbox-and-fire (central).** Before fire arises, is there matchbox, matchstick, fire? No fire beforehand; and box-and-stick merely present make no fire — they must be struck, they must *contact*. So the nearest cause of fire is contact (*sparśa*, the "rubbing"). But "rubbing" is not "for the fire" (fire is not yet there); the box is not fire, the stick is not fire, the rubbing is not fire — where, then, can fire's *arising* be shown, in box, stick or rubbing? In none: fire is found in none of the three. A thing found in none of the three — how does it "arise," and where could one point and say "it arose here"? Nowhere; no place or thing can be named. So "arising" cannot be applied where no arising can be shown, and "it never arose" is perfectly true. Yet if fire never arose, how is even *that* "right"? — because there is no way to know "arising" (birth) at all, and if "birth" is never obtained, then "it did not arise" (the absence of a non-existent) is likewise a mad statement; seeing this, "arising" simply cannot be shown.
- **Consciousness the illusion.** Hence *māyā-upamañca viññāṇaṁ*: fire is the illusion, and one builds the argument only by making the illusion real — "but it *did* arise!" — which is making the magic real. That fire becomes manifest in the world is exactly what "illusion" named: a thing that can arise in none of the three yet becomes manifest — and that manifesting of the never-arisen *is* suffering.
- **Birth befalls no one.** Where birth is not found, can a "born one" be found? No. Can "one to whom birth happens" be found? No. So who is born, who dies? No tale applies. The non-finding of birth is realised; "fire and I" (duality) forms only from not-knowing, and where birth is not found for the fire, the "I who obtains fire" is not born either. Seeing this — no one born, no one dies — is *aja-amara*, the unborn-undying, the transcending of birth, aging and death; and the self-view on this side is likewise not found.
- **Cessation.** The "person" arises only through not knowing the nature under discussion — and that is *nirodha*. Not knowing birth, a "person" arose, "this is a someone," a thing/birth taken up, a person on this side; but where birth cannot even be posited, the one "born" in ignorance of birth, seeing that birth cannot be posited, finds the delusion vanish — which is nibbāna. Does the un-born one die? No — that is the unborn-undying, transcending birth-aging-death; no birth being found, the this-side person is not found either, birth-in-ignorance and the born one arising together only for the one who does not know the contact-nature.

## Summary

This twenty-sixth good-friend discussion turns on birth and its impossibility, worked mainly through the matchbox-and-fire analysis, and closing on the transcending of birth, aging and death. It begins with a small experiment: asked whether one feels the hospital now, one recalls the previous moment as "I didn't feel it before," yet before that moment no notion of "not-feeling" had ever arisen — so the "not-feeling" now spoken of never existed as an idea at all. Any "mindfulness" placed there, the teacher notes, is a product of consciousness: only after a world, a dependent-arising and a consciousness have formed does one think "mindfulness," and only then does the whole split of mindful and inattentive appear. The place where both are absent cannot be discussed through consciousness, for that state is wisdom, transcending both; and one cannot even "place the mind" there, since the instant one tries to fix it a "thing" appears and consciousness intervenes — the very fixing of the mind being a consciousness-level. This is why the way is effortless: not by establishing and not by striving, as the deva-son learns when the Buddha says he crossed the flood "not halting, not straining." No instruction to do or not do, to think or not think, can therefore be given, since all such apply nowhere; and it cannot even be called a matter of prior merit, which would leave one waiting for merit to ripen, for it is a crossing beyond consciousness that appears only with the arising of wisdom.

The teacher then answers the student's worry that he has been calling the hearing of Dhamma useless. Not useless, he says: what happens is that we hold a "grasped Dhamma" — a dependent-arising, a bundle of aggregates, a duality of the existence and non-existence extremes — and, taking it as real and investigating with it, conclude "the world is a delusion." But this is understood only relative to the held Dhamma, a comparative verdict, so that the two extremes at once re-form: it becomes "true for me that the world is false," self-view rebuilds around a "someone who realised the world is a delusion," and the grasped truth and the world's falsehood stand as the two ends — precisely the posture of "the Dhamma is thus, the Buddha said so, therefore it is true." Seen by wisdom, beyond "I," no one is found who understood, and the seeing is no doable act, which is again why it is "not striving, not halting." He grants that a stream-enterer can recognise a stream-enterer and the stages be discerned — but never through talk, for wisdom is not visible in speech; talk only betrays the rapid fabricating-nature, the "junk," and between two who have truly seen, that junk does not come out. The junk, he specifies, is exactly the stock of phrases — "this is false," "it arises and ceases," "it is dependently arisen," "it arises from prior non-existence and ceases without remainder," "it never arose," "it is a delusion" — which one who has seen recognises as views to be released, and which no longer issue from him as held truths.

The heart of the talk is the matchbox and fire. Before fire arises there is no fire, and box and stick merely lying there make none; they must be struck, must contact — so contact is the nearest cause. Yet the "rubbing" is not itself the fire, and neither box nor stick is fire, so where can fire's arising be shown? In none of the three, for fire is found in none of them; and a thing found in none of the three cannot be pointed to as "arising here." "Arising" thus cannot be applied where no arising can be shown, and "it never arose" is quite true — though even that is only "right" because there is no way to know "arising" at all, so that "it did not arise," being the absence of a non-existent, is itself a mad statement. This, the teacher says, is why consciousness is called an illusion: fire is the illusion, and one sustains the argument only by making the illusion real with "but it did arise" — the manifesting in the world of what can arise in none of the three being exactly what "illusion" named, and that manifesting of the never-arisen being suffering itself. From here he draws the conclusion that birth befalls no one: where birth is not found, neither a "born one" nor "one to whom birth happens" can be found, so no tale of who is born or who dies applies; "fire and I" is a duality formed only from not-knowing, and where birth is not found for the fire, the "I who obtains fire" is not born either. To see this — no one born, no one dying — is the unborn-undying, the transcending of birth, aging and death; and in the closing segment he restates it as cessation: the "person" arises only through not knowing this very nature, so that where birth cannot even be posited, the one "born" in ignorance of birth, on seeing that birth cannot be posited, watches the delusion vanish — which is nibbāna — while the un-born one does not die, no birth being found and therefore no this-side person, birth-in-ignorance and the born one arising together only for the one who does not know the contact-nature.
