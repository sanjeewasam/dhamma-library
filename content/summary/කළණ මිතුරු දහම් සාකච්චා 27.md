## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- No sutta title is given and no continuous Pāli passage is quoted. The **magic-show** and the **President Premadasa / "trusted servant who was really the assassin"** episode (a real Sri Lankan event, used as an analogy) carry the discussion.
- Terms used: *anutpāda* (non-arising), *ārya-śūnyatā* (noble emptiness), *nitya-saññā* (permanence-perception), *anicca / dukkha / anatta*, *muni*, *rāga / dosa*, *sabhāva-dharma*, and the phrase "not standing, not straining." No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The twenty-seventh "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation exposing "the world is false" as itself an error, and unfolding non-arising (*anutpāda*) through the magic-show and the assassin-servant.
- **The magic-show.** A dove is pulled from a hat; we believe it only while we do not know how it is done — it is "magic" precisely because we cannot do it. Learn the trick and one knows "no such event actually happened." Yet we still assume that "an event happened where none did," and *that* supposed happening is what we take as real, which is the only reason the magic (and this moment, this world) seems to have value. Once the system is learned, was there any problem with the dove or the handkerchief? None — it was only a deception on *our* side, born of not-knowing; the deception arose not from the handkerchief or the dove but from our wrong understanding.
- **So "the world is false" is itself the error.** If our wrong understanding is corrected, what is left to call "false"? Nothing "true" was ever there to be called false; where nothing happened, there is nothing to call false either. To declare "the whole world is a delusion" is to take a non-existent thing as existing and then call it false — like making a non-existent demon and then trying to drive it away with incense. Applied to "I": "I do not exist; I am impermanent, suffering, not-self" — but such an "I" was never obtained, so this is an outright lie, a manufacturing of a non-existent "I" only to declare it impermanent and not-self, the delusion simply re-knotting.
- **Neither established nor striven against.** So it is neither "real" (to be established) nor "false" (to be struggled with): "not standing, not straining," a state where nothing occurred. One cannot even say "empty," for asking "empty of what?" rebuilds a non-existent thing (empty of beds, chairs, tables); seeing true noble emptiness, "empty" is just one more existence-assertion. Those caught here say "in the present moment I see impermanence, suffering, not-self" — but for one with wisdom no such things are found in the present moment, there being no connection to handkerchief or bird; *what*, after all, is impermanent, suffering, not-self? Nothing — a negative base, from which we build a positive thing and then "see impermanence" in the fabricated thing, watching our own manufactured demon as anicca-dukkha-anatta. This is where everyone is stuck; the suttas were given to *recognise* the demon, but we use them to *build* a new one, taking the texts as real and becoming bound to them until they too become a demon.
- **Non-arising through the assassin-servant.** President Premadasa was killed by a suicide bomber who had lived as his devoted, trusted servant, never suspected even subconsciously to be the assassin. Suppose the "servant" later learns from someone that this person is an assassin who came to kill him — like hearing the Buddha's Dhamma. Now not an inch of the assassin's limbs, face or shape changes; but the *view* changes utterly: seen before as "my obedient servant," now seen as "an assassin," and now, however many insist "he is a servant," no acceptance of "servant" arises — only suspicion and fear — and this does not revert. The key: was there ever really a "servant"? No — from the very start it was an assassin. So "a servant existed and then ceased" is wrong; the servant *never arose* (*anutpāda*); one cannot even apply the *word* "servant," for no such character ever existed. And one who has seen this non-arising would not even say "there is no servant," since the "no servant" would appear as another eternalism (a non-existent imagined into existence, then negated); grasping the annihilation-extreme rightly, one never associates with the eternalist extreme.
- **Applied to one's world.** Wife, son, television, chair, house, one's own name — all appear exactly as before, unchanged an inch; but the *sense that they exist* (the *nitya-saññā*) drops away, recognised as arising from not-knowing. As with Premadasa's corrected understanding, the persons look the same while the "existing" feeling is released. We always watch "an existing thing changing," never "an existing thing ceasing"; what is corrected is only the false sense of existence grasped through not-knowing. The nature "wife, son" was never obtainable; from a non-obtainable place I fabricated a wife and son, now real to me, seen walking and talking — yet never actually obtained. Nor can "there is no wife" be said, nor "no one is there"; neither existence nor non-existence applies — not-self is *dumb/silent* (*anatta goḷu*), and seeing it rightly one falls silent, becoming a *muni*. No "process" applies; even "eating, drinking" do not apply, seen rightly.
- **The practical objection, and the tree.** Can one live without these actions — how then does anything get done? The tree does not know it breathes, takes water or food, yet does it die? Its process runs though it knows nothing; likewise the system works, but to wisdom "there is a process, an action" does not even apply. And greed and aversion arise precisely through the *sense that an action is done to or for "me"*: one who waters the tree and one who cuts its fruit are alike to the tree, which receives no "action" (no "he watered me, he did not"), and so has no attachment to the giver and no aversion to the cutter. For us, watering breeds attachment to the giver and withholding breeds aversion, so the whole "my process, my doing" — the clinging and the resentment "he did this to me" — springs from grasping "a process is mine." The tree, unshaken, is the very nature of wisdom: conforming to the nature-dhamma at arahantship, where that nature has neither destruction nor origination, just as water is not destroyed by water nor wind by wind.

## Summary

This twenty-seventh good-friend discussion sets out to expose "the world is false" as itself a mistake, and to unfold non-arising through two vivid images. It begins with the magic-show: a dove is drawn from a hat, and we believe it only so long as we do not know how the trick is done — it is "magic" precisely because we cannot do it — but once the method is learned, one knows that no such event ever happened. And yet, the teacher points out, we go on assuming that an event *did* happen where none did, and it is exactly that supposed happening which we take for real, the only thing that lends the trick, and this world, its apparent value. Once the system is known, there was never any problem with the dove or the handkerchief; the whole thing was a deception on our own side, arising not from the objects but from our wrong understanding. From this he draws the sharp conclusion that "the world is false" is itself the error: if the wrong understanding is corrected, nothing remains to be called false, for nothing true was ever there, and where nothing happened there is nothing false either. To declare "the whole world is a delusion" is thus to take a non-existent thing as existent and then call it false — to make a non-existent demon and then drive it off with incense — and the same holds for the "I," since "I am impermanent, suffering, not-self" fashions a non-existent "I" only to negate it, the delusion simply re-knotting.

So the matter is neither to be established as real nor struggled against as false — "not standing, not straining," a state where nothing occurred. One cannot even say "empty," the teacher warns, for "empty of what?" only rebuilds a non-existent thing, and true noble emptiness shows "empty" to be one more existence-claim. Those who miss this say "in the present moment I see impermanence, suffering and not-self," but for one with wisdom nothing of the kind is found in the present moment, there being no link to handkerchief or bird; and what, after all, would be impermanent — nothing, a negative base, upon which we erect a positive thing and then contemplate our own manufactured demon as anicca, dukkha and anatta. This, he says, is exactly where everyone is caught: the suttas were given to help us *recognise* the demon, but we press them into building a new one, taking the texts as real until we are bound to them and they too become a demon.

The heart of the talk is the assassin-servant. President Premadasa was killed by a suicide bomber who had lived as his devoted, trusted servant, never once suspected to be his killer. Suppose that "servant" were to learn from another that the man is in fact an assassin come to kill him — which is like hearing the Buddha's Dhamma. Not an inch of the assassin's body changes, but the view is transformed: what was seen as "my obedient servant" is now seen as "an assassin," and no assurance from anyone can restore the belief "servant," only suspicion remaining, never reverting. And the decisive point is that there never was a "servant" at all — from the very beginning it was an assassin — so "a servant existed and then ceased" is wrong; the servant *never arose*, and one cannot even apply the word to him. Moreover, one who has seen this non-arising would not even say "there is no servant," since that negation would itself appear as an eternalism, a non-existent imagined into being only to be denied — so that grasping the annihilation-extreme rightly, one never touches the eternalist extreme. Applied to one's own world, wife and son, television and house and one's own name all appear exactly as before, unchanged, while only the *sense that they exist* falls away, recognised as born of not-knowing; we always watch an existing thing *changing*, never *ceasing*, and what is corrected is only that false sense of existence. The nature "wife, son" was never obtainable, yet from a non-obtainable place I fabricated them, and now they are real to me, walking and talking — though never actually obtained; and one can say neither "there is no wife" nor "no one is there," since neither existence nor non-existence applies, not-self being dumb, so that seeing it rightly one falls silent and becomes a *muni*, with no "process" applying and even "eating and drinking" not applying. To the practical objection that one cannot live without such actions, the teacher offers the tree, which does not know it breathes or draws water yet does not therefore die: its process runs though it knows nothing, and to wisdom "there is an action" does not even apply. Greed and aversion, he ends, arise only through the sense that an action is done to or for "me" — one who waters the tree and one who cuts its fruit being alike to the tree, which receives no "action" and so feels neither attachment to the giver nor aversion to the cutter — whereas for us watering breeds attachment and withholding breeds resentment, all the "my doing, he did this to me" springing from grasping "a process is mine." The tree, unshaken, is the very nature of wisdom, conforming at arahantship to a nature that has neither destruction nor origination, as water is not destroyed by water nor wind by wind.
