## Sutta referred to
- **Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta** (ධම්මචක්කප්පවත්තන සූත්‍රය) — named: cited for the two extremes *kāmasukhallikānuyoga* (pursuit of sense-pleasure) and *attakilamathānuyoga* (self-mortification), and for the middle.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed:
  - *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā … yadā paññāya passati* ("all formations are impermanent … when one sees with wisdom").
  - the **Bāhiya** teaching — "in the seen only the seen … you are not *there* (*na tena*), not *here*, not *in between* … this is the end of suffering" (Bāhiya named as a person).
  - *suññāgāra-gato vā araññagato vā* ("gone to an empty hut or to the forest").
  - *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* ("the meeting of the three is contact"); and the four unanswered (*avyākata*) alternatives about existence after death.
- **Aṅgulimāla** and **Rāhula** are named as persons. No further sutta title should be inferred.
- *(This is a three-speaker session: the teacher and two lay questioners.)*

## Main dhamma points
- The twenty-eighth "good-friend" dialogue; a three-speaker conversation gathering the recurring themes — relativity built on ignorance, the un-knowable equanimity, non-arising, the abolition of kamma-result, and the Bāhiya teaching.
- **"I feel the breath now" fabricates a prior "not-feeling."** Before, no notion "I am not feeling / I don't see this" ever arose; "non-existence" (absence) is never actually experienced, and not knowing that it cannot be experienced or thought, we take the present as "existent, arisen." From one un-known side we build the other side.
- **Relativity rests on not-knowing.** We do not know sleep; "I am awake" is built on "not asleep," which is built on the un-known sleep — so relativity (*sāpekṣatā*) itself is built on ignorance, the "awake" being relative to an un-known "sleep," which makes the relative un-arisen, mind-made, a mirage.
- **The un-knowable equanimity.** We are stuck in the "everything exists" extreme — a chair or a tree "exists" the moment it is thought of, though we never had a notion it persisted. The mind's middle (*upekkhā*) cannot be grasped and is un-understandable; the instant one says "equanimity exists" it can be upset. We *are* in equanimity yet do not know it, cannot even say "we are," and the very sense of "being" arises only because equanimity (not-knowing) is not known. It lies outside consciousness's range, since consciousness is "knowing" and the not-known cannot be known.
- Hence *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā … yadā paññāya passati*: only wisdom sees impermanence, for nothing can be gotten in the unknowable — neither "knowing" nor "not-knowing" is obtainable, and true ignorance seen shows even "not-knowing" to be un-obtainable, the very word "not-knowing" empty of itself.
- **Nothing arises or ceases anew.** Seeing arising (*samudaya*), its abandoning *is* the realisation of cessation; abandoning arising is transcending both extremes, a *non-standing*.
- **Refining the object endlessly does not reach non-arising.** Even under a microscope, ignorance too gets covered and thinned rather than seen; as one nears "nothing," one takes "there is nothing" as the residue and falls into annihilationism — "nothing" being one more obtaining. The Buddha's ignorance is a wisdom-level, never reachable by knowledge; science is a path led by knowing, itself the fruit of folly.
- **The two extremes and the mis-read middle.** From the *Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta*, the two extremes are to be transcended; but "the middle" is wrongly read as "a place free of both pleasure and pain." Rightly, even "a place free of pleasure and pain" is a wrong statement, for where there is no notion of breath or feeling no pleasure or pain applies, so to ask "is this feeling pleasant or painful?" is a wrong question — which is why the Buddha rejects such questions (*avyākata*): "does it exist after death? / not? / both? / neither?" are all wrong, and in explaining it both the explanation and the question are wrong, wisdom alone seeing both sides wrong.
- **The arahant beyond the sense-range.** We posit an arahant through the senses — the seen, heard, sensed, cognized, which are the four conceivings and the source of self-view — but the arahant-nature transcends all four and so cannot be grasped at the sense-level; even the Buddha "does not find an arahant" (Rāhula's attainment is recalled: the Buddha felt no special importance, since no such person was posited). Any question about it smuggles in a "being," so the question is framed by one still holding a this-side stance.
- **The Bāhiya teaching.** "In the seen only the seen … you are not *there*, not *here*, not *in between* — this, Bāhiya, is the end of suffering." When seeing arises, the seen thing stands "there" (the object side) and "I" stand "here" (this side), the middle (consciousness) connecting them and the person being born. In *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, eye is one end, form the other, consciousness the middle; where the three do not stand, no world and no "I" stands — so the Bāhiya "not there, not here, not between" is precisely the non-standing of the meeting-of-the-three, beyond a person's range.
- **Solitude.** The Buddha begins with "gone to an empty hut or to the forest" — this is done alone, being hard amid daily bustle; so one who has seen it should go to solitude to develop it.
- **Delusion never befalls a person.** The "person" is built *on* delusion, so delusion is not something a person "has"; when delusion is seen by wisdom the person ends, both going together. One's own birth arose upon delusion, so birth never actually befalls one, and when delusion dawns, both birth and death end. Who is born, who dies? "Not the one who dies is reborn, nor another" — with no room to insert anything, and birth cannot be pinned to birth; the concept "one who was, died, and is reborn" is built on root ignorance.
- **Kamma-result abolished.** If neither the same one nor another is reborn, "who carries the kamma?" — kamma forms only with the person-interpretation, the view "I existed, I did it, I receive the result." In the supramundane vision kamma-result is abolished, as in Aṅgulimāla's case: the killing of people was not cancelled or erased, but within the vision personhood was transcended and birth stopped, so to whom is any result given? Having transcended the very act of "killing," he has no past killing to recall and no future result — turning saṁsāra in reverse. Kamma is thus abolished not by erasing the deed but by the dropping of the "I did it" view, there being no centre left to receive a result.
- The session closes with the note that such things "get said" only through the good friend and the listener together, that a single word rightly caught unravels everything, and that clarity must come at one point.

## Summary

This twenty-eighth good-friend discussion, conducted among a teacher and two lay questioners, gathers the recurring themes of the series into one sweep. It opens with the familiar observation that "I feel the breath now" fabricates a prior "not-feeling" that was never actually experienced — for before, no notion "I am not feeling this" ever arose, and "non-existence" is never experienced at all; not knowing that absence cannot even be thought, we take the present as arisen, building one side from an un-known other side. The sleep example sharpens it: we do not know sleep, so "I am awake" rests on "not asleep," which rests on the un-known sleep, making relativity itself a construction upon ignorance, the "awake" merely relative to an un-known "sleep," and therefore un-arisen and mind-made, a mirage. From this the teacher turns to the un-knowable equanimity: we are stuck in the "everything exists" extreme, where a chair or a tree "exists" the moment it is thought of, and we cannot grasp the mind's middle, which is un-understandable and upset the instant one calls it "existent." We are in equanimity without knowing it, unable even to say "we are," the very sense of being arising only because that equanimity — the not-knowing — is not known; and this lies outside consciousness's range, since consciousness is knowing and the not-known cannot be known. Hence only wisdom sees impermanence, for nothing can be gotten in the unknowable, neither "knowing" nor "not-knowing" being obtainable, and even the word "not-knowing" is empty of itself.

Seeing this, nothing arises or ceases anew; the abandoning of arising is itself the realisation of cessation, a transcending of both extremes, a non-standing. The teacher warns that refining the object endlessly, even under a microscope, never reaches non-arising — ignorance only gets thinned and covered — and that as one nears "nothing," taking "there is nothing" as the residue is to fall into annihilationism, "nothing" being one more thing obtained; the Buddha's ignorance is a wisdom-level unreachable by knowledge, while science is a path led by knowing, itself the fruit of folly. Turning to the *Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta*, he notes that the two extremes of sense-indulgence and self-mortification are to be transcended, but that "the middle" is wrongly read as a place free of both pleasure and pain; rightly, even "a place free of pleasure and pain" is a wrong statement, for where there is no notion of breath or feeling neither pleasure nor pain applies, and to ask whether a feeling is pleasant or painful is a wrong question — exactly the kind the Buddha refuses, as with the four unanswered alternatives about existence after death, where both the explanation and the question are wrong and wisdom alone sees both sides wrong.

The discussion then places the arahant beyond the sense-range: we posit an arahant only through the seen, heard, sensed and cognized — the four conceivings that are the source of self-view — while the arahant-nature transcends all four and cannot be grasped at the sense-level, so that even the Buddha "does not find an arahant," as recalled in Rāhula's attainment, where he felt no special importance since no such person was posited; any question about it smuggles in a "being" and betrays a this-side stance. This is illumined by the Bāhiya teaching — "in the seen only the seen; you are not there, not here, not in between; this is the end of suffering" — which the teacher maps onto contact: when seeing arises, the seen thing stands "there" and "I" stand "here," the middle joining them and the person being born, exactly the meeting-of-the-three, where eye is one end, form the other, and consciousness the middle, so that the Bāhiya "not there, not here, not between" is the non-standing of that very meeting, beyond a person's range. He adds that the Buddha begins such practice with "gone to an empty hut or to the forest," since it is done alone and is hard amid daily bustle, so one who has seen it should withdraw to solitude to mature it.

Finally the conversation reaches delusion, birth and kamma. Delusion never befalls a person, the teacher insists, because the "person" is built *upon* delusion and is not its owner; when delusion is seen by wisdom the person ends, the two going together, so that one's own birth, having arisen upon delusion, never actually befalls one, and when delusion dawns both birth and death end. Who then is born, who dies? — "not the one who dies is reborn, nor another," with no room to insert anything and birth un-pinnable to birth, the whole concept of "one who was, died and is reborn" resting on root ignorance. And from this the abolition of kamma-result follows: if neither the same one nor another is reborn, the question "who carries the kamma?" collapses, for kamma forms only with the person-interpretation, the view "I existed, I did it, I receive the result." In the supramundane vision this is abolished, as with Aṅgulimāla — the killings were not cancelled or erased, but personhood was transcended and birth stopped, so that there is no one to whom a result is given; having transcended the very act of killing, he has neither a past deed to recall nor a future fruit, turning saṁsāra in reverse. Kamma is thus abolished not by erasing the deed but by the dropping of the "I did it" view, no centre remaining to receive a result. The session ends on the note that such things get said only through the good friend and the listener together, that a single word rightly caught unravels the whole, and that the clarity must come at one point.
