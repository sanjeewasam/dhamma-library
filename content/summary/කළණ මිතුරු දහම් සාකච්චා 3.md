## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā … yadā paññāya passati* ("all formations are impermanent … when one sees with wisdom"); *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* ("the meeting of the three is contact"); and *pubbe ananussutesu (dhammesu)* ("a dhamma not heard before").
- Terms used: *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen / heard / sensed / cognized), *phoṭṭhabba* (tangible), *muta / kāya*, *niyata-micchā-diṭṭhi* (fixed wrong view), *anutpāda* (non-arising). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- The third "good-friend" dialogue; a two-speaker conversation on how "seeing is not connected to the seen," the non-arising of an act, the refined three-hundred-rupee example, and a strong warning against nihilism.
- **The view to be formed: seeing is unconnected to the seen.** For wisdom to arise, one must realise that seeing has no connection to the seen — and that investigating while *holding on to the seen thing* never works. One must see instead that "within seeing, the seen is not caught": where there is seeing, the seen is not found.
- **The three-hundred-rupee example (refined).** Holding three hundred rupees in hand and intending to give two hundred, one gives three hundred: "three hundred" is in the hand, yet the three hundred is *not seen* — the very notion of "seeing" never formed, though the eye and the notes are present. This is what must be seen — that while looking, with the notes right there, no notion of "seeing" arose — and it is subtler than the earlier version of the example.
- **We wrongly hunt for a "non-seeing."** We investigate by knotting the seen to seeing, or else look for a "non-seeing" — but that is another error. The two-trains image: when two trains run alongside at the same speed, "going" does not apply, it is "standing still"; the verb "going" is not engaged. Where "going" is not born, "not-going" cannot be spoken — one is free of the action-word. So at the three-hundred handover, if seeing itself was not born, "non-seeing" does not apply either; if one "did not see," why grab "seeing" again? — one should see only that "seeing did not engage, was not born," the minimal words, so that where the seen stood before the eyes yet no seeing-notion formed, *neither* "seeing" *nor* "not-seeing" applies.
- **He gave by touch, not by seeing.** The giver handed the money at the *muta* (sensed/touch) level, not the *diṭṭha* (seen) level — by the hand, not by sight; only afterwards do we recount it as "he gave without seeing." And did "three hundred" reach the touch? No — only *some notes* (a tangible, *phoṭṭhabba*) reached the touch, never "three hundred." So even "three hundred" was never obtained through sight, touch, body or mind (had the mind grasped it, he would not have given three for two); the "three hundred given" was obtained nowhere, and yet he acted — which is why consciousness is the illusion: the mind registers only "I gave two hundred" (its intention), never catches its own error, and every mind therefore counts itself "right," the crossroads magician whose fault cannot be grasped.
- **The born-blind and the leaf-count.** A man blind from birth doing the same task also gets "two leaves, three leaves" by touch and can count by experience; we too, at the touch level, get only "the count of leaves." What is the difference between the born-blind and us? We carry a memory of previously-seen leaves and currency-values while he never saw — but to invoke that memory is exactly the error, for at the "there-and-there" level memory cannot be brought in; to the tangible-sense, both alike obtain only a tangible ("leaves"), and "three hundred" is obtained by neither through touch. So "three hundred" (the value) is a mind-fabrication, never obtained via hand, touch or sight.
- **The autopilot / "gas."** Our aggregates — eating, walking, all of it — run on autopilot ("gas"), like a plane that takes off and lands to precise figures with no need for a controlling pilot and no karmic steering: five thousand flights land right because the system runs. When it comes out right we credit "my good attention," when wrong "I lost attention" — but the gas *running right* is exactly saṁsāra, which does not show itself *as* saṁsāra precisely because the system mostly runs right; consciousness knots-and-delivers at any moment, rarely errs, and we file success under merit and error under kamma-result.
- **Nothing is really obtained.** Neither touch nor body is truly obtained; where the two (e.g. body and tangible) did not arise, no "feeling," no "non-feeling," and no meeting-of-the-three arose. We are like dementia patients — one lying on a bed does not register "I am on a bed"; "feeling" is irrelevant and cannot be spoken. Sitting and saying "there was a tangible, the seat" is all junk (*bailā*), all mind-constructs (*mano-dhamma*); the seen, heard, sensed and cognized are never-occurred mere thoughts (*hitalu*). Grasping these four is what we call "living," and where the four are not, where are beings or persons? — which is exactly what the Buddha points to; and the whole world of mother, father, son and daughter built from these thought-constructs is what "saṁsāra is suffering" means, for they were never actually obtained, and imagining they exist we suffer, colliding with a falsehood.
- **The strong warning against nihilism.** One must *never* tell anyone "there is no mother, no father, no houses, no vehicles" — that is fixed wrong view (*niyata-micchā-diṭṭhi*). The listener will ask "so there is no mother?" and fall into annihilationism; the fabricated mother and father, taken up and then negated as "no mother, no father," is completely wrong and must not even be uttered, but kept as one's own contemplation. Not seeing the non-arising (*anutpāda*) nature turns into "these things do not exist," which is fixed wrong view; even comparing "there it is like this, here like this" is a fault. This is why the Buddha found it so hard to teach a worldling, who either holds "things exist" or, told of non-arising, takes it as "these things do not exist" — and why *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā, yadā paññāya passati*: impermanence is realised only by wisdom, never by consciousness.

## Summary

This third good-friend discussion presses the point that seeing is not connected to the seen, and does so through a refined version of the three-hundred-rupee example. For wisdom to arise, the teacher says, one must realise that seeing has no connection to the seen at all, and that investigating while clinging to the seen thing never works; instead one must see that within seeing the seen is never caught. His example: a man holding three hundred rupees, meaning to give two hundred, hands over three hundred — the money is in his hand, yet "three hundred" is not seen, and indeed the very notion of "seeing" never formed, though eye and notes were both present. This, subtler than the earlier telling, is what must be seen: that while looking, with the notes right there, no notion of seeing arose. And he warns against the natural error of hunting for a "non-seeing" to replace it: as with two trains running alongside at equal speed, where "going" does not apply and there is only standing still, so where "going" is not born "not-going" cannot be spoken; likewise, if seeing itself was not born at the handover, then "non-seeing" does not apply either, and one should say only that seeing "did not engage," so that neither seeing nor not-seeing has any purchase.

He then locates how the deed actually happened: the man gave by touch, at the sensed level, not by sight, and only afterwards do we describe it as "giving without seeing." But even the touch never received "three hundred" — only some notes, a bare tangible, reached the hand, never the value "three hundred." So "three hundred" was obtained nowhere: not by sight, not by touch, not by body, and not by mind, for had the mind grasped it he would never have given three for two — and yet he acted, which is precisely why consciousness is the illusion, registering only its intention "I gave two hundred," never catching its own error, every mind counting itself right like the magician at the crossroads whose fault cannot be grasped. The born-blind man doing the same task sharpens this: he too gets "two leaves, three leaves" by touch and counts by experience, and we too, at the touch level, obtain only the count of leaves. What separates us from the blind man is merely our memory of previously-seen leaves and values — but to bring in that memory is exactly the error, since at the moment-to-moment level memory cannot be admitted, and to the tangible sense both alike get only "leaves," "three hundred" being obtained by neither. "Three hundred," then, is a mind-fabrication, never delivered by hand or eye.

From here he opens out the autopilot image: our whole life of eating and walking runs on "gas," like a plane taking off and landing to exact figures with no controlling pilot and no karmic steering, five thousand flights landing right because the system simply runs — and the system's *running right* is exactly saṁsāra, which never shows itself as saṁsāra precisely because it mostly succeeds, while we credit success to good attention and blame failure on lost attention. Nothing, he insists, is really obtained: where body and tangible did not arise, no feeling, no non-feeling and no meeting-of-the-three arose, and we are like dementia patients who, lying on a bed, do not register "I am on a bed"; to say "there was a tangible, the seat" is all junk, all mind-construct, the seen-heard-sensed-cognized being never-occurred mere thoughts. Grasping these four is what we call living, and where the four are absent there are no beings and no persons — which is what the Buddha points to — so that the whole world of mother, father, son and daughter, built from these thought-constructs, is exactly what "saṁsāra is suffering" means, since they were never obtained and, imagining them real, we suffer by colliding with a falsehood.

He ends on a warning he repeats with unusual force: one must never say to anyone "there is no mother, no father, no houses or vehicles," for that is fixed wrong view, and a listener told so will ask "then is there no mother?" and topple into annihilationism. The fabricated mother and father, taken up only to be negated as "no mother, no father," is wholly wrong and must not even be spoken, but kept as one's own contemplation; not seeing the non-arising nature turns straight into "these things do not exist," which is fixed wrong view, and even comparing "there it is thus, here it is thus" is a fault. This, he says, is exactly why the Buddha found it so hard to bring a worldling to this, since the worldling either holds that things exist or, hearing of non-arising, concludes that they do not — and why it stands that all formations are impermanent, seen only when one sees with wisdom, never by consciousness at all.
