## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *cetanāhaṁ bhikkhave kammaṁ vadāmi* ("intention, monks, is what I call kamma"); *puñña-pāpa-pahīnassa* ("for one who has abandoned merit and demerit"); and the precept formulas *pāṇātipātā veramaṇī sikkhāpadaṁ samādiyāmi* and *kāmesu micchācārā …*.
- Terms used: *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen / heard / sensed / cognized), *manasikāra* (attention), *saññā* (perception), *cetanā* (intention), *sīlabbata-parāmāsa* (clinging to rites and vows), *ariyakanta-sīla* (the virtue dear to the noble ones), *aparappaccaya* (independent of another), *brahma-loka*, *viññāṇa-māyā* (the illusion of consciousness). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- A continuation of the earlier "good-friend" dialogues (two speakers), applying the "no action is ever obtained" insight to sleep, kamma, the precepts, the arahant's virtue, and the dropping of "I heard this dhamma."
- **We do not act because we do; we do because we think.** What is built is "I do this because I think it" — there is no action anywhere apart from thought. In sleep one bends a leg, turns, bends an arm, and none of it is known — no action is known, no contact is known; and the waking state is really no different, for a leg one is not thinking about is not obtained at all.
- **Intention is the kamma.** *Cetanāhaṁ bhikkhave kammaṁ vadāmi*: when the hand is raised it is the intention that raises it; the hand and the "I" both form as the hand rises, and the raising-action itself forms by attention, by thought alone — one does not do the action "by privilege." An outside action, an outside experience, never occurred.
- **The dream test.** Seeing a dog chase you in a dream and running is the same as thinking a dog chases and running — both formed by thinking; there is no way to say which dog was "the real one that caught you," so both are one. Now, too, one lives as in a dream, only beginning to think of it as "an action."
- **The dementia patient.** A person with dementia remembers nothing recent, no children, no one — yet from an old memory calls out to a servant, "Yasavati," living wholly in a separate world; his last-working perceptions are his attention, and a present world is never obtained for him, not because no world exists but because, his perception-forming (attention) having stopped, only those few perceptions are obtained. This is exactly our own condition.
- **The precepts seen through.** For one who sees this, no animals-to-be-killed are obtained and no undertaking of precepts by wisdom is needed. As "I'll eat rice" obtains the rice (while eating before the TV one keeps no memory of eating or swallowing), so thinking "kill" obtains the animal and one then vows "I won't kill"; thinking "steal" obtains both the me and the object — so there is no object to steal and no me to steal.
- **The arahant's virtue: nothing to keep, nothing to break.** This is *ariyakanta-sīla*: killing cannot be done because no action is obtained, which is why arahants' deeds are said to be "in the mind only" — a mind in which wisdom has seen that not even an action is obtained. The deed, discerned as mere intention, means "doing" is never engaged, and where no action is engaged there is no undertaking "I will not do"; hence one can neither break nor keep a precept — *puñña-pāpa-pahīnassa*, the level of the arahant who has abandoned both merit and demerit. Transcending action is the bodiless *brahma-loka* level, where only the seen-heard-sensed-cognized operate and there is no body; transcending action, one does not even stand in action.
- **Cooling at the point of seeing.** The extinguishing is right at the place of seeing — not tomorrow, not next year; at each seeing it cools a little. Like a burning mountain that cannot be doused at once but is quenched bucket by bucket, developing this vision and cultivating the path cools a small spot at every seeing, until one day the whole mountain is extinguished.
- **From morning, only the consciousness-illusion.** The vehicles, fences, chairs and tables "since morning" were merely thought — no notion of "seeing" ever formed — so the whole "I existed since morning" is the illusion of consciousness; no "me" existed and no world existed, it was lived as in a dream, and even "I existed" and the entire past are obtained only in the present thinking moment. The knot consciousness strikes is the illusion itself: one did not die today, one rose, meditated and went to work, but "seeing" appeared only when attention was applied around ten, the past being fabricated by the mind now.
- **The two extremes made of not-knowing.** "Before I did not feel, now I feel" — both sides are built from not-knowing, and these are the two extremes, transcendable only if seen there. No amount of reading nonsense reaches it; one must be freed *together with* the dhamma, for only if freed with it does the vision come — while still holding onto the dhamma one cannot reach the vision.
- **Removing "my knowledge."** My "knowledge" too is made by consciousness — read or heard through the seen-heard-sensed-cognized, a perception built of the four perceptions; "I have dhamma-knowledge" is exactly the grasped thing that must be removed.
- **Independent of another (aparappaccaya).** The stream-enterer becomes independent of others: seeing hearing fully, "I heard this dhamma from so-and-so" drops away, for no dhamma-teacher, no dhamma-heard and no me is obtained; so the path advances not by asking anyone, the wisdom itself carrying it forward. Even "comprehension" is false — finally there is no "understanding" either; we suppose "the venerable said it and I heard it," but when hearing is rightly seen, the heard thing, the heard dhamma and the hearing-me are all born of not-knowing. And the little true stories one has grasped as dhamma — "the world is false, an illusion, built by *paṭiccasamuppāda*" — when *that* is freed, the vision comes, and "these were the teachings, this is what I understood" ends there too, comprehension being no event that happens anywhere.

## Summary

This dialogue carries forward the earlier good-friend conversations by pressing a single claim to its furthest reach: we do not act because we do, but do because we think, and there is no action anywhere apart from thought. The teacher opens from sleep — a leg bends, the body turns, an arm folds, and none of it is known; no action and no contact is known — and observes that waking is no different, since a leg one is not thinking about is never obtained. From this he draws out the meaning of *cetanāhaṁ bhikkhave kammaṁ vadāmi*: when the hand rises it is intention that raises it, the hand and the "I" forming together as it rises, the raising itself arising by attention and thought alone, so that one never performs an action "by privilege" and an outside action was never experienced at all. The dream sharpens it — being chased by a dog in a dream and running is the same as thinking a dog chases and running, both made by thinking, with no way to name which dog was real; and so, now too, one lives as in a dream and only begins to call it "an action."

He illustrates with a person with dementia who, remembering nothing recent, still calls to a servant by an old name and lives wholly in a separate world: his last-working perceptions are his whole attention, and a present world is never obtained for him — not because no world exists, but because, his perception-forming having stopped, only those few perceptions arise. This, the teacher says, is exactly our condition. From here the precepts are seen through: for one who sees this, no animals to be killed are obtained and no undertaking of precepts by wisdom is required, because thinking "kill" is what obtains the animal (just as "I'll eat rice" obtains the rice, though one eating before the television keeps no memory of eating), and thinking "steal" obtains both the me and the object, so there is neither an object to steal nor a me to steal. The arahant's virtue therefore has nothing to keep and nothing to break — the *ariyakanta-sīla* — for killing cannot be done where no action is obtained, which is why an arahant's deeds are "in the mind only": the deed, discerned as mere intention, means doing is never engaged, and where no action is engaged there is no vow "I will not do." This is *puñña-pāpa-pahīnassa*, the level beyond merit and demerit, the bodiless brahma-world where only the seen-heard-sensed-cognized operate and one does not even stand in action.

The extinguishing, he stresses, is not a future event but happens right at the place of seeing — cooling a little at each seeing, like a burning mountain quenched not at once but bucket by bucket as the vision is developed and the path cultivated, until one day the whole mountain goes out. Turning back on the day, he shows that the vehicles, fences, chairs and tables "since morning" were merely thought, no notion of seeing ever having formed, so that "I existed since morning" is the illusion of consciousness: no me and no world existed, it was lived as in a dream, and even the sense "I existed," together with the whole past, is obtained only in this present thinking moment. One did not die today — one rose, meditated and went to work — yet "seeing" appeared only when attention was applied around ten, the past being fabricated by the mind now; and "before I did not feel, now I feel" are two extremes both built of not-knowing, transcendable only where they are seen, never reached by reading, and freed only together with the dhamma itself.

Finally the discussion turns on "my knowledge." That knowledge too is made by consciousness — read or heard through the seen-heard-sensed-cognized, a perception built of the four perceptions — so "I have dhamma-knowledge" is the very thing to be removed. The stream-enterer becomes independent of another: when hearing is seen fully, "I heard this dhamma from so-and-so" drops, no teacher, no dhamma-heard and no me being obtained, and the path then advances not by asking anyone but by the wisdom carrying itself forward. Even comprehension is false — there is finally no "understanding" either; we suppose "the venerable said it and I heard it," but rightly seen, the heard thing, the heard dhamma and the hearing-me are all born of not-knowing. And the little true stories grasped as dhamma — "the world is illusion, built by dependent origination" — when even these are released, the vision comes and "this is what I was taught and understood" ends there too, comprehension proving to be no event that happens anywhere at all.
