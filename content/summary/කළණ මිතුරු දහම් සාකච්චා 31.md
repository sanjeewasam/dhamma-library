## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly referred to, unattributed: the formula *na ca so na ca añño* ("neither the same one nor another") — cited as "the Buddha himself said, neither this nor another."
- Terms used: *abhisaṅkhāra* / *abhisañcetayita* (the further-fashioned, volitionally-constructed), *nitya-saññā* (permanence-perception), *maññanā* (conceiving), *saññā* (perception), *viññāṇa-gocara* (the range of consciousness), *puthujjana* (worldling), *āgantuka* (adventitious/visitor), *santati* (continuity), *prārthanā* (aspiration/prayer). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue built around the oil-lamp flame, the not-thrown teacup, and the iceberg — showing that we never do "the very thing we thought," that a permanence-perception runs the whole show, and that action is forever outside the range of consciousness.
- **The oil-lamp flame.** A lamp's flame arises from *that* lamp's own oil, not from a previous or neighbouring lamp — a good image for the fact that the thing we thought is *not* what gets fabricated. The mind arises this moment from the causes assembled this moment; had a different datum been the basis, the mind would be constructed by those causes.
- **We never do the same thing we thought.** We assume "the same must happen," but that can neither be asserted nor even wished for; it arises only "there and there," never as a repetition — so aspiration/prayer is empty effort. This is the best illustration of our everyday errors: we frequently do not do the task we set out to do, because whatever object comes to this moment's mind steers the thoughts and acts.
- **"Neither the same one nor another."** The Buddha's *na ca so na ca añño* means one can say neither "connected" nor "unconnected" — a statement to which both alternatives fail to apply; "not another" does not posit some other thing apart from the event, but points to the non-occurring moment being "not there." People wrongly hear it as "so it *is* connected."
- **If it were the same, a permanent self would be real.** Were "we do the very thing we think" true, the world would have a real persistence: a permanently-existing person who plans and carries it out, the continuity (*santati*) becoming real. But since a sitting-and-doing person could never go wrong — and things *do* go wrong — no such foundation exists; support arises from a fresh cause there-and-there, never from the previous same one.
- **Going-right is the accident; erring is the nature.** "Going right" is a *similar perception* arising (not a similar mind), and rightly seen it is a coincidence, while erring is the very nature of things — the world itself is a coincidence. Yet the worldling treats *going-right* as the nature and files error under "a visitor's slip / momentary loss of mindfulness." The seeing-mind and the writing-mind are two different minds; to say "the seen itself is written" would make the view-side ("it can be seen") real and would mean error is impossible — which shows the thing simply cannot be done.
- **The teacup (a permanence-perception).** One carries a cup to reheat tea, yet the mind at the moment of taking never understands what it is carrying it for; it simply carries until the hand arrives. Were it truly understood, the cup could never be thrown — and indeed one may in anger fling a plate but not the cup: "the cup is not thrown" is a *nitya-saññā*, a permanence-perception. One tosses the tea but not the cup — and this cannot be attributed to mindfulness (there is nothing to point to as "mindfulness," since the cup was never thrown), nor to un-mindfulness (for then the cup too would have been flung).
- **The permanence-perception can never be known.** If it could be known, at the moment it operated we would have a notion "here it is operating," and then we could never err. We cannot say what object the mind held while tossing the tea; the taken object is never understood — which is exactly why we are deceived by "thinking." Consciousness never manifests without an object, and where no object is understood there is no notion "I have a mind"; since the breath from morning is not felt, one cannot even say a mind exists — like a dead man — and to get an object at all we must knot nose-and-breath together.
- **Action is outside the range of consciousness.** Consciousness never takes *action* as its object; it takes only nose, breath, sound, ear. Not knowing that action lies outside consciousness's range, we are deceived by whatever object we momentarily seize, and consciousness has no clarity about the object it takes. Hence one cannot conclude "everything is done by the mind" (the consciousness-only view), for the mind is not manifest — a mind exists only when an object is understood — and from morning, no sensation being understood, one cannot say whether a mind existed or ceased. How any of it happens therefore cannot be discussed, cannot be pinned down, cannot even be called "spontaneous"; in the un-known there is no interpretation to give, and it is all mere talk, mere thoughts.
- **Sleep and waking are indistinguishable here.** In sleep the limbs bend and stretch — who thought "bend," who decided "stretch"? It cannot be analysed; and it is no different now. In sleep there is no notion whether one is breathing, and there is none now either; there is no mindfulness of sitting, just as when asleep one does not know one is on the bed. The breath is unknown in sleep and unobtained in waking alike, so one need not sleep to lose it — where, then, is "sleep"? The present bears sleep's very characteristic.
- **The iceberg.** The fabricated distortion has a part we never perceive; we mostly dwell in the not-perceived, as in sleep, and the worldling mind knows only the small knowable tip. As a ship sees only the ice above water and decides by that, unaware of a submerged mass three or four times larger — so the Titanic struck the unseen part and its hull broke, the fault lying precisely in the invisible portion they could not factor — so we decide by this moment's thought together with an un-thinkable nature we do not know is there. This not-knowing of an unperceivable part is what "ignorance not knowing" means.
- **The fish and the water.** To see the fault in knowing, one must see that submerged part; if it is seen even for a single moment — this is "seeing impermanence" — the underneath shows and "this is how it happens" appears. As a fish does not perceive water because it lives in water, we do not perceive this as conceiving (*maññanā*) because we live within ignorance, within conceiving itself; only one who has seen the not-conceiving nature can one day see this as conceiving. It can be proven by no means whatever — a fish cannot be brought to think "water" until it sees dry land, and only by seeing the opposite nature can the thing be thought at all.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue opens on the flame of an oil lamp: the flame arises from that lamp's own oil, not from a previous or neighbouring lamp — a fitting image for the truth that the very thing we thought is never what gets fabricated. The mind arises in each moment from the causes assembled in that moment, so we cannot say "the same thing must happen," cannot even wish for it, and aspiration is therefore empty effort. This, the teacher says, is the best explanation of our ordinary errors: we so often fail to do the task we set out to do, because whatever object arrives at the present mind steers the thoughts and deeds. He invokes the Buddha's *na ca so na ca añño* — neither the same one nor another — to show that one can call the sequence neither connected nor unconnected, "not another" pointing not to some separate thing but to the non-occurring moment being simply "not there," though people mishear it as "so it is connected." Were it otherwise — were we really to do the very thing we think — the world would possess a real persistence, a permanent person who plans and executes, the continuity becoming actual; but since such a sitting-and-doing person could never go wrong, and things plainly do go wrong, no such foundation exists. Going-right, then, is the accident and erring the nature, the world itself a coincidence, even though the worldling reverses this and files his errors under "a momentary slip of mindfulness."

The heart of the talk is the teacup. One carries a cup to reheat the tea, yet at the moment of taking it the mind never understands what it is being carried for; it simply carries until the hand arrives. Were the purpose truly understood, the cup could never be thrown — and in fact a person may in anger fling a plate but never the cup, so that "the cup is not thrown" stands as a permanence-perception, a *nitya-saññā*. One discards the tea but keeps the cup, and this can be laid neither at the door of mindfulness (there being nothing to point to as mindfulness, since the cup was never thrown) nor of un-mindfulness (for then the cup too would have gone). The permanence-perception can never be known, the teacher insists: if it could, we would notice it operating and could then never err. We cannot say what object the mind held while tossing the tea, the taken object is never understood, and this is exactly why we are deceived by "thinking." Consciousness never manifests without an object, and where no object is understood there is no notion "I have a mind"; the breath from morning is unfelt, so one cannot even say a mind exists — like a dead man — and only by knotting nose to breath does an object arise at all.

From this he draws the decisive point that action is forever outside the range of consciousness: consciousness takes only nose, breath, sound and ear as objects, never an action, and not knowing this we are deceived by whatever object we momentarily seize, consciousness itself having no clarity about it. So one cannot conclude that "everything is done by the mind," for the mind is not manifest — it exists only when an object is understood — and since from morning no sensation is understood, one cannot say whether a mind arose or ceased, whether it exists or is stilled. How any of it happens cannot be discussed, pinned down, or even called spontaneous; in the un-known there is no interpretation to give, and it remains mere talk and mere thought. Sleep and waking prove indistinguishable here: in sleep the limbs bend and stretch with no one thinking "bend" or deciding "stretch," and the waking state cannot be analysed any differently; the breath is unknown in sleep and unobtained in waking, so one need not sleep to lose it, and the present already bears sleep's own characteristic.

He closes with two images for the unseen. The fabricated distortion has a portion we never perceive, and we dwell mostly in that not-perceived region as in sleep, the worldling knowing only the small knowable tip — as a ship sees only the ice above the water and decides by that, unaware of a submerged mass several times larger, so that the Titanic struck the invisible part and its hull broke, the fault lying exactly in what could not be seen. We too decide by this moment's thought together with an un-thinkable nature we do not know is there, and this ignorance of an unperceivable part is what "not-knowing" means. To see the fault in knowing, one must glimpse that submerged part, even for a single moment — which is "seeing impermanence" — and then the underneath shows itself and "this is how it happens" appears. As a fish cannot perceive water while living in it, we cannot perceive this as conceiving while living within ignorance and within conceiving itself; only one who has seen the not-conceiving nature can one day recognise this as conceiving, and until the opposite nature is seen — as a fish cannot think "water" until it sees dry land — the thing cannot be proven or even thought at all.
