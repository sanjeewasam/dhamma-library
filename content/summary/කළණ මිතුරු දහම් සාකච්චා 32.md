## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue whose core is the remote-control example, used to correct the view "I thought because I saw," and whose method is that cessation is realised *within* the seeing of arising, not in a separate "deep" contemplation.
- **Cutting the carrot / the tree with its root.** Breaking a carrot into pieces is still seeing-with-folly; dissecting what folly grasped achieves nothing, like a dog examining the dog it imagines in its reflection. An ordinary person "cuts a tree" by taking only the part above the ground, while the one who comes to the vision uproots it *with the root* — so meditation that merely suppresses the object is like felling the above-ground trunk while the buried root is never seen, and taking form and repeating "form is false, an illusion, a water-bubble" only cuts at ground level, after which one cannot even check whether the root remains.
- **The false "cessation" of a quiet mind.** When nothing comes to mind one thinks, "this is cessation" — yet seeing and hearing are still present in full, and one simply does not notice being immersed in them. Seeing must therefore be discerned through *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*.
- **Wise attention is just seeing the unwise as unwise.** *Yoniso-manasikāra* arises only by seeing *ayoniso-manasikāra* as unwise; the very capacity to see the unwise *as* unwise is itself the wise attention, and there is no separate wise attention alongside it. One who sees everything as unwise catches no "attention" at all, yet by that state we may say he dwells in wise attention.
- **The remote-control example.** We assume "I thought about the remote because I *know* the remote" — but was it because you knew it, or because you thought it, that it was known? The decisive question: *what is irrelevant to thinking?* The answer is the *outside* remote — one cannot think an outside remote, one thinks only an inside thing, so the external object is irrelevant to the thinking.
- **Correcting "I thought because I saw."** It is not that seeing came first and thinking followed; the seeing and the remote *both arise together with the thinking*, and the wrong attention is precisely the assumption "I thought because I saw." The remote is a previously-known thing, and only a previously-known thing was thought; as one thought, the "seeing" of the remote appeared along with it. Both the seen and the thought are inside things, so to suppose "I thought because it existed outside, because I saw it" is exactly the unwise attention (*ayoniso*).
- **The method: no separate depth.** The teacher stresses he shows no separate cessation and no separate "deep place" — he only corrects the view within the very same event, because cessation must be realised where arising is seen. "Deep thinking" is not a second kind of looking set against "plain talk"; the depth lies inside the ordinary event where we are caught, and the inability to see *that* exactly as it is, is the reason cessation is not seen.
- **No new knowing ever occurs.** Only the already-known was thought; there is no fresh "knowing" anywhere. As a man who has eaten all day and eats again at night takes in nothing new, so we never think or know anything new, and a "knowing" can never be done afresh. This is *abhisaṅkhāra* — thinking a thought already thought — and this is what *āsava* means: having thought, the mind keeps thinking, the influxes pouring down into it. One who realises that nothing new was ever known will not fall for the folly "I know / I can know," there being no place for it to establish.
- **"The place we stand is never seen."** For so long the head has gone off to the other side — the view — grasping a few "knowable" things and dwelling in thought, without seeing that one is *in* a knowing all along; not knowing *that*, one supposed "I can know." Today I think yesterday's television changed, but I never saw a television yesterday or today — I saw only the known thing in the mind.
- **The double falseness.** To say "the outside is false" is itself one more falsehood, for "outside" is still the deceived thing held in the mind and an outside was never obtained; declaring a never-obtained outside to be false is another lie, grosser than the first — like naming a child never born and then calling that name false. This is "poking sticks into the lie": taking the lie, turning it over and over, and finally pronouncing "it is a lie," from which no comprehension comes.
- **Critique of dissecting the aggregates.** Breaking the obtained lie of the *pañcupādānakkhandha* into earth-water-fire-air, colour-smell-taste-nutriment and the "pure octad" is only poking more sticks into the lie and then announcing "here there is a lie" — sinking from lie to lie, staying in the mud, never reaching dry ground. The right approach is not to call the false "false," but to see *how* even a "lie" came to be obtained, upon which there is nothing left even to call a lie.
- **Effortlessness and the end of questions.** *Samudaya* is the deception we build as "outside"; but "knowing an inside thing by consciousness" cannot be wisdom either, since if only a thought-thing was known, no knowing occurred. Refined to the end, we are in knowing-within-knowing, and once the awareness comes that we merely dwell in a knowing, no "new knowing" remains for one who has seen the folly — the matter ends there. With both the outside and the inside seen through, nothing is left to break; it becomes effortless (*nirāyāsa*), no outside-inside story forms, not even "a process" stands, nothing is obtained, and no question arises — for one who has this can watch a sermon and see at once that the speaker is "caught."

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue, preserved only as a fragment that opens mid-thought and breaks off at "[TO BE CONTINUED]," turns on a single worked example — the remote control — and on a method the teacher repeats throughout: that cessation is realised inside the seeing of arising, never in some separate deep contemplation. He begins by warning that dissecting a carrot, or breaking anything folly has grasped into pieces, is still seeing-with-folly, like a dog examining the dog it imagines in its own reflection. An ordinary man "cuts a tree" by taking only the part above the ground, whereas the one who reaches the vision uproots it with its root; so meditation that merely suppresses the object fells the visible trunk while leaving the buried root unseen, and repeating over form that "it is false, an illusion, a water-bubble" only cuts at ground level, after which one cannot even check whether the root survives. Even the quiet mind in which "nothing comes" is not cessation, for seeing and hearing remain in full and one simply fails to notice being immersed in them; seeing must instead be discerned through *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*, and wise attention turns out to be nothing but the capacity to see unwise attention *as* unwise — there is no separate *yoniso-manasikāra* alongside that seeing.

The remote-control example carries the weight of the talk. We assume "I thought about the remote because I know the remote," but the teacher presses the decisive question: what is *irrelevant* to the thinking? The answer is the outside remote, for one can never think an external object — one thinks only an inside thing. Hence the error to be corrected is "I thought because I saw": seeing does not precede thinking; rather the seeing and the remote both arise together *with* the thinking, and only a previously-known thing was ever thought. Both the seen and the thought are inside things, so the supposition "I thought because it existed outside, because I saw it" is exactly the unwise attention. The teacher is emphatic that he has shown no separate cessation and no separate deep place — only the same event, its view corrected — because cessation must be realised precisely where arising is seen; the depth is inside the ordinary event where we are caught, and the inability to see that event as it is, is the whole reason cessation goes unseen.

From here he draws out that no new knowing ever occurs: only the already-known was thought, and as a man who has eaten all day and eats again at night takes in nothing new, so we never know anything afresh and can never perform a "knowing" anew. This thinking of an already-thought thought is *abhisaṅkhāra*, and it is what *āsava* means — the mind, having thought, keeps thinking, the influxes pouring in — so that one who sees that nothing new was ever known will not be caught by the folly "I know, I can know," there being nowhere for it to establish. The trouble, he says, is that the place we stand is never seen: the head has long gone off to the side of the view, grasping a few "knowable" things while dwelling in thought, never noticing that one is *in* a knowing all along. He then exposes the double falseness by which practitioners go astray — to declare "the outside is false" is itself a further lie, since an outside was never obtained and "outside" is still the deceived thing in the mind, like naming a child never born and then calling the name false. Dissecting the five aggregates into earth-water-fire-air, colour-smell-taste-nutriment and the "pure octad" only pokes more sticks into the lie and then announces "here is a lie," sinking from lie to lie and never reaching dry ground; the right move is not to call the false "false," but to see *how* even a lie came to be obtained, whereupon nothing remains to be called a lie at all.

Finally the conversation refines cessation itself. *Samudaya* is the deception built as "outside," but "knowing an inside thing by consciousness" cannot be wisdom either, for if only a thought-thing was known, then no knowing occurred; pressed to the end, we are in knowing-within-knowing, and once the awareness dawns that we merely dwell in a knowing — as earlier one saw that one merely dwells in a hearing — no "new knowing" remains for the one who has seen the folly, and the matter simply ends. With both the outside and the inside seen through, nothing is left to break, it becomes effortless, no outside-inside story forms, not even "a process" stands, nothing is obtained and no question arises — such a one, the teacher notes, can listen to a sermon and see at once that the speaker is caught.
