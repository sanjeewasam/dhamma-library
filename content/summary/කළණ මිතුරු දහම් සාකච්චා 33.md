## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed:
  - *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ* ("consciousness is like an illusion / a magic-show") — cited twice, "this was not said idly."
  - *na te kāmā yāni citrāni loke … saṅkapparāgo purisassa kāmo* ("the pretty things in the world are not sensuality; a person's sensuality is the lust of intention").
  - *khīṇaṁ purāṇaṁ, navaṁ natthi sambhavaṁ* ("the old is exhausted, there is no arising of the new") — with *navaṁ* glossed as "new," *natthi* as "there is not," *sambhavaṁ* as "no re-arising."
- Terms used: *saṅkhāra* (formations), *manasikāra* (attention), *phassa* (contact), *cetanā* (intention), *māyā* (illusion), *āsava* / *āsava-pahāna* (influxes / their abandoning), *maññanā* (conceiving), *vāyo-dhātu* (wind-element), *nirāyāsa* / *āyāsa* (effortless / effort), *saññā* (perception: *sadda-, rasa-, gandha-, poṭṭhabba-saññā*), *aparappaccaya* (independent of another), and *dvitāva* (duality). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue built on the film-projector-and-negatives image, the two-bamboo-bundles image, and the breath — showing how consciousness animates still "frames" into a live world, why thinking is an effort laid over an effortless nature, and how the influxes are exhausted.
- **No action is ever obtained; thinking is its exact opposite.** In any "action," neither "there is an action" nor "there is not" is found; lying down, only if one thinks "I am lying down" does it seem one is in an action, and that is merely a thought — an intention, an idea. Were a real action happening in the body it would be felt continuously and need no thinking; the body never obtains an action, and that not-obtaining is never within the range of consciousness. So "I know because an action happens" must be dropped entirely: it is "I know because I thought." Thinking is the exact opposite of that action-less nature — a force-applied work, an effort (*āyāsa*) — whereas one could dwell effortlessly (*nirāyāsa*); we "beg and eat," building saṁsāra by begging.
- **The tree.** A tree takes in carbon dioxide, gives off oxygen, sways in the wind and stands still without it — none of which is relevant *to the tree*; and from morning your breathing is likewise irrelevant to you, so that "you and the tree are one." We impose on the tree a "process" it knows nothing of — inventing a subject, teaching it, burdening children's heads and handing them leaf-scraps called degrees — and in just this way we manufacture a problem where there is none. First one must see the *difference* between that effortless nature and this thinking.
- **The immaculate nature.** Seeing this, one cannot "meditate," for one is already in that nature; one can neither "be mindful" as a doing nor fall into un-mindfulness. One takes no action by thinking but rests in the immaculate (*niṣkalaṅka*) nature — a stable standing we have never obtained for lack of wisdom, not knowing that such an effortless, unknowable nature is even here; reached, it is there unchanged around the clock.
- **The projector and the negatives.** On the film-strip every frame is a *still* image; only when the projector runs, one negative after another falling on the screen, does a "moving picture" appear — yet what falls is a picture. In no single frame is there a bending leg or a waving arm, and nothing in a still picture moves; the projector, the light, the screen and the negative each have no waving arm — so the "waving" comes from nowhere. Each negative leaves a memory-trace; played fast in sequence (leg on the ground, then a fraction raised, then more), the strung-together traces make us "see" a leg move, though nothing moved and we saw only a few frames — a few *saṅkhāra* glimpsed in ignorance. Seen *as* mere frames, there are no moving, running, fighting people anywhere; the "action" only *appears* to be there — which is exactly why consciousness is a magician, and why *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ* was said.
- **The dream and fear.** A dog in a dream is only a "dog" attention, but saying "dog" brings the whole package — dogs bite, chase, we will run — already recorded in us; played through consciousness, these attention-frames create a vivid active vision, and once the activity becomes "real" one cannot stop the fear. Yet without a dog, without running, without biting, without blood, a "fear" still forms — so we were frightened purely by the knotted *saṅkhāra*-set. That set, becoming attention even in sleep, can fabricate an event and frighten; the event so made is "live," and to that live-ness any lust or hate can be attached (wanting to strike the dog, wanting to pluck the beautiful rose). But this "live-ness" comes from something never obtained — even the word "live" is too good for it, itself a lie — and it is precisely of this never-obtained live-ness that "illusion" and "arising and ceasing" are spoken.
- **Is there anything arisen to cease?** To stop walking there must be a walking man; seeing from the start that there is none, effort becomes effortless of itself, and with no arising there is nothing here to make cease.
- **The two bamboo-bundles.** The two bundles' "ability to stand upright" was in neither bundle; likewise, within the "dog" attention there is no "running" belonging to dogs. Does the upright-standing come from the two bundles or from the *cause*? — and where the cause is not obtained, no fruit is obtained. Here the cause is the fabricating, connecting nature made by attention, which is nowhere: it is *phassa*, the knotting, the one-to-one collision. As a knot between two threads is in neither thread, the knot tied by attention is in no single *saṅkhāra*; what is caught is only the knot, and untie it and there is nothing to untie — no knot was ever obtained. Seen as separate, the dog and I give no reason to fear or run; fear arises only *with the action*, and seeing that there is no such action anywhere removes the ground of fear.
- **The bird, and lust as intention.** Thinking "a bird," we insert a crying, singing one from within and make it live, so a whole living environment forms; a bird merely *pictured* rouses no desire. *Na te kāmā yāni citrāni loke, saṅkapparāgo purisassa kāmo* — the pictures hold no lust or hate; a bird that neither cries nor flies is like a stone, and our attachment arises only because we knotted the live-ness (its crying, its flying) onto it.
- **The breath and the danger of elements-analysis.** Before a breath is taken we know nothing of "a breath"; as "taking" is felt one may think "breath," and just that thought fabricates both the taken-breath and the feeling-me automatically, the two sides built by the action. Seen *as* mere attention, breath would be known as such — but the moment one says "breath is the wind-element (*vāyo-dhātu*), drawn through my nose, felt by me," the elements are knotted and the story goes live again, animating still negatives exactly like the film.
- **We are the film's director.** We pay to watch what we made with thought, building saṁsāra like growing-and-eating; when sleep comes there is no story till dawn — director and cast alike "dead" — so the directing was pointless, for he only made frames while *we* made them live and watched, consciousness stringing the moment-to-moment negatives (arising by attention-condition from the *āsava*) into a garland: the illusion, *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ*.
- **Confidence in comprehension, not in a person.** Seen thus, the dhamma needs no separate faith or blind devotion, being directly verified as certainly true, as the Buddha's word — and no words are needed once seen. Such confidence rests not in a *person* but in the comprehension itself: unshakable, non-transferable, *aparappaccaya*; whereas one confident because "a monk said it" loses that confidence if the monk falls away, the one with comprehension has no such dependence, and faith in that quality grows of itself.
- **The exhaustion of the influxes.** *Khīṇaṁ purāṇaṁ, navaṁ natthi sambhavaṁ*: it is folly that makes it "this moment," and if folly ceases this moment the old ones have no place — the old coming to show their live-magic here only through lack of wisdom. If wisdom operates now, the influxes can show no magic and tie no knot: this is *āsava-pahāna*, the old exhausted and nothing new built. Seeing a tree we build afresh "I saw this flower-tree months ago"; but if "flower-tree" is not knotted now to the "I-saw-before" action, no new one is built — both wear away, the old worn out and the new un-arisen, the sword cutting both sides, no leaping to the past and no building for the future.
- **I become the action, and duality is consciousness itself.** Only when "thinking" is thought does "I exist" appear — I too am born *from* the action, and had the action not arisen the taken-me and the taken-breath would not form. That very "thinking" place is the view, where I arise; and the "I who thinks," born from the arising of thinking itself, can never be caught — being the thinking, I never perceive "thinking," a chick of thinking that the blow can never catch. So a nowhere-existent "me" is born by attention-condition, the dog and I arising together and, through lack of skill, split into a separate dog and a separate me. This splitting of what arises wholly-from-one-place into two is *duality* — and duality is the very nature of consciousness.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue develops one sustained image — the film projector and its negatives — to show how consciousness turns still frames into a living, moving world. It begins by pressing that no action is ever obtained: in any "action" neither its presence nor its absence is found, and lying down it only *seems* one is acting if one thinks "I am lying down," which is merely a thought, an intention, an idea. Were a real action happening in the body it would be felt continuously and would need no thinking; the body never obtains an action, and that not-obtaining lies forever outside consciousness's range. So the assumption "I know because an action happens" must be dropped entirely — it is "I know because I thought" — and thinking is revealed as the exact opposite of that action-less nature, a force-applied work, an effort laid over what could be perfectly effortless. The tree makes this vivid: its intake of air, its swaying and stillness are irrelevant to the tree, just as one's breathing from morning is irrelevant to oneself, so that one and the tree are the same; we merely impose on the tree a "process" it knows nothing of, inventing subjects and burdening children's heads with it, and in just this way we manufacture a problem where none exists. Seeing this, one cannot "meditate" or "be mindful" as a doing, nor fall into un-mindfulness, but rests in an immaculate nature never before obtained for want of wisdom — a nature there unchanged around the clock.

The projector carries the heart of the teaching. Every frame on the film-strip is a still image; only as the projector runs, one negative after another striking the screen, does a moving picture appear, though what falls is only a picture. In no single frame is there a bending leg or a waving arm — nothing in a still picture moves, and neither projector, light, screen nor negative holds a waving arm — so the "waving" comes from nowhere: each negative leaves a memory-trace, and the traces, strung fast together, make us see a leg rise and move though nothing moved and only a few frames were seen, a few formations glimpsed in ignorance. Seen *as* frames, there are no moving or running or fighting people anywhere; the action merely *appears*, which is exactly why consciousness is called a magician and why "consciousness is like an illusion" was said. The dream shows the same: a dog in a dream is only a "dog" attention, but the word brings its whole recorded package — dogs bite and chase, we will run — and played through consciousness these frames build a vivid vision one cannot help fearing; yet without dog, running, biting or blood a fear still forms, proving that the knotted formation-set alone frightens us, becoming attention even in sleep and fabricating a "live" event onto which any lust or hate can be fastened. But this live-ness comes from something never obtained — even the word "live" is a lie — and it is precisely of this that "illusion" and "arising and ceasing" are spoken; for to stop a walking there must first be a walking man, and seeing from the start that there is none, effort falls away of itself and nothing remains here to make cease.

The two-bamboo-bundles image sharpens the mechanism: the bundles' ability to stand upright was in neither bundle but in their mutual leaning, and likewise the "running" of the dreamed dog belongs to no dog but to a fabricating, connecting nature made by attention — which is nowhere, and is precisely contact, the one-to-one collision, the knot. As a knot between two threads is in neither thread, the knot tied by attention is in no single formation; only the knot is caught, and to untie it is to find there was never a knot to untie. Seen separately, the dog and I give no ground for fear or flight; fear arises only *with the action*, and to see that no such action exists anywhere is to remove its very ground. The bird makes the same point about desire: thinking "a bird" we insert a crying, singing one and make it live, whereas a bird merely pictured rouses nothing — the world's pretty things are not sensuality, a person's sensuality being the lust of his own intention — so that our attachment arises only from the live-ness we knotted onto it. And the breath is the intimate case: before it is taken we know nothing of "a breath," but as "taking" is felt we think "breath," and that thought alone fabricates both the taken-breath and the feeling-me; the danger is that, having glimpsed breath as mere attention, one then says "breath is the wind-element, drawn through my nose, felt by me," re-knotting the elements and animating the still negatives all over again.

The dialogue closes on our authorship and its undoing. We are the director of the film, paying to watch what we made with thought and building saṁsāra like growing-and-eating; when sleep comes there is no story till dawn, director and cast alike as if dead, so the directing was pointless — he only made frames, while we made them live and watched, consciousness stringing the moment-to-moment negatives, arisen by attention-condition from the influxes, into a garland: the illusion, consciousness like a magic-show. Seen thus, the dhamma needs no separate faith or blind devotion, being directly verified as the Buddha's word, and such confidence rests not in a person but in the comprehension itself — unshakable, non-transferable, independent of another — so that one confident merely because "a monk said it" would lose it if the monk fell away, while the one with comprehension does not. This is the exhaustion of the influxes: the old is exhausted and no new arises, for it is folly that makes anything "this moment," and if wisdom operates now the old influxes can show no magic and tie no knot — the sword cutting both sides, no leaping to the past and no building for the future. Finally, "I" too am born from the action, appearing only when thinking is thought; that thinking-place is the view where I arise, and the "I who thinks," being the thinking itself, can never catch "thinking" — a chick that the blow can never strike. So a nowhere-existent me is born by attention-condition together with the dog, and through lack of skill the one arising is split into a separate dog and a separate me: this splitting is duality, and duality is the very nature of consciousness.
