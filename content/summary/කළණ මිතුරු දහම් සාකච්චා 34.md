## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: the *paṭiccasamuppāda* links *phassa-paccayā … saṅkhārā* ("with contact as condition, formations/intention") and *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* ("with ignorance as condition, formations").
- Terms used: *manasikāra* (attention), *cetanā* (intention), *phassa* (contact), *saṁsandana* (comparison), *natthi-paccaya* (the "not"-condition), *sati* / *asati* (mindfulness / un-mindfulness — Sinhala *sihiya* / *asihiya*), *nitya-saññā* (permanence-perception), *anicca* (impermanence), *anātma* / *ātmīya-bhāva* (not-self / selfhood), *avijjā* / *vijjā* (ignorance / true knowing), *suññatā* (emptiness), *prajñapti* (designation), *anyonya* (mutual conditioning). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue analysing contact as comparison, then dismantling the three times (past, present, future) and showing that mindfulness and un-mindfulness, as ordinarily spoken of, are two pieces of the same ignorance.
- **Attention, intention and contact arise together.** *Manasikāra* is never noticed on its own — we notice only after *cetanā* arises — and the two are practically one. In *phassa-paccayā saṅkhārā*, contact and attention occur together: contact is "the thing thought," and it is what enables attention, since attention is the *comparing* of two things.
- **Contact is the presence of two.** One cannot think "tall" without something shorter; two must exist to be compared, and that "two-ness" is *phassa* — the place where two touch. Same-size trees yield no "tall"; a shorter one is needed. Attention pronounces "tall/short"; contact is the comparison itself.
- **"Now audible / now felt" is asserted only against a "not."** "It is audible now" is known only relative to "it was not audible before," and if it were continuously audible one would never say "now audible"; so the present arises from a *not*-condition, "now present" being asserted against the compared not-state. The teacher cautions that this "not" is not itself ignorance — *we* merely think "the not."
- **The "not" is a second moment, not the first.** From morning there was no story "do I breathe or not," no awareness of breath at all, and not even an "I don't know" — the not-knowing itself was unknown. From such total not-knowing, the moment it is mentioned one says "now it is felt," and the "wasn't felt before" is compared instantly with "felt now": both arise together in a single moment, two chicks born together, two sides of one coin.
- **Past and present are built together, and both are false.** "I didn't know before, now I know" is an utter lie, because the not-known-before and the now-known arise together; the past is built in the present, and the present is built because the past was built — the two conditioning each other (*anyonya*), the "not"-condition conditioning the "is"-condition and vice versa. So the present is made real by first fabricating "before it wasn't felt" and then, relative to it, "now it is felt," and both the present and the past are false, seen so with the very eye.
- **No standing in the three times.** This moment cannot be obtained from a past, so no future can be built from it either; a future is built only from a past, and if past and present are false there is no future at all. The present is the *middle* of time, the past one extreme (a dead story) and the future the other (an unborn story, itself as good as dead); with both sides dead-like and neither obtained, no middle is obtained — one finds no place to stand in any of the three times.
- **The past is fabricated this instant.** As soon as "I take a breath now" is thought, the present forms, and from it the past is made — "I existed before, breathing, but did not feel it" — even a "board" being nailed on: "before, I was un-mindful because I had not set my mind." The present-me and the past-me are both made in one instant; the never-experienced story "the not-knowing wasn't there before" is so powerful that it fabricates a nonexistent me, a nonexistent feeling and a whole persistence ("I existed from morning").
- **Persistence is built from not-knowing.** A beautiful "persistence" from before until now is built out of not-knowing — that is, out of being in an unknowable, effortless nature and not knowing *that*. Lacking wisdom of that nature, "now feeling is real, before not-felt is real"; the unfelt-me existed and now the feeling-me exists, and "I set up mindfulness, so now I feel."
- **Mindfulness and un-mindfulness are two pieces of one folly.** *Sati* and *asati*, as ordinarily spoken of, are both born of ignorance — *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* — not the real mindfulness. To hide the earlier folly we bang the "un-mindful" label onto the fabricated past ("I was un-mindful then, thinking of other things") and the "mindful" label onto the fabricated present ("now mindfulness has arisen in me"); the thinking-place is called *sati* and the not-thinking-place *asati*. Both are seen only by one who wholly transcends them — seen to arise conditioned by not-knowing.
- **Seeing ignorance, true knowing arises.** To one who sees *avijjā*, *vijjā* arises: he sees the entire delusion of "this exists / is felt / is heard." Folly only saw with folly; nothing actually happened to hearing or seeing here — folly saw the very river-water it swam in. Ignorance is the bottom layer on which the whole edifice stands, and seeing this, one sees across the three times that the entire believed-in world was built upon not-knowing.
- **Selfhood is really not-self; permanence is really impermanence.** From morning till sleep every action builds this false "me"; rightly seen, selfhood (*ātmīya-bhāva*) shows itself as not-self (*anātma*). We keep making it permanent — "though unfelt, the breath was there, I existed" — and that *nitya-saññā* rests on "the breath-unfelt me continued from morning." Since this persistence is built from not-knowing, seeing the not-knowing lets impermanence (*anicca*) be understood of itself.
- **Even "emptiness" is a designation.** *Suññatā* too is a *prajñapti*: what is designated "empty"? We grasp never-obtained things — a never-obtained eye called impermanent, a never-obtained ear called impermanent — but once the not-obtained is seen, there is nothing to call permanent or impermanent. A thousand suttas and all the known dhamma could never *show* this here; where would one even think it? A little flash of light passes and "it is like this" is seen — and though this cannot be done merely by hearing a sermon, and needs such a discussion, it is nonetheless something doable in life.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue begins by analysing contact as comparison. Attention is never noticed on its own — only after intention arises — and attention, intention and contact effectively occur together, contact being "the thing thought" and the very enabler of attention, since attention is the comparing of two things. Contact, then, is the presence of two: one cannot think "tall" without something shorter, two must exist to be compared, and that two-ness is contact, the place where two touch, while attention merely pronounces "tall" or "short." Carried into experience, "it is audible now" is known only against "it wasn't audible before," and "breath is felt now" only against "it wasn't felt before" — the present asserted always against a *not*-condition. The teacher is careful to say this "not" is not itself ignorance; we merely *think* "the not." And crucially, the "not" that conditions the present is not the pure first state but a second moment: from morning there was no story about breathing at all, not even an "I don't know," the not-knowing itself being unknown, and only when it is mentioned does one instantly say "now it is felt," the "wasn't felt before" and the "felt now" arising together in a single moment like two sides of one coin.

From this the whole architecture of time collapses. "I didn't know before, now I know" is an utter lie, because the not-known-before and the now-known arise together; the past is built in the present, and the present is built because a past was built, the two mutually conditioning each other so that a past is drawn from the present and a present from the past. Both are therefore false, seen so with the very eye — and if it does not stand here, it stands nowhere: this moment cannot be obtained from a past, so no future can be built from it, a future being drawn only from a past, and if past and present are false there is no future at all. The present is the middle of time, the past one extreme and the future the other; but the past is a dead story and the future an unborn one, itself as good as dead, so with both sides dead-like and neither obtained, no middle is obtained either. The past, in truth, is fabricated this very instant: the moment "I take a breath now" is thought, the present forms, and from it the past — "I existed before, breathing, but did not feel it" — is made, even nailing on the label "I was un-mindful because I hadn't set my mind." The present-me and the past-me are both made in one instant, and the never-experienced story "the not-knowing wasn't there before" is so powerful that it fabricates a nonexistent me, a nonexistent feeling and a whole persistence "from morning."

That persistence, the teacher shows, is built entirely out of not-knowing — out of being in an unknowable, effortless nature and not knowing *that*. Lacking wisdom of it, one takes "now feeling is real, before not-felt is real," so that an unfelt-me existed and now a feeling-me exists, and "I set up mindfulness, so now I feel." But mindfulness and un-mindfulness, as ordinarily spoken of, are two pieces of the very same folly — *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* — not the real mindfulness at all: to conceal the earlier folly we bang "un-mindful" onto the fabricated past and "mindful" onto the fabricated present, calling the thinking-place *sati* and the not-thinking-place *asati*, and both are seen for what they are only by one who wholly transcends them, arising as they do conditioned by not-knowing. To such a one who sees ignorance, true knowing arises, and with it the entire delusion of "this exists, is felt, is heard" — for folly only ever saw with folly and nothing actually happened to hearing or seeing, ignorance being the bottom layer on which the whole edifice was raised; seeing this, one sees across the three times that the whole believed-in world was built upon not-knowing.

The talk closes on self, permanence and emptiness. From morning till sleep every action builds this false me, yet rightly seen, selfhood shows itself as not-self; and we keep making it permanent — "though unfelt, the breath was there, I existed" — a permanence-perception resting wholly on "the breath-unfelt me continued from morning." Since that persistence is built from not-knowing, seeing the not-knowing lets impermanence be understood of itself. Even "emptiness" is only a designation, for we grasp never-obtained things — a never-obtained eye called impermanent, a never-obtained ear called impermanent — whereas once the not-obtained is seen there is nothing left to call permanent or impermanent. And a thousand suttas or all the dhamma one knows could never show this here; where would one even think it? Only a little flash of light passes and "it is like this" is seen — something that cannot be done by merely hearing a sermon, that needs exactly such a living discussion, and yet is genuinely doable in life.
