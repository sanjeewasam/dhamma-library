## Sutta referred to
- **Cūḷasuññata Sutta** (චූලසුඤ්ඤත සූත්‍රය) — named by the questioner and quoted: *ye hi keci ānanda … imaṁ eva pārisuddhaṁ paramānuttaraṁ suññataṁ* and *paramānuttaraṁ suññataṁ upasampajja vihareyya … evañhi vo ānanda sikkhitabbaṁ* ("this very purified supreme emptiness … one should enter upon and dwell in supreme emptiness … thus, Ānanda, should you train").
- **Ānāpānasati Sutta** (ආනාපානසති සූත්‍රය) — named: the path from *kāyānupassanā* to *vedanānupassanā* to *aniccānupassanā*, "seeing nibbāna right here."
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *(saḷ)āyatanaṁ veditabbaṁ* ("the six bases should be known"); *manopubbaṅgamā dhammā, manoseṭṭhā manomayā* ("mind precedes all things, mind is their chief, they are mind-made"); the proliferation sequence *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti, yaṁ sañjānāti taṁ vitakketi, yaṁ vitakketi taṁ papañceti … papañca-saññā-saṅkhā samudācaranti* ("what one feels one perceives … what one thinks of one proliferates … perceptions born of proliferation assail one"); and the jhāna formula *savitakkaṁ savicāraṁ vivekajaṁ pītisukhaṁ* / *avitakkaṁ avicāraṁ*.
- Terms used: *avijjā* / *mūla-moha* (ignorance / root delusion), *viññāta* and *diṭṭha-suta-muta* (cognized / seen-heard-sensed), *cetanā*, *manasikāra*, *saḷāyatana* (six sense-bases), *paramānuttara-suññatā* (supreme emptiness), *kāya- / vacī- / mano- / citta-saṅkhāra*, *vedanā* / *saññā*, *papañca* (proliferation), *uccheda* / *sassata* (annihilationism / eternalism), *atthi* / *natthi* (is / is-not), *nirodha*, *jhāna* / *vipassanā*, *brahma-loka*. No further sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- A labelled question-and-answer session on the nature of ignorance itself, the meaning of supreme emptiness, and — at length — how meditators mistake the "breath gone un-felt" state for cessation and fall into the annihilationist extreme.
- **Ignorance is beyond even "cognizing."** The un-knowable nature is not the "not-felt" (which is merely the sensed, *muta*), nor is it *viññāta* — for cognizing, like the seen-heard-sensed, is itself known by intention. It is none of the four; the nature that can never even become *aware* of those four is what is called ignorance — better rendered as "folly" (*mūla-moha*), for clarity. Where cognizing operates there is awareness; folly is the not-knowing of any of them, a place where not even a mind can be spoken of, pure root delusion in which no such "nature" stands.
- **It cannot be put into words.** Any word builds awareness again — a cognizing arises the instant one tries to say it — and this is precisely the six sense-bases (*saḷāyatana*). The Buddha speaks of the wisdom that sees *beyond* all six bases, a place one can operate from beyond them, transcending both thinking and not-thinking; and the "not-thinking place" is itself formed only by thinking, two sides of one coin, so that to "see a not-thinking place" is something the mind can never do.
- **Only a handling, not a grasping.** There is an awareness even of the not-thinking nature, but only a *using* (*parihāraṇa*) — with no grasping of "exists / does not exist," no extremes. From morning the breathing and the working mind carry no awareness of breath, yet within that non-awareness a *functioning* mindfulness operates; this is not the ordinary person's thought-knowing ("I am bathing, sitting, walking"), and it is upon that un-knowable nature that wisdom works. It cannot even be lodged at "a feeling," for a feeling is still a mind-felt level, and there not even a bare feeling-perception can arise: say "breath" and one cannot catch that breath exists even as a feeling, nor think "before there was no breath."
- **The supreme emptiness is a nature, not a "thing."** Citing the *Cūḷasuññata Sutta*, the teacher agrees this *is* the supreme emptiness — but not a "there is": only the nature, no person, no one who feels it, no one who understands; having reached it, "they dwelt" (*vihariṁsu*). Emptiness cannot be a persisting thing, being a non-standing nature; to say it "exists" is slightly wrong, for a non-standing has no persistence — it is a wisdom-level in which one "dwells."
- **Breath rests on the perception of feeling.** In *ānāpānasati* the mind is placed on the breath, but what is watched is the *feeling*, and "breath exists" is asserted on the basis of feeling — though feeling is not the breath. Feeling arises from the attention-state; it is the action, the intention, and the intention it produces is "in-breath, out-breath" — all of it intention, formation. So breath is a world-phenomenon, an arisen-not-arisen nature, and "I take breath, I do it" is to be seen as *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*, a formation: this is "all formations" — *kāya-*, *vacī-*, *mano-*, *citta-saṅkhāra*. Even "felt by the body" is made by mind, *manopubbaṅgamā dhammā*; in-and-out breath is the bodily formation, while feeling and perception are the mental formation.
- **The proliferation sequence and the dog in the water.** Feeling the breath, perception recognizes "long / short": *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti … yaṁ vitakketi taṁ papañceti … papañca-saññā-saṅkhā samudācaranti* — the proliferation-perceptions come to control the person. As the dog deceived by its reflection, taking the "dog" formation to be real, then grows angry, runs and barks — the formation overwhelming it — so we dance to whatever tune the formation plays, controlled by a single fabricated form, a lie played out, unable to see it as a delusion or a dream.
- **The meditator's trap: mistaking the un-felt breath for cessation.** Watching "breath, breath, breath," after a while it is not felt; we think "the feeling I had has vanished." But the breath did not vanish — one has simply arrived at the nature one is always in, a nature of not-understanding feeling, a not-knowing, and we call it *samādhi*. There one falls into annihilationism: caught in the "not" (no breath, no feeling), the one who does not recognize it calls it "the absence," and this is how one goes to the brahma-worlds. The catch is only "the existing thing became absent"; but beneath it lies the deeper layer — not knowing that this *is* the never-knowable nature, ignorance gets caught in "the thing became absent," and there one thinks "nirodha."
- **The two layers.** The *first* layer is *atthi / natthi* — existent-becoming and non-existent-becoming — which is eternalism: one who has not seen dwells here in annihilation, the existing breath vanishing as it is watched, taken for cessation. The *second* layer, the one under discussion, is where one *cannot* say "it is not," because there is no "me" there and no intention arises — a "not" would arise only if intention did; yet the meditator, deceived, calls it "not" and slides into annihilationism, supposing the "breath" perception has ceased, when in truth, adhering to his meditation, he has merely lost the ability to watch the breath.
- **Comprehension versus jhāna-adherence.** Seen instead with comprehension, that nature can remain unchanged even with the eyes closed, the mind resting in the un-knowable nature — for where the nature is caught by wisdom it does not slide into "the existing thing vanished," and one need not take the breath, watch it and hold it a while to reach a "not"; both the not-nature and the existing-nature are un-caught and ever-present. In *ānāpānasati* one does not really "meditate"; what has happened is only an annihilationist catching.
- **The phone in two hands.** A phone in one hand and none in the other: looking, one says "the other hand has no phone" — but only by thinking of the hand that *had* one; of the ever-empty hand one cannot even say "no phone," since nothing was ever there. Remove the right-hand phone and both hands look the same, yet one can still speak of the right hand's *absence* — but of the left hand, the deepest layer, one cannot say "there is an absence," for the very story of "existing" was never born there. The right hand holds "a present thing gone absent," and it is to this right-hand nature that *samādhi* goes; the left-hand nature is where "existing" never arose at all.
- **Doubt about jhāna and vipassanā.** The teacher declines to affirm the common claim that one can examine subtle points inside jhāna, not having cultivated it, but questions it: *savitakkaṁ savicāraṁ vivekajaṁ pītisukhaṁ* — in-and-out breath is itself the thinking-and-examining that runs automatically, so how would one do insight amid it in the first jhāna, and the next stage is *avitakkaṁ avicāraṁ*, without thinking or examining, where one cannot go to emptiness either. Such conversation, he closes, brings a purification at some level — like a hoe-handle wearing down, unseen at the moment but found worn after a time.

## Summary

This labelled question-and-answer session, between a questioner and the teacher, begins by trying to locate ignorance itself. The un-knowable nature, the teacher says, is not the "not-felt" — which is merely the sensed — and not even cognizing, since cognizing, like the seen-heard-sensed, is itself known by intention; it is none of the four, and the nature that can never even become aware of those four is what is called ignorance, better named "folly" or root delusion. Where cognizing operates there is awareness, but folly is the not-knowing of any of them, a place where not even a mind can be spoken of, and no "nature" stands there at all. It cannot be put into words, for any word builds awareness again and a cognizing arises the instant one tries to speak it — which is precisely the six sense-bases; the Buddha speaks of a wisdom that sees beyond all six, transcending both thinking and not-thinking, and since the "not-thinking place" is itself formed only by thinking, to "see" it is something the mind can never do. There is an awareness even of that not-thinking nature, but only a handling of it, with no grasping of "exists" or "does not exist"; from morning the breathing and the working mind carry no awareness of breath, yet within that non-awareness a functioning mindfulness operates, and it is upon this un-knowable nature — not on the ordinary "I am sitting, walking" knowings — that wisdom works. It cannot even be lodged at "a feeling," for there not even a bare feeling-perception can arise.

Turning to the supreme emptiness, the questioner cites the *Cūḷasuññata Sutta*, and the teacher agrees that this is indeed the supreme emptiness — but not a "there is," only the nature, with no person, no one who feels it and no one who understands; having reached it, "they dwelt." Emptiness cannot be a persisting thing, being a non-standing nature, so to say it "exists" is already slightly wrong: it is a wisdom-level in which one dwells, not something that persists. From here the discussion moves to *ānāpānasati*. In watching the breath what is really watched is the *feeling*, and "breath exists" is asserted on the basis of feeling, though feeling is not the breath; feeling arises from the attention-state, is itself the action and the intention, and the intention it produces is "in-breath, out-breath" — all of it formation. So breath is a world-phenomenon, an arisen-not-arisen nature, and "I take breath, I do it" must be seen as ignorance-conditioned formation, one of the *kāya-*, *vacī-*, *mano-* and *citta-saṅkhāra*, even "felt by the body" being mind-made, for mind precedes all things. Feeling the breath and recognizing "long or short," the whole proliferation sequence unfolds — *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti … papañca-saññā-saṅkhā samudācaranti* — until the proliferation-perceptions come to control the person, exactly as the dog deceived by its reflection grows angry and runs and barks, overwhelmed by the fabricated form; and confidence in the Buddha's wisdom arises precisely from seeing this.

The heart of the session is the meditator's trap. Watching "breath, breath, breath," after a while it is not felt, and one thinks the feeling has vanished — but the breath did not vanish; one has merely arrived at the nature one is always in, a nature of not-understanding, a not-knowing, which we mistake for samādhi. There one falls into annihilationism, caught in the "not" of no breath and no feeling, and this is how one goes to the brahma-worlds; the catch is only "the existing thing became absent," and beneath it, not knowing that this is the never-knowable nature, ignorance takes it for cessation. The teacher lays out two layers: the first is *atthi / natthi*, existent- and non-existent-becoming, which is eternalism, where one who has not seen dwells in annihilation as the watched breath fades; the second, the layer under discussion, is where one *cannot* say "it is not," because there is no "me" and no intention there, a "not" arising only if intention did — yet the deceived meditator calls it "not" and, adhering to his meditation, has merely lost the ability to watch the breath. Seen instead with comprehension, that nature can remain unchanged even with eyes closed, the mind resting in the un-knowable, not sliding into "the existing thing vanished," and needing no technique of taking and holding the breath to reach a "not," since both the not-nature and the existing-nature are un-caught and ever-present.

The teacher illustrates with a phone held in one hand and absent from the other: one says "the other hand has no phone," but only by thinking of the hand that had one; of the ever-empty hand one cannot even say "no phone," since nothing was ever there. Remove the phone and both hands look alike, yet one can still speak of the right hand's *absence* — but of the left hand, the deepest layer, one cannot say "there is an absence," for the very story of "existing" was never born there. It is to this right-hand nature, "a present thing gone absent," that samādhi goes, whereas the left-hand nature is where "existing" never arose at all. Finally, pressed on whether insight can be done within jhāna, the teacher declines to affirm it, not having cultivated jhāna, but doubts the common claim: the in-and-out breath is itself the thinking-and-examining of the first jhāna that runs automatically, so how would one do insight amid it, and the next stage removes thinking and examining altogether, leaving no way to "go to emptiness." Such conversation, he ends, brings a purification at some level — like a hoe-handle wearing down, unseen in the moment but found worn after a time.
