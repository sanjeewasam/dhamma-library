## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue that walks the whole chain of dependent origination link by link, using the mirror to establish that cause and fruit arise *together*, and showing ignorance and contact operating at every link.
- **The mirror: cause and fruit arise together.** A mirror sitting there cannot be called "a mirror"; we distinguish it from plain glass only because it has the ability to form a reflection — yet that ability does not reside *in* the mirror, for a reflection arises only when a form comes before it, and the mirror becomes "a mirror" only at the moment a reflection falls. No words convince someone who doesn't know; only *seeing* a reflection form verifies "this is a mirror." So the cause (mirror) and the fruit (reflection) are not first-then-after: the very meaning "mirror" arises together with the reflection.
- **The ability is nowhere.** Alone, the mirror cannot produce a reflection; the form alone cannot either; the ability was in neither the mirror, nor the form, nor the reflection, but arose only when all three met at one moment. Where was it? Nowhere — it arose with the cause and the fruit together. This is *phassa*: the nature arising where the several meet, existing nowhere.
- **Contact cannot be located, and consciousness alone cannot make it.** Some confine contact to consciousness ("consciousness grasps it"), but contact needs two — eye and form — so it cannot be pointed to "here"; it is precisely the "cause of the two bamboo-bundles," a cause that exists nowhere. The "coming-together" itself cannot be pinpointed anywhere, and one who sees this contact ends saṁsāra, for saṁsāra is built entirely on a never-obtained cause — the very meeting that is not found when checked for its existence or non-existence.
- **Neither extreme, hence "paccayā."** One cannot say the reflection was "in the form," nor "in the mirror," nor that it "arose by itself"; when the two causes fail, one must not jump to a third ("uncaused" / "spontaneous"). It is neither *in* the causes nor *without* causes — and this is exactly why the word is *paccayā* ("conditioned by / because of"), not "contained in."
- **Ignorance and contact at every link.** Walking the chain: *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* — by ignorance a contact occurs and a formation forms, the two mutually conditioning; we think only through not-knowing, grasping "before it wasn't felt" because we do not know that the not-felt extreme was never obtained. *Saṅkhāra-paccayā viññāṇaṁ* — because a formation is not known *as* a formation, a little set of form-feeling-perception-formation is taken "as existing," and from that existence-grasping consciousness arises; the existence-grasping is the eternalist side, and because we do not know the annihilation side, we said "we knew a feeling."
- **Both extremes in one instant, everywhere.** Taking "nose and breath exist" is asserted only against "before it wasn't," so the "not" is born the very instant of thinking; if a formation forms, both extremes are already formed there, not separately but in one place. Thus at every *paccayā* the *atthi / natthi* pair conditions the forward movement, and within each born nature the two extremes are "handled" — the "not" side hiding within the "is" side.
- **Consciousness broken open: only the aggregates.** *Viññāṇa-paccayā nāma-rūpa* — inside consciousness too are conditioned form, feeling, perception and formations: "nose" appears as a form, a feeling connected to it, a set of perceptions to recognize it (long / short / thick / fine), and the formation-set of "all knowledge about the nose." Broken open, consciousness holds just this — a form, a feeling, a perception, a formation-set — which we call "a five-aggregate bundle assembled," all taken "as existing," which is the *paṭiccasamuppāda*'s way of grasping-as-existent.
- **Nāma-rūpa and designation.** The nose (a form) is described only through shape and colour — that is, feeling and perception describe a form (*rūppana*) — so it is really a *nominal* form (*nāmika-rūpa*), designated (*adhivacana*) by the mental factors, though we split it into "form-aggregate" and "name-aggregate." A chair is described by shape and colour, but remove shape and colour and where is the chair? The chair is real because of shape-and-colour, and shape-and-colour real because of the chair — struck to both sides, covered, unfindable, like hen and egg: searching one throws you to the other and the knot never unties. So one fabricates a demon and fears the demon — a mind-made fear.
- **Saḷāyatana and physical contact.** *Nāma-rūpa-paccayā saḷāyatana* — that very nāma-rūpa-set is what we are deceived into calling the six bases: "there is a nose, wind strikes it, a wind-current comes." *Saḷāyatana-paccayā phasso* — where a base is taken, "because of eye and form, seeing," or here "because of nose and breath, feeling"; and we further posit a *physical* contact ("the wind-current really struck my nose"), though the whole thing was a consciousness-level event and no contact was ever obtained. As with the two bamboo-bundles, nose-and-breath are merely why we think "breath was felt," and because "felt" we then tell the story of nose and form.
- **From feeling to becoming.** *Phassa-paccayā vedanā* — the feeling; and because of the feeling we speak of "nose and breath." *Vedanā-paccayā taṇhā* — craving arises for the felt feeling (subtle, pleasant, or rough → attachment or aversion); but what is really craved is the *me*, the "me" story having arisen from "it is felt *by me*." *Taṇhā-paccayā upādāna* — craving intensified into clinging, the clinging being why "I exist" is understood. *Upādāna-paccayā bhava* — becoming is taking "as existing" (one could as well take "as not-existing"). *Bhava-paccayā jāti-jarā-maraṇa* — once becoming occurs, birth, decay and death follow inevitably.
- **The whole chain runs on comparison of a never-obtained contact.** Craving, clinging and becoming each proceed by the *atthi / natthi* pair; a contact is engaged at every link, two being compared and the story carried forward — a base understood, so a nose and a wind-current felt by it; two understood, so feeling made real; feeling real, so craved; craved, so clung; clung, so "I exist." We crave a never-obtained contact, take it as feeling, and freedom lies in seeing that it was never obtained.
- **Touching the table.** Placing a hand on the table, one had no prior notion "there is a still hand here," nor "I have two hands." It is the *action* of touching that makes "something exists" understood, and that understanding is what makes "a hand exists on this side" understood — both formed from the one action, exactly as the reflection-moment makes mirror and form arise together; the hand and the table are born together from "touching." *(The transcript breaks off here.)*

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue, preserved as a fragment that opens mid-conversation and breaks off at "[TO BE CONTINUED]," walks the entire chain of dependent origination, and it does so by first establishing, through the mirror, that cause and fruit arise together. A mirror sitting there cannot be called "a mirror," and we distinguish it from plain glass only by its ability to form a reflection — yet that ability does not reside in the mirror, since a reflection arises only when a form comes before it, so the mirror becomes "a mirror" only at the instant a reflection falls, and no words, only the seeing of a reflection, can verify it. The cause and the fruit are therefore not first-and-after: the very meaning "mirror" arises together with the reflection. Pressing further, the teacher shows the reflection-ability was in neither the mirror, nor the form, nor the reflection, but arose only when all three met at one moment — nowhere at all, arising with cause and fruit together. This is contact: the nature arising where several meet while existing nowhere, which cannot be confined to consciousness (since contact needs two, eye and form), cannot be pointed to "here," and is precisely the un-locatable "cause of the two bamboo-bundles." One who sees this contact ends saṁsāra, for saṁsāra is built entirely on a never-obtained cause; and because the reflection is neither *in* the causes nor *without* causes, the word is rightly *paccayā*, "conditioned by," not "contained in."

From here the chain is walked with ignorance and contact found at every link. By ignorance a contact occurs and a formation forms, the two mutually conditioning, and we think only through not-knowing — grasping "before it wasn't felt" precisely because we do not know that the not-felt extreme was never obtained. Because a formation is not known as a formation, a little set of form, feeling, perception and formation is taken "as existing," and from that existence-grasping consciousness arises; the existence-grasping is the eternalist side, and not knowing the annihilation side, we say "we knew a feeling." The two extremes are born together in one instant: "nose and breath exist" is asserted only against "before it wasn't," so the "not" is born the very moment of thinking, and if a formation forms both extremes are already there in one place, the "not" hiding within the "is." Thus at every *paccayā* the *atthi / natthi* pair conditions the forward movement, and within each born nature the two extremes are handled.

Broken open, consciousness is found to hold only conditioned form, feeling, perception and formations: "nose" as a form, a feeling attached to it, perceptions to recognize it, and the formation-set of all knowledge about it — which we call a five-aggregate bundle "assembled," all taken as existing. The nose is described only through shape and colour, so it is a merely nominal form designated by the mental factors, though we split it into form-aggregate and name-aggregate; and as with a chair, which is real because of shape-and-colour and shape-and-colour real because of the chair, the two are struck together, covered and unfindable, like hen and egg whose knot never unties — so one fabricates a demon and then fears it. That very nāma-rūpa-set is what we are deceived into calling the six bases, saying "there is a nose, wind strikes it"; where a base is taken, "because of nose and breath, feeling" is posited, and we go further to imagine a physical contact — the wind-current really striking the nose — though the whole event was consciousness-level and no contact was ever obtained.

The chain then runs to its end: the felt feeling makes us speak of nose and breath; craving arises for the feeling, but what is really craved is the "me," born of "it is felt *by me*"; craving intensifies into clinging, which is why "I exist" is understood; clinging becomes "taking as existing," which is becoming; and once becoming occurs, birth, decay and death follow inevitably. Craving, clinging and becoming each proceed by the *atthi / natthi* pair, a contact engaged at every link, two compared and the story carried forward — a base understood, so a nose and its wind-current; two understood, so feeling made real; feeling real, so craved; craved, so clung; clung, so "I." We crave a never-obtained contact and take it as feeling, and the whole knot loosens only in seeing that it was never obtained. The teacher closes — before the transcript breaks off — with the table: placing a hand on it, one had no prior notion of "a hand here," and it is the very action of touching that makes both "something exists" and "a hand exists on this side" understood, both born together from the one action, exactly as the reflection-moment makes mirror and form arise together.
