- A **Majjhima discourse** — referred to (as "the Majjhima sutta," no title given): the statement that "*phassa* is one extreme, *phassa-samudaya* is one extreme, *phassa-nirodha* is the middle," and "seeing contact as contact, contact ceases."

## Main dhamma points
- A long two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue centred on the floor-tile gap as an image for contact, showing why the true nature of consciousness is beyond consciousness, why "because of" (*paccayā*) must be seen without falling to either extreme, and how meditators mistake the object-suppressed state for cessation.
- **Consciousness's true nature is beyond consciousness.** Its real nature — the equanimity middle-state — can never be known by consciousness, because consciousness *is* knowing; so one cannot think one's way to it, and only a good friend can point it out. This is why the good friend is "one hundred percent": alone it cannot be done, and it can be shown only to one who has already seen it. Transcending consciousness does not mean a new consciousness arises — the work is done with this very consciousness — and it calls for fewness of wishes (*alpicchatā*) and energy (*viriya*): great attachment to the five sense-cords scatters the mind, and an agitated mind, forever planning what next to eat, wear or watch, can never let this wisdom establish; a non-agitated mind is the equanimity itself.
- **"Because of" (paccayā) is the knot falling forward.** Analysing formations or aggregates yields no inner analysis — "aggregate" has no meaning within itself, its meaning being another idea. Asked where he lives, one says "Mīṭiyāgoḍa"; not known, he offers "Ambalangoḍa," then "the Galle district" — to show one place he fetches another, "poking sticks to find the heartwood," each attention failing and reaching for the next: this is *paccayā*, the knot falling forward. Likewise "addition" is meaning-empty — "five" is not in "twenty-three," and seeking the meaning of "addition" itself, thought fails; consciousness can only answer "two, three, five," because contact is outside its range.
- **Contact is un-graspable, like sleep.** The act itself is never caught: we know "going" only as going, cannot count our blinks, do not notice the limbs bending or the sitting-sensation or the breath — everywhere none of it is understood, as in the "defilement-sleep" into which beings are always fallen. Nothing is understood now and nothing asleep, so the supposed "now it is understood" is itself unfounded.
- **The graspable 180 and the un-graspable 180.** To see the fault of the graspable "there is a nose and breath," one must see the un-graspable side where neither feeling nor non-feeling applies; and seeing that, one sees the other extreme too — that feeling cannot be obtained *without* nose and breath. So it is "neither because of the two, nor without the two," and only one who sees both together — *imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti* together with *imasmiṁ asati idaṁ na hoti* — gains the knowledge of rise-and-fall (*udayabbaya-ñāṇa*); the ordinary mind sees only the arising side.
- **Do not force-fit the analogy onto suttas.** Knowing suttas by number and fitting a story to them only breeds the habit of comparison; comprehension must be directly verified, not fabricated by fitting a formula. If the principle is truly seen, it is seen everywhere.
- **The tile-gap.** Between two floor-tiles there is a formed line, but it belongs to neither tile — and seeing "the causes do not own the effect," one slides implicitly to "the line can exist *without* the two tiles," a separate independent line, just as "no chair here" implies "a chair somewhere else." This is why a Majjhima discourse calls it: *phassa* one extreme, *phassa-samudaya* one extreme, *phassa-nirodha* the middle — "seeing contact as contact, contact ceases." Asked about it, the Buddha said "do not think too much of that" — which the teacher notes is also the cunning way people hide their own inability, saving their skin, the answer consciousness gives.
- **Suppressing the object cannot reveal contact.** Remove the tiles and the line cannot be found; likewise meditation that suppresses the object makes contact un-seeable, so one thinks "no feeling" and takes it for cessation. The un-understood place is exactly where wisdom must establish, yet the worldling takes it for *nirodha* — the object-suppressed state feeling floaty and luminous, like absorption. The difference: this path does not suppress a risen object but sees the *non-arising* of any object, so that neither "hold an object" nor "release an object" applies.
- **The action is impossible — hence no striving, no standing.** If the whole train-carriage is already booked, one cannot "book" it by force — the action itself is impossible, and that impossibility is the knowledge to be established in; as long as one holds the action to be real and possible, a result and kamma form for it, but where it cannot be done even by force, no kamma forms, no falling back to the woeful states. Nor need one think "today I abandon booking," for where the act is verified impossible "not-doing" does not apply either — like telling one who cannot climb a tree "I won't climb today but will tomorrow." So the flood is crossed "neither standing nor striving": where nothing is caught, there is nothing to grasp, strive for, stand on, or escape — both questions are wrong, and wisdom is right at the "cannot" place. Meditators, by contrast, do both — standing and striving.
- **The line seen as the line; causes arise from a non-arisen place.** The "gap" is really a space we have named "line," in which "tile" was never engaged; seeing the line as line is neither "because of the two tiles" nor "without them," yet a wisdom stands right there — "causes arise from a non-arisen place." Contact is precisely the comprehension that the causes are un-arisen, so that *samudaya* and *nirodha* are both realized from the same point: at the non-arising, both origin and cessation are seen. As saying "light" takes a never-born darkness through "absence" to assert "presence," one must see that "darkness" itself cannot arise — realized only where wisdom establishes. To teach "the causes are un-arisen" one must borrow never-arisen causes (the two un-engaged tiles, the absent darkness), and to one who sees contact, "tile" is verified as a never-arisen cause.
- **The eye-and-form formula.** We live in *cakkhuñ ca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu-viññāṇaṁ, tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, but must look at neither eye nor form — only at how "seeing" got posited at the "dependent-upon" point. *Paṭicca* does not mean "where an eye or a form exists"; it is taken from a nature in which a "two-tiles story" never arose, and because the non-arising nature of contact is beyond worldling consciousness, we posit "seeing" at the *paccayā*. "Addition" is a meaning-empty cause, and giving meaning to a meaningless place produces "five" — a fruit absent in both causes, connected to neither, an unconnected union (*asambinna*), a baseless split; asked "did five come from two or three?", neither — so we make a jumble, joining by *paccayā* two things impossible to join.
- **Saṁsāra and nibbāna from one place; birth un-obtainable.** Consciousness arises continually by this dependent knot, but one who sees the dependent story realizes the knot *as* a knot — so the same point is a bridge to nibbāna for one and a trap of saṁsāra for another, two people going two ways from one place. Hence the arahant eats, walks and comes and goes just as we do, the deeds identical, but for the worldling the action is real and yields result, while the wisdom of the action's non-establishment makes the very same a bridge. Birth rightly seen can be obtained by no one, for it is not-knowing birth that builds a being and a thing: "mother" arises only when "child is born," neither first, both together — as in "neither the same one nor another." Through the meeting-of-the-three "I" am born; at seeing the seer is born, at hearing the hearer, the heard thing obtained the instant the hearer is, and nothing beyond — a nature no one can *see*, since the "one" is itself born of this very story.
- **Hard for the unwise; independent of another.** *Evaṁ dhammo ayaṁ dhammo duppaññassa* — never within the ordinary mind's range, unthinkable alone without a Buddha or good friend, which is why the good friend is "number one, one hundred percent." Yet wisdom comes not by hearing, nor by presence, nor by the friend's showing, nor by dhamma-hearing alone, but arises conditioned by these two as cause; once arisen it has no nameable cause ("because so-and-so said it"), being independent of another (*aparappaccaya*), un-handed-over, arising only within oneself, and after arising not even "I" own it. The teacher adds he would welcome anyone finding a flaw or a "theft" in what he says, since he took it from no one and cannot say where it came from; the session closes with mutual refuge in the Triple Gem.

## Summary

This long two-speaker good-friend dialogue — assembled from segments, its speaker labels and timestamps shifting partway and a transcriber's note intruding at the seam — is built around the gap between two floor-tiles as an image for contact. It opens by establishing that the true nature of consciousness, the equanimity middle-state, can never be known by consciousness itself, since consciousness *is* knowing; one cannot therefore think one's way to it, and only a good friend can point it out, which is why the good friend is "one hundred percent" and why it can be shown only to one who has already seen it. Transcending consciousness is done with this very consciousness, and it demands fewness of wishes and energy, for great attachment to the five sense-cords keeps the mind scattered and forever planning, and only a non-agitated mind — the equanimity itself — lets this wisdom establish. The teacher then shows that "because of" (*paccayā*) is the knot falling forward: analysing formations yields no inner meaning, as "aggregate" has none within itself, and as, asked where he lives, one fetches "Ambalangoḍa" and then "Galle" to point at "Mīṭiyāgoḍa" — poking sticks to find a heartwood, each attention failing into the next. "Addition," likewise, is meaning-empty: "five" is nowhere in "twenty-three," and consciousness, to which contact is invisible, can only answer "two, three, five."

Contact, he stresses, is un-graspable, like the sleep beings are always fallen into: we know "going" only as going, cannot count our blinks, and never notice the breath or the sitting-body, so that nothing is truly understood now any more than in sleep. To see the fault of the graspable "there is a nose and breath," one must see the un-graspable side where neither feeling nor non-feeling applies, and seeing that, one also sees that feeling cannot be had *without* nose and breath — so it is neither because of the two nor without them, and only one who holds both sides together, "when this is, that is" with "when this is not, that is not," gains the knowledge of rise-and-fall. He warns firmly against force-fitting this onto memorized suttas, which only breeds the habit of comparison, since comprehension must be directly verified. The tile-gap then carries the argument: the line between two tiles belongs to neither, and seeing that, one slides implicitly to "the line exists without the tiles," a separate independent thing — just as "no chair here" implies "a chair elsewhere." This is why a Majjhima discourse names contact one extreme, its origin another, and its cessation the middle, "seeing contact as contact, contact ceases"; and the Buddha's "do not think too much of that" is, the teacher wryly notes, also the answer people give to hide their own inability.

He shows that suppressing the object — pulling out the tiles — can never reveal contact but only makes it unfindable, so the meditator, reaching the un-understood, floaty, luminous state, mistakes it for cessation, when that un-understood place is exactly where wisdom must establish; the difference is that this path does not suppress a risen object but sees the non-arising of any object, so that neither holding nor releasing an object applies. From here comes the decisive image of impossibility: if the whole carriage is already booked, one cannot book it by force — the action is simply impossible, and that impossibility is the knowledge to rest in, for as long as the action is held real a result and kamma form, but where it cannot be done even by force no kamma forms and there is no falling back; nor need one resolve "today I abandon it," since where the act is impossible "not-doing" is as empty as telling one who cannot climb a tree that he will climb tomorrow. So the flood is crossed neither standing nor striving: where nothing is caught there is nothing to grasp, strive for, stand on or escape, both questions wrong and wisdom right at the "cannot" place — whereas meditators do both. The line seen as line is neither because of the two tiles nor without them, yet a wisdom stands there — "causes arise from a non-arisen place" — so that origin and cessation are realized together at the non-arising, exactly as "light" borrows a never-born darkness through absence, and one must see that darkness itself cannot arise.

Applied to the eye-and-form formula, we live in "dependent on eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises; the meeting of the three is contact," yet must look at neither eye nor form but at how "seeing" got posited at the "dependent-upon" — for *paṭicca* is taken from a nature in which no two-tiles story ever arose, and because that non-arising is invisible to worldling consciousness we posit "seeing" there, just as giving meaning to the meaning-empty "addition" produces a "five" that belongs to neither two nor three, an unconnected union, a baseless join of two things impossible to join. Consciousness runs on continually by this dependent knot, but one who sees the dependent story sees the knot as a knot, so that the same point is a bridge to nibbāna for one and a trap of saṁsāra for another — the arahant eating and walking exactly as we do, the deeds identical, yet real and result-bearing for the worldling and a bridge for the wise. Birth rightly seen can be obtained by none, for "mother" arises only as "child is born," both together, neither first — "neither the same one nor another"; through the meeting-of-the-three "I" am born, the seer at seeing and the hearer at hearing, and no one can see this because the "one" is itself born of the story. Finally, this dhamma is "hard for the unwise," never within the ordinary mind's range, unthinkable without a Buddha or good friend — hence the good friend is number one — and yet wisdom comes not by hearing or presence or the friend's showing alone but arises conditioned by these, and once arisen has no nameable cause, being independent of another, un-transferable, arising only within, and owned by no "I" even after; the teacher would welcome anyone finding a flaw in what he says, having taken it from no one, and the session ends in mutual refuge in the Triple Gem.
