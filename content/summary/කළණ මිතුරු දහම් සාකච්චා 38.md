## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *cetanāhaṁ bhikkhave kammaṁ (vadāmi)* ("intention, monks, is what I call kamma"); and *puñña-pāpa-pahīnassa* ("for one who has abandoned merit and demerit").
- Terms used: *samudaya* (arising), *maññanā* (conceiving), *phassa* (contact — "ear-and-sound knotted"), *cetanā* (intention), *kamma*, *ariyakanta-sīla* (the virtue dear to the noble ones), *pañca-sīla* / *pāṇātipātā* (five precepts / abstaining from killing), *avijjā* (ignorance), *paramārtha-satya* (ultimate truth), *nyāya* (theory), *parihāraṇa* (handling). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on how one who has seen the vision keeps functioning normally while the inner knot ceases, and on how kamma arises from intention (the knotted "flower"), not from the bare action.
- **The doing does not stop; a wisdom comes.** With the vision, "this is how it happens" is known, the doing still happens, but no knot falls inside. Like a sewing machine: the top thread strikes for the ordinary man *and* for the arahant, but the ordinary man has thread in the inner shuttle — because he has not seen arising, a knot forms — whereas one who has seen the vision hears without the inner "because of ear-and-sound" knot falling; and as the vision clears, the knotting on this side gradually decreases, which is how the path develops, maturing exactly to the degree one can hold the mind on the vision.
- **The knot wears away like a hoe-handle.** Maintaining mindfulness of the Four Noble Truths wears down the knotting, not all at once, the non-arising nature establishing within cessation only at the very end; the more skilled one is at keeping this mindfulness, the faster the wear — as hoeing a little each month leaves no visible wear, yet after three to six months the grip is worn to the shape of five fingers, wear being greatest where handling is greatest.
- **The arahant's handling is unchanged.** Though the knot ceases, he still replies to sounds — he is not dumb to questions. Asked "did you hear?" he says "yes, heard"; he does not say "there is no ear, no sound, where did you make a sound?" His angle has merely changed. But the questioner asks from the "sound-comes-to-ear" view, while the one with vision knows "hearing" is only a thought, a *maññanā*, a meaning-empty knot; so "does the arahant then not hear?" is itself a foolish question asked from our side, irrelevant to that nature — like asking one in whom ear-and-sound never arise, "does sound reach your ear too?" The view and the vision never intersect, and no question of the view arises within the vision.
- **The flower-breaking analysis of kamma.** "I break" — what comes to mind? A flower, a branch, a leaf, beans? "Breaking" alone has no meaning; those things are what one has knotted onto it, and this knotting must be recognized *as* knotting. Is "breaking" then merit or demerit? Nothing is inside "breaking," so neither can come from it: breaking a flower secretly ("thief, tell no one") is demerit and breaking it openly to offer is merit — but not by the breaking-action, rather because "flower" was knotted, for without the flower knotted "breaking" forms no intention and has no meaning.
- **Merit and demerit are the intention, not the act.** Merit and demerit are our *cetanā*, and *cetanā* cannot form from bare "breaking"; only when "flower" is knotted does "I broke a flower" arise, splitting two ways — secret to demerit, offering to merit — and it splits by the *flower*, not the breaking. So in the world merit and demerit come not from the action but from the intention knotted to it: *cetanāhaṁ bhikkhave kammaṁ*. "Breaking" is the same for everyone (separating flower from stalk), pure and empty in itself, going to neither merit nor demerit.
- **The two extremes and the five precepts.** Knotting "flower" to "breaking" forms both extremes at once — "one can break it / one can leave it unbroken," the existence-and-non-existence of the doing — exactly as knotting "animals" yields "one can kill or not-kill." Kamma leaps from the knot: "killing is demerit," "not-killing is merit," and one undertakes not-killing — which is how the five precepts form, the precept itself being the knot (animals knotted → can kill → can undertake *pāṇātipātā*), the undertaking arising only upon this action, to be either kept or broken.
- **Both doing and not-doing are kamma; transcending them is the noble virtue.** Kamma forms whether one does or does not, both being kamma, and seeing that "doing and not-doing are both kamma" transcends action: then no "killing" forms and no "not-killing" is engaged, neither merit nor demerit is assembled — *puñña-pāpa-pahīnassa*. This is *ariyakanta-sīla*, the virtue that is automatic for one with the vision, who can neither break nor keep a precept because no killing-action was ever seen — as one looks in a mirror with no need to think "the mirror is alive or not," the work simply happening.
- **The tree, and dropping the theory itself.** A tree takes in carbon dioxide and gives off oxygen, yet accrues no kamma — the work happens, water and food are taken as in us, but the tree does not knot "I breathe"; we knot "breath" onto this and give it life. From morning we never once thought "I am breathing," yet did not die, so we live like the tree, not because we think "breath." We have merely knotted a narrative, built a "process," then built an interpretation and hung upon it — and that interpretation, called "ultimate truth" or "theory," is what one grasps with knowledge when told to be free of it. Asked "what is seeing?", one who has discerned it says "it is built from ignorance"; the tree recognizes no oxygen, and we invent the gases and a taking-releasing story, make a verse for the tree, give it words like "range, diversity, application," and, hanging on the words, think we have "seen" — reciting like parrots, memorizing prescriptions without going to the doctor for the illness. As one who has crossed the river needs neither water nor raft, the theory too must drop for one who sees rightly; one cannot stay inside the theory, like leftover medicine once the illness is cured — which is why people cannot simply "write it down," clarity having to grow.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue turns on two linked points: that one who has seen the vision keeps functioning normally while the inner knot ceases, and that kamma arises from intention rather than from the bare action. The doing does not stop, the teacher says; a wisdom comes that "this is how it happens," and the doing continues while no knot falls inside. He gives the sewing machine: the top thread strikes for the ordinary man and the arahant alike, but the ordinary man has thread in the inner shuttle — because he has not seen arising, a knot forms — whereas the one with vision hears without the inner "because of ear-and-sound" knot, and as the vision clears the knotting gradually lessens, which is how the path develops, maturing precisely to the degree one can hold the mind on the vision. This wearing-away is like a hoe-handle: hoeing a little each month shows no visible wear, yet after three to six months the grip is worn to the shape of five fingers, and wear is greatest where handling is greatest — so too the knot wears down as mindfulness of the Four Noble Truths is maintained, the non-arising nature establishing within cessation only at the very end.

Crucially, the arahant's handling is unchanged though the knot ceases: he still replies to sounds and is not struck dumb by questions. Asked "did you hear?" he says "yes, heard," never "there is no ear, no sound." His angle has merely changed, while the questioner still asks from the "sound-comes-to-ear" view, and the one with vision knows "hearing" is only a thought, a conceiving, a meaning-empty knot. So the very question "does the arahant then not hear?" is a foolish one asked from our side, irrelevant to that nature — like asking one in whom ear-and-sound never arise whether sound reaches his ear — and the view and the vision never intersect. From here the teacher develops his central analysis through the breaking of a flower: "I break" brings to mind a flower, a branch, a leaf — but "breaking" alone has no meaning, those things being knotted onto it, and this knotting must be seen as knotting. Is breaking merit or demerit? Nothing is inside it; breaking a flower secretly is demerit and breaking it openly to offer is merit, but not by the breaking — rather because "flower" was knotted, for without the flower knotted "breaking" forms no intention at all.

Merit and demerit, then, are the intention, not the act: intention cannot form from bare "breaking," and only when "flower" is knotted does "I broke a flower" arise, splitting into secret-demerit and offering-merit, and splitting by the flower, not the breaking — which is why *cetanāhaṁ bhikkhave kammaṁ*, intention being what is called kamma, the breaking itself pure and empty and going to neither side. Knotting "flower" to "breaking" forms both extremes at once — one can break it or leave it — exactly as knotting "animals" yields "one can kill or not-kill"; kamma leaps from the knot, "killing" becomes demerit and "not-killing" merit, and one undertakes not-killing, which is how the five precepts form, the precept itself being the knot and the undertaking arising only upon the action. But kamma forms whether one acts or not, both being kamma, and seeing that both are kamma transcends action: no "killing" forms, no "not-killing" is engaged, neither merit nor demerit is assembled — *puñña-pāpa-pahīnassa* — and this is the noble virtue, automatic for one with the vision, who can neither keep nor break a precept because no killing-action was ever seen, just as one looks in a mirror without deciding whether it is alive.

Finally the teacher returns to the tree: it takes in carbon dioxide and gives off oxygen yet accrues no kamma, the work simply happening, water and food taken as in us, but the tree never knots "I breathe" — whereas we knot "breath" onto this and give it life. From morning we never once thought "I am breathing," yet did not die, so we live like the tree and not because we think "breath"; we have merely knotted a narrative, built a "process," then an interpretation, and hung upon it — and that interpretation, dignified as "ultimate truth" or "theory," is exactly what one grasps with knowledge when told to be free of it. Asked what seeing is, one who has discerned it says it is built from ignorance; the tree recognizes no oxygen, and we invent the gases, make a verse for the tree, give it learned words, and recite them like parrots, memorizing prescriptions without ever going to the doctor for the illness. As one who has crossed the river needs neither water nor raft, so the theory itself must drop for one who sees rightly, like medicine left over once the illness is cured — which is why the seeing cannot simply be written down, and clarity must be allowed to grow.
