## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Reference (not a sutta): the story of **Dharmāśoka** (or "a certain king") who, despite offering temples and doing much meritorious work, held an aversion toward a certain woman and was reborn directly as a python.
- Terms used: *bhava-taṇhā* / *vibhava-taṇhā* (craving through existence / through non-existence), *taṇhā*, *nitya-saññā* (permanence-perception), *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen-heard-sensed-cognized), *moha* (delusion), *nirodha* (cessation), *uccheda-anta* (the annihilation extreme), *gōcara / agōcara* (within / beyond consciousness's range). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on the two forms of craving — through "existence" and through "non-existence" — showing that the strongest craving hides in the very things one never thinks about, and so cannot be removed by consciousness.
- **The strongest craving is for the "certain," unmoving thing.** What is "most certainly permanent"? The never-moving rock, taken as permanent through *absence*. The greatest attachment is to the unmoving, for being one-hundred-percent sure it will not move, one does not even think about it twice — and that unthinking certainty *is* the highest craving; where one is fully sure, no second thought is needed.
- **This craving is beyond consciousness's range.** Craving is not the attachment one feels; where one is unsure there is binding and one must think, but where one is fully sure the craving is present yet ungraspable, not within consciousness's range. One cannot imagine a craving for what one never even thinks about — yet that is exactly where the deepest craving hides, in the seen-heard-sensed-cognized: we do not think "I see, I hear," but leap straight to "chair, table," not even in words.
- **Only thinkable cravings can be removed.** "Remove craving and you are right" is said only of the graspable, thinkable cravings; the craving lodged in the unthought, permanent things cannot be removed. This is *vibhava-taṇhā*, craving established through "absence," which the *bhava-taṇhā* side can never reach or see, since consciousness cannot remove what lies beyond its range.
- **Dharmāśoka's aversion.** Despite offering temples and doing every meritorious work, a fine-level aversion toward a certain woman, fixed at the seen-sensed level, persisted and led to rebirth as a python; the seeing had become permanent, so the aversion toward that seen-object was never severed — because such craving cannot be removed by ordinary means, only the thinkable ones can.
- **The neighbour's fence.** One does not think about the neighbours because they cause no trouble and do not move the fence — "what craving could I have toward them?" — yet that very neutral, untroubled state hides a craving for "no sound, no trouble," an un-seeable, un-knowable craving that cannot be removed until the annihilation-extreme, the never-obtained "absent" side, is seen.
- **Permanence-perception cannot be known.** Once something is made permanent it can no longer be known, and the four root permanence-perceptions *are* the seen-heard-sensed-cognized. The best proof: we say "seeing, hearing," yet cannot analyse them — we can only analyse "a form" or "a sound," never the act of seeing or hearing itself; asked "what is hearing?", one cannot say and instead speaks of the sound. So it was never really understood, only thought to be — and this is why wisdom is needed to see it, groping in the dark, realizing right there that "an un-understandable nature is present."
- **The meditator's danger.** When wisdom settles on that permanence-perception at the un-understood place, one who does not know gets caught there and, through meditation, calls it "cessation"; but the one with wisdom knows that is the dangerous place — the delusion unseen, which is why saṁsāra runs. To see delusion *as* delusion cannot be done by consciousness: sitting and breathing, one does not feel it, does not sense where one is, does not know one is breathing, and only one who sees *that* un-understood place realizes the permanence-perception is unknowable, whereupon it begins to wear away.
- **The two extremes are built from not-knowing.** Because "seeing is something I can do," there arise "seen / not-seen," "I saw / I did not see," "I heard / I did not hear"; saying "no sound now," one supposes "I know the non-hearing" — but that silent moment is precisely when the "hearing" view is most operative, and one cannot even tell the view is present. Not seeing the seen-heard-sensed, we fabricate "non-hearing, non-seeing, non-sensing, non-thinking" — the whole "absence" story — because we dwell without knowing the permanence; even in the silent moment we are in "hearing," yet no view has worn away. It is like a chip with no data: crush the chip to powder and there is no way to the data, crush the ear to powder and the sound-hearing story is over — so we are boxed into needing both.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue examines the two forms of craving — *bhava-taṇhā* through "existence" and *vibhava-taṇhā* through "non-existence" — and shows that the strongest craving hides exactly where one never looks. What, the teacher asks, is "most certainly permanent"? The never-moving rock, taken as permanent through its very absence of movement; and the greatest attachment is to the unmoving, because being fully sure it will never move, one does not even think of it twice — and that unthinking certainty is itself the highest craving. Craving, then, is not the attachment one consciously feels: where one is unsure there is binding and thinking, but where one is a hundred percent sure the craving is present yet ungraspable, lying beyond consciousness's range, so that one cannot even imagine a craving for what one never thinks about — which is exactly where the deepest craving hides, in the seen-heard-sensed-cognized, where we leap straight to "chair" and "table" without even the words "I see."

From this follows the decisive limitation: only the thinkable cravings can be removed, and "remove craving and you are right" is said only of these, while the craving lodged in the unthought permanent things cannot be reached — this being *vibhava-taṇhā*, craving established through absence, which the existence-side can never see, since consciousness cannot remove what lies outside its range. The teacher illustrates with Dharmāśoka, who, despite offering temples and every meritorious work, kept a fine aversion toward a certain woman and was reborn a python: the seeing had become permanent and the aversion toward that seen-object was never severed. The neighbour's fence makes the same point in reverse — one thinks nothing of neighbours who cause no trouble, yet that neutral, untroubled ease itself conceals a craving for "no trouble," an un-knowable craving removable only when the never-obtained "absent" side is seen.

The heart of the talk is that permanence-perception cannot be known: once a thing is made permanent it can no longer be known, and the four root permanence-perceptions are the seen-heard-sensed-cognized themselves. The best proof is that we say "seeing" and "hearing" but cannot analyse them — we can only analyse a form or a sound, never the act — so that asked "what is hearing?" one falls silent and speaks instead of the sound; it was therefore never understood, only supposed, which is why wisdom is needed to see it, a groping in the dark. When wisdom settles on that permanence-perception at the un-understood place, one who does not know is caught there and calls it "cessation" through meditation, while the one with wisdom knows this to be the dangerous place — the delusion unseen, the reason saṁsāra runs — since delusion cannot be seen as delusion by consciousness: sitting and breathing, one neither feels it nor knows one breathes, and only seeing that un-understood place reveals the permanence-perception as unknowable, whereupon it begins to wear away. And it is this not-knowing that built the two extremes: because "seeing is something I can do," there arise seen and not-seen, heard and not-heard, so that saying "no sound now" one supposes one knows the non-hearing, when in truth that silent moment is when the "hearing" view is most fully operative and cannot even be detected — for we are still within "hearing," no view worn away, boxed in like a chip and its data, where crushing the ear ends the whole story of sound.
