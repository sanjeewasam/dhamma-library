## Sutta referred to
- **Madhupiṇḍika Sutta** (මධුපිණ්ඩික සූත්‍රය) — named: the proliferation sequence *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti, yaṁ sañjānāti taṁ vitakketi, yaṁ vitakketi taṁ papañceti, papañca-saññā-saṅkhā samudācaranti, tato nidānaṁ purisaṁ … atīta-anāgata-paccuppannesu cakkhu-viññeyyesu rūpesu* ("what one feels one perceives … what one thinks of one proliferates … perceptions born of proliferation assail one … across past, future and present forms cognizable by the eye").
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *tajjo samannāhāro* ("the corresponding bringing-together / co-arisen"); *ahutvā sambhūtaṁ* ("having not been, come to be"); *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* ("with ignorance as condition, formations"); and *saṁsāra is anavarāgra / anamatagga* ("without discoverable beginning"). The "neither the dying one nor another is reborn" formula and the tapping of an arahant's skull ("his destination cannot be told") are also referred to.
- Terms used: *tajjo samannāhāro*, *papañca* (proliferation), *saññā* (perception), *vitakka* (thought), *vedanā* (feeling), *anusaya* (latent tendency), *marīciya* (mirage), *vikṛti* (distortion). No further sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a three-speaker session (Speaker 1 = teacher; Speaker 2 = "Lahiru"; Speaker 3 = "Akka"); the transcript carries a "[TO BE CONTINUED]" marker with a timestamp jump, so a stretch may be missing.

## Main dhamma points
- A three-speaker "good-friend" dialogue using the flame's combustion to show that an arisen thing has no locatable root, that "hearing" is rootless in the same way, and that the mind arises precisely from not-seeing this rootlessness.
- **The combustion born of itself.** A lamp's flame arises, yet its root cause — the combustion itself — exists nowhere: not in the oil, not in the flame, not anywhere; still, a feeling arises that "a combustion happened here." This is what *tajjo samannāhāro* points to — "born of itself," not "arisen when causes combine," a wholly different statement with no come-from-place.
- **Do not equate it to a word.** When "*ahutvā sambhūtaṁ*" is offered, the teacher warns against rushing to match words: this must be *realized*, not equated; one should hold no target and never ask "isn't this the same as that?", for matching one idea to another prevents realization and misses the latent-tendency level. As one waters a tree without thinking about its growth, one should simply listen and follow, and the understanding will come — but it cannot be reduced to an idea.
- **Arising itself is never understood — only the arisen thing.** We understand only the flame, never "arising"; asked to give a meaning to "combustion" or "arising," one cannot, and one cannot show where it came from. A thing born of itself has no cause to point to and no place it came from — hence no place it goes to either; to ask its end is to ask the end of what has no beginning.
- **A rootless thing is perceived "as existing."** We nonetheless see a tree and a flame "as existing" — a thing sprung from a rootless place is perceived as existent, though a rootless thing cannot really persist and "at a rootless place there is a thing" cannot truly be said. We never investigated the root; the instant a "thing" is obtained we merely *think* "this must have a root," and from that instant we watch "thing exists / thing vanishes" — which is exactly the surface story "a mind arises, a mind ceases," built from the obtained distortion.
- **Neither the dead one nor another.** Because we never see the flame's root, we keep saying "the fire came to this lamp *from* that lamp," and hold raw the notion "the man of the past life died and was reborn here" — a rootless thing grasped and given persistence. This is why the Buddha, asked whether the dying man himself is reborn, said "neither the dying man nor another": one cannot say a one existed, nor that another came — the birth has no cause, exactly as the combustion shows.
- **Hearing is rootless like combustion.** Whenever a mind arises we posit a root — "I heard" becomes "a sound exists, so it is heard." But "hearing" cannot be isolated or shown; asked what hearing is, one can only talk about the *sound*, knowing nothing of hearing itself. Look right at "hearing," as in meditation, and its root cannot be found within it — so the "hearing" mind arises precisely from not-seeing that hearing has no come-from-place; the very non-comprehension that "no source is found" is the condition for the mind to arise.
- **No root, no end — the arahant's skull.** A wisdom established at a rootless place has no showable destination: consciousness always has a root (its arising has a ceasing), but this wisdom has none visible, and where no root is seen no end can be shown. Hence when an arahant's skull was tapped, "his destination could not be told," though for others the rebirth-destination could — for to a wisdom operating where no root is seen, no destination can be pointed out.
- **The fabricated "sound" and the Madhupiṇḍika sequence.** Because hearing has no root, we go to "it happened by a sound" — a perception knotted where nothing was: *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti*, one perceives as "sound" a thing not even in it. So "hearing" never actually happened, there is no root, and to a rootless place we assign a root, "it was the sound," and a beautiful knotted story runs. Then the sound is traced to a bird, a dog, a car, a crow, and "where is it going?" grows — which is where science speaks (the Big-Bang, planetary tales) — proliferation built beyond: *yaṁ sañjānāti taṁ vitakketi, yaṁ vitakketi taṁ papañceti*, and toward the proliferation likes, dislikes and *rāga-dveṣa-moha* arise, until *papañca-saññā-saṅkhā samudācaranti*, perceptions of proliferation assail the person across past, future and present forms — the *Madhupiṇḍika Sutta*, whose passage sits near its end after a long exposition applying it to every sense-base.
- **Beginningless saṁsāra and the mirage.** Because there is no root and no end, saṁsāra is *anavarāgra*, without discoverable beginning; within the persistence we seem always to get a root and an end — a birth and a death — but no beginning or end can be laid down, and it is exactly in this rootless-endless place that "root and end" appear ever-present. If one can well recognize the "sound"-mind, it cannot be forced onward to "a bird" or "a car," and beyond it one cannot even think *which* sound when "sound" is said — for "sound" is only a recognition, a perception with no things inside it, yet we feel there are things within sounds, which is why the Buddha called it a mirage (*marīciya*) and why *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*: the perception "sound," rightly seen, is a recognizing where nothing is recognized.

## Summary

This three-speaker good-friend dialogue uses the combustion of a lamp's flame to open a single point: an arisen thing has no locatable root. The flame arises, yet the combustion itself is nowhere — not in the oil, not in the flame, not anywhere — while a feeling nonetheless arises that "a combustion happened here," which is exactly what *tajjo samannāhāro* names: "born of itself," not "arisen when causes combine," a wholly different statement with no come-from-place. When a listener offers a matching Pāli phrase, the teacher warns firmly against equating this with any word: it must be realized, not matched, and one should hold no target and never ask "isn't this the same as that?", since fitting one idea to another prevents realization and misses the latent-tendency level — as one waters a tree without pondering its growth, one simply listens and follows, and the understanding comes without being reducible to an idea. From the flame he draws that "arising" itself is never understood, only the arisen thing; asked to give a meaning to combustion or to show where it came from, one cannot, and a thing born of itself, having no cause to point to and no place it came from, has no place it goes to either.

Yet we perceive such rootless things "as existing" — we see a tree, we see a flame — though a rootless thing cannot truly persist; we never investigated the root, and the instant a "thing" is obtained we merely think it must have one, whereupon we watch "thing exists, thing vanishes," which is precisely the surface story "a mind arises, a mind ceases," built from the obtained distortion. Because we never see the flame's root, we keep saying the fire came "from" the previous lamp, and hold raw the notion that a past-life person died and was reborn here — a rootless thing grasped and given persistence. This, the teacher shows, is why the Buddha, asked whether the dying man himself is reborn, answered "neither the dying man nor another": one can neither say a one existed nor that another came, for the birth has no cause, exactly as the combustion shows. He then turns this onto the mind: whenever a mind arises we posit a root, so "I heard" becomes "a sound exists, therefore it is heard" — but "hearing," like combustion, cannot be isolated or shown, and asked what it is, one can only speak of the sound, knowing nothing of hearing itself. Looking directly at "hearing" as in meditation, no root is found within it, so the hearing-mind arises precisely from not-seeing that hearing has no source; the very non-comprehension that "no source is found" is the condition for the mind's arising.

Because a rootless thing has no findable end, a wisdom established at that rootless place has no showable destination — consciousness always has a root and therefore a ceasing, but this wisdom has none visible — which is why, when an arahant's skull was tapped, "his destination could not be told," though for others it could. Not seeing hearing's rootlessness, we go instead to "it happened by a sound," knotting a perception where nothing was, so that one perceives as "sound" a thing not even in it; "hearing" never actually happened, and to a rootless place we assign a root, and a knotted story runs — the sound traced to a bird, a dog, a car, a crow, and "where is it going?" expanding into the very place where science speaks of the Big-Bang and planetary origins. This is the Madhupiṇḍika sequence: what one feels one perceives, what one perceives one thinks about, what one thinks about one proliferates, and the perceptions of proliferation assail the person across past, future and present forms — a passage sitting near the end of that important sutta after a long application to every sense-base. And because there is neither root nor end, saṁsāra is called beginningless: within the persistence a birth and a death always seem present, yet no beginning or end can be laid down, and it is exactly in this rootless-endless place that "root and end" appear ever-present. If the "sound"-mind is well recognized it cannot be forced onward to "a bird" or "a car," and beyond it one cannot even think which sound when "sound" is said — for "sound" is only a recognition, a perception with no things inside, though we feel there are things within sounds, which is why the Buddha called it a mirage and why formations are conditioned by ignorance: the perception "sound," rightly seen, is a recognizing where nothing at all is recognized.
