## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named by title. The **teaching given to Bāhiya** is referred to: "in the cognized, only the cognized" / "train yourself so that knowing is only knowing."
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed:
  - *hetuṁ paṭicca sambhūtaṁ, hetu-bhaṅgā nirujjhati* ("arisen dependent on a cause, it ceases with the breakup of the cause") and *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ* ("whatever is of a nature to arise, all that is of a nature to cease").
  - *imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti, imassa uppādā idaṁ uppajjati* ("when this is, that is; from the arising of this, that arises").
  - *yo paṭiccasamuppādaṁ passati so dhammaṁ passati* and *yo dhammaṁ passati so maṁ passati* ("who sees dependent origination sees the dhamma; who sees the dhamma sees me").
  - *saṅkhāra-paccayā viññāṇaṁ*; *paccuppannaṁ yo dhammaṁ tattha tattha vipassati* ("whatever is present, one discerns it there and there").
  - *majjhe mantā na lippati* ("the sage clings not even to the middle").
  - *ettha nāmañca rūpañca asesaṁ uparujjhati; viññāṇaṁ anidassanaṁ anantaṁ sabbato-pabhaṁ; ettha āpo ca paṭhavī, tejo, vāyo na gādhati* ("here name-and-form cease without remainder; consciousness non-manifesting, infinite, all-luminous; here water, earth, fire and air find no footing").
- The twenty-four conditions (*atthi-, natthi-, samanantara-, anantara-, purejāta-, sahajāta-, aññamañña-paccaya …*) are named in passing.
- Terms: *paṭiccasamuppāda*, *anicca*, *manō-dhamma*, *nimitta* (marks), *nāma-mātra* (mere designation), *pañcupādānakkhandha*, *āsava*, *natthi-paccaya*, *prabhāsvara* (luminous), *anidassana* (non-manifesting).

## Main dhamma points
- A multi-speaker "good-friend" dialogue (teacher Lasantha and questioners) examining, through "a sound is heard," how each consciousness arises fresh with no object inside it, why the world's own dependent-origination "theory" contains the fault that reveals impermanence, and how a "bird" is built only from our own stored marks.
- **One present instant holds only one consciousness.** Taking "a sound is heard": in a single present-moment instant only one consciousness arises. When one thinks "sound," within that consciousness there is only "sound" — no "what was heard"; when one thinks "hearing," within it there is only "hearing" — no sound. So "hearing" has no base or object within it, the heard thing never having arisen there, and this must be examined at the mental-phenomena level, within this fathom-long body, not "out in the world."
- **The world's theory contains its own fault.** By the theory *imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti*, an effect appears only where its cause is present — fire only where matchbox and matchstick rub, sound present wherever hearing is. But when "hearing" arose the sound had *not* arisen within it: only the fruit appeared, not the cause, which does not fit the theory. So to see dependent origination is to transcend the world, because the world's own theory is the fault — a "conditioning" that seems to happen at a place where nothing actually arose, and this mirage of "seeming conditioned where nothing arose" *is* delusion.
- **Impermanence is seen in the theory's fault, not by following it.** One does not find impermanence by tracing "sound → hearing," but by seeing that cause and fruit are *not* built. Hence *yo paṭiccasamuppādaṁ passati so dhammaṁ passati* and *yo dhammaṁ passati so maṁ passati*: seeing the fault in dependent-arising is seeing nibbāna, which is seeing the Buddha — for the Buddha is nibbāna, not the body. The Buddha showed dependent origination as a *raft* to cross by, not an object to revere with "sādhu"; those who merely admire the theory and watch it remain in the world, whereas cessation is found precisely *through* seeing arising.
- **The mind arises because a formation is not seen as a formation.** *Saṅkhāra-paccayā viññāṇaṁ*: while "the bird's sound" is not seen as mere intention, proliferation runs — where is the bird, what kind, near or far, big or small — conceit and agitation and the inner-outer division building up; but seen as just an arisen intention, the proliferation stops, "bird" recognized a while yet no actual bird found within it.
- **The "bird" is built from our own marks.** If we knot our stored marks (shape, colour, the firmness of earth), our perceptions and feelings onto the word, a real live flying bird is obtained and the five clinging-aggregates become real. A small child who does not know birds gets no such image from the word "bird," and a foreigner with no Sinhala gets none either — proof that the word "bird" is a mere sound holding no marks, and that the marks come from us, from our influxes, having no connection to the word at all; knotting them on is exactly "becoming dependently arisen." Delete those stored marks and "bird," heard, produces nothing but the bare word.
- **Both extremes are the world; the middle is a mere designation.** Saying "no bird here" builds a whole world of "no-bird," the annihilation extreme, through the non-existence-condition; so in examining one must not drift to "then it was never known" (*natthi*), since both "known / exists" (*atthi*) and "not-known / absent" (*natthi*) are the world. The two-question test shows it: asked "do you know the bird?", one cannot say "no" (the word *was* known) and cannot say "yes, a bird exists" (no thing was obtained) — neither extreme applies, so the world is not associated with. This is the Bāhiya instruction: knowing is only knowing, a mere designation.
- **Descending to the world knowingly, unattached.** One thus knows "bird" as bird, uses the word perfectly, answers "yes, I know it, it's over there" — descending to the deluded person's level with full awareness, like joining a child's game and calling sand "rice, delicious" without believing there is rice — seeing at once the other's delusion and one's own non-attachment, like a water-drop on a lotus leaf that wets but does not cling. Both the world and the world's cessation are seen by one and the same wisdom.
- **Not even a middle to cling to.** *Majjhe mantā na lippati*: when the two ends drop, the "middle" dissolves too, for a middle requires two ends; rise and fall are seen together and the middle is seen but un-clung, there being no middle left to grasp.
- **Where nothing is known, name-and-form cease and consciousness does not manifest.** With the knot undone, no marks arise, so name-and-form do not arise: *ettha nāmañca rūpañca asesaṁ uparujjhati*. Where there is no thing to be known there is no reason for a consciousness to manifest — *viññāṇaṁ anidassanaṁ anantaṁ sabbato-pabhaṁ*, non-manifesting and luminous, free of marks and defilements — and where no form is established the four great elements find no footing, *ettha āpo ca paṭhavī, tejo, vāyo na gādhati*. Yet this is not "not knowing": one knows, but knows in the very knowing that knowing is empty of knowing — the refrain of insight, a vision that must dawn wordlessly and cannot be caught by a thousand words, ripening only with cultivation.

## Summary

This long multi-speaker good-friend dialogue — stitched from four segments and marred by a duplicated block where one exchange repeats verbatim about ten times — takes the ordinary "a sound is heard" and works it down to the mental-phenomena level. In a single present-moment instant only one consciousness arises: think "sound," and within that consciousness there is only "sound," no "what was heard"; think "hearing," and within it there is only "hearing," no sound. So "hearing" carries no object inside it, the heard thing never having arisen there, and dependent origination is therefore not something built "out in the world" but something to be examined within this fathom-long body. From this the teacher exposes a fault in the world's own theory: by *imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti*, an effect appears only where its cause is present — fire only where the matchbox and stick rub — yet when "hearing" arose the sound had not arisen within it, so only the fruit appeared and not the cause, which does not fit the theory at all. To see dependent origination, then, is to transcend the world, because the world's theory itself is the fault: a "conditioning" that seems to occur where nothing actually arose, and this mirage is precisely delusion.

Impermanence, he stresses, is found not by tracing "sound produces hearing" but by seeing that cause and fruit are never really built — which is why *yo paṭiccasamuppādaṁ passati so dhammaṁ passati* and *yo dhammaṁ passati so maṁ passati*: to see the fault in dependent-arising is to see nibbāna, and to see nibbāna is to see the Buddha, who is nibbāna and not the body. The Buddha offered dependent origination as a raft to cross by, not an idol to praise; those who merely admire the theory and keep watching it stay in the world, while cessation is found precisely through seeing arising. Turning to how the mind builds its objects, he shows that a consciousness arises only because a formation is not seen as a formation — *saṅkhāra-paccayā viññāṇaṁ* — so that while "the bird's sound" is not seen as mere intention, proliferation runs on into where and what kind of bird, conceit and agitation and the inner-outer split building up; seen as just an arisen intention, it stops. The "bird" itself, he demonstrates, is assembled entirely from our own stored marks — shape, colour, the firmness of earth — knotted onto a bare word: a child who does not know birds, and a foreigner with no Sinhala, get no image from "bird," proving the word holds no marks and that the marks come from us, with no connection to the word, so that knotting them on is exactly what "becoming dependently arisen" means. Delete those marks and "bird," heard, yields nothing but the sound.

He then guards both flanks. Saying "no bird here" merely builds a world of "no-bird," the annihilation extreme reached through the non-existence-condition, so in examining one must not slide to "then it was never known," since both "it exists" and "it is absent" are equally the world. The two-question test seals it: asked "do you know the bird?", one cannot answer "no," for the word was indeed known, nor "yes, a bird exists," for no thing was obtained — neither extreme applies, and the world is not associated with. This is the Bāhiya instruction, that knowing is only knowing, a mere designation; and living by it one still knows "bird" as bird, uses the word flawlessly, and answers "yes, it's over there," descending to the deluded person's level with full awareness — like joining a child's game and calling sand "rice, delicious" without believing there is rice — seeing at once the other's delusion and one's own unattachment, a water-drop on a lotus leaf that wets but never clings. And when the two ends drop, even the middle dissolves, for a middle needs two ends: rise and fall are seen together, the middle seen but un-clung, *majjhe mantā na lippati*. With the knot undone no marks arise, so name-and-form cease without remainder, consciousness does not manifest but is luminous and infinite, and the four great elements find no footing where no form is established — yet this is not "not knowing," for one knows while knowing that the knowing is empty of knowing, a wordless vision that ripens only with cultivation and can never be caught by a thousand words.
