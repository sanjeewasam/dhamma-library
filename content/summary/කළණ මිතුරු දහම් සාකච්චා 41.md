## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue distinguishing the *false* "non-arising" reached by subtracting a grasped thing to zero — which looks exactly like nibbāna but is eternalism in disguise — from the *true* cessation, in which arising and vanishing are seen together in one instant.
- **The saucer, and "grasping" as contact.** Hold a saucer and ask "is it in the hand?"; say "no saucer," and "no saucer" still obtains a saucer through absence; ask "describe the saucer," and one says it was seen with the eye; ask "how did the eye come to be?", and it too is obtained by seeing. So "it exists" is asserted while holding the saucer, and "it does not exist" is asserted while holding the saucer too — both grasp it, and this grasping *is* contact.
- **Analysing the saucer never reaches non-obtaining.** Everyone tries to build a saucer from the saucer's own pieces — "made of the four great elements," "of *rūpa-kalāpas*," "arising and ceasing," and now the newest fashion, "a dependently-arisen saucer" — all of which still give the saucer persistence. But seeing dependent arising is not proving the saucer or how it is made; it is seeing that a saucer cannot be obtained at all, which is the very essence of the teaching.
- **"Nothing here" is not cessation.** Showing an empty hand and saying "nothing there" is still a grasping — a "something" is held in the head and then negated. Subtracting the grasped thing to get zero yields the annihilation extreme ("there is no saucer"), not cessation; seeing the absence of a thought-thing is not seeing non-arising.
- **The most dangerous view.** The "it has not arisen" that appears through subtraction is the most dangerous view precisely because it looks exactly like nibbāna: holding "arisen," one hunts for "non-arising," but the "there is no saucer" so reached is itself one more arising, one more grasped thing — the "best view" one gets trapped in, indistinguishable from nibbāna, un-findable and un-conveyable to one who has not gone beyond.
- **The direction of ahutvā sambhūtaṁ.** "Having not been, come to be" must be read from non-being to being, not the reverse: to one who *knows* non-being (*ahutvā*), being (*sambhūta*) is rightly obtained. Even some monks mis-explain it as going from a grasped, arisen thing toward non-being — but one cannot travel from the obtained thing to non-being; one must see arising *from* the non-arising place, and only there does *samudaya* appear rightly. The earlier move of grasping the saucer and looking for "no saucer" is exactly the wrong direction, the false cessation, and the ancient other-sectarians, skilled as they were, got caught precisely here, reaching only a "non-arising relative to the arisen," which is still eternalism.
- **The automatic door.** When no person is present the door stays shut, the sensor continuously registering "absence" and opening only when a person comes; likewise the "not-feeling" state we take for cessation is consciousness actively registering absence, a persistence — the persistence *of* not-knowing. So the "before I recalled it, it had not arisen" state is not non-arising but the arising of not-knowing.
- **Arising and not-arising are born in one instant.** "I did not know before, now I know" cannot be described at its "before" (no description exists there), so it seems past and dead; but the not-knowing is born in the very same instant as the knowing, before-and-after not separated — right where feeling arises, not-feeling arises too. Seeing feeling and not-feeling together in one instant, catching the un-manifest "shut-door" side in *this* moment, is the real seeing, and then there is no talk of a previous moment.
- **This is contemplating arising-and-vanishing together.** It is *udaya* and *vaya* seen together — *samudaya-vaya-dhammānupassī*, not arising-contemplation or vanishing-contemplation alone — and this is the moment of true cessation, the moment of Buddhahood. We are separated from it by "the gap," receiving arising separately and our own "absence" separately, which is why we have "exists" and "does not exist" as two.
- **Knowing and not-knowing are one coin.** "Feeling exists now" is asserted because "not-feeling" was taken as non-existent; but if not-feeling is also present at this same moment, one cannot call it "feeling," a contradiction — and seeing that contradiction is wisdom, un-catchable in words. Looking at feeling, the "not-feeling" extreme collapses; looking at not-feeling, the "feeling" extreme collapses; the two negate each other, and seeing both together transcends both extremes. Both are eternalist, like two faces of one coin that can never be taken singly — split the coin and it has two faces again — and their common groundlessness must dawn as a wisdom.
- **Naming the un-nameable rebuilds a "middle."** Put a word or simile on this un-nameable nature and a "middle" to stand on immediately reappears — as we named the two bamboo-bundles' "cause," which was not a real cause seen but only a *name* given to an unshowable nature. Hunting a name again, consciousness lodges somewhere, grabbing a middle; so "cause" must be seen not as a thing existing in the world but as never obtained, and to name the never-obtained again is exactly to re-posit "a dependently-arisen thing," giving feeling-and-not-feeling a base once more. Of the two — feeling through contact, and body-and-tangible through feeling — neither can be said to exist; and in discussing them, "it cannot be said to exist" itself arises, which is comprehension, the middle. To that no base can be given: one can say neither "contact exists" nor "contact's origin exists," and *that* is cessation — contact-cessation is the knowledge that arises, "cessation" being merely a name for that seeing, a knowledge that the Buddha somewhere called "a construction," because it sees the two ends, arising and vanishing, constructed together at one glance — two things that cannot persist appearing to persist, which is what the knowledge catches.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue is built to separate two things that look identical: the false "non-arising" reached by subtracting a grasped object down to zero, which resembles nibbāna exactly, and the true cessation, in which arising and vanishing are seen together in a single instant. The teacher begins with a saucer: hold it and ask "is it in the hand?", say "no saucer" and find that "no saucer" still obtains a saucer through absence, ask "describe it" and one says it was seen with the eye, ask "how did the eye come to be?" and it too is obtained by seeing — so "it exists" and "it does not exist" are both asserted while holding the saucer, and this holding is itself contact. Everyone, he says, tries to build a saucer from its own pieces — the four elements, the *rūpa-kalāpas*, "arising and ceasing," and now the fashionable "dependently-arisen saucer" — all of which merely re-grant the saucer persistence, whereas seeing dependent arising is not proving the saucer but seeing that a saucer cannot be obtained at all. Even "nothing here" is not cessation, for a "something" is first held in the head and then negated; subtracting the grasped thing to zero yields only the annihilation extreme.

The gravest danger, he warns, is that the "it has not arisen" produced by this subtraction looks exactly like nibbāna: holding "arisen" and hunting "non-arising," one reaches a "there is no saucer" that is itself one more arising, the "best view" in which one is trapped, indistinguishable from the goal and impossible to convey to anyone who has not gone beyond. Reading *ahutvā sambhūtaṁ*, he fixes the direction: one must move from non-being to being, so that to one who knows non-being, being is rightly obtained — and going the other way, from a grasped arisen thing toward non-being, is the very error some monks make and the trap in which the skilled ancient sectarians were caught, reaching only a "non-arising relative to the arisen," which is still eternalism. The automatic door sharpens it: when no one is present the door stays shut because the sensor is continuously registering absence, and likewise the "not-feeling" we mistake for cessation is consciousness actively registering absence — a persistence, the persistence of not-knowing, so that the "before I recalled it, it had not arisen" state is not non-arising but the arising of not-knowing.

The resolution is that arising and not-arising are born in one and the same instant. "I did not know before, now I know" cannot be described at its "before," so it seems past and dead; but the not-knowing is born together with the knowing, before-and-after not separated, right where feeling arises not-feeling arises too — and seeing feeling and not-feeling together in this moment, catching the un-manifest "shut-door" side now, is the real seeing, after which there is no previous moment to speak of. This is *udaya* and *vaya* contemplated together, *samudaya-vaya-dhammānupassī*, the moment of true cessation and of Buddhahood, from which we are cut off only by "the gap" that hands us arising and absence separately and so gives us "exists" and "does not exist" as two. Knowing and not-knowing, he shows, are two faces of one coin that can never be taken singly: "feeling exists" is said only because "not-feeling" was taken as non-existent, and if not-feeling is equally present now, one cannot call it feeling — a contradiction whose seeing is wisdom, for looking at feeling collapses the not-feeling extreme and looking at not-feeling collapses the feeling extreme, both negating each other until seeing them together transcends both. Finally he guards against the last trap: to put a word or simile on this un-nameable nature is to rebuild a "middle" to stand on, exactly as naming the bamboo-bundles' "cause" was only a name for an unshowable nature; so "cause" must be seen as never obtained, and to name the never-obtained again is to re-posit "a dependently-arisen thing." Of feeling and body-and-tangible neither can be said to exist, and in discussing them the very "it cannot be said to exist" that arises is comprehension, the middle — a middle to which no base can be given, neither "contact exists" nor "its origin exists," and this is cessation itself, contact-cessation being the knowledge that arises, "cessation" a mere name for that seeing, a knowledge the Buddha once called a construction because it catches, at a single glance, two ends that cannot persist appearing to persist.
