## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on the tea-and-cup example, showing that "memory" is itself a non-persistence, that no "one" is present at any action, and that "I forgot / I slept / I erred" are all empty prattle resting on a fictitious ever-present self.
- **Look at the short window, not the later recollection.** We say the event "is remembered" because we recount it days later — but the place to look is the shortest window, the two minutes carrying the tea from the kitchen to the sink; if memory truly operated it would be present *there*, and there it is absent. The days-later "post-mortem," done after the whole thing is over and mis-seen, is not the real thing.
- **Persistence is non-persistence.** Examined at that two-minute window, "persistence" reveals itself as "non-persistence" — and looking right there one is stumped, with nowhere far to go, the whole matter seen on the spot.
- **We only discuss the failures.** "If it had gone as intended" we would credit "my good thinking," but it is not good thinking — when the knot strikes we are deceived by thought, and we talk this way only because the task went *wrong*; had it gone right we would never discuss it, success staying invisible. To the worldling mind the knot registers only where it strikes hard (goes wrong), leaving one speechless.
- **No object, and no "one," at the action.** In the tea-and-cup case "reheating" is not known, only "tossing" is — so what was the object? One cannot say a mind held any object there; had a different object come it would be understood, and it is not, so it is neither in "reheating" nor in "tossing," a place with no object at all. And "I tossed it" is said only afterward: there is no "one who tossed" present at the tossing, for if a one were tossing he would necessarily know "I need not toss this — it is the cup." A "one" appears only when a consciousness "I am tossing" arises, the person being born together with that consciousness; within the actual nature there is no one for any action, and we merely knot-and-think a "me."
- **Recollection is beyond one's own range.** "I do not know why I tossed it, there is no cause, no memory" — so what one now "recalls" is not even within one's range, like trying to discuss sleep ("how is sleep? how is sleep?"), which cannot be done because it is not within consciousness's range. Hence "I tossed it forgetting" means "I did it knowing nothing at all," as if asleep; we fabricate and patch the missed spot into a story and fold it into our persistence.
- **The self-story collapses.** Every such statement conceals the defect "I made a mistake" — but can one err while one is present? If present, one would know at the very moment of erring. So "I slept, I forgot, I erred" are all empty statements resting on a fictitious ever-present self, and seeing this, the "the me exists" story falls away entirely, for if a me existed it would catch the error as it happened.
- **A heap of frivolous prattle.** All of this is *samphappalāpa* — "I slept, I forgot, I erred," hollow talk — and we live in an unimaginably large heap of such empty prattle; seeing it rightly, one sees what the knot really is, how large a knot is tied, and is left asking what one is even struggling and flailing about.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue takes up the familiar tea-and-cup example — carrying a cup of tea to the sink to reheat it — to expose how "memory" is a fiction and how no "one" is ever present at an action. We call the event "remembered," the teacher observes, only because we recount it days later; but the place to look is the shortest window, the two minutes from kitchen to sink, and if memory genuinely operated it would be present there, whereas there it is wholly absent. Examined at that window, "persistence" turns out to be non-persistence, and one is stumped on the spot, with nowhere further to go. The later "post-mortem" is done only after the whole thing is over and mis-seen, and it is telling that we discuss such matters only when they go *wrong*: had the task gone as intended we would credit "my good thinking" and never speak of it at all, success remaining invisible, so that to the worldling mind the knot registers only where it strikes hard and leaves one speechless.

Pressing into the action itself, the teacher shows there was no object and no agent there. "Reheating" was never known, only "tossing" — so what was the object? No mind held one, for had a different object come it would have been understood, and it was not; the place has no object at all, being neither in "reheating" nor in "tossing." And "I tossed it" is said only afterward: there is no "one who tossed" present at the tossing, since a one who was tossing would have known "I need not toss this, it is the cup." A "one" appears only when a consciousness "I am tossing" arises, the person born together with that consciousness, while within the actual nature no one attends any action and we merely knot-and-think a "me." What one now "recalls" is therefore not even within one's own range, like trying to describe sleep, so that "I tossed it forgetting" means "I did it knowing nothing at all," as if asleep — a missed spot fabricated into a story and folded into one's persistence. Every such statement, finally, hides the claim "I made a mistake," yet one cannot err while present, for a present self would catch the error as it happened; so "I slept, I forgot, I erred" are all empty statements resting on a fictitious ever-present self, all of it *samphappalāpa*, frivolous prattle, an unimaginably large heap of it — and seeing this rightly, one sees at last what the knot is, how great a knot is tied, and is left to wonder what one is even flailing about.
