## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named by title.
- **The Vakkali episode** is referred to: the Buddha's "do not see me by form; gazing at my body is useless" (and, implicitly, "one who sees the dhamma sees me").
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* ("with ignorance as condition, formations"); the contrast of *ākāravatī saddhā* (faith grounded in reasons) with *amūlikā saddhā* (rootless / blind faith); and the thirty-two parts of the body (*dvattiṁsākāra*).
- Terms used: *biṅduva* (zero), *pratya* (condition), *nätta-anta* / *uccheda* (the annihilation extreme), *aparappaccaya* (independent of another), *kalyāṇa-mitta* (good friend), *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen-heard-sensed-cognized), *sammuti* (convention). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a two-speaker session; the transcript carries two "[TO BE CONTINUED]" markers and ends "[END OF AUDIO]," so there may be small gaps.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue continuing the "zero" theme: comprehension comes from seeing that the zero was never obtained, "ignorance-conditioned formations" means taking a condition where none can be taken, and the dhamma, being self-verified, makes one independent and cannot be edited.
- **We see no fault in "things exist."** "Beings and things exist" is understood and we find no fault in it, which is why we are stuck in sorrow; but seeing that the *zero* can never be experienced or obtained brings wisdom, and understanding "what this one thing is" comes precisely from seeing that the zero was never obtained.
- **The absence-story rests on the present positing.** "Not-feeling is absent, so feeling is present": the whole "not-hearing, not-seeing, not-feeling" absence-story arises only because in this very moment we posit feeling, hearing and seeing — so the matter is really "seeing that the annihilation extreme was never obtained." Only one who sees the real condition sees that no condition is obtained, and from that condition-less place we assert "no-not-feeling" and "no-not-hearing," all of which is broken by the zero.
- **Ignorance-conditioned formations = taking a condition where none can be taken.** Where no condition can be found — where one cannot even think "absent" — is exactly where we take a condition, and this is the defect the Buddha named as *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*. Seeing it, one understands *why* he said it; otherwise it is mere words recited like a parrot without the realization.
- **Self-clarity, step by step.** As it clears, one sees within oneself "this is why he said this, that is why he said that," the sequence self-evident; applied to many examples it is all one thing, understood bit by bit until a whole block suddenly breaks free, like stuck debris dislodging at once. "The zero is never obtained" registers as a stated fact, yet each telling shows a little more clearly whether that is what is actually happening now.
- **The zero is itself one more fabricated thing.** The zero appears *as* a thing — the long-persisting "self" now showing up as a zero — which cannot be grasped conventionally; and since the zero was built by taking a never-obtained condition, the "zero" too is just the "absence" one fabricated. Both sides, the thing and its zero, are hollow and empty, and in this un-graspable place there is finally nothing left to break — hard even to hear, but a wholly different matter when seen within oneself.
- **Self-verification, matching the Buddha's word, is unshakable.** Cleaned up, it fits the Buddha's word beautifully, and one then accepts it not because "I said so" or "the Buddha said so" but because one sees it oneself — and being self-seen, it cannot be broken or shaken by anyone, like eyewitness evidence, this seeing-within-oneself being the very start of the path.
- **The good friend, then independence.** One who reaches it becomes *aparappaccaya*, needing no further looking-after; the good friend is the helmsman only until the wisdom establishes, bringing the person back each time they jump the track and training the "track-balance" — like a child held from behind and caught when falling, until one day it walks alone and is held no more, after which that wisdom, not a person, carries the path forward. One with genuine need never lets go or grows disillusioned, and seeing the true impermanence even for a single moment is seeing the Buddha in that moment, after which confidence in the Buddha cannot be broken.
- **Reasoned faith, not blind faith.** This is *ākāravatī saddhā*, faith grounded in reasons, not *amūlikā saddhā*, the rootless faith that rests on "so-and-so said it, the monk said it"; the one who has arrived here rests on no one's telling and cannot be shaken.
- **Vakkali: not to be seen by form.** The Buddha told Vakkali not to see him by form because, could he be seen by form, the four views would become real again — "seeing" itself would be made real and the Buddha grasped *through* a view. The four are the seen-heard-sensed-cognized, four views from which his teaching frees one, so a Buddha who transcended them would never say "look at my form."
- **The thirty-two parts, and the un-editable dhamma.** The list of the body's parts omits the eye, ear and nose, because in a dhamma taught by that wisdom "eye, ear, nose" name *views*, not organs; taken the other way — "the ear is right here on the body" — the whole thing turns false. Nothing in the dhamma is out of place, it is perfectly consistent, and the Buddha nowhere said "revise this": not a single word can be edited.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue continues the "zero" theme from the previous session and turns it toward how comprehension dawns, why it makes one independent, and why the dhamma is flawless. We find no fault, the teacher notes, in "beings and things exist," which is exactly why we remain caught in sorrow; but wisdom comes when one sees that the *zero* can never be experienced or obtained, and understanding "what this one thing is" arises precisely from seeing that the zero was never obtained. The whole absence-story — "not-hearing, not-seeing, not-feeling" — exists only because in this very moment we posit feeling, hearing and seeing, so the real point is that the annihilation extreme was never obtained, and only one who sees the genuine condition sees that no condition is obtained at all. From that condition-less place we assert "no-not-feeling," and all of it is broken by the zero — which is exactly why the Buddha said *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*: taking a condition where none can be taken, at a place where one cannot even think "absent." Seeing this, one grasps why he said it, whereas without the realization the phrase is only parroted words.

As the seeing clears, the teacher describes, one finds within oneself the reason for each of the Buddha's statements, the sequence becoming self-evident and, though understood only bit by bit, sometimes breaking free all at once like stuck debris dislodging. Even the zero, he cautions, is one more fabricated thing: it appears *as* a thing, the long-persisting "self" now showing up as a zero, and since it was built by taking a never-obtained condition, the zero too is merely the "absence" one fabricated, both the thing and its zero hollow and empty, leaving nothing finally to break. This is hard even to hear, but a wholly different matter when seen within oneself — and such self-verification, once cleaned up, fits the Buddha's word so exactly that one accepts it not on anyone's authority, not even the Buddha's, but because one sees it oneself, which makes it unshakable, like eyewitness evidence, and marks the true entry to the path. The one who reaches it becomes independent of another; the good friend serves as helmsman only until the wisdom establishes, bringing the person back each time they leave the track and training their balance, exactly as a child is held and caught until the day it walks alone — after which the wisdom itself, not any person, carries the path. One with real need never abandons the search or grows disillusioned, and seeing the genuine impermanence even for a single moment is seeing the Buddha in that moment, so that confidence in him can never afterward be broken. This, the teacher stresses, is reasoned faith, *ākāravatī saddhā*, not the rootless faith that leans on "so-and-so said it."

The dialogue closes on the Buddha's own words and their consistency. He told Vakkali not to see him by form because, could he be seen by form, the four views would be made real again and the Buddha grasped through a view; the four are the seen-heard-sensed-cognized, and a Buddha who transcended them would never invite one to look at his form. In the same way, the list of the body's thirty-two parts omits the eye, ear and nose, because in a dhamma taught by that wisdom those words name *views*, not organs, and to place "the ear here on the body" would turn the whole teaching false. Nothing in the dhamma is out of place, the teacher concludes; it is perfectly consistent, the Buddha nowhere asked that it be revised, and not a single word of it can be edited.
