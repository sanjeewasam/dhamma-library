## Main dhamma points
- A three-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on why craving-through-absence is invisible to the mind, why suppressing perception is dangerous, why Satipaṭṭhāna has no contemplation of perception, and how "feeling" is a conceiving fabricated together with "breath."
- **Craving-through-absence is un-graspable.** *Vibhava-taṇhā* must be shown to be beyond the mind's grasp: where no object comes to mind — a room with no rubbish, a meditation with no sound — the mind has no cause to take up "rubbish" or "sound," yet a craving is present there. Of the three cravings (*kāma-*, *bhava-*, *vibhava-taṇhā*), all belong to the world; and just as one does not want a disease and so craves its *absence*, one has a maximal craving for the rubbish-free, the healthy, the silent — a craving established through absence, invisible to consciousness like the annihilation extreme.
- **Anger comes from the craving for absence.** When someone dumps rubbish in a clean place we think the anger came from the rubbish, but its real source is the craving for the rubbish-free state; being so attached to "no rubbish," the dumping sparks anger — so it is the invisible craving-for-absence that triggers it, while one supposes "no object here, so no craving."
- **Suppressing perception is the danger.** Watching the breath, the breath fades and feeling fades with it — suppressing perception veils feeling — and "no feeling, no breath" is taken for cessation; but feeling was not discerned, only veiled, and the comfort in that "no-feeling" is the unnoticed craving-for-the-absence-of-feeling. Suppressing perception leads to the perception-less plane, as with Uddaka Rāmaputta and Āḷāra Kālāma, and when the breath-perception re-arises one falls back from it, no one knowing where. This is why perception must not be suppressed.
- **Feeling cannot be discerned by mindfulness.** To discern feeling one must see that feeling can never be recognized or grasped — but if feeling is made to fade, the story simply ends and one calls it cessation. Discerning feeling is not "seeing feeling by feeling," and cannot be done by mindfulness, for the moment one thinks "feeling," feeling appears, and watching the feeling that appears on thinking is useless. Feeling must be seen where no breath is engaged, and where no breath is engaged no "feeling" can be understood either; the two are knotted so tightly that "breath" is spoken only with "feeling," and asked "do you feel the breath?", feeling arises with the very answer. Most of the day feeling is in a not-obtained state, and thinking of it makes it "obtained" — so one must realize the *not-obtained-ness*, which the mind cannot do.
- **Why Satipaṭṭhāna has no contemplation of perception.** The four foundations map to the five aggregates — body-contemplation to form, feeling-contemplation to feeling, mind-contemplation to formations, dhamma-contemplation to consciousness — but perception is left blank, because it is not a persisting thing (like a mirage). Its removal is not a separate method: *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti* — perception is bound to *feeling*, so it is discerned by rightly seeing feeling through feeling-contemplation. Not knowing feeling *as* feeling is exactly why we lean into perception, which then proliferates into thought.
- **The five similes carry one meaning.** Though perception got no contemplation, the Buddha gave it a *simile* — the mirage — because it too is not-obtained; and the five aggregate-similes (foam, bubble, mirage, plantain-with-no-heartwood, magic-show) all carry the single meaning "not-obtained," the water-bubble formed where roof-water meets ground-water belonging to neither, standing established nowhere.
- **The four elements are ways of recognizing one feeling.** Feeling the breath, one recognizes "hard" (earth), "wet" (water), "hot" (fire), "moving" (air), and from these infers a "form"; but across all four there is only one bare feeling, no four feelings, the difference too subtle to see. Feeling has no four divisions — the four are set out in front of the one feeling — and to separate what cannot be separated within feeling *is* perception: "chair or table?" cannot be told by feeling, and neither is owned by feeling.
- **Feeling is a fabricated conceiving.** "I think breath because breath exists" is backwards: feeling arises, and only when "feeling" is thought does "breath" begin to be understood; were breath a real thing it would be felt whenever present, but it is thought only when thought — *abhisañcetayita*, thought-upon-thought, a conceiving not known as conceiving, which is why perception arises. Asked "do you feel the breath?", the thinking arises in the *mind-base*, not the body-base — proof that "feeling" is a mind-thing thought-only, that a *body-base* is never actually obtained, and that thinking of a never-obtained body-base is exactly *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ*, the illusion of consciousness.
- **The inseparable knot, and its cessation.** Not knowing feeling (here the *sensed*, *muta*) as a conceiving, we knot a thing to it — "it came from the stone, the table, the chair, the water" — and this thing is knotted to the thought-only story, not to any real feeling; seen as the conceiving it is, no "breath" or "stone" is ever knotted to it. Suppress the breath-perception and "feeling" cannot arise, for feeling arises only *with* the breath-perception — *imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti* — so feeling can never be seen without a breath, the two fitting so perfectly that feeling becomes un-breakably "real," always a breath on the other side that cannot be denied; and equally *imasmiṁ asati idaṁ na hoti*, for when the breath-object is watched until it fades unfelt, this being absent, that is not.

## Summary

This three-speaker good-friend dialogue opens by showing that *vibhava-taṇhā*, craving through non-existence, is beyond the mind's grasp: where no object comes to mind — a room with no rubbish, a meditation with no sound — the mind has no cause to take one up, yet a craving is present. All three cravings belong to the world, and just as one does not want a disease and so craves its very absence, one has a maximal, invisible craving for the rubbish-free and the healthy, established through absence. The teacher makes it vivid: when rubbish is dumped in a clean place we blame the rubbish for our anger, but its real source is our attachment to "no rubbish," so it is the unseen craving-for-absence that fires the anger while we suppose there is "no object, and so no craving, here." He then turns to the meditative danger: watching the breath until it and feeling fade, one takes "no feeling, no breath" for cessation, but feeling was only veiled by suppressing perception, and the comfort in that emptiness is itself the unnoticed craving for the absence of feeling. Suppressing perception leads to the perception-less plane of Uddaka Rāmaputta and Āḷāra Kālāma, from which one falls back when the perception re-arises, no one knowing where — which is why perception must never be suppressed.

Feeling, he insists, cannot be discerned by mindfulness, for the instant one thinks "feeling" it appears, and watching that is useless; feeling must be seen where no breath is engaged, yet where no breath is engaged no feeling can be understood either, the two so knotted that "breath" is spoken only with "feeling" and, asked "do you feel it?", feeling arises with the answer. Most of the day feeling is in a not-obtained state, and it is this not-obtained-ness one must realize — which the mind cannot do. This leads to the dialogue's central discovery: the Satipaṭṭhāna's four contemplations cover form, feeling, formations and consciousness, but leave perception blank, because perception is no persisting thing and, by *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti*, is bound to feeling, so it is discerned simply by seeing feeling rightly. Not knowing feeling as feeling is why we lean into perception, which then proliferates into thought; and though perception got no contemplation, it got a *simile* — the mirage — one of the five aggregate-images (foam, bubble, mirage, plantain-with-no-heartwood, magic-show) that all mean the same thing, not-obtained, the water-bubble belonging to neither the roof-water nor the ground-water and standing nowhere.

The four elements, he shows, are merely ways of recognizing the one feeling: hardness, wetness, heat and movement are all the same bare feeling, with no four divisions within feeling itself, so that to separate the inseparable — "chair or table?", neither owned by feeling — is exactly what perception is. And feeling itself is a fabricated conceiving: "I think breath because breath exists" is backwards, for feeling arises first and only when "feeling" is thought does "breath" come to be understood, breath being thought only when thought rather than felt whenever present — *abhisañcetayita*, thought-upon-thought. Asked "do you feel the breath?", the thinking arises in the mind-base, not the body-base, proving that "feeling" is a mind-thing thought-only, that a body-base is never obtained, and that thinking of a never-obtained body-base is precisely *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ*, the illusion of consciousness. Not knowing this conceiving as a conceiving, we knot a thing to it — "it came from the stone, the table, the water" — knotting it to a thought-only story rather than any real feeling, whereas seen as the conceiving it is, nothing is ever knotted to it. Finally he closes the knot: suppress the breath-perception and feeling cannot arise, for feeling arises only *with* it — *imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti* — so feeling can never be seen without a breath, the fit so perfect that feeling becomes un-breakably real, always a breath on the other side; and equally, when the breath-object is watched until it fades unfelt, *imasmiṁ asati idaṁ na hoti*, this being absent, that is not.
