- Terms used: *ādīnava* (danger / fault), *yoniso-manasikāra* / *ayoniso-manasikāra* (wise / unwise attention), *upekkhā* (equanimity), *palibodha* (impediments), *gäṭa* (knot). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a short two-speaker session (a questioner and the teacher).

## Main dhamma points
- A short two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on how, in meditation, seeing the *fault* of intrusive thoughts — rather than forcing them away — stops the knotting of itself.
- **See the fault, do not force the stopping.** As one meditates and a scattered object arrives, recognizing "this is unnecessary rubbish flowing in" gradually stops such thoughts from coming; the question is not whether to force them to stop but whether to see their fault — and seeing the fault, the knotting ceases.
- **Ajahn Chah's counting.** In a book of Ajahn Chah's, the instruction is to *count* what comes: sitting to meditate, first count the external impediments — a sound that becomes an object is "one," a recollection counted is "two," and so on — just as recognizing a thief *as* a thief stops the thief from coming, thieves not coming to a house where they know they are seen.
- **Recognizing devalues; the value drops and thinking stops.** As long as the mind does not recognize the object, we give room to the thinking because we do not know its danger; seeing the danger more and more, the mind itself does the very "developing of equanimity," no longer accepting the incoming thoughts — the mind kept in a good, alert state, waiting to catch the "thieves," so that whereas before a thought would come and one would run along with it, now one does not take it up so tightly.
- **It works from within, effortlessly.** The thoughts are not driven out by force; seeing the "rubbish" nature well, the taught points come to work from within, without even the words — "is this not the rubbish coming?" — and as the value given to the thought drops, the thinking-habit stops of itself.
- **Seeing the unwise as unwise.** One day, sitting to meditate, it is just like ordinary sitting — nothing special happens, no change, nothing to do, and while sitting quietly nothing comes to mind, yet the moment something comes it is seen instantly. This is seeing the unwise attention *as* unwise; it is exactly this capacity we lack, which is why we have handed ourselves over to an "unwise mindfulness," well-fed on words.

## Summary

This short two-speaker good-friend dialogue is a practical exchange on how intrusive thoughts subside in meditation. As one sits and a scattered object arrives, the teacher explains, recognizing it as "unnecessary rubbish flowing in" gradually stops such thoughts, and the point is not to force them to stop but to see their fault — for seeing the fault, the knotting ceases of itself. He recalls Ajahn Chah's method of counting the distractions: a sound that becomes an object is "one," a recollection is "two," and so on, working like recognizing a thief as a thief, since thieves do not come to a house where they know they are seen. The deeper principle is that recognition devalues the thought: as long as the mind does not recognize the object it gives room to the thinking, not knowing its danger, but seeing the danger more and more, the mind itself performs the very cultivation of equanimity, no longer accepting the incoming thoughts and staying alert to catch them, so that a thought one would once have run along with is no longer taken up tightly. None of this is done by force; seeing the "rubbish" nature well, the teaching comes to work from within, wordlessly — "is this not the rubbish coming?" — and as the value given to the thought falls away, the thinking-habit stops on its own. He closes with a personal note: sitting to meditate is often just like ordinary sitting, nothing special happening and nothing to do, yet the instant something arises it is seen — and this seeing of the unwise attention as unwise is exactly the capacity we lack, which is why we have surrendered instead to an "unwise mindfulness," well-fed on words.
