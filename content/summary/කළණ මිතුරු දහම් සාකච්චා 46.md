## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named by title.
- **The Vacchagotta questions** are referred to: the *avyākata* — "does the perfected one exist after death? not exist? both? neither?" — all four rejected by the Buddha as wrongly framed.
- **A deva's question** is referred to: "Do you grieve? Do you delight?", to which the Buddha replied "What have I gained, that I should delight? What have I lost, that I should grieve?"
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *majjhe mantā na lippati* ("the sage clings not even to the middle").
- Terms used: *avyākata* (unanswered), *samudaya* / *nirodha* / *udaya-vaya*, *phassa* / *sparśa* (contact), *pemīma* (seeing), *pañcupādānakkhandha* (five clinging-aggregates), *moha* / *mahā-sāvadya* (grave-fault delusion), *dārāgänīma* (grasping/holding), *pävätma* / *nopävätma* (persistence / non-persistence). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a two-speaker session (teacher "Lasantha ayya" and a questioner); the transcript carries a transcriber's meta-line and a "[TO BE CONTINUED]" marker.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue using the darkness-and-light image to show that "exists" and "does not exist" arise together in one instant, that this is what cessation means, and that we are forever "standing in" seeing — the middle, which is contact.
- **The Vacchagotta questions.** The Buddha rejected all four alternatives about the perfected one after death, because one cannot say "exists" of what is not manifest, nor "does not exist" of what is not manifest — "I feel now" is said only if a feeling is manifest now.
- **Darkness and light arise together.** Lighting a lamp in the dark, ordinary experience says "darkness vanished, light appeared" in one instant; but subtly, the very notion "darkness" arises only *with* "light" — in pure darkness, never knowing light, there is no notion of darkness — so darkness and light are born together, and the darkness that arose right there is what we then call "vanished."
- **"Light" is only a name for darkness's absence.** One need not even bring in "light": it is merely the name given to "darkness gone," a synonym, so no new "light" is obtained; the notion "darkness" arose and that same thing was found absent, its ceasing lying *within* its arising.
- **Both extremes born together; nothing gained or lost.** Darkness-exists and darkness-not-exists arise together in one instant, the ceasing seen within the arising, not as a separate event — so nothing new is obtained and, since darkness never pre-existed, nothing really ceases. This is seeing arising *within* cessation: seeing the two extremes together in one instant *is* what "cessation" means, *samudaya* including both *udaya* and *vaya*, and in that cessation-knowledge no five-clinging-aggregates or formation is fabricated. As the deva was told, nothing was gained to delight in and nothing lost to grieve over — no one is born and no one dies — and the depth of Vacchagotta's question is that its fruit is only contentment, nothing gained.
- **Words must be set aside.** The Buddha himself said to lay the words aside to say this, and even the teacher's own phrase "it arises and ceases within the arising" is, strictly, wrong — to the wisdom established in the vision, no "arose / ceased" story applies — yet such words must be used to teach another; the reality is word-transcending, which is exactly what Vacchagotta pointed to.
- **Persistence is standing in "seeing."** What we have long received is that existence and non-existence are always obtained *together*, every moment — where feeling is obtained, non-feeling too — taken via "existence," the sensor working around the clock, and this very nature is persistence. The analysis is reversible: light is "darkness absent" and darkness is "light absent," one analysis split in two, and that splitting is exactly "conditioning." "How do you say light exists now?" — "on the basis of seeing"; seeing based on light, light based on the eye, and light's meaning being "darkness's absence." So one asserts "light exists" only from where one *stands*, standing in seeing — the scale-pivot that has the capacity to birth both sides, and that pivot-cause is seeing, which is contact.
- **Seeing is the five clinging-aggregates.** Remove both light and darkness and "seeing" still remains, permanent and sensor-like, working whether a corpse comes or not — we are stuck standing in seeing, and that standing *is* the five clinging-aggregates, "form exists, feeling…" arising upon it. We never notice we are standing in hearing or seeing: hearing is manifest to us only as *sound*, and we accept "hearing" through "there is a sound" — that immersion being the persistence, the "grasping" Vacchagotta meant. So "watching impermanence" is really failing to see that one is *standing* in hearing, trying to see hearing's impermanence through the sound obtained, the mind thrown to the object where one is caught.
- **The middle cannot be reached from the ends; contact is non-persistence.** Standing in hearing one says "sound exists" or "sound absent," but one can never look at the two ends and arrive at a middle, for there is no middle to be obtained; seeing this *contact* is seeing non-persistence — seen, it shows as non-persistence and saṁsāra stops; unseen, it is a persistence and saṁsāra runs. The Buddha noted that the "middle" is no place: one person sees it and saṁsāra runs, another sees the same and saṁsāra stops — so grave a spot is delusion, the very place we have planted our foot and call grasping. Examined, no two extremes are obtained, neither exists nor not-exists, so seeing the two extremes is not obtaining two but transcending both; and to that wisdom not even a middle stands to be clung to — *majjhe mantā na lippati* — a place so subtle that the instant a word is placed on it, it decays, the two seen finally to differ not by a hair's tenth, the two being one.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue uses the darkness-and-light image to unfold how "exists" and "does not exist" arise together in a single instant. It begins from Vacchagotta, whose four questions about the perfected one after death the Buddha rejected as wrongly framed, since one cannot call "existent" what is not manifest, nor "non-existent" what is not manifest — just as "I feel now" is said only when a feeling is manifest. The lamp makes this precise: lighting it in the dark, ordinary experience reports "darkness vanished, light appeared" in one instant, but the very notion "darkness" arises only together with "light," for in pure darkness that has never known light there is no notion of darkness at all; so darkness and light are born together, and the darkness that arose right there is exactly what we then call "vanished." "Light," the teacher stresses, is merely the name given to "darkness's absence," a synonym, so no new light is ever obtained, and since darkness never pre-existed to cease, nothing truly ceases either — the ceasing lies within the arising, and both extremes are born in one instant.

This, he shows, is what cessation means: seeing arising and ceasing, the exists-extreme and the not-exists-extreme, together in one instant, *samudaya* itself including both *udaya* and *vaya*, so that in the cessation-knowledge no clinging-aggregate or formation is fabricated, nothing new obtained and nothing really lost. The deva who asked whether the Buddha grieves or delights was told, "What have I gained, that I should delight? What have I lost, that I should grieve?" — for seeing the two extremes, nothing is gained and nothing let go, no one born and no one dying; and the depth of Vacchagotta's question is that its only fruit is contentment. All of this, the teacher insists, must be said with the words set aside, as the Buddha himself directed, and even his own formula "it ceases within its arising" is strictly wrong to the wisdom established in the vision, though such words are needed to teach another — the reality being word-transcending, which is just what Vacchagotta pointed to.

From here the dialogue locates our bondage precisely. What we have long received is that existence and non-existence are always obtained together, every moment — where feeling is, non-feeling too — taken through "existence," the sensor running around the clock, and this nature is persistence itself. The analysis is reversible, light being "darkness absent" and darkness "light absent," one analysis split into two, and that very splitting is what "conditioning" means. Asked how one says "light exists now," one answers "on the basis of seeing" — seeing based on light, light on the eye, light's meaning being darkness's absence — so one asserts "light exists" only from where one *stands*, standing in seeing, the scale-pivot with the capacity to birth both sides, and that pivot-cause is seeing, which is contact. Remove both light and darkness and "seeing" still remains, permanent and sensor-like, working whether a corpse comes or not, and this standing in seeing *is* the five clinging-aggregates upon which "form exists, feeling…" arise. We never notice we are standing in hearing, which is manifest to us only as sound so that we accept "hearing" through "there is a sound" — that immersion being the persistence, the "grasping" Vacchagotta named, and the reason "watching impermanence" so easily becomes merely trying to see hearing's impermanence through the obtained sound, the mind thrown to the object and caught there. One can never reach a middle from the two ends, for there is no middle to obtain; seeing this contact is seeing non-persistence, which stops saṁsāra, while not seeing it leaves a persistence that keeps saṁsāra running — so that, as the Buddha observed, one person sees the "middle" and saṁsāra runs while another sees the same and it stops, so grave a place is delusion. Examined, no two extremes are obtained, neither exists nor not-exists, so seeing the two is not obtaining two but transcending both, and to that wisdom not even a middle stands to be clung to — *majjhe mantā na lippati* — a place so fine that the moment a word is set on it, it decays, the two seen at last to differ not by a hair's tenth, the two being one.
