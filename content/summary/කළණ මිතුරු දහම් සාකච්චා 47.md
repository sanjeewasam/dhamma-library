## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named by title (the content is that of the **Khajjanīya Sutta**, SN 22.79).
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed:
  - *rūpaṁ rūpattāya saṅkhataṁ abhisaṅkharonti; vedanaṁ vedanattāya … saññaṁ saññattāya … viññāṇaṁ viññāṇattāya saṅkhataṁ abhisaṅkharonti* ("they construct the constructed form for the sake of form … feeling for feeling … perception for perception … consciousness for consciousness") — with *rūppana* ("constructing for the sake of a form"), *adukkhamasukha* (neither-painful-nor-pleasant), and the definitions of *vedanā* (from *vedeti*, "it feels") and *saññā* (from *sañjānāti*, "it recognizes").
  - *khīṇaṁ purāṇaṁ, navaṁ natthi sambhavaṁ* ("the old is exhausted, there is no arising of the new").
  - *anissito ca viharati* ("and he dwells independent").
  - the formless-attainment terms *na-saññī-nāsaññī* / *visaññā-saññī* (neither-perceiving-nor-non-perceiving; perception un-distorted).
- Terms used: *saṅkhāra* / *saṅkhata* / *abhisaṅkhāra*, *kāma- / bhava- / vibhava-taṇhā*, *saññā-nānatva* (diversity of perception), *adhivacana* (designation), *āsava* and *āsavakkhaya-ñāṇa* (influxes / knowledge of their destruction), *moha* (delusion), *vicikicchā* (doubt), *nissita* / *anissita* (dependent / independent). No sutta should be inferred.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue unpacking the *Khajjanīya* definition of formations — "they construct the constructed" — through the chair, the cake and science's endless refinement, and showing that perception is only a naming with nothing inside it.
- **"They construct the constructed."** *Rūpaṁ rūpattāya saṅkhataṁ abhisaṅkharonti* means a thing constructed by cause-and-effect is specially re-constructed *for the sake of arising again as that very thing* — form for the sake of form, feeling for feeling, perception for perception, consciousness for consciousness. Formation is "going to think, separating and knotting, out of not-knowing"; the original forming is unseen, that unseen forming is called a *saṅkhata*, and taking it we re-construct (*abhisaṅkharoti*) toward re-arising.
- **From craving to re-construction.** The first assembling of "chair" from a set of perceptions is *kāma-taṇhā*; taking that constructed "chair" and modifying it — plastic chair, then cushion chair, then iron chair, a piece added each time — is *abhisaṅkharaṇa*, which builds an influx we do not recognize as one, and which is *bhava-* and *vibhava-taṇhā*. The child's "cake" makes the same point: the one notion "cake," later met as a differently-shaped sweet, is told "this too is cake, this is orange-cake," so the single constructed notion merely diversifies into sub-types — a *saññā-nānatva*, perception re-constructed for perception's sake.
- **Taking it as "existent" is the error.** Whether or not one re-constructs, the moment "cake" was assembled it is already *bhava*; taking it as "existent" is the mistake, and the mistake is precisely the weakness of not seeing the forming.
- **Science is endless perception-diversity.** The chair "made of four elements," then "electron-proton-neutron," then "a special energy / quantum," then "some kind of wave" — the same thing modified and updated endlessly, because, not knowing it is a mirage that was never obtained, one can recognize it in ever new ways. This is why the world's "causes" have no end, a cause found by looking for a cause; whereas the Buddha's cause of suffering — craving — is the same now as twenty-five centuries ago, and a true cause once found can never be replaced.
- **Rūppana: form designated as form.** *Rūppana* is "constructing for the sake of a form": the mental factors — shape, colour, the firmness of earth — designate (*adhivacana*) "chair" as a form, and this designating of the form-body is what makes a form a form.
- **Perception is only a naming, with nothing inside.** *Saññā* means "it recognizes," but "recognizing" is not a meaning that comes to us — a thing-to-be-recognized seems to arise; the naming is only a recognizing-action, with no red-thing there. The proof: if recognition were a thing, teaching a child the words would suffice, yet "chair" the word has no shape or colour, and a foreigner (like a small child) told "bed" gets no image, so we must attach languages to forms because a language is only a set of perceptions, mere hints for recognizing with no things inside — we make the things ourselves, and "chair" yields chairs only once we apply our self-made form to it.
- **The mirage: recognizing the never-obtained as obtained.** Recognition is like a mirage — thinking of a never-obtained thing as obtained. The fault is not "the mirage-water was there and vanished," for going to "water absent" still accepts water; rightly, the very obtaining of water where nothing is obtained is the problem, and seeing that, one thinks neither "water present" nor "water absent" — perception seen as perception.
- **The arahant recognizes, but only as recognizing.** Walking the road he recognizes "vehicles," else he would hit lamp-posts, but knows recognizing is only recognizing, and that suffices for freedom; he steps aside from vehicles while we step aside because "a solid body would collide," and to him this very obtaining of a dense body and its impacts is seen *as* suffering — the human world being the getting of a body that suffers collisions and can be killed. He sees both sides, the world as world and its non-obtaining by wisdom, using it as one plays toy-cars with a child, his existence-craving severed. That we do not walk un-recognizing (or we would hit the posts) shows recognizing happens, yet the recognized things are not edited or re-constructed within — a state where one can say neither "there is a perception" nor "there is no perception," fitting the neither-perceiving-nor-non-perceiving attainment beyond which the arahant is spoken of.
- **Independent, like the lotus leaf.** *Anissito ca viharati*: in that wisdom nothing is settled upon; one functions like everyone else but, as a bonus, with the wisdom, un-settled, whereas the worldling is boxed into existent-and-non-existent and settles on things. The wise one associates only as association and acts only as action — using "bowl" merely as a designation for functioning, the mind never building "a thing of such shape" — walking in mud unsmeared, a water-drop on the lotus leaf, the lotus of wisdom blooming untouched though rooted in the water.
- **The old exhausted, no new arising.** *Khīṇaṁ purāṇaṁ, navaṁ natthi sambhavaṁ*: the old constructs are still used but hollowed by wisdom, since re-construction needs the *saṅkhata* to persist and wisdom empties it; like winnowing paddy and removing the germ so it cannot sprout, the knowledge of the destruction of the influxes stops the sprouting and empties even the seeds already made, being stronger than the defilements, so that one never again, even by mistake, sees a thing inside the recognizing. In practice one watches the naming *as* naming — "I am naming," which is attention, not a thing — for being unable to stop there and making it a thing is exactly existence-craving; and the "foolish" questions one asks are precisely how doubt wears away, their foolishness having to become visible bit by bit, since questions persisted only because delusion was unrecognized, and as delusion is seen the questions diminish day by day.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue works through the *Khajjanīya* definition of formations — "they construct the constructed form for the sake of form, feeling for feeling, perception for perception, consciousness for consciousness" — to show what re-construction is and how perception is only a naming with nothing inside it. A thing built by cause-and-effect, the teacher explains, is specially re-constructed for the sake of arising again as that very thing: the original forming goes unseen, that unseen forming is called a *saṅkhata*, and taking it, we re-construct toward re-arising. The chair illustrates it: assembling "chair" from a set of perceptions is craving's first move, and then modifying that constructed "chair" into plastic, cushion and iron chairs — a piece added each time — is re-construction, which quietly builds an influx we never recognize as one. The child's "cake" makes the same point, the single notion "cake" merely diversifying into orange-cake and honey-cake, a diversity of perception re-constructed for perception's sake; and the crucial correction is that the thing is already "existence" the moment it is assembled, so that taking it as "existent" is the very error, an error rooted in not seeing the forming.

Science, he shows, is nothing but this endless perception-diversity: the chair "of four elements," then of "electron-proton-neutron," then of "a special energy or quantum," then of "some kind of wave" — the same thing updated without end, because, not knowing it a never-obtained mirage, one can recognize it in ever new ways; and this is why the world's causes are endless, a cause found by looking for a cause, whereas the Buddha's cause of suffering, craving, is the same today as twenty-five centuries ago, a true cause that can never be replaced. Turning to *rūppana*, he shows that shape, colour and firmness designate "chair" as a form, this designating being what makes a form a form; and turning to *saññā*, he shows that "recognizing" is not a meaning that reaches us but only a naming-action, with no red-thing there. The proof is decisive: if recognition were a thing, the words alone would carry it, yet "chair" has no shape or colour and a foreigner or small child gets no image from the word, so languages must be attached to forms, a language being only a set of perceptual hints with no things inside — we make the things ourselves, and the word yields chairs only once our self-made form is applied. Recognition is thus a mirage, the obtaining of water where nothing is obtained, and the fault lies not in "the water vanished" — since "water absent" still accepts water — but in that very obtaining, seeing which one thinks neither "present" nor "absent."

Finally the teacher shows what this looks like as freedom. The arahant still recognizes "vehicles," or he would hit the lamp-posts, but knows recognizing is only recognizing, and that suffices; he steps aside as we do, but where we step aside because "a solid body would collide," he sees that very obtaining of a dense, collidable, killable body *as* suffering, seeing both the world as world and its non-obtaining by wisdom, and using it as one plays toy-cars with a child, his existence-craving cut. That we do not walk un-recognizing shows recognizing still happens, yet the recognized things are not edited within — a state describable neither as "there is perception" nor "there is none," fitting the neither-perceiving-nor-non-perceiving attainment the arahant is said to transcend. So he dwells independent, *anissito*, settling on nothing, functioning like everyone else but with the wisdom as a bonus, associating only as association and acting only as action, the mind never building "a thing of such shape" — walking in mud unsmeared, a water-drop on the lotus leaf. The old constructs are still used but hollowed by wisdom, for re-construction needs the constructed thing to persist and wisdom empties it: like removing the germ from paddy so it cannot sprout, the knowledge of the destruction of the influxes stops the sprouting and empties even the seeds already made, being stronger than the defilements, so that one never again sees a thing inside the recognizing. The old is exhausted and no new arises; and the very "foolish" questions one keeps asking are how doubt itself wears away, their foolishness becoming visible only as delusion is recognized, the questions diminishing day by day.
