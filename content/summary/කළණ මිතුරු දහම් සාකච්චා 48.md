## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- **Sunīta** and **Sopāka** are named (arahants of humble origin).
- Referred to: the episode of a grief-stricken nun to whom the Buddha said "establish mindfulness, sister"; and the simile of "a fingernail-tip of soil" (the seen being as the dirt under a nail against the great earth).
- Terms used: the three lower fetters — *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* (self-view), *vicikicchā* (doubt about the Four Noble Truths), *sīlabbata-parāmāsa* (clinging to rites and vows) — with *rāga* / *dveṣa* (lust / aversion), *sotāpanna* / *sakadāgāmī* / *anāgāmī* / arahant, *ñāṇa* / *darśana* (knowledge / vision), *manasikāra* (attention), *gōcara* (within range). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a two-speaker session; the transcript ends with a **duplicated block** (the "he is across" sentence repeated three times) and closes "[The End]."

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on why wisdom cannot be reduced to describable characteristics or a step-by-step course, and how the breaking of self-view, not a sequence of stages, is what lets lust and aversion wear away.
- **Wisdom is a vision, not a describable thing.** To give wisdom's "characteristics" would make wisdom into a thing again; but wisdom is where attention cannot operate — a standing beyond all attention, only a *vision*, not something done. As one plays "house" with a child, saying "house" without needing to re-think "there is no house here, only name-and-form," so the moment the word leaves one already dwells in the non-engaging nature, without a second thought.
- **The path develops by the degree of wisdom-use, continuously.** How much the wisdom is used determines the path's development, and the wisdom can be missed at times — which is why the Buddha recalled a grief-stricken nun with "establish mindfulness, sister." This mindfulness is a continuous alertness, not a part-time thing set aside during work and resumed at home; the skill is how quickly one returns to the wisdom, "like flexing a bent arm" — not by placing the mind on it but by a wakeful alertness always present, like a current or CCTV left ON whether images fall on the screen or not.
- **It is one knowledge, not a four-stage course.** Lust and aversion do not shake loose while self-view persists, for lust arises toward "my things" and aversion where things are "not mine" or "not as I want" — all from the view. So the *center* must break: self-view, the "me." When the three lower fetters break — self-view freed, doubt gone, all rites and vows dropped — the practice of abandoning lust and aversion begins, the "me / mine" feeling waning; and the labels *sotāpanna*, *sakadāgāmī* (lust and aversion a little tamed), *anāgāmī* (wholly gone) are only markers of the *degree* of wearing, not a four-year college one completes year by year. It all happens within one and the same knowledge.
- **The madman analogy.** We call a madman "mad" from a set of *characteristics*, never having experienced madness; likewise a stream-enterer is described only from a list of marks. But to truly see madness one must actually become mad — and once mad, the madman does not understand "mad"; so too, seeing the view *as* view, "a view" is not understood, and a nature one has actually become is not understood *as* itself. The stream-enterer's vision is thus like thinking about a madman without being mad — describing from outside — while only one who has become that nature has the vision of what it is.
- **Acting from characteristics is not the vision.** If one grasps the listed marks and thinks "ah, the stream-enterer thinks thus, catching neither extreme — if I think this way I am right," one is merely play-acting a role from learned characteristics. And are there even characteristics? In that un-knowable nature what mark could there be? To grant nibbāna characteristics would make wisdom, once again, an attention-built "format" one thinks from. But this is not a search for anyone's vision: if one has seen *how* one's own "me / mine" feeling arises, nothing more need be sought — that seeing itself is the vision.
- **Not a step-by-step course.** People ask whether it can be grasped by proceeding at the ordinary cognitive level, stage by stage, or whether the Buddha would have made it a course — first year, second, then graduation — as with school and campus. But it cannot be reached that way ("sotāpanna = first year, then the next stage, then anāgāmī"), which is precisely why the seen is likened to a fingernail-tip of soil rather than a course one completes; if it could be learned step by step, any clever doctor or engineer could do it, and though Sunīta and Sopāka understood it, it is not a matter of brainpower proceeding stage by stage.
- **The raft and the far shore.** Those still on the raft, watching people on the far shore, imagine "the one across must think thus, must feel thus," building dreams; but the one who has crossed has no notion of the raft, of the people on it, or of what he imagined from this side, and does not even feel "the far shore" specially — he is simply there.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue turns on a single warning: wisdom cannot be reduced to describable characteristics or to a step-by-step course. To spell out wisdom's "characteristics," the teacher says, would make wisdom a thing again, whereas wisdom is exactly where attention cannot operate — a standing beyond all attention, only a vision, not something done. He likens it to playing "house" with a child: one says "house" and plays along without needing to re-think each time "there is no house here, only name-and-form," so that the instant the word is spoken one already dwells in the non-engaging nature, without a second thought. The path, he explains, develops according to how much the wisdom is used, and the wisdom can be missed — which is why the Buddha recalled a grief-stricken nun with "establish mindfulness, sister" — so this mindfulness is a continuous alertness, not something set aside during work and resumed at home; the skill is how swiftly one returns to it, like flexing a bent arm, not by placing the mind on it but by a wakeful alertness always present, like a current left ON whether or not images fall on the screen.

Pressed on whether the path unfolds in the labelled sequence — the three fetters, then lust and aversion — the teacher answers that it is one knowledge, not a four-stage course. Lust and aversion cannot shake loose while self-view persists, for lust arises toward "my things" and aversion where things are "not mine," all springing from the view; so the center, the "me," must break, and when the three lower fetters go — self-view freed, doubt gone, all rites and vows dropped — the wearing of lust and aversion begins, the "me / mine" feeling thinning. The names sotāpanna, sakadāgāmī and anāgāmī merely mark the *degree* of this wearing, not years of a college completed one by one; it is all one and the same knowledge. He then sharpens the point with the madman: we call a madman "mad" only from a set of characteristics, never having experienced madness, just as a stream-enterer is described only from marks — but to truly see madness one must become mad, and once mad one does not understand "mad," so a nature one has actually become is never understood as itself. The stream-enterer's vision is thus like thinking about a madman without being mad, a description from outside, while only one who has become that nature has the real vision.

To grasp the listed marks and think "the stream-enterer thinks thus — if I think this way, catching neither extreme, I am right" is therefore only to play-act a role from learned characteristics; and in truth there are no characteristics at all, for in that un-knowable nature no mark can stand, and to grant nibbāna characteristics would make wisdom once more an attention-built format one merely thinks from. But this, the teacher insists, is not a search for anyone else's vision: if one has seen *how* one's own "me / mine" feeling arises, that seeing itself is the vision, and nothing further need be hunted. So the common hope of reaching it by a step-by-step course — first year the stream-enterer, then the next stage, then the non-returner, graduating like school and campus — is refuted, for it cannot be reached that way, which is exactly why the seen is likened to a fingernail-tip of soil against the great earth rather than a course to be completed; were it learnable stage by stage any clever doctor or engineer could master it, and though Sunīta and Sopāka realized it, it is no matter of brainpower proceeding by degrees. He closes with the raft: those still aboard, watching the far shore, imagine how the one across must think and feel, building dreams, but the one who has crossed has no notion of the raft or of his earlier imaginings and does not even feel "the far shore" specially — he is simply there.
