## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named by title.
- **The Anāthapiṇḍikovāda teaching** is referred to (Ven. **Sāriputta**'s deep discourse to **Anāthapiṇḍika** on his deathbed — showing binding to eye, contact, feeling, form, the elements and consciousness each in turn, MN 143).
- **A Majjhima statement** is referred to: "*phassa* is one extreme, *phassa-samudaya* is one extreme, *phassa-nirodha* is the middle."
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *cetanāhaṁ bhikkhave kammaṁ vadāmi* ("intention, monks, is what I call kamma"); *cakkhuñ ca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu-viññāṇaṁ, tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* ("dependent on eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises; the meeting of the three is contact"); the *paṭiccasamuppāda* chain *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā, saṅkhāra-paccayā viññāṇaṁ, viññāṇa-paccayā nāma-rūpaṁ*; and the formula "neither the same one nor another."
- Terms: *phassa* / *sparśa* (contact), *phassa-samudaya* / *phassa-nirodha*, *paccayā* / *pratya* (condition), *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, *gōcara* / *agōcara* (within / beyond consciousness's range), *uccheda* (annihilation extreme), *cetanā* / *kamma*, *diṭṭhi* (view), *manāyatana* / *cakkhāyatana* (mind-base / eye-base), *avabodha* (comprehension) versus *tērum-ganīma* (mere understanding), *kalyāṇa-mitta*, *aparappaccaya* (independent of another). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a two-/three-speaker session (Man, Woman, and "the lady/nōnā"); the transcript opens with a transcriber's line and is heavy with affirmation-fillers.
- **The talk's own title** names its theme: "falling into the extremes of contact (phassa) and contact-origin (phassa-samudaya)."

## Main dhamma points
- A "good-friend" dialogue on why contact is caught neither in its conditions nor apart from them, why it is beyond consciousness's range so that the very "I" is born from it, and how "seeing with the eye" and "not seeing" are equally views.
- **Contact sits at the "because-of" point.** Because of matchbox and matchstick we speak of "rubbing" — a contact — and this is where contact-origin occurs: contact is engaged exactly where the condition is engaged, the "because-of" (*paccayā*) place itself being contact. For eye and form, seeing; for ear and sound, hearing.
- **Contact is in neither cause.** "Rubbing" belongs neither to the matchbox nor to the matchstick, so contact was in neither cause; a real "contact-thing" could not simply sit in the matchbox to be transferred, or it would have to catch fire. The rubbing itself gives rise to "the two as cause" and "fire as fruit" together, not-before-not-after, so contact never pre-exists in the conditions.
- **The two extremes named.** Taking "contact was in the two causes" is one extreme — *phassa-samudaya*, the arising-from-conditions side; and taking "since the causes do not own it, contact exists *without* the conditions, on its own, spontaneously" is the other extreme. Neither holds: not because of the causes, and not without them — and even to call it "independent" is the second extreme, where words tangle.
- **Cessation is non-arising; the whole chain hangs on this.** *Phassa-nirodha* means contact was never born anywhere, the point where the "cause-and-fruit exists" story dissolves, both cause and fruit being un-arisen and having arisen only with contact as their root. Not seeing contact *as* contact is precisely why a cause-and-effect dhamma gets assembled — the "because-of" point *is* contact — so seeing it rightly, no such dhamma can be constructed, and the whole of dependent origination turns on it. To re-take "contact is in the cause," in order to "examine dependent origination," is only to re-bind oneself to the cause through ignorance and make the cause permanent again.
- **Contact is beyond consciousness, and "I" am born from it.** Whatever the mind constructs shows itself as real, so until contact is comprehended one cannot escape it, because the very "I" is born from *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*. A self born from contact can never see contact's fault, and the world can never prove the meeting-of-the-three "false," since the mind itself is assembled by it — consciousness cannot be called false, for wherever a mind is constructed the constructing shows as real, and "I" am a fruit of that constructing-delusion, part of it.
- **Why the Buddha affirms it.** Asked, the Buddha says "yes, this too exists," but he taught the meeting-of-the-three to be *escaped*, as one warns "there is a pit ahead, mind it" — not to be memorized but to be stepped around. So people parrot "the meeting of the three, the Buddha said so," because only the graspable ends — the two conditions and the fruit — are within consciousness's range as *words*, while the reality, contact's cessation, is caught only by a wordless vision.
- **Understanding is not comprehension.** The danger is to mistake *understanding* for *comprehension*, which are as far apart as sky and earth: taking the given points as facts and reciting them is exactly how the two are confused. The Buddha need not have come to tell the world that eye and form exist and a meeting-of-the-three occurs — that is the world's ordinary process — but pointed it out to show *how* a "world" is assembled with contact primary, so that seeing it one escapes. Because the "because-of" is un-understood and beyond range, we grasp only the two sides as words and never the connecting tie, which must be caught not by memorizing nor from the two ends but by the un-thinkable *paccayā* itself.
- **Neither the same one nor another.** Asked whether the dead man himself is reborn, one can say neither "he himself" nor "another" — as fire is made neither by the matchbox-and-stick alone nor by something apart — so birth cannot be spoken and is beyond anyone's range. Logic can never grasp it, for logic offers only "the same man reborn" or "someone else," while the Buddha transcends both, and the world lives in these two extremes.
- **The "absence" is never obtained.** "No nose-and-breath, so no feeling": the "not" we use is only the absence of the obtained thing, and the annihilation extreme was never really obtained or known — so one must first see that this "absence" was never got, without which "the absent is not there" is meaningless and "it exists" equally meaningless, "exists" getting its meaning only from "the absent is not there." As real forgetting cannot be understood at all and "I forgot" is understood only through the remembered thing, both "forgot" and "remembered" come from the same thing.
- **The stupa: intention the same, view different.** Thinking of the Ruvanvälisǟya stupa raises a wholesome intention with merit, just as recalling any good deed does; and at home one thinks only of Ruvanvälisǟya, not of other stupas, so the intentions near the stupa and at home do not differ, both being *cetanā*, both kamma — "I saw the stupa with my own eye" being the deed. The difference is only the *view*: near the stupa "eye" is primary and the stupa becomes "real" through *cakkhuñ ca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu-viññāṇaṁ, tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, while at home "mind" is primary and "I cannot see it" becomes real — both equally views, the stupa having arisen in the mind in either case. So there is no special joy in having gone nor sorrow in not going; the "me who went and saw" is the constructed self.
- **The deep lay teaching, and the self-carried path.** This "persistence is non-persistence" is the teaching Ven. Sāriputta gave the dying stream-enterer Anāthapiṇḍika — showing the binding to eye, contact, feeling, form, elements and consciousness each in turn — so deep a discourse that even he had never heard the like, and which shows a layperson can comprehend the dhamma; the same principle discussed plainly here is what that difficult sutta contains. Once seen, the stream-enterer proceeds on his own toward the further path, independent of another; and though such things cannot be grasped without a good friend, one is grateful to have found one — the session closing in mutual refuge in the Triple Gem.

## Summary

This "good-friend" dialogue takes as its explicit theme the two extremes of contact and contact-origin, and works from the matchbox and matchstick. Because of the two, we speak of "rubbing" — a contact — and this is where contact-origin occurs, the "because-of" point itself being contact, just as for eye and form there is seeing and for ear and sound there is hearing. But "rubbing" belongs to neither the matchbox nor the matchstick, so contact was in neither cause; a real contact-thing could not sit in the matchbox waiting to be transferred, or it would catch fire, and in truth the rubbing itself gives rise to "the two as cause" and "fire as fruit" together, not before and not after, so contact never pre-exists in the conditions. From this the two extremes are named: to say "contact was in the two causes" is contact-origin, the arising-from-conditions side, and to say "since the causes do not own it, contact exists on its own" is the other extreme — neither holding, for it is neither because of the causes nor without them, and even to call it "independent" is the second extreme, where words tangle. Cessation, then, is non-arising: contact was never born anywhere, both cause and fruit being un-arisen and arising only with contact as root, so that not seeing contact as contact is precisely why a cause-and-effect dhamma is assembled — and the whole of dependent origination turns on this, so that to re-take "contact is in the cause" is only to re-bind oneself to the cause through ignorance.

The dialogue then presses why this cannot be escaped by ordinary means: whatever the mind constructs shows itself as real, and the very "I" is born from the meeting-of-the-three, so a self born from contact can never see contact's fault, and the world can never prove the meeting-of-the-three false, since the mind itself is assembled by it. This is why the Buddha, asked, affirms "yes, this too exists," yet taught it to be *escaped*, as one warns of a pit ahead to be stepped around rather than memorized — and why people instead parrot the phrase, since only the graspable ends, the two conditions and the fruit, fall within consciousness's range as words, while the reality is caught only by a wordless vision. The great danger, the speakers agree, is mistaking understanding for comprehension, as far apart as sky and earth: the Buddha need not have told the world that eye and form exist, for that is its ordinary process, but pointed it out to show *how* a world is assembled with contact primary, so that seeing it one escapes. And because the connecting "because-of" is beyond range, we grasp only the two sides as words and never the tie, which must be caught not by memorizing nor from the two ends but by the un-thinkable condition itself. So too "neither the same one nor another": as fire is made neither by the matchbox-and-stick alone nor by anything apart, the dead man is neither reborn as himself nor as another, birth being unspeakable and beyond logic, which can only offer "the same man" or "someone else" while the Buddha transcends both.

Finally the talk turns practical through the "absence" and the stupa. The "not" in "no breath, so no feeling" is only the absence of the obtained thing, and since the annihilation extreme was never obtained, one must first see that this absence was never got, without which neither "the absent is not there" nor "it exists" has meaning — just as real forgetting cannot be understood at all and "I forgot" comes only from the remembered thing. The Ruvanvälisǟya stupa then shows that thinking of it raises the same wholesome intention whether one stands before it or sits at home, both being intention and kamma, "I saw it with my own eye" being the deed; the only difference is the view — near the stupa "eye" is primary and it becomes real through eye-consciousness and the meeting-of-the-three, at home "mind" is primary and "I cannot see it" becomes real — both equally views, so that there is no special joy in going nor sorrow in staying, the "me who went and saw" being the constructed self. This, the speakers note, is the very teaching Ven. Sāriputta gave the dying stream-enterer Anāthapiṇḍika, showing his binding to eye, contact, feeling, form, elements and consciousness each in turn — a discourse so deep that even he had never heard its like, and one proving that a layperson can comprehend the dhamma, the same principle set out plainly here being what that difficult sutta contains. Once seen, the stream-enterer carries the path forward on his own, independent of another; and though none of this can be grasped without a good friend, one is grateful to have found one — the session ending in mutual refuge in the Triple Gem.
