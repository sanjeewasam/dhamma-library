## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *cakkhuñ ca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu-viññāṇaṁ, tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* ("dependent on eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises; the meeting of the three is contact").
- Terms used: *abhisañcetayita* / *abhisaṅkhāra* (further-fashioned thought), *saṅkhata* (the constructed), *cetanā* / *kamma* (intention / action), *purāṇa-kamma* / *nava-kamma* (old / new kamma), *kamma-phala-vāda* (the doctrine of kamma-and-result), *nāma-rūpa*, *diṭṭhi* (view), *sakkāya* (self / the existing-body view), *gōcara* / *agōcara* (within / beyond consciousness's range), *nirodha* (cessation), *kalyāṇa-mitta* (good friend). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a two-speaker session (teacher and a questioner addressed as "mahaththaya").

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue showing that "an eye exists" is understood only as a construction arising with seeing, that kamma is this present taking-as-existent rather than a past deed, and that "seeing" itself is a view beyond consciousness's range.
- **"An eye exists" is a construction arising with seeing.** "The eye existed before / exists now" is understood only when the notion "eye" arises — only with seeing — just as breath is understood not because it is there but when it is thought, together with its feeling. The understanding and the seeing-action build together, not-before-not-after, and because we do not see the fault in that building we take it "as existent," exactly as we miss the fault when feeling and perception are knotted to the breath.
- **This is re-fashioning (abhisañcetayita).** "There is an eye" is itself a constructed intention: the day one first understood "there is an eye," that is where it was constructed, seeing and the seen constructed together, and within that grasping "an eye and a form" was built. That constructed thing is then repeatedly modified and updated — *abhisañcetayita*, re-thinking an already-thought thing, though we feel not "re-thinking a thought" but "thinking a real thing" — and because the first constructing went unseen, "there is an eye" is built as a wrong comprehension.
- **Kamma is the taking-as-existent, not the past deed.** The very grasp "the eye exists" is the kamma; the grasp itself has become kamma, and this is the *saṅkhata*, the re-fashioning. The teacher does not think the Buddha, teaching a vision to *transcend* kamma, meant "the present is the fruiting of past deeds" — that is the doctrine of kamma-and-result, which happens anyway without a Buddha; a Buddha comes to free beings caught in that doctrine, and "new kamma" too names the present intention, the taking, not a deed-store.
- **"Did you come?" — the action is proven only by knotted things.** Asked on the road "did you come?", one proves "I came" by reciting "I got off the bus, passed the shop, this was there" — citing the attention-known *things* as evidence for the action. So an action is thought about only from those knotted things, and because one cannot see attention *as* attention, the thought-things are taken as *seen* things: the thought becomes "a seeing," and "a seeing" becomes real.
- **Two results of not seeing attention as attention.** First, "I saw" gets fixed — "that shop, that tree, I saw it" is constructed, a kamma born of the wrong view; second, that fixed "saw" is then used to confirm "I came," the seen-set re-applied elsewhere as evidence, though nothing was actually seen there. So "coming," the action, is never understood-as-coming by consciousness — it is never within consciousness's range — and only the *nāma-rūpa*, the knotted shop and tree and form, are; yet those are not recognized as nāma-rūpa, because of the wrong view "there is a seeing," so they are taken as "things seen."
- **"Seeing" itself is a view.** We are born, from birth, holding the "seeing" view; not recognizing it as a view, we knot seen-things to it, and once seen-things are knotted, "seeing is a view" can never be caught, because seeing becomes real (things seen exist, therefore seeing is real). This is exactly the knot where kamma forms — a thicket-knot in which the seen becomes real, so seeing becomes real, so "the seer" on this side is established: "I exist" fixed makes "I see" real, the seer real makes seeing real, and "it is seen by me" becomes real. So it can never be understood, for the very understanding "there is a seeing and a seer" *is* the view; what is felt as "understanding" is precisely why the view is real. We cannot see "seeing" as a view because we have never experienced non-seeing and so have no notion what "seeing" is — its un-knowability being exactly why "seeing is a view" goes unseen, a view being beyond consciousness's range.
- **The meeting of the three, and the self born from the view.** When eye and form meet, "seeing fixed" is understood — *cakkhuñ ca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu-viññāṇaṁ, tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* — so both "there is an eye" and "there is a form" are taken as existent, through not knowing that seeing is only a view. Form is utterly irrelevant to seeing, yet not knowing non-seeing we cannot know what seeing is. Seeing this view *as* a view, "I arise," and where "I" arises from the view neither a seer nor a seeing can arise, both blocked; but seeing is confirmed by seen-things and does not own them, which consciousness can never realize, so "seeing is a view" stays veiled by the very things knotted to it — these being the incidental results of not seeing the view, a nonexistent story of "a form exists, an eye that sees it exists," and "eye" itself a thing obtained only through not-knowing.
- **Realization, not rote; and the good friend.** To hold "seeing is un-understandable" as a mere doctrine-point is to keep the raft on one's head — people talk holding the raft aloft, saying "I see the dhamma" while only reciting the analyses, which does not purify the vision. For the point to be *realized* one must see a view *as* a view; only then is the un-knowability realized, and only then cessation; until then one has merely "heard the talk." And "matching it to a sutta" — finding the passage, memorizing its sentences and page — cannot by itself wear away lust and aversion, for if it genuinely fits the dhamma the vision must form *within* oneself; were sutta-matching enough, no good friend would be needed. But since seeing-within cannot be done from a sutta, a good friend is needed to draw out the *meaning* and show it — and as the meaning is drawn out it transcends the point, increasingly seen to be beyond words, so that the "raft" image fits the dhamma exactly: it was given to untie, but since one cannot untie it alone, a good friend is needed.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue works from the ordinary "there is an eye" down to the recognition that "seeing" is itself a view. "The eye existed before, exists now," the teacher shows, is understood only when the notion "eye" arises, and only with seeing — just as breath is understood not because it is present but when it is thought, together with its feeling. The understanding and the seeing-action build together, not before and not after, and because we never see the fault in that building we take it as existent. This is exactly *abhisañcetayita*: "there is an eye" is a constructed intention, built the day one first understood it, seeing and the seen constructed together, and then repeatedly modified and updated, though we feel not "re-thinking a thought" but "thinking a real thing." And the crucial turn is that this grasp *is* the kamma — the very taking-as-existent, not a past deed fruiting; the teacher rejects reading the Buddha's teaching as the doctrine of kamma-and-result, since a past deed fruits anyway without a Buddha, whereas a Buddha comes to free beings from that doctrine, "new kamma" naming the present intention itself.

He makes the point concrete with "did you come?": to prove "I came" one recounts getting off the bus and passing the shop, citing the attention-known things as evidence for the action — so an action is thought about only from knotted things, and because one cannot see attention as attention, the thought-things are taken as *seen* things, the thought becoming "a seeing" and "a seeing" becoming real. Two results follow from not seeing attention as attention: "I saw" gets fixed as a kamma of the wrong view, and that fixed "saw" is then used to confirm "I came," the seen-set re-applied as evidence though nothing was actually seen. So the action, "coming," is never understood as coming by consciousness — it is never within its range — and only the *nāma-rūpa* are, yet these are not recognized as such because of the wrong view "there is a seeing," and so are taken as things seen. From here the teacher exposes the deepest point: "seeing" itself is a view we hold from birth, and not recognizing it as a view we knot seen-things to it, whereupon "seeing is a view" can never be caught, because seeing becomes real once things seen exist — a thicket-knot in which the seen is real, so seeing is real, so "the seer" is established, "I exist" making "I see" real and "it is seen by me" real. The very "understanding" that there is a seeing and a seer *is* the view, and since we have never experienced non-seeing we have no notion what seeing is, its un-knowability being exactly why it goes unseen, a view lying beyond consciousness's range.

When eye and form meet, then, "seeing fixed" is understood through the meeting of the three, so both eye and form are taken as existent by not knowing that seeing is only a view — form being utterly irrelevant to seeing, which is only seeing. Seeing this view as a view, "I arise," and where "I" arises from the view neither seer nor seeing can arise; but seeing is confirmed by seen-things it does not own, which consciousness can never realize, so the view stays veiled by the very things knotted to it, spinning a nonexistent story of "a form exists, an eye that sees it exists," with "eye" itself obtained only through not-knowing. Finally the teacher warns that to hold all this as a mere doctrine is to keep the raft on one's head, reciting the analyses while claiming to "see the dhamma," which never purifies the vision; the point is realized only when a view is seen *as* a view, and only then is cessation realized. Matching it to a sutta — memorizing the passage and page — cannot by itself wear away lust and aversion, for if it truly fits the dhamma the vision must form within oneself; were sutta-matching enough, no good friend would be needed, but since seeing-within cannot be had from a sutta, a good friend is needed to draw out the meaning and show it — and as the meaning is drawn out it is increasingly seen to transcend all words, so that the raft, given to untie and impossible to untie alone, fits the dhamma exactly.
