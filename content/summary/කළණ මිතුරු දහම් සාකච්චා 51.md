## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue arguing that the wisdom cannot be gotten from the scriptures alone, that "watching arising-and-ceasing" is an effort contradicting the effortless path, and that "sound" and "taste" are only perceptions — a distortion that is nothing but language.
- **Non-arising cessation has nothing arising-and-ceasing to watch.** Showing a bubble's ceasing and seeing "the bubble never arose" are two different things; arahants are not watching arising-and-ceasing, for the Buddha calls the path effortless, and an effort cannot exist within an effortless nature — watching "arising and ceasing" *is* an effort. So a wisdom established in *anutpāda-nirodha*, the un-arising nature, has no arising-and-ceasing to observe.
- **A knowledge cannot be written, so texts distort it.** A knowledge cannot be put in a book or turned into words, and writing an effortless nature turns it into an effort, so it inevitably reads as "arising-and-ceasing," a wrong interpretation matching the writer's level — exactly as one who has not seen it says "the five clinging-aggregates arise and cease." What must be realized is that the arising-story and the ceasing-story are seen to be *not obtained*, which is why the five similes — foam, bubble, mirage, plantain-with-no-heartwood, magic-show — were given, all showing not-obtained, consciousness itself a magic-show of which nothing is obtained.
- **Ordinary "arising and ceasing" needs no dhamma.** That a formed bubble bursts, that black hair greys and skin wrinkles, is obvious without any teaching; were the Buddha merely saying "things arise and cease," his teaching would be no different from common knowledge — hence *mā piṭaka-sampadāya*, do not take it merely because it stands in the scriptures.
- **The good friend is first, above the scriptures.** If the dhamma could be shown from the scriptures, no good friend would be needed; but the Buddha placed the good friend first, and the Sāsana endures on a good friend precisely because this cannot be gotten from the scriptures. The scriptures are words; wisdom is an extraordinary insight beyond words, and only one in whom that vision has arisen can, using the words, get another to "let go the words and grasp the meaning" — taking the meaning being exactly what no one can do alone, which is the good friend's whole function: to draw out the meaning beyond the words and free one from being stuck in them.
- **Later scribes inevitably wrote "arising and ceasing."** The texts were written long after, not by the Buddha nor at the first councils, passed person to person; the Buddha knew writing would distort it and forbade rendering it into Sanskrit for the same reason. A writer who had not seen the vision could generate no "non-arising" idea and could only write "arising and ceasing" — so one must ask a good friend whether the meaning, taken thus, actually works.
- **The scriptures then read as self-contradictory.** In one place the path is "effortless, nothing to do, nowhere to stand, no striving"; in another "you must watch the arising and watch the ceasing," which is effort, a standing, a striving — a contradiction that forces one either to doubt the Buddha or the scribe, and getting boxed into pondering it is exactly the fault of taking the text at face value, lumping the scriptures together with the good friend and making them primary, which the Buddha forbade.
- **Rise-and-fall knowledge, rightly.** For a worldling in ignorance, arising-and-ceasing — the birth and death of the five clinging-aggregates — is real, and that is saṁsāra; but a wisdom that has seen non-arising, transcending both stories, sees in that very seeing the *knowledge of rise-and-fall*: while ignorance operates an arising-ceasing story appears, yet to one who has seen ignorance as ignorance, that story is the knot of saṁsāra recognized *as a distortion*. So the arahant sees origin and cessation together, but as the fabrication seen for a fabrication, not as a real "watch it arise, watch it cease."
- **The cup, and the perception behind it.** The worldling, not discerning form, feeling, perception, formations and consciousness, makes the recognized "cup" a thing, and once a thing it has birth, decay, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief and despair, a change — all valid for one who took it as existent; but the arahant recognizes the cup only as perception, a mere recognition with no thing, so no birth or death applies. We distinguish "the cat's sound" and "the crow's sound" as two and think of a cat and a crow — but within the recognizing intention no "heard quality" is actually experienced now; thinking "crow's sound" delivers only a thought, "such a thing exists, this is what I recall," never a felt sound. As thinking "mango taste" would spare one buying a mango if a thought could deliver the taste, so no sound and no taste is ever obtained by the mind, which can never go beyond intention.
- **Perception is only language; the mirage rightly seen.** So "cat's sound" and "crow's sound" are both merely recognized "suches," fabricated, and "I heard it there" and "it was recalled now" are equally fabricated — for recognition itself is the distortion, and recognition is nothing but naming, which is why the Buddha said perception's distortion is language. Seeing this fabrication, there is no arising-and-ceasing story in perception; the mirage was so called not to show "this water is absent" — since "water absent" still posits water — but to show that the very *obtaining* of water is a non-obtaining. That formed water vanishes any ordinary person grasps without a dhamma; the Buddha pointed instead to perception, "water" being only a recognition and not a thing recognized, that recognition itself a non-obtaining and never a recognition *of* an obtained thing — sound being merely one of the perceptions (form-, taste-, smell-, tangible-, dhamma-perception) never once actually obtained.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue mounts a sustained argument that the wisdom cannot be gotten from the scriptures alone, and that the common reading of "watch the arising and ceasing" is a distortion. Showing a bubble's ceasing and seeing that the bubble never arose, the teacher begins, are two different things; arahants are not watching arising-and-ceasing, for the Buddha calls the path effortless and an effort cannot exist within an effortless nature — watching arising and ceasing being itself an effort. A wisdom established in non-arising cessation therefore has nothing arising-and-ceasing to observe. This, he says, is exactly why one should not take the teaching merely because it stands in the scriptures: a knowledge cannot be written or turned into words, and writing an effortless nature turns it into an effort, so it inevitably comes out as "arising and ceasing," a wrong reading matching the writer's level — just as one who has not seen it says "the five clinging-aggregates arise and cease." What must be realized is that the arising-story and the ceasing-story are seen to be *not obtained*, which is why the five similes — foam, bubble, mirage, plantain-with-no-heartwood, magic-show — were all given to show not-obtained, consciousness itself a magic-show. And that a formed bubble bursts, or black hair greys, needs no teaching at all, so were the Buddha merely saying "things arise and cease," his teaching would be nothing special.

From this follows the polemic's core: the good friend stands first, above the scriptures. If the dhamma could be shown from the texts, no good friend would be needed, but the Buddha placed the good friend first and said the Sāsana endures on a good friend, precisely because this cannot be had from the scriptures, which are only words, whereas wisdom is an extraordinary insight beyond words that only one who has seen it can transmit, using the words to get another to "let go the words and grasp the meaning" — the taking of the meaning being exactly what no one can do alone. The teacher stresses that the texts were written long after the Buddha, passed hand to hand, and that he forbade rendering them into Sanskrit knowing writing would distort them; a scribe who had not seen the vision could generate no "non-arising" idea and could only write "arising and ceasing," so that the scriptures end up self-contradictory — effortless in one place, demanding one "watch the arising and ceasing" in another — and getting boxed into such contradictions is exactly the fault of taking the text at face value and lumping it together with the good friend as primary, which the Buddha forbade. Rightly reconciled, arising-and-ceasing is real for a worldling in ignorance, that being saṁsāra, but to a wisdom that has seen non-arising the same is recognized as a distortion, and *that* seeing is the true knowledge of rise-and-fall, in which origin and cessation are seen together as a fabrication known for a fabrication.

Finally the teacher turns the whole point onto perception. The worldling makes the recognized "cup" a thing, and once a thing it carries birth, decay, death and all the sufferings, valid for one who took it as existent; but the arahant recognizes it only as perception, a mere recognition with no thing, so no birth or death applies. Demonstrating with the crow's and the cat's sounds, he shows that although we take them as two and think of a cat and a crow, within the recognizing intention no "heard quality" is actually experienced — thinking "crow's sound" yields only a thought, "such a thing exists, this is what I recall," never a felt sound, just as thinking "mango taste" would spare one buying a mango if a thought could deliver taste. Seeing this, there is no arising-and-ceasing story in perception at all; and the mirage was so called not to show that "this water is absent" — since "water absent" still posits water — but to show that the very obtaining of water is a non-obtaining, "water" being only a recognition and never a thing recognized, that recognition itself a non-obtaining, and sound merely one of the perceptions never once actually obtained.
