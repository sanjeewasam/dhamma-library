## Sutta referred to
- **Kālakārāma Sutta** (කාලකාරාම සූත්‍රය) — named: "one does not conceive of an un-seen" (*nodiṭṭhaṁ* — the Buddha has no notion that "there are un-seen things").
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti* ("when this is, that is"); *diṭṭhe diṭṭhaṁ* ("in the seen, only the seen").
- Terms used: *diṭṭhi* (view), *uccheda* / *śāśvata* (annihilationism / eternalism), *pemīma* / *nopemīma* (seeing / non-seeing), *jātyandha* (born-blind), *nitya-saññā* (permanence-perception), *phassa* / *sparśa* (contact), *saṁsāra* / *nibbāna*. No further sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** the transcript is essentially a single continuous exposition (a questioner's replies, if any, are not preserved).

## Main dhamma points
- A "good-friend" exposition showing that "no chair here" is really eternalism in disguise, that non-seeing is never experienced so seeing has no birth, and that the un-obtainable middle is contact — the one place from which both saṁsāra and its ending arise.
- **The theory is our own view.** "When this is, that is" must be seen to be our own view: we live in "a form exists, therefore it is seen," and the question is which view we fall into when we understand "no form."
- **"No chair" leans to annihilation but is really eternalism.** Asked "is a chair there?", one is utterly sure "no chair is seen," which at first glance leans to annihilation, "not seen" made real; but one must draw out, from within that very "not seen," how it is in fact eternalism — for in the world "existence" has value only from "non-existence," "breath felt now" getting its value only from "not felt before," so that we are caught assuming "there is a not-feeling," and "not seen" likewise harbours an eternalism.
- **The born-blind test.** To speak of "the chair's absence" one must use the very "chair" and must have *seeing*; a born-blind man can decide neither "chair present" nor "chair absent." So "no chair now" is said with seeing fully active — seeing is ON both when the chair is present and when it is absent, and we never once "put a hand on" seeing itself.
- **Non-seeing is never experienced; seeing has no birth.** Therefore non-seeing can never be experienced in life, and from birth it never was — the *Kālakārāma Sutta*'s "one does not conceive of an un-seen," whereas we assume there are un-seen things; even "darkness, cannot see" is a darkness *seen*. This shatters the arising-and-ceasing story: as a bulb must first be OFF to be seen turning ON, "seeing" could arise from a "non-seeing" only if non-seeing were ever obtained — but it is not, so "now seeing arose in me" can never truly be experienced, birth can never be experienced, and with no arising there is nothing to see cease.
- **"Arising and ceasing" is the opposite of impermanence.** We do not even see the place we stand — always in seeing, ON — and not knowing we are ON, "it was not seen" is built, which then relativizes into "the non-seeing vanished, seeing arose," the whole chain running on; so "arising-and-ceasing" is as opposite to impermanence as sky to earth.
- **Since non-seeing is never obtained, "seeing" has no meaning.** As "ON" is meaningful only if "OFF" is known, and OFF can never be got, "seeing" can never be got either — so we have never truly had a notion of "seeing," and at the born-blind level we too are born-blind. In *diṭṭhe diṭṭhaṁ*, "in the seen only the seen," it is not that a seeing exists and one settles in it: seeing is empty of seeing, exactly matching the born-blind.
- **The sound test and the permanence-perception.** Stop talking, ask "do you hear?", and one says "not heard" — but that is said holding a *remembered* sound, comparing "the earlier sound is now gone"; were hearing-capacity absent one could not even register "my sound is now absent," so hearing is fully present. We do not even know the place we stand, in hearing, and "not heard" is said in ignorance — from which the other side is proven, that neither "hearing exists" nor "hearing is absent" is rightly known, nothing left over, this seeing itself being a knowledge. This is the permanence-perception recognized: we are always *in* hearing.
- **Do not knot the two sides or call the sound false.** One should not reconcile the two by declaring "the heard sound is false" — rather see the sound *as* sound, perception as perception, since perception is utterly irrelevant to feeling and sound irrelevant to hearing. The "when this is, that is" theory formed by knotting an unrelated thing to it, and deceived, we hold "hearing" and break the heard-things to escape hearing, whereupon the middle becomes real, "ear and sound are false" veils hearing, and "discerning hearing" is never even obtained — which is why meditation done without understanding, suppressing the mind-object and the ear-sound, leaves one caught in the "hearing" knot, unable to find the middle or even to realize one is caught in it.
- **The middle seen rightly is the two ends un-arisen.** Breaking the two sides can never reveal the middle, which only veils; but seeing the middle rightly, one sees the two ends as a *non-arisen* place — being in hearing-only, no non-hearing ever existed and no non-seeing ever existed, so "seeing" too, whose meaning came only from "non-seeing is not there," is not obtained. Both realized as un-obtained, it is realized not that the two extremes *vanish* but that both were *non-arisen*; and it is not-knowing that makes one try to break the two ends to find a middle.
- **Contact is the un-obtainable middle; one place, two roads.** Breaking the matchbox and matchstick (neither holds fire) leads to "so no fire arises," but one must see *both causes* are un-arisen; breaking cause into fruit can never reach the middle, because the middle is contact — the "rubbing," which is neither matchbox, matchstick, nor fire. If rubbing does not happen fire does not arise, and if fire does not arise no "cause for fire" arises — both un-arisen — so until contact is seen the non-arising of cause-and-fruit is never realized. For the worldling, contact is the saṁsāra-trap; for the arahant, seeing that same contact, it becomes a bridge to nibbāna — the two happening from one place, for through contact saṁsāra is built, yet if contact is caught by wisdom saṁsāra is not built there; nibbāna is not apart from saṁsāra, nor is it saṁsāra.

## Summary

This "good-friend" exposition, delivered as a single continuous flow, sets out to show that "no chair here" is really eternalism in disguise, that non-seeing is never experienced, and that the un-obtainable middle is contact. The theory "when this is, that is," the teacher begins, must be recognized as our own view: we live in "a form exists, therefore it is seen," and the real question is which view we fall into when we understand "no form." Asked whether a chair is present, one is utterly certain "no chair is seen," which at first glance leans to annihilation, "not seen" made real — but one must draw out from within that very "not seen" how it is in fact eternalism, since in the world "existence" has value only from "non-existence," "breath felt now" getting its value only from "not felt before," so that we are always caught assuming "there is a not-feeling." The born-blind man clinches it: to speak of "the chair's absence" one must use the very chair and must have seeing, whereas a born-blind man can decide neither presence nor absence; so "no chair now" is said with seeing fully ON, ON alike when the chair is present and absent, and we never once put a hand on seeing itself.

From this the teacher draws the decisive point that non-seeing can never be experienced in life and never was from birth — the *Kālakārāma Sutta*'s "one does not conceive of an un-seen," though we assume un-seen things exist, and even "darkness, cannot see" is a darkness seen. This shatters the arising-and-ceasing story: as a bulb must first be OFF to be seen turning ON, "seeing" could arise from a "non-seeing" only if non-seeing were ever obtained, but it never is, so "now seeing arose in me" can never truly be experienced, birth can never be experienced, and with no arising there is nothing to see cease — making "arising-and-ceasing" as opposite to impermanence as sky to earth. Indeed we never see the place we stand, always in seeing and ON, and not knowing we are ON we build "it was not seen," which then relativizes into "the non-seeing vanished, seeing arose." And since non-seeing is never obtained, "seeing" itself has no meaning, as ON is meaningful only when OFF is known — so we have never truly had a notion of seeing, and at the born-blind level we too are born-blind, exactly as "in the seen only the seen" means not that a seeing exists and one settles in it, but that seeing is empty of seeing.

The sound test carries the same lesson: stop talking, ask "do you hear?", and one says "not heard," but only by holding a remembered sound and comparing "the earlier one is now gone" — for were hearing absent one could not even register "my sound is now absent," so hearing is fully present, and "not heard" is said in ignorance of the very place one stands. From this the other side is proven, that neither "hearing exists" nor "hearing is absent" is rightly known, nothing left over, the seeing itself a knowledge and the permanence-perception recognized: we are always in hearing. One should not, therefore, reconcile the two sides by calling the heard sound false, but see the sound as sound, perception as perception, since it is by knotting an unrelated thing that the "when this is, that is" theory formed; deceived, we hold "hearing" and break the heard-things to escape it, whereupon the middle becomes real and "discerning hearing" is never obtained — the very trap of meditation done without understanding. Breaking the two sides can never reveal the middle, but seeing the middle rightly shows the two ends as a non-arisen nature, both realized as un-obtained not by vanishing but as never having arisen. Finally the matchbox and matchstick show that the middle is contact — the rubbing that is neither matchbox, matchstick, nor fire — so that if rubbing does not happen fire does not arise, and if fire does not arise no cause for fire arises, both un-arisen; until contact is seen the non-arising of cause-and-fruit is never realized. For the worldling contact is the saṁsāra-trap; for the arahant, seeing that same contact, it becomes a bridge to nibbāna — the two arising from one place, for through contact saṁsāra is built, yet if contact is caught by wisdom saṁsāra is not built there, nibbāna being neither apart from saṁsāra nor saṁsāra itself.
