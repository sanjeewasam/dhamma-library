## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue drawing on the *Manasē Māyāva* teaching of duality, showing that "bed" and "earth" are mere words with no thing inside, that recognizing knots un-related marks to a word (which is "conditioning"), and that examining a word to its end reveals its meaning-emptiness.
- **Consciousness's nature is duality.** Because of recognizing, a duality forms — consciousness splitting everything into two sides where there are none, which is exactly the illusion; form, feeling, perception and formations have no two-sidedness in themselves, and it is the naming of those by consciousness that makes "two sides." Consciousness turns everything into "things."
- **Nothing can be done to an arisen consciousness; only recognize it.** A consciousness once arisen is already arisen-and-ceased, and from it the incidental fruits, the latent defilements, are produced — being caught by it is "worse than being struck by a demon." One cannot stop the defilement-production; the only thing to do is to recognize consciousness rightly, whereupon it has no cause to arise. We are bound to it because we do not know its magical marks, and seeing how the two-sidedness is constructed is the only way the non-arising shows, for what is truly there is only a non-arisen nature that appears as arising because we do not understand.
- **Sound is recognized only against silence.** Hearing a sound, we think "hearing arose newly, just now," but the unseen side is that the mind is sitting immersed in "no sound"; to recognize "a sound," one must first know silence, and one who does not know silence cannot even think "a sound." So "a sound now" is recognized relative to the "not" we stand in, never to anything in this present moment, and the mind can never recognize anything alone, always against its opposite; the present consciousness is meanwhile knotted to a fresh, unrelated consciousness already arisen-and-ceased.
- **"Bed" is a mere word with no thing inside.** What is recognized as "bed" — hardness, box-shape, cross-slats, softness, colour — is none of it present within the word; a child who never heard "bed" gets no image, the marks coming from our own stored influxes. Examined separately, "brown" alone cannot yield "bed," nor "square," nor "hard," so the marks have no connection to the word, yet knotting them to "bed" makes a real bed — and this knotting is exactly "conditioning," the dependently-arisen arising, a knot that cannot be put into words.
- **"Earth," examined, is meaning-empty.** Describe "earth" as "a flat, solid surface" and the two marks, flatness and solidity, are separate recognitions, neither yielding "earth" alone (all flat or all hard things are not earth), so neither has any connection to the word. Why then do we call these two "earth" only now? Because we dwell immersed in "earth," having once knotted "hardness-and-flatness = earth" in memory — that immersion being craving, the value we grant "earth" resting on that knot, which is "conditioning," un-verbalizable. One with no such clinging gets no "earth" from "hard" or "flat"; "earth" is a knot we tied.
- **The point of examining is to see meaning-emptiness.** "Earth" can be given no definition that equals it, so it is empty of meaning, and to see this by wisdom is the whole purpose — *yathābhūta-ñāṇa-dassana*, the non-fitting, the un-definability. But words are themselves the meanings the world assigns, so the meaning-emptiness of a definition can never be seen *through* definitions — like trying to cut the fish on the pig's own back — and the knot will not loosen while the childhood-assigned value stays concreted onto it. So the dhamma is "a heap of words" given precisely to see the words' own pithlessness, no easy task, impossible to reach by taking a conclusion from a discourse; only a subtle, wordless flash shows it — "look upstream" (*paṭisota*), against the current, for it is by giving meaning to every arising that we float downstream with the saṁsāric flood, and seeing the constructs' meaning-emptiness is how formations become un-formed: *vissaṅkhāra-gataṁ cittaṁ*.
- **Cessation within origination.** Seeing that "earth" has no describable meaning, wisdom stops right there — "earth is earth, meaning-empty" — and then no measure of beautiful or ugly, near or far can stand, no attachment or aversion possible, one turned as if to stone at the end of the search. And what is understood must not be even one of the words spoken — not "earth," not "hardness," not even "emptiness" — for the comprehension is the very meaning-emptiness, and even "empty" cannot be taken as a word, since where no meaning ever arose "empty" too cannot be said.
- **Function without clinging, like water on a lotus leaf.** For a consciousness to arise an attention "this is earth" must occur, and where even that cannot happen at most "earth" is thought with no further mind forced up — this being formation discerned within formation, *yathābhūta-ñāṇa-dassana*; beyond it no formation about "bed" or "earth" can be made, since "I sit on the earth" already assigns "earth" the meaning "a flat thing to sit on." Yet one still uses "earth," knowing it as earth, to function and be extinguished — shuffling the mere word-units to live in the world, a water-drop on a lotus leaf, no meaning left to cling to or collide with though all the world's words are spoken. This is not "not thinking or not talking"; cessation is realized *within* origination, a thing arising yet only emptiness found within the arising — and even "empty" is one more word to drop. The vision must dawn as immediately as "bed" comes to mind when the word is heard; it can be said many ways, but from whatever ford one enters, it is the one path to nibbāna.

## Summary

This long two-speaker good-friend dialogue draws on the *Manasē Māyāva* teaching of duality to show that "bed" and "earth" are mere words with no thing inside them. Consciousness's very nature, the teacher begins, is to split everything into two sides where there are none — the illusion itself — for form, feeling, perception and formations have no two-sidedness, and it is their naming by consciousness that manufactures "two sides." Nothing can be done to a consciousness once arisen, since it is already arisen-and-ceased and from it the latent defilements are produced, being caught by it "worse than a demon's blow"; the only thing to do is to recognize consciousness rightly, whereupon it has no cause to arise, and seeing how the two-sidedness is constructed is the sole way the non-arising shows, since what is truly there is only a non-arisen nature that appears as arising through our not understanding. Sound proves it: hearing a sound we think "hearing arose just now," but the unseen side is that the mind sits immersed in "no sound," and one who does not know silence cannot even think "a sound," so "a sound now" is recognized only against the "not" we stand in, never against anything in this moment — the mind never recognizing anything alone.

The heart of the talk is the patient examination of "bed" and then "earth." What is recognized as "bed" — hardness, box-shape, softness, colour — is none of it in the word, a child who never heard it getting no image; and examined separately, "brown" cannot yield "bed," nor "square," nor "hard," so the marks have no connection to the word, yet knotting them makes a real bed, and this knotting is exactly what "conditioning" means, a knot that cannot be put into words. "Earth" carries the argument furthest: described as "a flat, solid surface," its two marks are separate recognitions, neither yielding "earth" alone since all flat or hard things are not earth, so we call these two "earth" only because we dwell immersed in it, having once knotted "hardness-and-flatness = earth" in memory — that immersion being craving, and the knot being "conditioning," which no words can analyse. One with no such clinging gets no "earth" from "hard" or "flat"; "earth" is simply a knot we tied. And the whole point of the examination, the teacher stresses, is to see the *meaning-emptiness*: "earth" can be given no definition that equals it, so it is empty of meaning, and to see this by wisdom is *yathābhūta-ñāṇa-dassana*. But since words are themselves the meanings the world assigns, the meaning-emptiness can never be seen *through* definitions — like cutting the fish on the pig's own back — and the knot will not loosen while the childhood value stays concreted onto it; the dhamma is therefore "a heap of words" given precisely to reveal the words' own pithlessness, reachable by no conclusion drawn from a discourse but only by a wordless flash, "looking upstream" against the very current down which we have floated by giving meaning to every arising.

Seeing that "earth" has no describable meaning, wisdom stops right there, and then no measure of beautiful or ugly, near or far can stand and no attachment or aversion is possible, one turned as if to stone at the end of the search — and what is understood must not be even one of the words spoken, not "earth," not "hardness," not even "emptiness," for the comprehension is the meaning-emptiness itself, "empty" being un-sayable where no meaning ever arose.
