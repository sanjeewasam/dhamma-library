## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *piṭṭhāda nosita, vāyamda nokara* ("neither standing nor striving [I crossed over]") — the flood-crossing image; and the Buddha's statement that "if anything were permanent even for an instant, I would not call it suffering."
- Terms used: *samudaya* / *nirodha* (arising / cessation), *nitya-saññā* (permanence-perception), *anicca* (impermanence), *gōcara* / *agōcara* (within / beyond consciousness's range), *prajñā* (wisdom), *saṁsāra-dukkha*, *nirodha-magga* (the path of cessation). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a short two-speaker session (teacher and a questioner).

## Main dhamma points
- A short two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on how seeing arising rightly *is* cessation, on why the permanence-perception is beyond consciousness's range, and on why the path cannot be built by thought.
- **Seeing arising rightly is cessation.** Seeing *samudaya* correctly is itself what was called *nirodha* — there is no separate cessation.
- **Permanence is never within consciousness's range, so there is nothing impermanent to watch.** The permanence-perception never becomes an object for consciousness, so there is nothing "impermanent" to observe; if a "permanent" thing were to *become*, then "not-becoming" would be impermanence, but since permanence is never within the mind's range no permanent thing is ever established, so neither "it became" nor "it did not become" can be said — there is no permanence at all.
- **The great earth.** We are entirely certain the great earth will not split and drop us, so it is "permanent" to us, yet that very certainty is beyond the mind's range; seeing the permanence-perception rightly, it appears neither "become" nor "not-become," for there is no permanence. This is what grows even for lay householders — "since no thing became, what is permanent?" — and seeing arising, cessation is realized, that vision being the developing of the path of cessation. It is why so many suppose they are "watching impermanence," when they are only watching a thing built by not knowing the true permanence-perception — a matter to ponder for a year, not a month.
- **Even the dhamma-analysis re-becomes a consciousness.** Taking the true nature as a "matter" and examining it by wisdom does not bring the nature itself but builds yet another consciousness, the mind re-forming through the dhamma; and the analysis rightly shows this fault — that even this dhamma becomes a cause for consciousness to arise again. So the nature in which "no arising-story is engaged" can neither be thought into being nor made to appear by "not-thinking"; it grows only as a tree grows — watered rightly, it grows — and can be built by no method.
- **Neither standing nor striving.** For this reason one crosses over "neither standing nor striving."

## Summary

This short two-speaker good-friend dialogue turns on a single equation: seeing arising rightly *is* cessation, there being no separate *nirodha*. The teacher shows that the permanence-perception is never within consciousness's range, so there is nothing "impermanent" to watch — for if a "permanent" thing were to become, then "not-becoming" would be impermanence, but since permanence never becomes an object for the mind, no permanent thing is ever established, and neither "it became" nor "it did not become" can be said, there being no permanence at all. The great earth makes it concrete: we are utterly certain it will not split beneath us, so it stands as "permanent" to us, yet that very certainty is beyond the mind's range, and seeing the permanence-perception rightly it appears neither become nor not-become. This, he notes, is what grows even for lay householders — "since no thing became, what is permanent?" — and seeing arising, cessation is realized, that seeing being the very developing of the path, which is why so many mistakenly suppose they are "watching impermanence" while only watching a thing built from not knowing the true permanence-perception, a matter worth pondering for a year rather than a month. Finally the teacher observes that even taking the true nature as a "matter" and examining it by wisdom does not deliver the nature but builds another consciousness, the mind re-forming through the dhamma — the analysis itself rightly exposing this fault, that even the dhamma becomes a cause for consciousness to arise again — so that the nature in which no arising-story is engaged can neither be thought into being nor made to appear by not-thinking, but grows only as a tree grows when watered rightly. And for this reason, he concludes, one crosses the flood neither standing nor striving.
