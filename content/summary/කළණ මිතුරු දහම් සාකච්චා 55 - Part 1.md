## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *paccuppannaṁ yo dhammaṁ tattha tattha vipassati* ("one discerns each presently-arisen phenomenon there and there" — from the Bhaddekaratta verse); *imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti* ("when this is, that is"); and the four *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen-heard-sensed-cognized) as *maññanā* (conceivings).
- Terms used: *muta* (the sensed), *maññanā* (conceiving), *manasikāra* (attention), *viññāṇa* (consciousness), *nimitta* (marks / signs), *rūpa-upādāna* (clinging to form), *saṅkhāra* / *sabbe saṅkhārā*, *nāma-mātra* (mere name), *artha-śūnyatā* (meaning-emptiness). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a two-speaker session (teacher "Lasantha ayya" and a questioner); this is **Part 1** of a two-part talk.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue built entirely on the salt-taste, showing that the sensed cannot be conveyed by word, writing or form, that "salt" heard becomes an image only from our own stored marks, and that the word itself is meaning-empty.
- **The sensed cannot be communicated.** Hearing, seeing, sensing and knowing are the four ways to express something to another, yet the felt quality cannot be written or described — the salt-taste being the clearest case, sayable in no words and by no writing.
- **Words carry none of the experience.** Asked "what do you feel?" one gives two words — "feeling" and "a hard / salty quality" — which the listener understands; but knowing those two words, the listener gets not a fraction of the experienced quality, so within "I knew" nothing was actually known, only mere words with no capacity to convey the felt quality.
- **Recall contains no quality either.** Recalling "I felt salt-taste before," an attention arises, but the previously-experienced quality is present in no thought-moment — even now, thinking "salt taste," no taste is felt, the eating-time and the thinking-time equally being only thought, and in neither does the quality appear. It is a mere knowing, not a word, not an attention, not the thing we suppose.
- **Conceiving is about a thing not in the thought.** It arises by attention-condition, yet is not *in* the attention; a conceiving needs attention but is about a thing not within the thinking — which is exactly *maññanā*, and the Buddha's seen-heard-sensed-cognized are these conceivings, existing nowhere and arising nowhere. The felt quality cannot be shown by holding up a salt-crystal, cannot be written, cannot be spoken — so it is only something one can conceive, and can never be said to "exist" or "not exist" anywhere; the sensed is a nature un-thinkable, un-writable, never found in any image we conceive.
- **One consciousness at a time.** Saying "salt taste," "salt" and "taste" are two attentions, two consciousnesses, since one can never think both in the same instant; so thinking "salt," only the one "salt" consciousness arose. Taken alone, thinking "salt" makes a white, crystalline image arise — and nothing more than that recognition can be known.
- **The image comes from our stored marks, not the word.** A two-year-old hearing "salt" gets no such image, only a bare word — so the same sound becomes an image for us and a mere word for the child because of the marks (round, white, square) we have stored and attached to it, which the child lacks. The image therefore arises from the *nimitta*, not from the word, and word and image are two things knotted together.
- **Form-clinging so tight the knot is invisible.** We cannot even see that "word" and "image" are two — to us "salt" delivers a whole crystalline image directly, to the child only a word — and this is *rūpa-upādāna*, so tight that the "mere word" side is invisible and the salt-crystal appears the instant it is said. This is why we cannot sort out the formation: we have knotted all formations to forms so tightly the knot cannot be seen, like milk mixed with water that even a swan cannot separate — so "chair" delivers a chair and life never reveals the knot within.
- **The word is meaning-empty; one point sees all.** There is no thing inside the word "salt" to make it real or an image; establishing the present-moment on this word and practicing the vision, one need say nothing of the whole, for seeing it from one point is to see all formations. The stored marks were in us and not one was in the word, so asked to describe "the meaning of the word 'salt'," one finds none — not because it was merely asserted but because one has now clearly *seen* that the marks are not in it, not even "round" or "white" being inside the word.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue, the first of two parts, is built entirely on the taste of salt. Hearing, seeing, sensing and knowing, the teacher begins, are the four ways we express something to another, yet the felt quality can be neither written nor described — salt-taste being the clearest case, sayable in no words. Asked "what do you feel?" one gives two words, "feeling" and "a hard, salty quality," which the listener understands; but knowing those two words, the listener receives not a fraction of the experienced quality, so that within "I knew" nothing was actually known, only mere words. Recall fares no better: remembering "I felt salt-taste before," an attention arises, but the experienced quality is present in no thought-moment — even now, thinking "salt taste," no taste is felt, the eating-time and the thinking-time equally being only thought, and in neither does the quality appear. It is only a mere knowing, not a word, not an attention, not the thing we suppose. From this the teacher draws that the felt quality arises by attention-condition yet is not *in* the attention, being about a thing not within the thinking — which is exactly conceiving, *maññanā* — and that the Buddha's seen-heard-sensed-cognized are these conceivings, existing nowhere: the quality cannot be shown by holding up a salt-crystal, cannot be written, cannot be spoken, so it is only something one can conceive, and can never be said to exist or not exist anywhere.

He then turns to how the illusion is built. Saying "salt taste," "salt" and "taste" are two attentions, two consciousnesses, since one cannot think both in the same instant, so thinking "salt" only the one "salt" consciousness arises, and taken alone it makes a white, crystalline image arise, with nothing more than that recognition knowable. But a two-year-old hearing "salt" gets no such image, only a bare word — so the same sound becomes an image for us and a mere word for the child because of the marks we have stored and attached to it, which the child lacks; the image therefore arises from the marks, not the word, and word and image are two knotted things. And this is the decisive point: we cannot even see that word and image are two, for to us "salt" delivers a whole crystalline image directly while to the child it is only a word — this being form-clinging so tight that the "mere word" side is invisible, like milk mixed with water that even a swan cannot separate, so that "chair" delivers a chair and life never reveals the knot. There is, in the end, no thing inside the word "salt" to make it real, and the marks were all in us with not one in the word; so asked to describe the word's meaning, one finds none — not by assertion but by clearly seeing that not even "round" or "white" is inside it — and establishing the present-moment on this single word, seeing it from one point, one sees all formations.
