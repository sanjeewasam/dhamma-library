## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named by title.
- Reference (a modern work, not a sutta): **Ven. Katukurunde Ñāṇananda**, who states in a discourse that the Buddha taught the whole true-nature is distorted by *perception*, and perception is distorted by *language*.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *kataṁ karaṇīyaṁ, nāparaṁ itthattāya* ("what was to be done is done, there is no more of this state"); *gacchanto vā gacchāmī ti pajānāti* ("walking, one knows 'I am walking'" — Satipaṭṭhāna); and the flood-crossing image, "neither striving nor standing."
- Terms used: *shabda- / nāma-mātra* (mere sound / mere name), *maññanā* (conceiving), *artha-śūnyatā* (meaning-emptiness), *saṅkhāra* / *sabbe saṅkhārā*, *saṅkhārupekkhā-ñāṇa* (knowledge of equanimity toward formations), *saññā-vikṛti* / *bhāṣā* (distortion of perception / language), *viññāṇa*, *paccuppanna* (present-moment), *phassa* / *sparśa* (contact), *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*, *nirāyāsa* (effortless), *yathābhūta* (as-it-really-is). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a two-speaker session (teacher "Lasantha ayya" and a questioner); this is **Part 2**, completing the salt-taste talk.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue completing the salt-taste analysis, showing that knowing is empty of a thing known, that language is the distortion of perception, and that "salt," "taste" and "tongue" are three consciousnesses that never meet, so contact occurs only through delusion.
- **Knowing is empty of the known.** A word is a mere sound-unit that can be given no meaning, so within "knowing" no thing is actually known — the knowing is empty; knowing is only conceiving, called *maññanā* precisely because it has no meaning. Within "salt" the imagined white, crystalline, hard qualities are not there, so "salt" is wholly empty, and wisdom sees "within knowing, the known-thing was never obtained" — knowing is empty of knowing.
- **All things are alike mere words.** Among "black rock, water, fire," no difference or description can be shown — all are mere sound-units — and seeing by wisdom that no thing is obtained within the word is impermanence, cessation, non-arising, which is the meaning given to formations: as the plantain has no heartwood however far it is peeled, so all formations are the same. One who sees this has no lust or aversion toward formations and sees all formations impermanent by one wisdom — what the Buddha called *saṅkhārupekkhā-ñāṇa*, in which all formations are seen with equanimity, an equanimity that is not the worldly "neither pleasure nor pain" but the seeing of "no special quality," rose-flower and dung-heap alike, water and fire alike, no meaning at all.
- **Language is the distortion of perception.** As Ven. Ñāṇananda states, the whole true-nature is distorted by perception, and perception by language: perception does not appear to us *as* perception because language has veiled the mere-perception fact — language having no meaning, being only a meaning-empty perception-unit, yet the "mere perception" being hidden by that very language. When a taste is felt there is a recognition, and to that we give the name "salt," and to it "taste" — two names given by language.
- **One consciousness at a time; salt, taste and tongue never meet.** The "salt" consciousness ceases, then the "taste" consciousness arises; the felt quality never comes through the word "taste," a child who never tasted it thinking "taste" but getting no quality, so "taste" is conceiving a non-existent thing with no meaning. "Salt" seen as salt and "taste" as taste, each arising and ceasing, can be knotted only in the present-moment illusion, for at the instant "taste" arises "salt" has already arisen-and-ceased, so the two can never meet — and keeping the mind in the present-moment, the knot cannot be tied.
- **Contact occurs only through delusion.** It is by not seeing these arising-ceasing consciousnesses that "salt" is not seen as salt and "taste" not as taste, and the two get knotted, whereupon a sense-base "tongue" is born by the view — "tongue" too being only colour and shape, giving a child no image, and irrelevant to the ceased "salt" consciousness — so that "salt and tongue" knotted yields "the tongue felt salt-taste," and adding "taste" makes three consciousnesses knotted into "I felt salt-taste on my tongue," a knot that can never really happen. Contact lasts only while delusion lasts, being the not-seeing of arising-ceasing consciousnesses; and even the "feeling" consciousness, when it arises, finds no salt or taste present, so in the present moment no felt-thing is obtained and no feeling arises even for an instant — to the wisdom that saw the seen-heard-sensed-cognized as conceivings, no "it was felt, heard, seen" ever engages even momentarily, this being the yathābhūta vision, discerned by subtle insight rather than merely thought.
- **Nothing to remove or cling to; neither standing nor striving.** Seeing this, nothing is found to discard and nothing to cling to, so one crosses the flood "neither striving nor standing," as the Buddha answered the deva: nothing being obtained, there is nothing to strive for or stand on, no "thing not in the salt" to imagine and no "taste in the salt" to cling to — neither clinging nor collision, neither discarding nor grasping, both absent, effortless, *kataṁ karaṇīyaṁ, nāparaṁ itthattāya*, what was to be done done because there is nothing to grasp, collide with, discard or hold.
- **Function normally, unattached.** As one practices, a day comes when this is seen with the very attention that arises, not a following mind, with a mirror-reflection's immediacy needing no separate thought. One can still know anything fearlessly — "chair," anything — yet within the knowing no known-thing is obtained, and there the cessation-wisdom establishes. This is not being like a stone, recognizing nothing; one knows everything exactly as it is, the rock as rock, eating when eating, sleeping knowing "sleeping" — *gacchanto vā gacchāmī ti pajānāti*, walking, one knows one walks — just that, whereas we instead knot; so one keeps watching thus, constantly recalling these points, and that is enough.

## Summary

This second part completes the salt-taste analysis by pressing that knowing is empty of a thing known. A word, the teacher shows, is a mere sound-unit that can be given no meaning, so within "knowing" no thing is actually known, the knowing itself being empty and only a conceiving — called *maññanā* precisely because it has no meaning — for within "salt" the imagined white, crystalline, hard qualities are not present, and wisdom sees that "within knowing, the known-thing was never obtained." Extended to all things, "black rock, water, fire" show no difference or description, all being mere sound-units, and to see by wisdom that no thing is obtained within a word is impermanence, cessation, non-arising — the very meaning given to formations, which are like the plantain that has no heartwood however far it is peeled. One who sees this has neither lust nor aversion toward formations and sees all formations impermanent by one wisdom, the *saṅkhārupekkhā-ñāṇa*, an equanimity not of "neither pleasure nor pain" but of "no special quality," in which rose-flower and dung-heap, water and fire, are alike. And the root of the whole problem, as Ven. Ñāṇananda states, is that the true-nature is distorted by perception and perception by language, which veils the fact that perception is merely perception, so that to a felt recognition we give the name "salt," and to it "taste," by language.

The heart of the talk is that salt, taste and tongue never meet. The "salt" consciousness ceases before the "taste" consciousness arises, and the felt quality never comes through the word "taste" — a child thinking "taste" gets no quality — so "taste" is conceiving a non-existent thing; "salt" seen as salt and "taste" as taste, each arising and ceasing, can be knotted only in the present-moment illusion, for at the instant "taste" arises "salt" is already ceased, and keeping the mind in the present-moment the knot cannot be tied. It is only by not seeing these arising-ceasing consciousnesses that the two are knotted and a "tongue" born by the view, so that "the tongue felt salt-taste" is a knot of three consciousnesses that can never really happen — contact lasting only while delusion lasts, and even the "feeling" consciousness, when it arises, finding no salt or taste present, so that no felt-thing is obtained in the present moment and, to the wisdom that saw the four as conceivings, no "it was felt, heard, seen" ever engages even for an instant. Seeing this, nothing is found to discard and nothing to cling to, so one crosses the flood "neither striving nor standing," nothing being obtained to strive for or stand on — *kataṁ karaṇīyaṁ, nāparaṁ itthattāya*, what was to be done is done. And this is not being like a stone: one still knows everything exactly as it is, eating when eating and knowing "sleeping" when asleep, as Satipaṭṭhāna's "walking, one knows one walks" — the difference being only that where we knot, the wise one, within the very knowing, finds no known-thing and lets the cessation-wisdom establish, keeping watch thus and recalling these points, which is enough.
