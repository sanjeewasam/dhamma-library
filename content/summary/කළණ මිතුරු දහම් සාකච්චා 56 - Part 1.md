## Sutta referred to

- Not explicitly named, though the Pāli phrases "cetanāhaṃ bhikkhave kammaṃ vadāmī" (I call cetanā 'kamma', monks) and "diṭṭha suta muta" are referenced. The Buddha-quality of "tādī" (such-ness, seeing things as they are) is also cited.

## Main dhamma points

- When a sense impression arises (e.g., hearing), viññāṇa does not stay with the bare impression. It immediately constructs two fabricated causes beyond the impression itself — the thing sensed and the one sensing (e.g., "the ground" and "my body" for bodily feeling).
- Nothing in the body moves without **cetanā** (intention) first arising. The body sits still indefinitely unless a cetanā arises to move it. This demonstrates that action (kamma) is cetanā, not a physical event.
- The quality of **tādī** — "whatever is such, it is just so" (yamak' evaṃ tathā eva) — means staying with what is actually present rather than going beyond it into fabrication.
- **Vedanā/muta** (feeling/the sensed): when one merely thinks "salt taste" without actual contact, the characteristic quality (gati) does not arise. The mind knows it is only thinking. But when actually tasting salt, the quality appears. The teacher asks: what is the difference between these two situations? This reveals the nature of **mano viññāṇa** versus actual sense viññāṇa.
- The feeling-mind (dænīma) must be isolated from its fabricated objects. One must first see it as just a citta (mind-moment), then see that the fabricated causes (the thing felt and the feeler) are not found within the feeling itself.
- Ajahn Chah is quoted: the mind is like a thief — when you see the thief's tricks, the thief hides (i.e., the fabricating mind ceases to arise). Similarly, Māra flees when recognised.
- The practice of **pirisin dækīma** (clear comprehension) is seeing the boru (falseness/fabrication) as boru — not finding a truth, but seeing through the illusion repeatedly until the capacity to see through it strengthens.
- The teacher emphasises that one should not look for a separate "non-arising" to discover — it is already visible within the feeling-mind when one stops going beyond it into the two fabricated endpoints.

## Summary

This discussion focuses on how viññāṇa operates at the moment of sense contact, specifically bodily feeling (muta/kāya viññāṇa). The teacher begins by pointing out that when we say "I feel something," we have already gone beyond the bare feeling and constructed two things that are not found within the feeling itself: the object felt (e.g., the ground) and the one feeling (e.g., my body). This construction is so automatic that we never notice the feeling as a bare mind-moment.

He demonstrates this through cetanā: nothing in the body moves — not a hand, not a foot — without an intention first arising. If one sits still, the body remains exactly as placed for hours, because no cetanā has arisen to change it. This is what the Buddha meant by "cetanāhaṃ bhikkhave kammaṃ vadāmī" — kamma is cetanā, not the physical movement. Even turning to look when someone speaks involves a cetanā arising first, though this happens so quickly it is usually missed.

The teacher then introduces an experiential exercise. He asks the listener to think "salt taste" right now, while sitting. The mind thinks it, but the actual quality (gati) of saltiness does not appear. The mind knows perfectly well it is only thinking, not tasting. Yet when one actually puts salt on the tongue, the quality arises and one says "I taste salt." The teacher asks: what is the difference? In both cases one "thinks about salt taste," but in only one does the quality manifest. This reveals that mano viññāṇa (the thinking mind) and actual sense viññāṇa operate differently — and that the thinking mind cannot produce the actual sensory quality by intention alone.

The practical instruction is to isolate the "feeling" mind — to see it as just a citta, separate from the fabricated "thing felt" and "person feeling." When one stays with the bare feeling without jumping to the two constructed endpoints, one begins to see that those endpoints were never in the feeling itself. The teacher quotes Ajahn Chah: the mind is like a māyākāraya (magician/thief) — the more you watch its tricks, the more it "hides," meaning the fabricating tendency ceases to operate. This is the same principle as Māra fleeing when recognised by the Buddha. The practice is not to find some special truth, but to keep seeing the fabrication as fabrication (boru dihā balanna — keep looking at the falseness) until that seeing becomes strong enough that the fabrication cannot sustain itself.
