## Sutta referred to

- Not explicitly named. The Pāli phrases "na hidaṃ attakataṃ bimbaṃ, na parakataṃ idaṃ" (this form is neither self-made nor other-made) and the classification of viññāṇa types (cakkhu, sota, jivhā, kāya, mano viññāṇa) from the standard Abhidhamma/Sutta classification are referenced.

## Main dhamma points

- **Jivhā viññāṇa** (tongue-consciousness) has one sole function: to know about a tongue and a taste. It cannot go beyond that. When it arises, one is completely captured by its presentation — one cannot refuse or modify the experience.
- One cannot generate jivhā viññāṇa by thinking. When you think "salt taste" without actual contact, only mano viññāṇa arises — proving the experience is not self-made (na attakataṃ). Equally, one cannot suppress the taste-quality when jivhā viññāṇa does arise — proving it is also not controllable.
- The word "feeling" (dænīma) contains **no information** about what is felt. It is **artha śūnya** (devoid of meaning regarding the object). A small child sitting on the ground has the contact but no concept of "feeling" — the concept does not deliver the experience.
- **Pañcupādānakkhandha** is to be **parijānāti** (comprehended/seen through), not eliminated or modified. One does not go to either extreme of affirming or denying it.
- The collision of two existing physical things (e.g., tongue and salt on a sleeping person) does **not** produce feeling. An anaesthetised patient's leg can be cut without feeling arising. This proves feeling does not arise from the contact of persisting physical entities.
- This realisation moves the inquiry from the lōkiya (worldly/gross) level to the **mano dhamma** level: feeling arises through **manasikāra** (attention/mental engagement), not through physical collision. This is the transition the teacher calls "jumping from the worldly convention."
- The fabrication of "the thing felt" (dænen dē) is added by the mind to "feeling" — but it is not found within the feeling-viññāṇa itself. Seeing this is the entry point to understanding mañññanā (conceiving/proliferation).

## Summary

This discussion (continuing from Part 1) deepens the analysis of vedanā using the salt-taste example. The teacher begins by examining jivhā viññāṇa: its sole function is to register the presence of a tongue and a taste. When it arises during eating, one is completely captured — the quality (gati) is experienced as 100% real. One cannot refuse it or go beyond what it presents. This is the māyā (illusion) of viññāṇa: in that moment, "I" and "the world" (a tongue and salt) are both born, and both feel entirely real.

The teacher then demonstrates a critical contrast. When one merely thinks "salt taste" without contact, only mano viññāṇa arises. The characteristic quality of saltiness never appears. The mind knows perfectly well it is "only thinking." This proves two things: first, viññāṇa cannot be generated at will (na attakataṃ — not self-made); second, mano viññāṇa and sense viññāṇa operate differently, even though both are "mind."

From here the teacher introduces the key insight: the word "feeling" (dænīma), examined carefully, contains no information about what is felt. It is artha śūnya — devoid of meaning regarding any object. He illustrates with a child who has never learned the concept "hard" or "feeling": the child sits on the ground (contact exists), but no concept of "feeling something" arises. The mere word "feeling," told to the child, produces nothing — not because the child lacks a tongue or body, but because there are no āsava (influxes/habitual associations) to fill the concept with content. This shows that the "felt object" is not contained within the feeling-viññāṇa; it is added later.

The teacher then pushes toward what he calls the mano dhamma transition. If we take the worldly view — tongue exists, salt exists, both are present — does feeling arise? He points to the sleeping person with salt on their tongue, or the anaesthetised patient: contact exists, but no feeling arises. Therefore, feeling does not arise from the collision of two persisting physical entities. This is the moment when the inquiry "jumps from the worldly convention" (lōkayā maṭṭamen pannā) into the dhamma level: feeling arises through manasikāra (mental engagement), through a viññāṇa process, not through physical causation. One moves from the gross (tongue-and-salt) level to recognising that the entire event is a manō dhamma — a mental phenomenon — and the fabrication of "two things colliding" was itself the illusion to see through.

The teacher emphasises throughout that pañcupādānakkhandha is to be pirisin dakīma (clearly comprehended), not eliminated. One does not say "feeling doesn't exist" (that would be the natthi extreme), nor does one accept the fabricated content as real (that would be the atthi extreme). The practice is to keep seeing the boru (fabrication) as boru, without going to either extreme.
