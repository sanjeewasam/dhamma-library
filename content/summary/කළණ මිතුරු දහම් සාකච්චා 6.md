## Sutta referred to
- Not explicitly named.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *aññam eva saññā uppajjati, aññam eva saññā nirujjhati* ("one perception arises, quite another perception ceases").
- Terms used: *saññā* (perception), *viññāṇa* (consciousness), *pävätma* (persistence), *diṭṭhi* (view), *sati* / *asati* (mindfulness / un-mindfulness), *kaṇā-muṭṭi* (the blindfold pot-breaking game). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Note on the source:** a two-speaker session (teacher and a questioner addressed as "mahaththaya").

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on how "going right" and "going wrong" are both accidents, so there is no doer and no error, and the self persists only by taking "erring" as real.
- **The written text contradicts "seeing cannot be written."** A scribe wrote by observation, and by the world's account that is "right"; but that "writing what is seen" is itself an accident, and one never sees in life that "seeing" and "writing" are two contradictory stories that do not co-occur — seen rightly, they are utterly contradictory.
- **No "right" or "wrong" in any task.** There are really not two stories, success and failure, for the nature is "impossible to knot," so what goes right, and what goes specially wrong? To see the knot cannot be tied, one must see *aññam eva saññā uppajjati, aññam eva saññā nirujjhati* — one perception arises and quite another ceases — and seeing that, "two perceptions get knotted" can never happen.
- **No mind, no doer, no planner.** Seeing this, one sees that no mind persists as a "seeing," no mind sustains a "hearing," and there is no knower or planner here; the effort lies in "I can do this," which *is* the persistence, and seeing "it cannot be done," the effort ends. The persistence is built only by not seeing this, and the belief "I can do it right" is exactly why, having erred, one tries again — that very knot being why "a one" keeps being born, ring after ring.
- **The blindfold game: both are accidents.** As in the blindfold pot-breaking game, one strikes without seeing: if the pot breaks, no doer did it, and if it does not break, no one failed — both are accidents. Seeing both as accidents, the erring-one has nothing to ponder about "why did I err," for erring is anyway the nature (blindfolded, one will more likely miss) and "going right" is the rare accident. To the world "going right" is the common reality and "going wrong" the rare exception, but to one who has seen this, erring itself is his wisdom, and there is nothing special to rejoice in about "going right" — for one did not even do it.
- **The self is the view that made "erring" real.** No one accepts "it cannot be done," just as one would not accept "there is no girl"; truly nothing was done and there was no doer, and "a one" is only the view that made "erring" real, so that even "a mind" is not here but merely scaffolding we are already caught in by the view. Seeing this one event, the construction of all the other sets is seen — one point shows it everywhere; and the knot is to be seen *as* a knot, for we suppose a knot was tied only because we did not see that a knot cannot be tied.
- **The jug spill.** The teacher's own morning: setting a water-jug on the carpet, then sweeping some dirt but sweeping the wrong way with his back to the jug, his own leg struck it and a little spilled. While sweeping, "sweeping" was never known by mindfulness, the action itself unknown, and "a jug is there" was never a condition to this mind, so the collision cannot even be spoken of; and having hit it, there is no agitation — this nature, why would it be agitated? If it goes right there is nothing special to rejoice in; the one established in this simply rights the tipped cup and carries on.
- **Both right and wrong yield persistence.** If neither analysis, "succeeded" or "failed," applies, the whole examination ends there, since the world has only these two to talk about; but whichever of the two one goes to, consciousness gets a footing, a persistence — "consciousness arises" being "I exist" — so both "right" and "wrong" alike build "my persistence."

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue turns on a single recognition: "going right" and "going wrong" are both accidents, so there is neither doer nor error, and the self persists only by taking "erring" as real. A scribe, the teacher notes, wrote the dhamma by observation, and by the world's account that is right — yet "writing what is seen" is itself an accident, and one never sees in life that "seeing" and "writing" are two contradictory stories that never co-occur. Pressed on this, the questioner draws out that there are really not two stories, success and failure, for the nature is impossible to knot, so nothing truly goes right and nothing specially wrong; to see that the knot cannot be tied, one must see *aññam eva saññā uppajjati, aññam eva saññā nirujjhati* — one perception arises and quite another ceases — and seeing this, "two perceptions get knotted" can never happen. From this it follows that no mind persists as a "seeing," no mind sustains a "hearing," and there is no knower or planner here; the effort is in the belief "I can do this," which is the persistence itself, and seeing "it cannot be done," the effort ends, whereas the belief "I can do it right" is exactly why, having erred, one tries again — the very knot by which "a one" keeps being born, ring after ring.

The blindfold pot-breaking game clinches it: striking without seeing, one neither "does" the breaking if it breaks nor "fails" if it does not, both being accidents, so the erring-one has nothing to ponder about "why did I err," since erring is the nature and "going right" the rare accident — the reverse of the world's view, in which going right is real and going wrong the exception. To one who has seen this, erring itself is his wisdom, and there is nothing to rejoice in about going right, for one did not even do it; no one, though, accepts "it cannot be done," and "a one" is only the view that made erring real, so that even "a mind" is not truly here but scaffolding we are caught in by the view — and seeing this one event, the construction of all the rest is seen from a single point. The teacher illustrates with his own spilled jug that morning: sweeping with his back to it, his leg struck it, yet "sweeping" was never known by mindfulness, the jug was never a condition to the mind, the collision cannot even be spoken of, and there is no agitation — this nature having nothing to be agitated about, and nothing to rejoice in if it goes right. Finally he shows that if neither "succeeded" nor "failed" applies, the whole examination ends, since the world has only these two; but whichever one goes to, consciousness gets a footing, a persistence — "consciousness arises" being "I exist" — so that both right and wrong alike build "my persistence."
