## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on effortful versus effortless mindfulness, showing that "mindfulness" is itself the delusion, that a "knowing" is never actually met, and that both doing and not-doing alike give consciousness a footing and belong to saṁsāra.
- **Effortful mindfulness grasps an object.** We are always trying to hold the mind with effort (*āyāsa*), and that very holding means an object is being grasped — as with an external chair, saying "use the chair with mindfulness, know the chair-ness by mindfulness"; we do the same with the foot-and-ground contact, thinking a "knowing" arises there and that we must become skilled at focusing mindfulness on it.
- **Mindfulness itself is the delusion.** To keep something called "mindfulness" going is precisely the delusion. In the real, extraordinary mindfulness what dawns is that *a "knowing" is never met at any moment* — for "knowing" and "not-knowing," which we take as a separately-cut, felt thing ("here, now, it is being felt"), have no such graspable nature; this is not a nature in which the mind can be specially held.
- **We never actually felt it.** All day — sitting, walking, sleeping — a "knowing" arises with every act, yet we could never keep mindfulness on it and do not even know it; a nature we "do not even know" is what is here at every moment. Only when doing walking-meditation and thinking do we imagine "now I feel the knowing"; from morning we walked and never felt such a knowing, just as at the point attention is applied no "seeing" is to be gained, so "seeing" does not apply and "knowing" does not apply — like two trains passing each other, no "knowing" engages there.
- **Do not grasp it as a point either.** Understanding this, one should not then walk "to realize it," for if we are caught by it as a *point*, "a point" is a fine resting-place for consciousness to lodge — consciousness boxes us straight into one place, imprisoning us. The teacher declines to hand it over as a fact to be grasped, because seeing it, one is established effortlessly (*nirāyāsa*), whereas grasping the understood point makes consciousness lodge with that point too; this is realized directly (*prratyakṣa*), a difference like earth from sky, and it is to be let happen of itself.
- **Neither standing nor striving; not-doing is also striving.** Everything here points to one *buddha-vacana*: *piṭṭhāda nosita, vāyāmda nokara* — neither standing nor striving. To think "I must do exactly this" is wrong; but to conclude "then I must do nothing," stop the walking and sit in a chair pondering the point, is equally wrong, a falling into the being (*bhava*) of "I must not do." Not-doing is itself a striving — killing a mosquito is a *vāyāma*, and *not* killing it, restraining the hand, is an even greater striving; we suppose no-action has no effort, but there is a "doing" in the "I must not do," a guarding that is *sīlabbata-parāmāsa*, the clinging to a rite of keeping the hand from moving.
- **The Buddha did neither.** "Standing" here means killing (accepting the death, letting the hand go), and "not-standing/striving" means guarding against it; the Buddha did neither — neither killed nor refrained. The worldly, run-of-the-mill mind can only ever touch one of the two, but the Buddha stood in neither, and *that* is the Buddha-nature the puthujjana mind cannot even conceive, since our mind is only ever "doing" or "not-doing" and a nature transcending both cannot be thought by a mind caught in the two.
- **Both are centred on "I" and both are saṁsāra.** Without the I-sense (*mamatva*) there is no mosquito that bites "me"; both killing and not-killing are centred on the "I," and whether one guards the precept or breaks it, both are done by "me" and both are saṁsāra. Within this knowledge *sīlabbata-parāmāsa* has no room, so on becoming *sotāpanna* it falls away and *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* breaks — and when "I" breaks, no mosquito is even found to kill; transcending the two, "mosquito" and "I" alike become void coins.
- **The stopped-Buddha of the Aṅgulimāla episode.** Cessation is reached only through the very arising (*samudaya tuḷinma nirodhaya*) — one must move within the constructed nature to leap free of it; and just as one would form the "there-is" state (*atthi-pattiya*) out of the "there-is-not" state (*natthi-pattiya*), a felt-thing cannot be manufactured out of a place where no felt-thing was ever obtained. Hence the Buddha, walking, tells Aṅgulimāla "I have stopped; you have not": his stopping transcends both walking and halting, for there is no "walking" from which one stops — even as he walks, it is a knowledge-vision (*ñāṇa-dassana*) surpassing the very story of "walking," action wholly transcended by action, the kamma-forming volition (*cetanā*) surpassed, so that "I have stopped in saṁsāra; you still run" — and Aṅgulimāla, being wise, thereby grew clear-minded.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue turns on the recognition that "mindfulness" is itself the delusion. We are forever trying to hold the mind by effort, the teacher observes, and that very holding means an object is being grasped — as when we are told to "use the chair mindfully" or to focus mindfulness on the foot-and-ground contact, imagining a "knowing" arises there that we must become skilled at watching. But to keep something called mindfulness going is precisely the delusion, for in real, extraordinary mindfulness what dawns is that a "knowing" is never met at any moment: "knowing" and "not-knowing," which we take as a separately-cut felt thing, have no such graspable nature. All day, sitting and walking and sleeping, a knowing supposedly arises with every act, yet we could never keep mindfulness on it and do not even know it — and a nature we "do not even know" is what is present at every moment. Only in walking-meditation, by thinking, do we imagine we now feel the knowing; from morning we walked and felt no such thing, for at the point attention is applied no "seeing" is to be gained, so neither "seeing" nor "knowing" applies — like two trains passing, nothing engages there.

From this it follows that one must not then walk in order to realize it, since being caught by it as a *point* only gives consciousness a fine resting-place to lodge and box us into one spot; the teacher therefore refuses to hand it over as a fact, because seeing it one is established effortlessly, whereas grasping the understood point makes consciousness lodge there too. Everything points to a single word of the Buddha — neither standing nor striving. To think "I must do exactly this" is wrong, but to conclude "then I must do nothing," stop, and sit pondering is equally wrong, a falling into the being of "I must not do"; for not-doing is itself a striving. Killing a mosquito is a striving, and not killing it — restraining the hand — is a greater one, a guarding that is clinging to a rite. "Standing" is the killing and "striving/guarding" the refraining, and the Buddha did neither, whereas the worldly mind can only touch one of the two; that neither-nor is the Buddha-nature the puthujjana mind cannot conceive. Both killing and not-killing are centred on the "I," and both are saṁsāra, so that within this knowledge the clinging to rites has no room, on stream-entry it falls away and self-view breaks, and when "I" breaks no mosquito is even found to kill. Cessation, moreover, is reached only through the very arising — one moves within the constructed nature to leap free of it — and so the Buddha, walking, tells Aṅgulimāla "I have stopped; you have not": his stopping transcends both walking and halting, there being no "walking" to stop from, a knowledge-vision surpassing the story of walking and the kamma-forming volition itself, by which "I have stopped in saṁsāra; you still run" — and Aṅgulimāla, being wise, grew clear-minded.
