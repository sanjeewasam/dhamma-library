## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue continuing the "unborn darkness" image, showing that seeing the unarisen condition is itself seeing arising-and-cessation, that the state is neither knowing nor not-knowing, and that this seeing must become an effortless, gapless wisdom rather than a "knowledge" one grasps.
- **Seeing the unarisen condition ends the search for a cause.** As "day" is taken from the non-existence of the borne "unborn darkness," and "light" likewise, seeing that unborn darkness there *is* both arising and cessation. To see the arisen condition is to see the unarisen, so there is no longer anywhere to go toward a cause and no cause can be found; seeing the unarisen condition, arising-and-cessation are seen right there and nothing engages beyond it.
- **What was met is itself a non-becoming, and meaning-empty.** What was "met" is seen as a non-becoming, for taking existence out of non-existence — existence from the non-existent unborn darkness — is a meaning-empty thing, and that is precisely what "seeing arising" rightly means. Right there anicca, dukkha and anatta are directly realized, which is where permanent-pleasant-self is shed: what was taken permanent seen as impermanent, the pleasant as suffering, the self as not-self.
- **The state is neither knowing nor not-knowing.** This seen nature can be applied everywhere, and where wisdom sees, nothing is any longer "understood" — our equanimity is a level at which nothing is understood, because where wisdom truly sees, not even "I understood something" can arise, no point engaging there. Right now, in fact, we are neither knowing nor not-knowing: non-knowing is not met, so "knowing" is not understood either — as darkness is not met and the non-existence of darkness is not met, so non-knowing is not met, and it is the *non-existence of non-knowing* that we call "knowing," which itself is not met; the non-existence of non-seeing is what "seeing" means, the non-existence of non-hearing what "hearing" means.
- **"I understood" is still only knowledge, not wisdom.** So long as "I understood it" engages, one is not yet free of it — that is not wisdom but only a *knowledge about* it, which must become automatic. When wisdom comes it reaches the place where nothing engages, and even "I understood" falls away; there an extraordinary mindfulness arises that cannot be understood as anything.
- **An unknown nature; ordinary mindfulness cannot reach it.** If the nature is not understood and a self-invested mindfulness arises toward it, that mindfulness cannot understand it either — like a fish that cannot make out something on land. One must reach that "unknown nature," where ignorance must meet ignorance itself, so that "ignorance" can no longer be understood as a thing; a mindfulness that arises within an unknown nature cannot be something consciousness understands — which is exactly why it is called extraordinary, a place transcending consciousness.
- **Why it is not grasped, and why numbers dwindle.** Even a venerable stating it is not at a level to be understood: of forty or forty-five monks and fifteen or twenty lay present the first day, fewer come the second and fewer the third — no surprise where it is not grasped. Those who bear it a while see the non-existent side, yet think the non-existent side *exists*, and so, taking "the non-existence of the borne exists," they go again toward non-existence, supposing "right" out of the very thing they bear — for that both existence and non-existence are met is unthinkable to a run-of-the-mill mind, being outside its range, while the mind that has left that range cannot be made to grasp it at their scale.
- **Constant meditation and gapless concentration.** For one to whom it has struck even a little, this does not stop — it becomes a constant meditation, not merely a session set aside but held throughout the whole handling of the seen-heard-sensed-cognized; this is the concentration that establishes with no gap, the *ānantarika-samādhi*, which is wisdom, and this wisdom must go on. Then neither delight nor sorrow engages and there is nothing to be gained — the state of "what if gained, what if not," no delight at gain and no sorrow at loss — because right there is the seeing: in an unborn nature we carry on all this business caught in view and knotted to view, carrying the very distortion, the conceiving, since the seen-heard-sensed-cognized are four conceivings (*maññanā*).
- **The taste of Dhamma; and the endless running that is saṁsāra.** One must always watch it, and watching, the other side turns in place of the apparent "truth"; there is no taste like the taste of Dhamma — *sabba-dānaṁ dhamma-dānaṁ jināti, sabba-raso dhamma-raso jināti*. Whoever it strikes even a little will not let go, for whatever else is taken, consciousness carries it forward to yet another point and does not end or stop here — no stopping, another point always engaging — while the one who sees rightly understands "alas, this just goes on again," which is saṁsāra itself; the speaker closes that however many Dhamma talks he heard he understood only "this just goes on," and meeting his friend set it right.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue continues the image of the "unborn darkness," showing that to see the unarisen condition is already to see arising-and-cessation. As "day" and "light" are taken from the non-existence of the borne unborn darkness, seeing that darkness there is both samudaya and nirodha; to see the arisen condition is to see the unarisen, so there is nowhere left to go toward a cause and none can be found, arising-and-cessation being seen right there with nothing engaging beyond. What was "met" is itself a non-becoming, for taking existence out of non-existence is meaning-empty, and this is what seeing arising rightly means — the place where anicca, dukkha and anatta are directly realized and permanent-pleasant-self is shed, the permanent seen impermanent, the pleasant seen as suffering, the self as not-self. Applied everywhere, this seeing leaves nothing "understood": our equanimity is a level at which nothing is understood, for where wisdom truly sees, not even "I understood" can arise. Right now we are neither knowing nor not-knowing — non-knowing is not met, so "knowing" is not understood either, just as darkness and the non-existence of darkness are alike not met; it is the non-existence of non-knowing that we call "knowing," the non-existence of non-seeing that we call "seeing," the non-existence of non-hearing that we call "hearing."

So long as "I understood it" still engages, the speaker presses, one has only a knowledge about it, not wisdom, and that knowledge must become automatic; when wisdom comes it reaches the place where nothing engages, "I understood" falls away, and an extraordinary mindfulness arises that cannot be understood as anything. If the nature is not understood and a self-invested mindfulness turns toward it, that mindfulness cannot grasp it — like a fish unable to make out something on land — for one must reach an "unknown nature" where ignorance meets ignorance itself and "ignorance" can no longer be understood as a thing; a mindfulness arising within an unknown nature is not something consciousness can understand, which is why it is extraordinary, transcending consciousness. This is why even a venerable's statement of it is not grasped and why numbers dwindle from day to day: those who bear it a while see the non-existent side yet think it exists, and go again toward non-existence, supposing "right" from the very thing they bear, since that both existence and non-existence are met is outside a worldly mind's range. For one to whom it has struck, it becomes constant meditation held throughout the handling of the seen-heard-sensed-cognized — the gapless *ānantarika-samādhi*, which is wisdom and must keep going — so that neither delight nor sorrow engages and there is nothing to gain, no delight at gain nor sorrow at loss, because in an unborn nature we otherwise carry on caught in view, bearing the distortion, the four conceivings of the seen-heard-sensed-cognized. One must always watch it, and watching, the other side turns in place of the apparent truth; there is no taste like the taste of Dhamma. Whoever it strikes will not let go, for consciousness otherwise carries whatever is taken forward to another point without ever stopping — which the one who sees knows as saṁsāra itself — and the speaker closes that, for all the talks he had heard, he understood only "this just goes on," until meeting his friend set it right.
