## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on how "hearing" and "seeing" are themselves the birth of "I," how the world knows only sound and never hearing, and how contact (*phassa*) is empty of any thing — so that even "I understood the Dhamma" is a fresh birth of self.
- **The true-nature cannot be made an object of consciousness.** The nature of "being in hearing," like that of knowing, can never be understood, never made an object to a consciousness; and it is precisely because we do not know this incapacity that we suppose we can "make formations." Seeing arises, and it is in that seeing that "I" am born — not that a born "I" sees, but that at the very seeing "I" is born; seeing is only a reflection, but we take it as "I see," and that taking is the view (*diṭṭhi*).
- **Seeing/hearing is where "I" is born, and cannot be met.** "I exist" is inferred only because there are things seen and heard; but if "I" am born at the very hearing, then when hearing is seen as hearing, it is realized that "hearing" is never met — and where hearing is not met, the view "I am born through hearing" cannot stand. We instead take it the other way round, "because I exist, there is hearing," which is the distortion (*vikṛti*).
- **The world knows sound, not hearing; the born-deaf example.** The Buddha said to see hearing as hearing, to be disciplined in hearing by hearing; seeing hearing as hearing, wisdom settles where no "hearing" is met. In ten seconds of silence one says "I heard nothing" — yet hearing must have been present to know "no sound," so hearing was fully there; the world, though, has only ever called *sound* "hearing" and does not recognize hearing at all. To one born deaf, a hand-clap and its absence are alike nothing, for he does not recognize hearing; and we, taking "I didn't hear" while hearing was present, recognize neither hearing nor non-hearing, and so are no different from the born-deaf — which is the depth of "though having eyes, like one born blind; though having ears, like one deaf." All our "heard / not heard" talk grasps only present or absent *sound*, never hearing itself.
- **The three characteristics appear at the un-met point.** Seeing the self-characteristic as self-characteristic — for hearing and seeing are what have become "self" and furnish self-persistence — reveals the not-self characteristic; and right there the suffering-characteristic appears, since knotting things onto an un-met seen-and-heard, the very construction, is *saṅkhāra-dukkha*, while the realization that hearing is never met, never born, is the impermanence-characteristic. Thus the three characteristics stand together, beautifully, at one point — a place consciousness cannot reach by thinking, which is why for most of the day nothing is remembered and we live in un-mindfulness, double-checking whether we shut the door or added the salt, because the action was never known *by* the action at the time.
- **Action has no form, so cannot be cognized; it is empty.** Consciousness can grasp only what is made of name-and-form, and an action has no form, so it is not cognizable: told "I am breaking," one pictures some object broken — a flower, a chair — but "I broke a thing" cannot say *what* was broken, for the broken-thing is not contained in the breaking; likewise "eating" adds "I" and "rice" from outside, neither being in the act of eating. So no eaten-thing and no eater is born within the act — we breed them, and at the very "eating," "I" is born and, being born, puts "rice" to itself.
- **Contact is the birth of self; freedom from kamma.** This is *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*: at contact "I" is born — not an "I" relevant to the contact, but "I" born the instant contact comes, since *phassa* is action and action is empty of any thing, so where nothing is met no knot can be tied, and it is where contact ceases as contact that we build the whole story, "I am eating rice," which is the view. And since *cetanāhaṁ bhikkhave kammaṁ*, to see that the action is never met is freedom from kamma — Aṅgulimāla freed though he killed nine-hundred-and-ninety-nine or ten thousand, freed *from the action*, having realized volition, by volition, as never-met.
- **Even "I understood the Dhamma" is a fresh birth of self.** The moment one says "I understood the Dhamma," Māra is there, "I" born again — the view exactly so; the Dhamma too was called a raft, and to carry it on one's head is to be born by it and go straight back to saṁsāra. Grasping "the world is false — the Dhamma" and grasping the world land in the same pit; the analysis the world made, taken *as* "Dhamma" and not let go, is only a slightly different name for the same knot. This cannot be got like licking milk off a sharp knife, so it needs a good friend (*kalyāṇa-mitta*) to point it out; even "watching" it is a knot, "loosening the knot" is another knot, "I am done" a further birth — knot upon knot — yet thinking pressed thus finally exhausts thought, as felling a tree stroke by stroke will one day bring it down.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue turns on the recognition that "hearing" and "seeing" are themselves the birth of "I." The true-nature of being-in-hearing, like knowing, can never be made an object to consciousness, and it is because we do not know this incapacity that we imagine we can make formations; seeing arises and it is *in* the seeing that "I" is born — not that a born self sees, but that at the very seeing self is born, seeing being only a reflection which we misread as "I see," and that misreading is the view. "I exist" is inferred only from things seen and heard, but if "I" am born at the hearing, then seeing hearing as hearing reveals that hearing is never met, and where it is not met the view "I am born through hearing" collapses — whereas we take it backwards, "because I exist there is hearing," which is the distortion. The world, in fact, knows only sound and not hearing: in ten seconds of silence one says "I heard nothing," yet hearing had to be present to register "no sound," so hearing was fully there; one born deaf finds a hand-clap and its absence alike nothing because he does not recognize hearing, and we, taking "I didn't hear" while hearing was present, recognize neither hearing nor its absence and are thus no different from the born-deaf — the depth of "though having eyes, like one born blind; though having ears, like one deaf" — since all our "heard / not heard" grasps only present or absent sound.

At this un-met point the three characteristics stand together: seeing the self-characteristic as self-characteristic, since hearing and seeing are what became "self" and give self-persistence, reveals the not-self characteristic; the suffering-characteristic appears there too, for knotting things onto an un-met seen-and-heard is *saṅkhāra-dukkha*; and the realization that hearing is never met, never born, is impermanence — a place consciousness cannot reach by thought, which is why we live largely in un-mindfulness, unsure whether we shut the door, because the action was never known by the action. For an action has no form and so cannot be cognized, consciousness grasping only name-and-form: "I am breaking" cannot say what was broken, the broken-thing not being in the breaking, and "eating" adds "I" and "rice" from outside, so no eaten-thing and no eater is born within the act — we breed them, and at the very "eating" self is born and puts rice to itself.
