## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue built almost entirely on a flip-book (flip-animation) simile, showing that an *action/process* is never actually met — only ever one still frame at the present moment — so that the "walking man," the seen world, and the "I" who sees are all stitched from the memory of dead frames, and seeing this non-arising of action is seeing the cessation of self-identity.
- **Hearing is the birth of "I"; the question of "to whom" collapses.** When the event "hearing" arises, the idea "a sound" arises, and at the very naming "a sound" the hearer is built on the other side — no found-sound without a built hearer — so "I hear" has no basis and is a false statement. Asked "to whom did the sound occur?", the question itself is negated, for there was never anyone here; and once "someone hears" is seen as the falsehood, no discussion of "the sound is good / bad" has any ground either.
- **Forming is not a thing; we only ever meet formed things.** "Forming" (the process) is utterly different — sky-from-earth different — from "formed things"; we meet formed things (a man walking, not the walking; a bird, not the flying; rice, not the eating), never the forming. The five clinging-aggregates come to us *as things* — form a thing, feeling a thing — but to see them as a process one must first see that the process is not a thing, and even then must realize that no such "action" was there at all, or one is caught by the action.
- **The flip-book: only one still frame at a time.** In a flip-book every page holds one still picture, and at any instant only one page turns and only one picture is met — never a previous turn (it has "died," ended) nor a next turn (not yet "born") — for an action exists only in the present moment. This is *yathā-svabhāva*: the action can be met only now. Yet within that single still frame we meet "a walking man" — one still picture plus one turn, but taken as a moving man.
- **The walking man is the undeclared (avyākata) question.** Asked "why / from what does the walking man arise here?", no cause can be shown; the question is like a dog deceived by its own reflection in water, then asking where that "dog" was born, where it goes when it dies — a chain of questions each groundless, arising only from the delusion, which is why a Buddha set such questions aside as *avyākata*, declaring "this question does not apply, brahmin." To answer them is to accept the delusion, as one would have to describe the water-dog's tail and four legs.
- **A process is never met; craving is the seamstress that stitches dead frames.** Since only one action is ever met in the present moment, "a process is met" is false — no process is ever met. The "process" is built by taking the *memory* of a ceased ("dead") prior frame and knotting it to the present still frame: first page's memory knotted to the second, those two to the third, and so on, an invisible seam running through — this stitching-together is craving (*taṇhā*), also called *sibbinī*, the seamstress, and the knot is not tied to a live present experience but onto dead experiences. This stitching *is* forming (*saṅkharaṇa*), and it cannot be seen.
- **Cause and fruit alike are memory-built; hence the whole chain of dependent arising.** Give the stitched "walking man" a cause and it is a cause fabricated to fit the delusion — the "process" as cause, the "man" as its fruit — both raised in memory from dead frames, not in this moment. The man may then be found beautiful or ugly (lust and aversion), clung to (*upādāna*), a "becoming" made of him (*bhava*), so that thinking of him revives the memory again — the man's arising from memory-knots being precisely what "I went to town, I saw such-and-such" is made of, all fabricated within, from the stitched knot. Not seeing that this walking, active appearance was itself made by the knot is ignorance (*avijjā*).
- **The three that arise together, none of them real.** Besides the first delusion ("how was the man made? — made of memory"), a second arises: "the man appeared *to me*," an "I" standing to whom he appeared; and a third links them — the man made real and the "I" made real are joined by "seeing," so three appear together: the man, the one to whom he appears, and the seeing that occurred. Not one of the three can be raised as real, and there is nothing left to ask about the seeing, the man, or the "I" — this is the whole cessation-nature, *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ*, what Koṇḍañña realized, self-evident to one who sees thus whether or not a Buddha states it.
- **This is not made by self or another; it arises by conditions.** Just as one cannot look at a chair without the thought "chair," this recognizing is not one's own doing — *na hidaṁ atta-kataṁ bimbaṁ* (not made by self), *para-kataṁ na-idaṁ* (nor made by another) — but a happening by the help of conditions; a shape, a colour, an earth-quality serve as conditions, and this bundle of *nāma-rūpa* met each instant has no thing in it and no active quality, only a recognition, with no one and nothing grasped.
- **Taste: the mango is not in the taste, but in one's own marks.** "Mango-taste" contains no mango — told "taste," one who never tasted a mango gets a bare sensation and no mango arises, because the shape-and-colour marks (*nimitta*) are not in him; the mango-image comes from one's own stored marks, not from the taste or the word. The word "mango" too holds no colour or shape; a literate stranger reading "mango" forms an intention but, lacking the marks, builds no form. For a stream of arising-ceasing bare sensations, adding "a bird" gives a persistence, but the bird too was met only through colour and shape.
- **The arahant knows the mango, but no form is constructed.** An arahant eating a mango cannot fail to recognize "mango," yet within that recognition no marks arise, so no form is built — "form is not associated" for the arahant, because wisdom stands in the non-arisen nature (*nohaṭagat svabhāva*): as we cannot force the belief that a real living person is in a mirror, our mind rejecting the person-marks there, so the arahant's wisdom finds no marks arising in the formation and no form appears, known at the level of *sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ*. Perception is recognized as mere perception, saññā fully comprehended (*saññā pariññā*).
- **Cessation seen, right view, stream-entry.** When perception is fully comprehended, things are no longer met: knowing becomes empty of a thing known, hence hearing too is empty, contact empty — "the mango-recognition is empty of recognition, no form is met" — this is the nature seen within cessation. Examining this same point again and again, a day comes when it strikes the mind like a blow with a stick, the dense delusion cracking bit by bit until the mind, unprompted, itself recalls at each seeing and hearing "this has no marks; this is not a form," and settles in the not-a-form nature — after which one cannot force it back into a form. When this knowledge arises there is no reverse: as the Buddha said the stream-enterer does not again fall to the four lower realms, so when this nature is clear beyond question, that itself is right view, and one who has come to right view does not die without becoming a stream-enterer — what is needed is only that one's own vision be clear enough, seen without a residue of doubt, with no need of many suttas or Pāli, taking one raw present delusion and diving in to see its true nature.

## Summary

This long two-speaker good-friend dialogue is built almost entirely on a flip-book simile, and its whole burden is that an *action* or *process* is never actually met, so that the seen world, the "walking man," and the "I" who sees are all stitched from the memory of dead frames — and to see this non-arising of action is to see the cessation of self-identity. The teacher, addressed as Lasantha, begins from hearing: when "hearing" arises, "a sound" arises, and at the very naming the hearer is built on the other side, so "I hear" has no basis and the question "to whom did the sound occur?" collapses, there having never been anyone here. Forming, he insists, is not a thing and is sky-from-earth different from formed things: we always meet a man, not the walking; rice, not the eating; the five clinging-aggregates come as things, and to see them as a process one must first see the process is not a thing, and then that no such action was there at all. The flip-book shows how: every page holds one still picture, and at any instant only one page turns and only one picture is met — never the previous turn, which has died, nor the next, not yet born — for an action lives only in the present moment; yet within that single still frame we meet "a walking man." Asked from what that man arises, no cause can be shown; the question is like a dog deceived by its reflection asking where that dog came from and goes — groundless questions born of the delusion, which is why a Buddha set such things aside as *avyākata*.

Since only one action is ever met now, "a process is met" is simply false; the "process" is fabricated by knotting the *memory* of each ceased frame onto the present still frame, seam upon invisible seam — a stitching that is craving, the seamstress *sibbinī*, tying knots not to a live experience but to dead ones, and this stitching is forming itself, which cannot be seen. Give the stitched man a cause and it is a cause invented to fit the delusion, the "process" as cause and the "man" as fruit, both raised in memory; he is then found beautiful or ugly, clung to, made into a becoming, revived whenever thought of — "I saw such-and-such" being wholly made of these knots — and not to see this is ignorance. Moreover three arise together and not one is real: the man, the "I" to whom he appeared, and the "seeing" that links them — *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ*, what Koṇḍañña realized. None of it is made by self or another (*na hidaṁ atta-kataṁ bimbaṁ, para-kataṁ na-idaṁ*) but happens by conditions — a shape, a colour, an earth-quality — the bundle of *nāma-rūpa* met each instant holding no thing and no activity, only a recognition. Extended to taste, "mango-taste" contains no mango: one who never tasted gets only a bare sensation, the mango-image coming from one's own stored marks, not the taste or the word. The arahant still recognizes "mango," but within that recognition no marks arise, so no form is built — "form is not associated," wisdom standing in the non-arisen nature as our own mind refuses to believe a real person lives in a mirror; perception is known as mere perception, fully comprehended. Then things are no longer met, knowing becoming empty of a known thing, hearing empty, contact empty — the nature seen within cessation — and examining this same point until it strikes the mind like a blow, the delusion cracks and the mind settles, unforced, in the not-a-form nature, from which it cannot be pushed back. When this knowledge arises there is no reverse; as the stream-enterer does not fall again to the lower realms, this clear seeing is itself right view, and one who reaches it does not die without stream-entry — needing not many suttas but only that one's own vision be clear enough, taking one raw present delusion and diving in to see its true nature.
