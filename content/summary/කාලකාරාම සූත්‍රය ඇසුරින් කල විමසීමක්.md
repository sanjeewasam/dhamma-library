## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" examination based on the Kālakārāma Sutta, showing that thinking and the thing-thought arise together (so there is no thinker and no separately-stored thought), that past-future-present are all fabricated in the present, and that the four elements, "hardness," and even "space" are only namings by convention that distort perception — a condition never actually met.
- **Thinking and the thought arise together; the four-cornered trap.** Saying "I cannot think of it, but it was in my memory, I knew it" reveals a strange view: the thing could not be thought, yet is claimed to have been "stored." Seeing rightly that no thought is found apart from thinking, one sees "this being, this is" — thinking and the thought arise together — and the view of "a forgetter who persisted" dissolves. But falling to the first knot ("there is a thing thought apart from thinking"), three more auto-assemble in the Kālakārāma pattern: "there is a not-thought" (things I did not think, existing elsewhere), "there is a to-be-thought" (something I could think if I tried), and "there is a thinker" — and one is caught in the snare.
- **Past dead, future unborn, and the fabricated "present."** People long misheard "forget the past, the future is not come, dwell in the present" as a stable middle: but a "dead past" is set up against a "lived past," and a "not-yet-born future" against "a thing to be born," so that fixing a "present moment" between them merely makes all three stable — and if the vision is right, even the "middle" is not found. Discerning "there and there" (*tattha tattha vipassati*), one cannot slide to past or future; the "present" we fix is really *manasikāra-sambhavā sabbe dhammā* — all things originating from attention — for it is because we do not know the condition-born nature that a "present moment" is built in the middle, and giving thinking a permanence makes "this is something I knew all along."
- **All things rooted in contact; recognizing a chair.** Whenever anything is recognized — "chair" — it seems the very chair one has long known, never that this bundle of *nāma-rūpa* arises here by condition, attention-rooted; life presents it as "I recalled a thing that exists." Seen rightly, *phassa-mūlakā sabbe dhammā* — all things rooted in contact — and the words fall away like the raft, for once the vision is there the Pāli phrasings are no longer a problem.
- **Elements are namings; from diversity of element, diversity of perception.** Water is named from the "flowing quality," a chair from the "hard quality," each a perception — and by the diversity of "element" the one felt nature is recognized in two ways (water, chair), which is how distortion happens: we can never see the two as the same, having already named them two by language. The felt quality itself is the same in all four; because it is not known *as* attention, we elaborate it into four names — earth, water, fire, air — which are really *vedanā* recognized variously. Even "element" is a naming by perception, empty of itself; molten iron shows it — if "hardness" were a fixed thing in iron, it could never become liquid, so the whole basis collapses, and "hardness" is seen to be a recognition, not a met thing. The one who takes the quality as real gets boxed into "this is the kind of quality recognized"; seen through, even the diversity of elements falls from the "Dhamma."
- **Space is the un-met "not-hard," named.** We say devas, petas and spirits pass through walls, separating beings by clinging to "feeling": walls are "solid" because *felt*, and those beings "do not feel" — so it is by holding onto feeling that we sort beings, grasping the very absence of our own feeling as "the other." Where there is no wall we call it "space," but "space" is only the un-known "there-is-no-hardness" given a name; looked at as space, it is not a space at all, for within "space" no "hard" is met, and the not-met absence-of-hard is named "space" — perception's error, naming a thing out of nothing. Equally, "hard" itself is only "there is no emptiness/space," the un-met condition; so both "space" and "hard" are interpretations laid over an un-met condition, each empty of itself.
- **The condition is never met; realizing exhaustion, cessation, no-fruit.** Since a "hard" condition is never actually met, watching shows the condition is not met — *prratya-kṣaya*, the exhaustion of condition — and when the condition itself is not met, no "arising" (*samudaya*) can be spoken of, so what is realized is cessation (*nirodha*). To describe "the chair is built thus and thus" is not seeing arising but fitting a cause to a fruit; seeing the causes never fit the fruit, one slips unknowingly toward "there is no fruit" — but true seeing of arising forbids the "it does not exist" side too, for if the condition itself is unmet, what talk is there of a chair at all? (Awake / asleep works the same: "awake" is only the named "there-is-no-sleep.")
- **Neither annihilation nor eternity; the world lives on existence-and-non-existence.** Naming makes a thing, and a thing falls at once into "it is right / it is not," pulling one out toward realization on one side and, on the annihilation (*uccheda*) side, stripping away even what appears — which is why the Buddha said the world makes use of *existence and non-existence*, of view. The Kevaṭṭa question is reframed for exactly this reason: not "where do the four elements cease" (which would still grant them as existing things to be destroyed) but "where do they find no footing." This is a practical Dhamma seen in one raw present delusion, needing no store of suttas once one's own vision is clear.

## Summary

This two-speaker examination, framed on the Kālakārāma Sutta, turns on the recognition that thinking and the thing-thought arise together, so there is neither a thinker nor a separately-stored thought. To say "I cannot think of it, yet it was in my memory" betrays a strange view, for seen rightly no thought is found apart from thinking — "this being, this is" — and the notion of "a forgetter who persisted" dissolves; but the moment one falls to the first knot, three more auto-assemble in the sutta's pattern: a not-thought existing elsewhere, a to-be-thought recoverable by effort, and a thinker, and the snare closes. The same structure governs time: a "dead past" is set against a "lived past" and an "unborn future" against "a thing to be born," so fixing a "present moment" between them only makes all three stable, whereas right discernment "there and there" finds not even a middle — the "present" we fix being really *manasikāra-sambhavā sabbe dhammā*, all things originating from attention, built because we do not know the condition-born nature and reinforced by lending thinking a permanence. Whenever a "chair" is recognized it seems the very chair long known, never a *nāma-rūpa* arising here by condition; seen rightly, *phassa-mūlakā sabbe dhammā*, and the words fall away like the raft.

The heart of the talk is that the four elements, "hardness," and "space" are only namings that distort perception. Water is named from a flowing quality, a chair from a hard quality, and by the "diversity of element" the one felt nature is split into two recognitions that can never be seen as the same, since language has already made them two; the felt quality is identical in all four, and only because it is not known as attention do we elaborate it into earth, water, fire, air, which are *vedanā* variously recognized. Molten iron proves it — were hardness a fixed thing in iron it could never liquefy — so hardness is a recognition, not a met thing, and even "element" is empty of itself. "Space," likewise, is only the un-known "there-is-no-hardness" given a name, not a space at all, and "hard" only the un-met "there-is-no-space"; each is an interpretation laid over an un-met condition. And since the condition is never actually met, watching reveals its exhaustion (*prratya-kṣaya*): where the condition is unmet no arising can be spoken of, so cessation is realized, while merely describing "the chair is built thus" fits a cause to a fruit and slides unknowingly toward "there is no fruit" — true seeing of arising forbidding that annihilationist side too. This is why the Buddha said the world lives on existence and non-existence, on view, and why the Kevaṭṭa question is reframed from "where do the elements cease" to "where do they find no footing" — a practical Dhamma seen in one raw present delusion, needing no store of suttas once one's own vision is clear.
