## Main dhamma points
- A group "good-friend" dialogue arguing that contact (*phassa/passa*) — the seeing that seems most certain — is never actually met, so that the seen thing, the "I" who sees, and the seeing itself are all un-met, and the mindfulness that stands where no "person" arose belongs to no person: the seeing of self-identity's non-arising.
- **"Why do we think" cannot be answered by thinking.** Ordinarily we reason "we think because these things exist," and "because it is seen it exists, because it exists it is seen" — each supporting the other, so once caught in this knot it seems perfectly reasonable and "100%, 200% sure," and no one feels thinking is at fault. The sensed (*muta*) side confirms it too: told "there was no seeing," one is invited to grasp *that* and see what is met — and it supports the same conclusion.
- **"Watch the action" means: see that it cannot be met.** The instruction to watch a chosen action is not "watch it because it exists," but as with "bring mustard seed from a house where none has died" — the point is not that such houses exist but that their non-existence is realized. So "watch the action" means look whether the action supposed to be caught is ever actually met, to realize its non-being, not its being. Only a *pañca-upādāna-skandha* — a knot of name-and-form — is ever cognizable, because consciousness needs a condition to think.
- **The chair is not in its conditions, nor they in it.** Asked what "chair" means, no meaning can be given from the word "chair" — a foreigner told "chair" gets nothing — so to show its non-being one must bring out the conditions: shape, colour, hardness, four legs, the marks (*saññā*) by which a chair is recognized. But none of these is the chair: brownness is not the chair, hardness is not the chair, four-legs is not the chair, and within the thought "chair" no "brown," no "hard," no "four legs" ever arose. So seeing the condition, one sees the conditions never arose within the thought — hence no fruit can be spoken of.
- **Neither "related" nor "unrelated"; contact is seeing the flaw in the seeming-understood.** Since the conditions are not found in "chair," we conclude "these are unrelated," falling to the "it does not exist" extreme — yet that is wrong, for without the conditions no chair can be spoken of either, so "unrelated" cannot be said. Both "related" and "unrelated" are wrong; and *this* — seeing the flaw in what seems understood — is what it is to see contact rightly. Contact is the very "because / condition," the action itself, and to see it is to see it cannot be met.
- **Seeing is never met: the un-seen crowds, the salad example.** We say "form exists because it is seen," yet in town countless forms are present and only a few are seen; had "seeing" been genuinely present, all forms would necessarily be seen. A lady in France made a salad, set it on the table, ate her rice, and only on rising saw the salad — "oh, I didn't see it." Both eye and form were present, so seeing does not arise merely because the two are present; and the question "why didn't I see it though it was there" arises precisely from not knowing that both the seeing and the salad are never met. In that event neither "I saw" nor "I didn't see" arose, and no "the salad exists / does not exist" arose either.
- **No person is born where seeing and seen are un-met.** If a form is grasped, a "person" is built; where the salad was not met, no "I was there" and no "I was not there" can be said, there being no evidence either way — so the event is an "unknown nature" in which no person arose. The mindfulness that stands where no personhood arose cannot belong to a person, cannot be "mine"; it transcends the *sakkāya* view. This non-arising of the person is *anutpāda*, the seeing of cessation — and it can be seen only through just such ordinary scenes.
- **The knot builds "I saw"; watching it, conceiving wears away.** When the knot is tied — "I saw the salad" — the past-present-future formations of "I saw, I see, I will see" are built through this "seeing"; but seeing that the seeing and the seen both stand in not-knowing, these formations are no longer built but seen being built, the flaw seen, and the "I think it is seen" gradually diminishes — this is the wearing-away of conceiving (*maññanā*). The worldling's very question — "the salad was surely here, I made it, I sat and ate, why didn't I see it?" — arises only from lacking the knowledge that both are un-met; the problem is neither in the thing nor in the seeing but in not seeing the arising built of these three, each of which — the "I," the seeing, and the seen — never arose.

## Summary

This group good-friend dialogue argues that contact — the seeing that feels most certain — is never actually met, so that the seen thing, the "I" who sees, and the seeing itself are all un-met. Ordinarily, the teacher notes, we reason "we think because things exist," and "because it is seen it exists, because it exists it is seen," each half propping the other so that the knot feels perfectly reasonable and no one suspects thinking is at fault; the sensed side confirms it too. But the instruction to "watch an action" is not "watch it because it exists" — it is like "bring mustard seed from a house where none has died," whose point is to realize such a house is never found: so one watches to see whether the action can ever be met, and to realize its non-being. Only a knot of name-and-form, a *pañca-upādāna-skandha*, is ever cognizable, since consciousness needs a condition. The chair shows it: no meaning issues from the word "chair," and its conditions — shape, colour, hardness, four legs — are none of them the chair, while within the thought "chair" no such condition ever arose; so seeing the condition, one sees the conditions never arose within the thought, and no fruit can be spoken of. We then conclude "chair and conditions are unrelated," falling to the "it does not exist" extreme — yet that too is wrong, for without the conditions no chair can be spoken of, so neither "related" nor "unrelated" can be said, and this very seeing of the flaw in the seeming-understood is what it is to see contact.

Seeing, likewise, is never met: in town countless forms are present and only a few seen, whereas had seeing been genuinely present all forms would be seen. The salad example clinches it — a lady made a salad, set it down, ate her rice, and saw it only on rising, "oh, I didn't see it," though both eye and form were present; the question "why didn't I see it though it was there" arises only from not knowing that both the seeing and the salad are never met, and in that event neither "I saw" nor "I didn't see," neither "it exists" nor "it does not," ever arose. Where the salad was not met no "person" is born, no "I was there" or "I was not there" being sayable, so the event is an unknown nature in which no person arose; and the mindfulness that stands there belongs to no person, cannot be "mine," and transcends the *sakkāya* view — this non-arising of the person being *anutpāda*, the seeing of cessation, visible only through such ordinary scenes. When the knot "I saw the salad" is tied, the past-present-future formations of "I saw, I see, I will see" are built through the seeing; but seeing that both the seeing and the seen stand in not-knowing, these are no longer built but seen being built, the flaw seen, and the "I think it is seen" gradually wears away — the wearing-away of conceiving. The worldling's own question — the salad was surely here, I made it and ate, why did I not see it — arises only from lacking this knowledge, the problem lying neither in the thing nor in the seeing but in not seeing the arising built of three that never arose: the "I," the seeing, and the seen.
