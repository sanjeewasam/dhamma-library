## Main dhamma points
- A group "good-friend" dialogue continuing the salad example, showing that the very "mistake" of not-seeing, which we blame on ourselves, is the golden bridge out of saṁsāra; that worldly success is knot-tying and the best trap; and that existence and non-existence are seen together at one point, "non-existence" itself being only a word.
- **The blamed "mistake" is the way out; don't stitch it back to "me."** The equanimous, un-knowable nature is exactly what wisdom must stand in — yet we knot even that back to our account, "alas, I missed it because my mind was elsewhere," making a fault of it and pinning it on ourselves. But that very "mistake" — the action, the doing, the unseen salad — is the bridge out of saṁsāra, "the escape from saṁsāra lies within saṁsāra," so it must be *seen*, not converted into a fault to be corrected next time by better mindfulness.
- **Worldly success is the best trap.** What we call the worldling's competence — working "mindfully," being skilled at tying the knot beautifully — is precisely the snare, for had the salad been seen on sitting down there would be no problem and no talk today, and it is only the *not*-seeing that opens the door. The one who tries hard to remember, reciting "salad, salad," is the very one who did not see, who does not know the true nature of seeing-and-seen-and-person, and so keeps trying to tie the knot; worldly success is the finest trap for saṁsāra, which is why the Dhamma turns everything upside-down — *pubbe ananussutesu dhammesu*, a lamp lit in the dark, never a thing heard before.
- **We seek causes only because we do not know the true nature.** We repent a lapse — "why didn't I see it, my mindfulness is weak" — and hunt reasons ("what was I thinking then?"), then devise methods to remember next time, dancing a whole jig to keep it up; but in truth we are not "in thought" but in un-mindfulness, near-madness, and one who has seen this un-mindfulness has nothing to repent, for not-getting-knotted *is* the nature. The reasons we fit are the world's ready answers, and it is exactly those fitted answers that veil the true nature — we fit them only because we do not know it. Seen rightly, these let-go points are the gold-worth places of escape, for "no person was actually born there" is a priceless recognition.
- **Neither "I see" nor "I don't see" arose; annihilation is never met.** In that moment there was no "the seen thing," no "one who saw," and no "one who did not see" — no thought "I do not see a salad" ever arising, so *uccheda* (annihilationism) is never met at all; both are absent, "I see a salad" un-arisen and "I do not see a salad" un-arisen. These are the subtle points to watch, for what persists here is an un-arisen nature — and "it persists" does not mean an un-arisen nature persists, there being nothing there to speak of, which is itself non-existence, the very not-self.
- **Existence and non-existence seen at one point; "non-existence" is only a word.** The seeing of that non-existence, of not-self, must not be over-analyzed into "it never arose," for to give such an account is to overshoot the question; the nature stops there, and that stopping is cessation — while it is the *not-knowing* that is existence. So seeing existence rightly is itself seeing non-existence, both at one point, whereas people take an existence and then hunt a separate non-existence. Ultimately "non-existence" cannot even be spoken, the word itself losing meaning, for we only lay down the word "non-existence" because an "existence" was built; where no existence was engaged, "non-existence" too has no meaning — which is why the Buddha taught the whole body of Dhamma to show *existence*, there being no separate "non-existence" standing apart.
- **Seeing the knot as a knot is non-existence.** Existence arose only from not seeing the true nature, so the true nature is seen only by fully discerning existence — one must have the skill to draw out non-existence from the seeing of existence, never as a separate exercise; without an event, without the seen-and-seeing, there is nothing to call "non-existence," and where nothing was built there is nothing to say. Hence the knot itself must be seen: to see this knot *as* a knot is what "non-existence" means, for seeing rightly how existence is built through this very knot, what is realized is non-existence.

## Summary

This second part continues the salad example and turns on a reversal: the very "mistake" of not-seeing, which we blame on ourselves, is the golden bridge out of saṁsāra. The equanimous, un-knowable nature is where wisdom must stand, yet we knot even it back to our account — "I missed it because my mind was elsewhere" — making a fault of it, whereas that lapse, the unseen salad, is precisely the escape, since "the escape from saṁsāra lies within saṁsāra," and it must be seen rather than converted into a fault to be fixed by better mindfulness next time. For what we call the worldling's competence — working mindfully, tying the knot skilfully — is itself the trap: had the salad been seen on sitting there would be no problem, and only the not-seeing opens the door; the one reciting "salad, salad" to remember is the very one who did not see and does not know the true nature, so worldly success is the finest snare, which is why the Dhamma turns everything upside-down, *pubbe ananussutesu dhammesu*, a lamp in the dark. We repent lapses and hunt causes and devise methods only because we do not know the true nature; in truth we live not "in thought" but in un-mindfulness, and one who sees this has nothing to repent, since not-getting-knotted is the nature, and the reasons we fit are the world's ready answers that veil the true nature.

In that moment, moreover, there was no seen thing, no one who saw, and no one who did not see: no "I do not see a salad" ever arose, so annihilationism is never met, both "I see" and "I do not see" alike un-arisen — these being the subtle points to watch, where what "persists" is an un-arisen nature about which nothing can be said, which is itself non-existence and not-self. This seeing of non-existence must not be over-analyzed into "it never arose," for that overshoots; the nature simply stops, and that stopping is cessation, while the not-knowing is existence — so seeing existence rightly is itself seeing non-existence, both at one point, not an existence with a separate non-existence hunted afterward. In the end "non-existence" cannot even be spoken, the word losing its meaning, for we lay it down only because an existence was built, and where no existence was engaged "non-existence" too is meaningless — which is why the Buddha taught the whole body of Dhamma to show existence, there being no separate non-existence apart. Existence arose only from not seeing the true nature, so the true nature is seen only by fully discerning existence, drawing out non-existence from that seeing and never as a separate exercise; the knot itself must therefore be seen, for to see this knot as a knot is what non-existence means — seeing rightly how existence is built through this very knot, what is realized is non-existence.
