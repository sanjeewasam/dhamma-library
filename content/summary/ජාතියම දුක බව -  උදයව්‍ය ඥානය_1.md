## Sutta referred to

- **Jātipidukkhā** (birth itself is suffering) from the Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta and the standard dukkha exposition is the central reference. The teacher distinguishes "birth itself is suffering" (jātiyam dukka) from "suffering after birth" — the first paṭicca samuppāda link is the key.
- The **diṭṭha suta muta viññāta** (seen, heard, sensed, cognised) formula is used to establish that all four are always "arrived" (lābilam), never having arisen freshly or ceased.
- The **sensor door analogy** (from earlier discussions) is reused: the sensor is always ON — presence or absence of a person makes no difference to the sensor's state.

## Main dhamma points

- **Nittya saññā (permanence perception) is invisible.** When travelling, one searches for phone, bag, purse — never for the bed or floor one was sitting on. Those are so nittya (permanent) that no attention goes to them. This is the structure of our entire perceptual field: what has not arisen via manasikāra in this moment (walls, ceiling, sky, floor) is taken as nittya with absolute certainty. That certainty is itself the nittya saññā — and it is invisible.
- **The 360-degree analogy for sassata/ucchēda.** Existence (pavattuma, saṃsāra) is 360 degrees. At any moment, only the currently active āyatana angle (approximately 180°) is encountered. The remaining 180° — what is not currently perceived — is the ucchēda extreme. But this ucchēda can never be encountered: the moment one tries to grasp "non-existence," it becomes sassata (a permanent fact about absence). This is why ucchēda is "unthinkable" (hitannavaṭ bǣri).
- **Silence exercise for hearing.** The teacher goes silent for ten seconds and asks: did you hear anything? The participant says no. But to determine "no sound," the participant needed hearing capacity (æsīmē hækiyāva) to be active. Even during silence, hearing was present — it was the invariant that detected the absence of sound. This proves hearing is nittya: no change whether sound is present or absent.
- **Hearing is always yedilam (applied).** Hearing capacity does not turn on when sound arrives or off when sound departs. It is always already engaged. This constant engagement is itself avijjā — a nittya pavattuma (permanent existence) that is never recognised as such. Ordinary viññāṇa shows only "hearing exists" or "hearing doesn't exist" based on sound — it never shows the invariant hearing underneath.
- **All four — diṭṭha suta muta viññāta — are lābilam (always arrived).** Seeing, hearing, sensing, cognising are not events that come and go. They are always present as capacities. This permanent presence IS the pavattuma (existence) that constitutes our being. We live within these four at all times. But we cannot "catch" this — the moment we try, we create another concept.
- **Jātiyam dukka — birth ITSELF is suffering.** Not suffering after birth, but the haṭagænīma (arising) itself. The phone, the hand, the child — no dukkha can be found in any of them because they are so nittya that dukkha is invisible within them. Buddha's teaching points to the haṭagænīma itself — the arising, the lābīma (receiving), the hamba vīma (finding) — as dukkha. This is invisible precisely because of the nittya foundation.
- **Seeing anicca = seeing the wrongly-constructed nittya foundation.** The nittya bhāvaya (permanence) was constructed through avijjā — through not knowing. When one sees that this entire permanent foundation is itself a haṭagænīma (arising), one sees anicca. This is udayavyaya ñāṇa: not seeing things arise and pass away, but seeing that the permanent foundation itself is a wrongly-constructed arising.
- **Neither "arose and ceased" nor "never arose" applies.** If hearing has always been present with no variation, one cannot say it "arose and ceased" (haṭagen niruddha vunā). Nor can one say it "never arose" (haṭagenam nǣ) — that statement also fails because the pavattuma is evident. Every vigraha (analysis) — arose, ceased, never arose, always existed — is an attempt to describe a yathā svabhāvaya (actual nature) that refuses all descriptions.

## Summary

This is Part 1 of a three-part discussion on "ජාතියම දුක බව — උදයව්‍ය ඥානය" (Birth itself is suffering — Udayavyaya ñāṇa). The teacher opens with a deceptively simple observation: when travelling, we search for phone, bag, and purse, but never for the bed or floor — because these are so nittya (permanent) that they escape all attention. This everyday fact becomes the template for the entire discussion.

The 360-degree analogy establishes the structural invisibility of ucchēda: our existence at any moment is the full 360°, but we only encounter the currently active āyatana angle. The rest — the ucchēda extreme — can never be grasped. Any attempt to encounter "non-existence" immediately converts it into sassata (a permanent fact). This is why the Buddha's teaching is so difficult: the very ground one stands on is invisible.

The silence exercise makes this concrete. The teacher goes silent for ten seconds. The participant reports hearing nothing — but had to use hearing capacity to determine this. Hearing was active during silence and during speech alike; no change occurred in hearing itself. Sound's presence or absence is irrelevant to hearing's state. This invariance is nittya bhāvaya — a permanent existence that ordinary viññāṇa never displays. We use sound to determine whether hearing "exists" or "doesn't exist," but hearing itself never changes.

The teacher extends this to all four sense-capacities: diṭṭha (seen), suta (heard), muta (sensed), viññāta (cognised) are all lābilam — always arrived, never freshly arising. These four constitute our pavattuma (existence). We live within them at all times. But we cannot catch this because the nittya foundation is itself invisible — just as one never searches for the floor one sits on.

This leads to the core teaching: jātiyam dukka — birth itself is suffering. Not what happens after birth, but the haṭagænīma (arising) itself. The phone, the hand, the child — we cannot find dukkha in any of them because the nittya foundation makes dukkha invisible. Buddha points to the arising itself — the permanent presence of the four capacities — as dukkha. Seeing this wrongly-constructed nittya foundation for what it is — seeing that the permanent ground is itself a haṭagænīma — is what "seeing anicca" means. This is udayavyaya ñāṇa.

The discussion closes with a critical negation: if hearing has always been present, one cannot say it "arose and ceased," nor can one say it "never arose." Every analytical position fails. The yathā svabhāvaya refuses all descriptions — and the recognition that all descriptions fail is where avijjā becomes visible.
