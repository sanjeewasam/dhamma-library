## Sutta referred to

- **"Dukkhe lōkō paṭiṭṭhitō"** (the world is established on suffering) is quoted to show that the world itself — not events within it — is dukkha.
- **Caturārya sacca** (Four Noble Truths) is referenced as what a Buddha comes to show: the varaḍi padanama (wrong foundation) of the nittya bhāvaya.
- **"Avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā"** — the opening of paṭiccasamuppāda — is cited to explain why the formula begins with avijjā: the entire lōkaya (world) arises from not-knowing.
- **"Majjhē mantā na lippati"** is quoted for sammā diṭṭhi: seeing both anta (extremes) correctly, one is not smeared by either.
- The **diṭṭha suta muta viññāta** formula continues from Part 1 as the structural basis of pavattuma (existence).
- **Udayavyaya ñāṇa** is named explicitly as the nuvaṇa (wisdom) that sees through the nittya foundation.

## Main dhamma points

- **The varaḍi padanama (wrong foundation) of hearing.** We determine "hearing exists" when sound is present and "hearing doesn't exist" when sound is absent — using sound as the measure. But hearing capacity was invariant across both states. So the asti/nāsti (exists/doesn't exist) division is built on a varaḍi padanama — a foundation constructed from avijjā, not from yathā svabhāvaya. The "wrong" in the foundation is not a moral judgement but a structural fact: the division into two was never warranted by the underlying reality.
- **The child analogy for invisible dukkha.** The child is made nittya (permanent) through avijjā. Once nittya, dukkha within the child itself is invisible — only dukkha from the child (aging, illness, death of the child) is encountered. The child-as-haṭagænīma (the child as arising-itself) is never seen as dukkha. This parallels peṇīma (seeing): seeing has never been encountered as seeing-itself because it was never pirisin̆da dækīma'd (discerned). Its nittya foundation makes its dukkha invisible.
- **Saṅkhāra arise from asti/nāsti.** A song is heard → rāga (desire to keep hearing). The song stops → dveṣa (displeasure at absence). Both reactions require the asti/nāsti framework: "hearing exists now" vs "hearing doesn't exist now." Without this wrong division, neither rāga nor dveṣa could form. Kamma is built on asti/nāsti — every volitional act presupposes the wrong foundation of hearing-exists/hearing-doesn't-exist.
- **Āryā dukkha vs ordinary dukkha.** Ordinary dukkha is what comes from perceived things (child's illness, loss of a song). Āryā dukkha is the padanama itself — the nittya foundation on which the entire perceptual apparatus stands. It is hitāganna bǣri (unthinkable) — one cannot force oneself to see it as wrong because the foundation feels self-evidently correct.
- **Mūla avijjā sits in the padanama.** The padanama (foundation) of hearing — always present, always "correct" — is itself mūla avijjā (foundational ignorance). The not-knowing is not a separate thing layered on top; it IS the padanama. Seeing the padanama's varaḍi (wrongness) requires a nuvaṇa (wisdom) that exceeds viññāṇa, because viññāṇa is the instrument that operates on and confirms the padanama.
- **Udayavyaya ñāṇa is the crossing point.** Before this ñāṇa, one sees diṭṭha suta muta viññāta as nittya, sukha, attā. After it, one sees the same four as anicca, dukkha, anattā. The gap between these two is the kaḍaim (threshold) — and until one crosses it, the nittya padanama appears self-evidently correct. The crossing is not gradual understanding but a prajñā that sees the padanama's wrongness all at once — sammā diṭṭhi.
- **Anicca must be seen in the aprakṛta (non-manifest) too.** We have been seeing anicca only in prakṛta (manifest) things — forms arising and ceasing, sounds coming and going. But anicca must also be seen in the ucchēda extreme — in the "non-existence" that was never encountered. The nittya bhāvaya of hearing that operates invisibly during silence is itself anicca — but seeing this requires the udayavyaya ñāṇa.

## Summary

This is Part 2 of the "ජාතියම දුක බව — උදයව්‍ය ඥානය" discussion. The teacher deepens the examination of the varaḍi padanama (wrong foundation) established in Part 1, showing how the entire edifice of saṅkhāra, kamma, and lōkaya is built on the invisible nittya bhāvaya of the four sense-capacities.

The discussion opens by retracing the hearing analysis: we determined "hearing exists" from sound's presence and "hearing doesn't exist" from sound's absence — both measured by sound, not by hearing itself. Since hearing was invariant across both states, the asti/nāsti division was never warranted. This wrong foundation is not an intellectual error correctable by better thinking — it is mūla avijjā embedded in the very structure of perception.

The child analogy illustrates invisible dukkha: once the child is made nittya, dukkha of the child-as-arising is invisible. Only secondary dukkha (the child ages, gets sick, dies) is encountered. Buddha's teaching points to haṭagænīma itself — the arising, the finding, the perceiving — as dukkha. But this is invisible precisely because of the nittya foundation.

The teacher shows how saṅkhāra are built on asti/nāsti: a song heard produces rāga, a song's absence produces dveṣa. Both require the wrong foundation — "hearing exists now" vs "hearing doesn't exist now." Without this division, neither rāga nor dveṣa could arise. This is how kamma is generated from the invisible nittya bhāvaya.

The āryā dukkha / ordinary dukkha distinction follows: ordinary dukkha comes from perceived things; āryā dukkha is the padanama itself. A Buddha comes to show that the padanama — which feels self-evidently correct — is itself the dukkha. "Dukkhe lōkō paṭiṭṭhitō" — the world is established on dukkha means the world-as-haṭagænīma, not events within the world.

The teacher then names udayavyaya ñāṇa explicitly: the nuvaṇa that sees diṭṭha suta muta viññāta as anicca, dukkha, anattā rather than as nittya, sukha, attā. Before this ñāṇa, the gap between the two views is uncrossable — the nittya padanama appears self-evidently correct. After it, sammā diṭṭhi arises: seeing both anta correctly, one is "not smeared" (majjhē mantā na lippati) by either.

The discussion closes with an important pointer: anicca has so far been sought only in the prakṛta (manifest) — forms and sounds arising and ceasing. But it must also be seen in the aprakṛta — the ucchēda extreme, the "non-existence" that was never encountered. The nittya bhāvaya of hearing during silence is itself anicca — but this cannot be seen without the udayavyaya ñāṇa. The participant's confusion about nirodha is acknowledged as natural — the padanama's wrongness is hitāganna bǣri (unthinkable) by design.
