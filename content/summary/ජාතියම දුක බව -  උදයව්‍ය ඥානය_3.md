## Sutta referred to

- **"Dukkhe lōkō paṭiṭṭhitō"** (the world is established on suffering) is cited again, now clarified: the world rests on dukkha means diṭṭha suta muta viññāta — the four sense-capacities themselves, not events within them.
- **"Dukkhe ñāṇaṃ, samudaye ñāṇaṃ"** — the Four Noble Truths are called ñāṇa (wisdom-knowledge), not viññāṇa (consciousness-knowledge), because the āryā dukkha in the padanama cannot be accessed by ordinary viññāṇa.
- **Diṭṭha suta muta viññāta** formula continues as the structural centre — all four are shown to be mañññanā (conceptual constructions) built on avijjā.
- Ven. **Rēvata Thero** is referenced for the teaching that seeing mañññanā as mañññanā is itself seeing anicca, and for the phone analogy ("ōnnē ekēmayi tiyennē" — it's right there in it).
- The **fish-in-water and pig-in-mud** similes are used: born in it, living in it, dying in it — the medium is never detected from within.

## Main dhamma points

- **Mañññanā (conceptual construction) is the nittya padanama.** Both "hearing exists" and "hearing doesn't exist" are mañññanā — neither is ættha (true). The division into asti/nāsti was built on avijjā. Seeing this mañññanā as mañññanā — not replacing it with a "correct" view — is what seeing anicca means. Rēvata Thero is cited: mañññanāva mañññanāvak vidihata dækkot, that person saw anicca.
- **The ucchēda extreme was never experienced.** Non-hearing (noæsīma) was never experientially encountered — hearing was always active even during silence. But "hearing exists now" is said relative to "hearing didn't exist before" — a state that was never actually experienced. The waking state is made ættha relative to a sleep that was never experienced. Both sides of the asti/nāsti division rest on an unencountered ucchēda.
- **Mañññanā seen as mañññanā collapses rāga/dveṣa.** Once the padanama's wrongness is seen — that both asti and nāsti are mañññanā — the person cannot divide the world into "heard" vs "not-heard." Without this division, rāga (desire for what is heard) and dveṣa (aversion to what is not heard) cannot form. This is not suppression — the mechanism that generates them is dismantled at the root.
- **Nothing remains to be done (nirāyāsa).** Once the padanama's varaḍi (wrongness) is seen, there is no separate "anicca to find," no "nirodha to achieve," no "haṭagænīma to stop." The vigraha phrases — "arose and ceased," "never arose," "existed before and doesn't now" — are all attempts to describe within mañññanā what can only be seen by the nuvaṇa that transcends mañññanā. To the person with this nuvaṇa, such phrases are vihiḷu kathā (jokes).
- **The unborn-child analogy for redundant negation.** If a child was never born, saying "that child was never born" is redundant — there is no child to negate. Similarly, once the mañññanā foundation is seen for what it is, saying "it never arose" (ipadilam nǣ) is itself mañññanā — grasping "arising" to deny it. Both assertion and negation require the same wrong foundation.
- **Fish-in-water / pig-in-mud.** Diṭṭha suta muta viññāta is the medium: born in it, living in it, dying in it. The fish never encounters "water" as water. The pig never encounters "mud" as mud. Similarly, we never encounter the mañññanā padanama as mañññanā — because every encounter is already within it. This is why āryā dukkha requires ñāṇa, not viññāṇa: dukkhe ñāṇaṃ, not dukkhe viññāṇaṃ.
- **Standing-on-feet analogy (from earlier discussions) reused.** The ground beneath one's feet is covered by the very act of standing. No indriya (sense faculty) and no thought (hitala) can reach it. Just so, the mañññanā padanama of diṭṭha suta muta viññāta is covered by the very operation of viññāṇa — indriya-gōcara can never reach it.
- **Udayavyaya ñāṇa sees both udaya and vyaya together.** Not sequentially — "first arising, then ceasing" — but simultaneously, as one padanama. This simultaneous seeing is what reveals the padanama's mañññanā nature. Once seen, saṃsāra becomes transparent — like a scan — and every position anyone takes ("it arose," "it never arose," "it ceased") is instantly locatable within the mañññanā structure.

## Summary

This is Part 3 — the concluding segment — of the "ජාතියම දුක බව — උදයව්‍ය ඥානය" discussion. The teacher brings the analysis to its culmination: the nittya padanama of diṭṭha suta muta viññāta is itself mañññanā (conceptual construction), and seeing it as mañññanā — without replacing it with any other view — is what seeing anicca means.

The discussion opens with the teacher reiterating the structural problem: we remain on the nittya padanama because its wrongness has never been encountered. The udayavyaya ñāṇa that crosses this threshold is not ordinary understanding — it sees the mūla avijjā embedded in diṭṭha suta muta viññāta itself. When it is seen, the person encounters not diṭṭha or suta but the varaḍi padanama — the wrong foundation built from nodannakam (not-knowing).

The teacher then shows how the asti/nāsti division is built on mañññanā: non-hearing was never experienced (hearing was active even during silence), yet "hearing exists now" is asserted relative to that unencountered non-hearing — just as "I am awake" is asserted relative to a sleep that was never experienced. Both "hearing exists" and "hearing doesn't exist" are mañññanā. The nittya foundation that seemed self-evidently correct IS mañññanā — and seeing it as such is what Rēvata Thero calls "seeing anicca."

The consequences follow immediately: once the asti/nāsti division is seen as mañññanā, rāga and dveṣa cannot form. The person cannot divide experience into "heard" vs "not-heard" — so desire for the heard and aversion to its absence have no substrate. This is not effort or suppression — the generative mechanism is dismantled. The path is nirāyāsa (effortless).

The teacher addresses the natural confusion: "If the padanama is seen, what about haṭagena niruddha vunā (arose and ceased)? What about ipadilam nǣ (never arose)?" Both are rejected as vihiḷu kathā (jokes) to the person with this nuvaṇa. The unborn-child analogy makes this vivid: if a child was never born, saying "that child was never born" is saying nothing — there is no child to negate. Similarly, once the mañññanā padanama is seen, negating it is itself mañññanā.

The fish-in-water and pig-in-mud similes drive home why this is so difficult: diṭṭha suta muta viññāta is the medium we are born in, live in, and die in. The medium is never detected from within. This is why Buddha calls the seeing of āryā dukkha a ñāṇa — it cannot be accessed by the viññāṇa that operates within the medium. The standing-on-feet analogy is reused: the ground beneath one's feet is covered by standing; no indriya can reach it.

The discussion closes with the teacher noting that the udayavyaya ñāṇa sees both udaya and vyaya simultaneously — not as sequential events but as one padanama. Once seen, saṃsāra becomes transparent. Every position — "arose," "ceased," "never arose" — is locatable within the mañññanā structure. Nibbāna is "within oneself" (taman tulama tiyenavā) — not because it is a hidden treasure to be found, but because the mañññanā padanama one stands on is itself the only obstruction, and seeing it is all that is required.
