## Sutta referred to

- **Madhupiṇḍika Sutta (MN 18)** — the proliferation chain is quoted directly: "cakkhuñca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu viññāṇaṃ … yaṃ vedeti taṃ sañjānāti, yaṃ sañjānāti taṃ vitakketi, yaṃ vitakketi taṃ papañceti … papañca saññā samudā caranti" (dependent on eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises … what one feels one recognises, what one recognises one thinks about, what one thinks about one proliferates … proliferation-perceptions assail the person).
- The phrase "phassa paññattiyā paññāpessati iti tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso" (contact is a designation; when it is designated, a convergence of three becomes contact) is cited, likely from the **Mahāvedalla Sutta (MN 43)** or a related Abhidhamma source.
- **Udayabbaya ñāṇa** (knowledge of arising and passing away) is named explicitly.

## Main dhamma points

- The **tile analogy** (ටයිල් උපමාව): two tiles placed side by side are separated by a line (ඉර). The line does not belong to either tile, yet without both tiles the line cannot exist. Equally, the line is not a place where tiles are found — it is precisely where they do not apply.
- This creates a double bind: one cannot understand the line through the tiles (the line contains no "tile-ness"), nor can one see the line without the tiles (remove them and the line vanishes). **Phassa cannot be comprehended either from its conditions or apart from them.**
- The line (phassa/contact) is what makes two tiles appear as two — it is the origin of duality. Yet within the line itself, no "tile" story has ever arisen. This is **nirodha** seen from within samudaya itself.
- Both the establishment (piṭiṭīma) and the non-establishment (nopiṭiṭīma) of the two sides are visible from the same point — the line. This is **udayabbaya ñāṇa**: seeing arising and ceasing simultaneously, not sequentially.
- "Phassa paññattiyā paññāpessati" — contact is a paññatti (designation), not a persisting thing. When phassa is "designated," a convergence of three (tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati) occurs, and the entire chain of vedanā → saññā → vitakka → papañca unfolds.
- The Madhupiṇḍika Sutta chain is mapped onto the analogy: from the "line" (phassa), a "seeing" is designated (paññāpessati); from that, the two sides (eye and form) are recognised (sañjānāti); from recognition, thinking about them begins (vitakketi); from thinking, one takes them as existing (papañceti — proliferation). Papañca saññā samudā caranti — these proliferation-perceptions then assail the person with rāga and dosa.
- The teacher stresses that phassa — like the line — has never been "found" (hambu vecca næti ekak). It is viññāṇa agōcara (beyond the range of viññāṇa). Yet from this very unfound-ness, the entire pævatvīma (continuity/existence) of "eye and form" is projected.
- Seeing the non-establishment of the two tiles from the line is how anattā becomes manifest. Seeing the non-establishment itself is anicca. That a non-established pair nevertheless appears to establish is dukkha. All three characteristics (ti-lakkhaṇa) become visible from the correct seeing of phassa.

## Summary

This discussion builds its entire argument on a single concrete image: two floor tiles placed edge to edge, separated by a visible line (ඉර). The teacher begins by asking a deceptively simple question — does this line belong to either tile? It does not, because the line is not found within either tile. But neither can one say the line exists independently of the tiles: remove both tiles and no line remains. The teacher shows that both conclusions — "the line belongs to the tiles" and "the line exists apart from the tiles" — are wrong. This double impossibility is the analogy for phassa (contact).

The teacher then deepens the analysis. The line is precisely the place where neither tile is found. Yet it is also the line that makes two tiles visible as two. Without the line, one would not even know there are two tiles; with the line, the story of "two tiles" appears. This maps directly onto phassa: contact is what makes "eye" and "form" appear as a pair, yet contact itself contains neither eye nor form. It is viññāṇa agōcara — a point that viññāṇa cannot colonise.

From here, the teacher introduces the Pāli phrase "phassa paññattiyā paññāpessati" — contact is a paññatti (designation), a pænavīmak (something set up), not something that was already sitting there waiting to be found. When this designation occurs, a convergence of three (tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati) takes place: eye, form, and eye-consciousness arise together. A "seeing" is designated — and in that very act, the two hetu (conditions) are retroactively constructed. This is the mechanism of papañca laid bare.

The teacher then traces the full Madhupiṇḍika Sutta sequence: what is felt (vedeti) is recognised (sañjānāti) — one identifies "eye and form." What is recognised is thought about (vitakketi) — one begins to investigate "what is this?" What is thought about is proliferated (papañceti) — one concludes "there is an eye, there is a form, they really exist." And these proliferation-perceptions (papañca saññā) then assail the person, generating rāga and dosa. The entire chain unfolds from a single point — phassa — which itself was never a "thing found."

The discussion concludes by showing how this single seeing yields all three ti-lakkhaṇa simultaneously. From the line, one sees that both tiles never truly established — this is anicca (impermanence, but understood as non-establishment, not as "something arose and then ceased"). One sees that despite this non-establishment, there appears to be establishment — this is dukkha (the stress of an impossible situation being maintained). And one sees that the line itself, where both sides claim ownership, belongs to neither — this is anattā. The teacher identifies this as udayabbaya ñāṇa: both arising and ceasing are seen in one act of understanding from phassa itself, not by first watching arising and then later discovering ceasing.
