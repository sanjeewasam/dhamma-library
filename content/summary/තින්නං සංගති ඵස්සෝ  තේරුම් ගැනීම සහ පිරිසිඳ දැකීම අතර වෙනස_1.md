## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on the difference between *understanding* "the meeting of the three is contact" and *discerning* it, showing that contact is the birth of self-view built on two things (body and the tangible) that are never met, so that even "I have understood *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*" is a fresh knot.
- **A "knowing" is never met, so it cannot be seen as a knot.** Only after a "knowing" is built can one speak of "foot and ground," for the talk arises with the knowing; before that, where the talk arises cannot be grasped — this is why "the origin must be seen at its very origin" (*yoni*), for seen at the origin what appears is the *non*-arising. No basis can be shown for the knowing, neither "because of these causes" nor "not because of them," and only one who sees "it springs from where the knowing is never met" recognizes it *as* a knot.
- **The equanimity before the knowing is un-cognizable, so its baselessness is hidden.** The un-met place is never met by us; before the knowing is built we stand in an equanimity that can be grasped in no way and is not understood — and because it is not understood, that whole side is shut off, so we never see that the knowing is built on no basis. Once built, consciousness understands clearly "it was felt because of foot-and-ground, or nose-and-breath," and, holding those two causes, thinks "because of them" or "not because of them," but either way the two have already been taken as existing.
- **Two never-met things are shown as the cause; conceiving them births the mind.** To see the un-met place is to see the baselessness, whereupon consciousness cannot fabricate the lie *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*; but instead consciousness veils the "cannot" by saying "I did not feel it because I did not attend to it," and so the very not-cognizability of the sense-base (*āyatana*) — that body and the tangible can never be met *as* body and tangible — is covered over. This veiling of the "cannot" is itself the cause of consciousness's birth: consciousness arises precisely because the un-met is veiled, so *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*. Body and the tangible are two un-arisen, never-met things, and it is by conceiving these two that the mind arises, an illusion (*māyā*) inseparable from the fabrication — for a persisting thing cannot arise from two never-met things, so *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* is a wholly un-arisen nature, a mere appearance produced by misapprehension.
- **Understanding it is a further misapprehension; the Dhamma only points to the knot.** A Buddha taught *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* to show the flaw in taking "there is a body, there is the tangible, and when they meet consciousness arises" — but this cannot be seen while one thinks *from* the three, for the very thinking is the misapprehension. So to "understand" *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati* is a deeper misapprehension: what is grasped as "the Dhamma" is only the same knot again, since the suttas are a *means* (*pariyāya*) to reveal and loosen the knot, not a doctrine to memorize. Understood as arising-from-conditions, consciousness itself arises from cessation-natures — *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ* — for what arises from never-met causes is itself un-arisen; hence the puthujjana's *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* is, in short, *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, "there is an eye, a form, I see; an ear, a sound, I hear," which is his very birth.
- **Effortless, because it never arose; the loom image.** When the view clears and wisdom stands in the true nature, this knot cannot be tied, and this happens effortlessly (*nirāyāsa*) — because what never arose need not be removed, cracked or broken, whereas to "remove" a built thing would take effort and only entangle one further, like being caught deeper in a thicket. Like the loom: the upper thread runs only for use, but there is no lower-shuttle thread, so nothing knots into a becoming; the wise one still functions — eats when eating, "sees when seeing" — looking outwardly identical to the worldling, since the upper thread is the same, but the emptiness of the lower shuttle is un-graspable, so no one can tell from another's behaviour where their wisdom stands.
- **Sakkāya-diṭṭhi, the "why didn't I notice" knot, and the Sañcetanā doubt.** The worldling's whole foundation is the *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati* knot — "I, and a nose, and a breath, that is what I feel" — and from it "I go on in saṁsāra." The "why didn't I hear / feel it before?" reflex adds yet another knot: consciousness, to cover the never-met sense-base, says "because I did not focus," veiling the not-met with a further sense-base, and irrelevant thoughts knot on. And because feeling now is "100% real," the mind cannot see itself as false, so the doubt of the Sañcetanā Sutta arises — "how could I be here now unless I had breathed since morning?" — which fixes a "present" as fully real, makes "I exist now" certain, and thereby makes the past un-deniable, manufacturing self-ness where there is only not-self, lending a fabricated persistence to breath, nose and consciousness that none of them has.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue distinguishes *understanding* "the meeting of the three is contact" from *discerning* it, showing that contact is the birth of self-view built on two things — body and the tangible — that are never actually met. A "knowing" is never met, the teacher observes, so only after it is built can one speak of foot-and-ground, the talk arising with the knowing; before that, where the talk arises cannot be grasped, which is why the origin must be seen at its very origin, for seen there what appears is the non-arising. No basis can be shown for the knowing, neither "because of these causes" nor "not because of them," and only one who sees that it springs from where the knowing is never met recognizes it as a knot. The equanimity before the knowing is un-cognizable and unrecognized, so its baselessness stays hidden; once the knowing is built, consciousness clearly "understands" it was felt because of foot-and-ground or nose-and-breath, and, holding those causes, thinks "because of them" or "not because of them," either way already taking the two as existing. To see the un-met place is to see the baselessness, whereupon consciousness cannot fabricate *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* — but it instead veils the "cannot" with "I did not feel it because I did not attend," covering over that the sense-base can never be met as body-and-tangible, and this veiling is itself the cause of consciousness's birth, so *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*.

Body and the tangible being two never-met things, it is by conceiving them that the mind arises, an illusion inseparable from the fabrication, for a persisting thing cannot arise from two never-met things — so *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* is a wholly un-arisen nature, a mere appearance produced by misapprehension. A Buddha taught it to expose the flaw in "there is a body, there is the tangible, and when they meet consciousness arises," which cannot be seen while one thinks from the three; so even to "understand" *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati* is a deeper misapprehension, what is grasped as "the Dhamma" being only the same knot again, since the suttas are a means to loosen the knot, not a doctrine to memorize — consciousness arising from never-met causes being itself un-arisen, *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ*, so that the puthujjana's *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* is in short *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, his very birth.
