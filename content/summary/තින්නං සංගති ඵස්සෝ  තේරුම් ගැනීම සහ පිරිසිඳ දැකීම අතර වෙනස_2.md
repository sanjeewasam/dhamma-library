## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue completing the *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* talk, showing that the "present" is a fabricated middle that spreads past and future to either side, that a persistence and self-ness taken by non-comprehension are not really there, and that the knot cannot understand itself — so association with the wise is needed.
- **The fabricated middle spreads past and future.** Once "I exist now" is made real, "how could I be here unless I had breathed since morning?" makes the past real, and "I will persist" makes the future real; from this middle both past and future beautifully spread to either side — not a past that was there arising here, but past and future running because of the not-knowing at the middle. This middle can never be grasped from either side, because it is shown *from* the very place the middle is fabricated: making the middle here fabricates the two sides there, and constant talk of the two sides only further confirms the middle.
- **The middle is set up by non-comprehension, not real.** When wisdom comes to this middle, the "middle" is seen to be no real middle but one established purely by misapprehension and non-comprehension — a flaw of view. So one must clearly grasp that this does *not* destroy some existing thing: a persistence, a self-ness, a permanence are taken by non-comprehension, and no truly-existing permanence or self-ness is met here at all.
- **Suffering is never met; the child-and-father mirage.** As with the child and father, to the child no "mirage" is met but only water, while to the father it is a mirage — so the child's level of suffering can never be told, for what the child meets is a never-met thing taken as water, an utter suffering relative to the father. Until suffering is seen at that level, "nibbāna" sounds only like "destroying an existing thing"; the one in non-comprehension takes it so, because he stands on the view that *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati* is real, and from that base any attempt to understand nibbāna yields only "these things do not exist."
- **The knot cannot understand itself; the true nature is never met by thinking.** Seeing "permanent" as a mistake means seeing that the never-met, unknown nature is this thing's real, *yathābhūta* nature — and it is by *not* knowing that "it is met" can never be found out by thinking, since thought and reasoning themselves spring from lack of wisdom about that unknown nature. So the middle can never be taken by thinking; catching the flaw in thinking-and-reasoning *is* seeing *saṅkhāra-dukkha*, which is precisely the "there is such a thing" taken by non-comprehension. From that mindset, however much one reasons, the result is always "these things do not exist."
- **Understanding the knot with the knot stays on the same track.** To catch the flaw of *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* with yet another *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* is to run on the same track and never reach the true nature; the comprehension must come from the cessation-nature, and one must also see that *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* itself arises from this very misapprehension — so it can never be understood by means of itself. The sense-base's non-arising, veiled again by another sense-base, cannot be seen unless pointed out close at hand; one only builds some other frame, for one thinks from within one's own range, and one's own non-comprehension cannot be purified by one's own thinking — not even by listening to a talk alone on YouTube.
- **Hence association with the wise.** This is so deep it can only be shown finger-close by a companion, which is exactly why the teacher had "given association with the wise" (*saṁsēvana*) for this — for relative to one's own frame one can never reach the truth by oneself, and one's own misapprehension cannot understand itself.

## Summary

This second part completes the *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* talk by showing that the "present" is a fabricated middle. Once "I exist now" is made real, the doubt "how could I be here unless I had breathed since morning?" makes the past real and "I will persist" makes the future real, so from this middle both past and future spread to either side — not a past that was there, but past and future running because of the not-knowing at the middle, which can never be grasped from either side since it is shown from the very place it is fabricated, each mention of the two sides only confirming it further. When wisdom comes, the "middle" is seen to be no real middle but one set up purely by misapprehension, a flaw of view — so one must grasp clearly that this destroys no existing thing: the persistence, self-ness and permanence are taken by non-comprehension, and no truly-existing permanence or self is met at all. As with the child to whom the mirage is only water while to the father it is a mirage, the child's suffering can never be told, and until suffering is seen at that level "nibbāna" sounds only like destroying an existing thing — which is how the one in non-comprehension takes it, standing on the view that *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati* is real, from which base any attempt to understand nibbāna yields only "these things do not exist."
