## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue arguing that the true nature is "cannot-be-known," un-cognizable by consciousness — that our only difference from a tree is the *thinking* that veils this — and that the path is therefore effortless, since where nothing can be done, neither doing nor not-doing applies.
- **The whole process runs without knowing, like a tree.** Ordinarily thinking "arises by itself," un-stoppable by force — an underlying-tendency level that never ceases — and we hold the view that something can be known through it. But the worldling walks without knowing "walking," sits without knowing chair-or-body, breathes without knowing nose-or-breath: the entire process runs, like a tree, without any understanding, for a tree takes in carbon dioxide and gives off oxygen with no knowledge of doing so, and our natural state is no different.
- **Our only "difference" from the tree is the thinking that hides the sameness.** The one thing a tree lacks and we seem to have is that we "can think" and know something by thinking — yet seen by wisdom there is no difference between the tree and us, and it is precisely by not knowing this sameness that we lend value to "thinking." The nature of it is: we cannot understand, cannot know; that "cannot" is identical for tree and for us, and is un-cognizable to consciousness — which is exactly why it seems that by "knowing" (i.e. by attention) we gain something, since consciousness *is* knowing and always presents "it can be known," veiling the "cannot," which is our real nature.
- **The "cannot" cannot be known by thinking.** Can the "cannot-know" be grasped? It cannot be known by thinking, for that would be one more knowing. Seeing the flaw in thinking-and-reasoning is seeing *saṅkhāra-dukkha*; but from within the view that "I can think and thereby know," one only builds more view — "the more one tries to understand, the more the view is built, and the 'cannot' is veiled."
- **The brahma-world of walkers: seeking stopping by walking.** As "our Vaṅkagiri thero" tells it, in a certain brahma-world beings are born already walking and never stop; told by someone that in another world there is such a thing as "stopping," they — whose whole habit is walking — go this way and that trying to *do* something to stop, seeking a method, because they do not know they are walking, and "stopping" can be understood only by first seeing "I am walking, walking." Handled carefully, the point is that it is by not knowing their own nature that they seek a method; likewise we, born into the "cannot-know," try to stop saṁsāra by thinking, because thinking is the only habit we know, as walking is theirs — even seeking nibbāna by doing, by thinking, by understanding. In truth, seen rightly, our very standing-place is a stopping, we already stand in nibbāna, but it is veiled because consciousness has fallen across it.
- **Cause-and-fruit arises within the trying-to-understand.** It is within the very effort to understand that the law of cause-and-fruit is born: not knowing the un-know-able nature, from thinking arises "there is an ear, a sound, hearing; a nose, a breath, and their contact is why it is felt," one side built as cause and the other as fruit — *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā, saṅkhāra-paccayā viññāṇaṁ*, rooted in the not-seeing. A tree has no concept "I take in CO₂ and give off O₂, therefore I am a tree, I persist by this"; we, however, describe even the tree with the foolish concepts we built for ourselves, and cannot see through the simile to our being tree-like, because we apply to the tree the very "I-can" process that is our delusion — so the tree-simile must be taken only for the "cannot," not for the concepts.
- **Effortless, because nothing can be done; neither doing nor not-doing.** If one sees this nature *as* the "cannot," sees that saṁsāra is lived in ignorance and wisdom arises toward the not-knowing — seeing ignorance as ignorance — then what is there to do, and what to refrain from? Nothing can be done where nothing is met, so one neither takes a stand nor strives, *piṭṭhāda nosita vāyāmda nokara*, and thus crosses over — the path being effortless because the nature within not-knowing is itself effortless. Both "doing" and "not-doing" spring from the view "I can," a conceiving built on the seen-heard-sensed-cognized; and one cannot escape either extreme without seeing this — "I do it" and "I refrain, meditate, stop" alike being fabricated, the not-doing itself a further doing driven by "I can not-do."
- **Only view seen as view realizes the "cannot"; harder than the hair-split.** This cannot be practiced-into like a skill: unlike splitting a horse-hair into seven and hitting one strand with an arrow, which by trying and practicing one could eventually manage, removing *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* cannot be *done* at all — not "harder," but of a wholly different kind, since the view is exactly the four (seen-heard-sensed-cognized) by which "I exist" is felt, and the conceiving "I can hear and see" arises from not seeing conceiving *as* conceiving. What is built cannot see its own building from within, so view can never be seen through view; only a wisdom that sees view *as* view realizes the "cannot" — and that very realization becomes the wisdom, the falling-onto-the-path.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue argues that the true nature is "cannot-be-known," un-cognizable by consciousness, and that our only apparent difference from a tree is the thinking that veils this. Thinking arises by itself, un-stoppable by force, and we hold that something can be known through it; yet the worldling walks without knowing "walking," sits without knowing chair-or-body, breathes without knowing nose-or-breath — the whole process running like a tree that takes in carbon dioxide and gives off oxygen with no knowledge of doing so. The one thing we seem to have that the tree lacks is that we "can think" and thereby know, but seen by wisdom there is no difference, and it is by not knowing this sameness that we prize thinking; our real nature is the "cannot," identical for tree and for us, un-cognizable to consciousness, which is why it seems that by knowing we gain something, since consciousness is knowing and always presents "it can be known," veiling the "cannot." That "cannot" can never be grasped by thinking, for that is one more knowing; seeing the flaw in thinking-and-reasoning is seeing *saṅkhāra-dukkha*, whereas from within "I can think and know" one only builds more view.

The brahma-world simile of "our Vaṅkagiri thero" makes it vivid: beings born already walking, never stopping, told that in another world there is "stopping," go this way and that trying to *do* something to stop, seeking a method, because they do not know they are walking — and "stopping" can be understood only by first seeing "I am walking." So we, born into the "cannot-know," try to stop saṁsāra by thinking, thinking being our only habit as walking is theirs, even seeking nibbāna by doing and understanding, when in truth our very standing-place is a stopping and we already stand in nibbāna, veiled because consciousness has fallen across it. Within this trying-to-understand the law of cause-and-fruit is born — from not knowing the un-know-able nature, thinking builds "there is an ear, a sound, hearing," one side as cause and one as fruit, *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā, saṅkhāra-paccayā viññāṇaṁ* — and we even describe the tree with our own foolish concepts rather than seeing, through the simile, our tree-like "cannot." Seeing this nature as the "cannot," and ignorance as ignorance, there is nothing to do and nothing to refrain from, so one neither stands nor strives, *piṭṭhāda nosita vāyāmda nokara*, crossing over effortlessly, since the nature within not-knowing is itself effortless — both doing and not-doing springing from the view "I can," a conceiving on the seen-heard-sensed-cognized, the not-doing itself a further doing. And this cannot be practiced-into: unlike splitting a horse-hair into seven and hitting one strand, which trying could eventually achieve, removing *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* cannot be done at all, for the view is the very four by which "I exist" is felt, and what is built cannot see its own building — so view is never seen through view, and only a wisdom that sees view as view realizes the "cannot," that realization itself becoming the wisdom and the falling-onto-the-path.
