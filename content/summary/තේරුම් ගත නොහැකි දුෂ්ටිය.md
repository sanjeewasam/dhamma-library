## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue showing that the "weight" of an action lives in volition, not the limb; that thinking "I scratch" is built on a not-there grasped first; and that non-fabrication and the absolute can never be understood relatively — so the path needs both hearing the Dhamma and a good friend.
- **The weight is in the volition, not the arm.** Sitting idle, the arm's weight is not felt; only when attention "I am holding the arm up" arises is the weight there, in that very event. The arm does not rise by itself but from an arisen volition, and we lend value — "I lifted an arm" — so the "weight" story comes with the "arm" being built for that volition; volition and arm cannot be separated, the volition having become the arm, and seen as volition no arm is found.
- **Thinking "I scratch" is built on a not-there grasped first.** Merely thinking "I must scratch my back" does not move the hand; in a real itch, prompted by a mosquito bite, one thinks and scratches, but told simply to "think you scratch your back," one only thinks and the hand goes nowhere. This is because before thinking one already holds "I have no wish to scratch" — the *not-there* is grasped, and only then, on the there-side, does one think "I scratch"; the hand does not move precisely because one holds the not-there ("no need to scratch"), a condition one cannot even see, so "I only thought it, I did not do it" arises from the very "merely thinking." One thinks because told to, not from any real wish, and this shows how "I can do it" is built on a hidden not-there.
- **Non-fabrication is not a fabricated thing's negation.** The Buddha says fabrication (*saṅkhāra*) happens by foolishness, by not-knowing, in a place that was never fabricated; but being 100% caught in "I was fabricated," we fit even the Buddha's words to that trap, so when he says "it is not fabricated" we take it to mean "this chair is not fabricated" — a *relative* negation of a fabricated thing, always sliding from the "there-is" to a "there-is-not." Written in a sutta, "non-fabrication" is read relative to the fabricated, and Dhamma understood relatively can never free — which is why it cannot be done alone.
- **The absolute can never be thought; understanding is itself the obstacle.** A person always thinks relatively, conditioning one thing on another, so from that relativity no *absolute* meaning can ever be drawn from a sutta — one only conceives and imagines, never catching the absolute, and one cannot even use a sutta to catch the flaw of this fitting. Understanding itself is therefore an obstacle to seeing the absolute nature, for "to understand" is to be conditioned, to grasp a known thing and think, whereas the absolute is a place with no relativity, no condition met, where thinking cannot operate at all.
- **Only one who has reached right view can show it; hence the good friend.** This non-fabrication — not the negation of a fabricated thing — can be shown only by one who has reached the level of the wisdom that saw the not-knowing *as* not-knowing, right view; it cannot be shown to one who has not seen it, for the unseeing one is inside fabrication like a fish in water, and whatever he "shows" is only the fabricated chair with its own negation, forever going from "there-is" to "there-is-not." This is where a Buddha's worth and the hearing of the true Dhamma come in, and why association with the wise is set as "number one" alongside hearing: because caught in a relative existence one can never think one's way to it, even what is caught by hearing being understood only by fitting it to some known relative — that very understanding blocking the absolute, since to understand is to condition, and the absolute meets no condition.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue, stitched from two parts, first shows that the "weight" of an action lives in volition, not the limb: an idle arm has no felt weight, and only when the attention "I am holding my arm up" arises is the weight there, in that very event — the arm rising not by itself but from a volition to which we lend value, so that "arm" and "weight" are built together, volition and arm inseparable, and seen as volition no arm is found. The point sharpens with scratching: merely thinking "I must scratch my back" moves no hand, for before thinking one already holds "I have no wish to scratch," grasping the not-there first and only then, on the there-side, thinking "I scratch"; the hand stays still precisely because one holds that hidden not-there, and "I only thought it, I did not do it" arises from the very "merely thinking" — one thinking only because told to, not from any real wish, which lays bare how "I can do it" rests on a not-there one cannot even see.

The second part turns to non-fabrication. The Buddha says fabrication happens by foolishness in a place never fabricated, but being wholly caught in "I was fabricated," we fit his words to that trap, so "it is not fabricated" is heard as "this chair is not fabricated" — a relative negation of a fabricated thing, always sliding from "there-is" to "there-is-not." Written in a sutta, non-fabrication is read relative to the fabricated, and Dhamma understood relatively can never free; a person always thinks relatively, conditioning one thing on another, so no absolute meaning can be drawn from a sutta by thinking, and one cannot even use a sutta to catch the flaw of this fitting — understanding itself being the obstacle, since to understand is to be conditioned, whereas the absolute meets no condition and thinking cannot operate there. Hence this non-fabrication, which is not a fabricated thing's negation, can be shown only by one who has reached right view, the wisdom that saw not-knowing as not-knowing; it cannot be shown to the unseeing, who is inside fabrication like a fish in water and can only produce the fabricated chair with its own negation. This is where a Buddha's worth and the hearing of the true Dhamma come in, and why association with the wise is set as "number one" alongside hearing — because caught in a relative existence one can never think one's way to the absolute, even what is caught by hearing being understood only by fitting it to a known relative, that very understanding blocking the absolute nature.
