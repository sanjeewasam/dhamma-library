## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on how "understanding" is itself a delusion — a conceiving of something that can never really be understood — so that "I" is born in the very understanding, "mindfulness" as usually cultivated is holding the thief, and cessation is realized only within arising.
- **Understanding is a conceiving of the un-understandable.** Walking is not "understood" while walking; only when attention is applied — "I am walking" — does the "understanding" appear, and that applying of attention *is* the "cognized" of seen-heard-sensed-cognized, which is conceiving (*maññanā*). Yet we do not experience even *this* as "thinking"; thinking is never itself understood, which is why walking proceeds by itself with no "I am going," breathing goes on without our knowing nose-or-breath, and "feeling" is not something cognizable to the mind. Not knowing this to be conceiving, we take the thinking as real "because we cannot understand it."
- **The four are conceivings; "I" is born in the understanding.** "I exist" is understood through the four — hearing, seeing, sensing, thinking — each a conceiving, in one of which we are always engaged, like a fish in water; when one thinks of walking, "I exist" is understood, and it is in that very understanding that "I" is born. Because the way this "I"-view arises through thinking is not seen *as* a view, it seems "there is a separate me, and a separate walking I understood," and the two are knotted — whereas rightly seen, "I am born through the walking," not that a pre-existing "I" knots a walking to itself.
- **The un-knowable is what "view" means; recognition is unrelated to any thing.** These seen-heard-sensed-cognized stand in a never-knowable nature — that is exactly why they are called *diṭṭhi* (view): things the mind cannot know — and it is from that un-knowable place that "I exist" is understood, this being the un-graspable point. Feeling is never met when one merely sits, so if feeling is an un-knowable and one does not know it as such, then thinking *about* that un-knowable builds "I exist, I breathe." Recognition (*saññā*) has no thing attached to it: in a dream a dog is recognized with no dog and no eye present, so recognition is only recognition, unrelated to any object — yet we suppose "if it is recognized, there is a thing," being deceived by attention, which cannot even be recognized *as* attention, so a never-met thing is taken as "there is."
- **Consciousness inescapable; the phone and the bell.** There is no escaping this "it can be known" of consciousness, and we do not even know a nature free of it exists, present all the while; that is why it cannot be grasped and why the account cannot be understood. As a house-bell is silent until struck, or a phone's being switched on is realized only when a call comes, we never register the ever-on state without a sound — and it is by not registering it, by not knowing this nature, that we come to think about it at all, and take "it can be known" as understood.
- **"Mindfulness" as usually cultivated is holding the thief.** We give great value to knowing — "I knew it, my mindfulness is good; now I feel the breath, the nose is there" — and this "keeping mindfulness" is grasping the very thief, consciousness's fabricated "there is something," which cannot be grasped from any side; the Buddha compared it to a magician showing magic at a four-way crossroads. Because it feels 100% real once the knot is tied, we think "now I am keeping good mindfulness," when all that is done is thinking about a never-met thing — this daily practice being *ayoniso-manasikāra*, unwise attention. To one who has seen this, the whole day's "practice" is seen as unwise attention; for it to become *yoniso-manasikāra*, one must see that "I" is being built through the very "walking," that consciousness is deceiving one with its magic, confirming "I exist" through the pretence of knowing an un-knowable.
- **A seeing, not a doing; cessation realized only within arising.** This is only a vision, a seeing, with nothing to *do*: one must see that we live in delusion and act by unwise attention, and from that seeing the cessation of ignorance follows — seeing that whatever we do is done caught in ignorance, caught in consciousness. This is why the path rests 100% on the good friend and not on oneself, for only one who has seen it, who has gone beyond words, can point it out — it cannot be written, only shown through examples using the very things we foolishly know, breath and nose, since this cessation cannot be viewed on its own but must be seen *within* an arising. The abandoning of arising is itself the realization of cessation; seeing arising, its very arising appears as a non-arising, and that appearing-as-non-arising is what "realizing cessation" means — not a separate cessation standing apart, just as seeing the dream as false is its cessation.

## Summary

This two-speaker good-friend dialogue turns on how "understanding" is itself a delusion. Walking is not understood while walking; only when attention "I am walking" is applied does the understanding appear, and that applying of attention is the "cognized" of the four, a conceiving — yet we do not experience even this as thinking, thinking never being itself understood, which is why walking, breathing and sitting proceed with no idea of leg, ground, breath or body, and "feeling" is not cognizable to the mind. Not knowing this to be conceiving, we take the thinking as real because we cannot understand it. "I exist" is understood only through the four seen-heard-sensed-cognized, each a conceiving in one of which we are always engaged like a fish in water, and it is in the very understanding of "walking" that "I" is born — but because this arising of the "I"-view through thinking is not seen as a view, it seems there is a separate me and a separate walking I understood, and the two are knotted, whereas rightly "I am born through the walking." These four stand in a never-knowable nature — which is exactly why they are called view, things the mind cannot know — and recognition itself has no object attached: a dog is recognized in a dream with no dog and no eye, so recognition is only recognition, and we are deceived by attention, which cannot even be recognized as attention, into taking a never-met thing as "there is."

There is no escaping consciousness's "it can be known," the teacher presses, and we do not even know a nature free of it exists, present all the while — as a bell is silent until struck and a phone's being on is noticed only when a call comes, we never register the ever-on state, and by not knowing this nature we come to think about it and take "it can be known" as understood. Hence the great value we give to knowing, and the "mindfulness" we keep, is grasping the very thief — consciousness's fabricated "there is something," which the Buddha likened to a magician's show at a crossroads — so that "now I am keeping good mindfulness" is only thinking about a never-met thing, the day's practice being unwise attention. For it to become wise attention one must see that "I" is being built through the very walking and that consciousness is confirming "I exist" by the pretence of knowing an un-knowable. This is a seeing, not a doing: seeing that we act by unwise attention caught in ignorance, from which the cessation of ignorance follows — which is why the path rests wholly on the good friend, since only one who has gone beyond words can point it out, not in writing but through examples using the very breath and nose we foolishly know, for this cessation cannot be viewed alone but only within an arising. The abandoning of arising is itself the realization of cessation: seeing arising, its arising appears as a non-arising, and that appearing-as-non-arising is what realizing cessation means — not a separate cessation apart, just as seeing the dream as false is its cessation.
