The standard paṭiccasamuppāda formula "cakkhuñca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu viññāṇaṃ" is quoted. The term "rūpakāye adhivacana samphassa" (designation-contact regarding the form-body) is used. The phrase "ahutva sambhūtaṃ hutvā na bhavissati" is referenced in the Q&A section. "Avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā" is invoked as the root mechanism.

## Main dhamma points

- A "chair" is analysed to show how rūpa is constructed: vedanā, saññā, saṅkhāra, and viññāṇa together produce the designation "chair." This is **rūpakāye adhivacana samphassa** — a definition-contact, not the discovery of an independently existing thing.
- **Saññā** (recognition/identification) is something that can never actually be performed. A child who has never learned "green" cannot identify it no matter how long they look. The shape "square" cannot be extracted from the word "square" alone. Recognition requires āsava (habitual associations) — without them, the word delivers nothing.
- Saññā is therefore a **marīci** (mirage): not that one recognises something and later finds it isn't there, but that the act of recognising itself is the mirage — something that was never there being "identified."
- **Language** (bhāṣā) is the vikṛti (distortion) of saññā. Language names a place where recognition cannot occur and makes it appear as though something has been done. Seeing through the vigraha (analysis/definition) is impossible precisely because the vigraha is where the delusion originates.
- The common approach of taking a thing known through viññāṇa, analysing it into rūpa-vedanā-saññā-saṅkhāra components, and concluding "it doesn't really exist" is compared to breaking cookies without affecting the cookie-cutter — the mould (moha/delusion at the root) remains untouched.
- In meditation (ānāpānasati example): when breath and nose both become non-manifest (aprakṛta), one cannot determine whether one is in a state of feeling or non-feeling. The danger is falling into **ucchēda** (annihilationism) — taking the non-manifestation as "non-existence" and settling into that. This leads to asaññā realms (realms without perception) or brahma-loka, not liberation.
- The TV screen analogy: the screen is always "on" — images appearing or disappearing on it do not change the screen itself. The screen corresponds to our yathā svabhāva (actual nature). Not knowing the screen, we watch figures on it and construct stories of arising and ceasing — but the screen has no arising-ceasing story.
- **Anuppāda nirodha** — cessation means non-arising, not "something arose and then ceased." The phrase "ahutva sambhūtaṃ" (having not been, it came to be) cannot mean "prior non-existence arose" because that would contradict anuppāda. If even a single moment of arising is admitted, the Buddha would not call it anuppāda nirodha.
- **Yoniso manasikāra** is not a technique one performs. It is the seeing of ayoniso manasikāra as ayoniso. As long as one does not know the not-knowing (nodanna kam), any "yoniso manasikāra" one performs is itself ayoniso — because it takes something produced by avijjā and works on it.
- An asāmānya sihiya (extraordinary mindfulness) must arise that sees the range where one does not think — the paṭisandhi (background) from which manasikāra itself springs. Only when one sees the not-knowing as not-knowing does yoniso manasikāra naturally establish itself without effort.

## Summary

This is a special group discussion (විශේෂ සාකච්ඡාව) in which Mr. Lasantha gives an extended exposition on how recognition (saññā) and analysis (vigraha) function, and why the common analytical approach to insight cannot reach the root of delusion.

The talk begins with a familiar exercise: analysing a "chair" into its nāmadhamma components — colour, shape, hardness (paṭhavī quality). These components, examined closely, reveal that no "chair" was ever found; what occurred was a nāmika rūpaya (nominally constructed form), produced through adhivacana samphassa — a designation-contact whereby nāmadhamma defines and thereby appears to discover a rūpa. The teacher then shows that this same analysis, when taken to its conclusion ("so the chair doesn't really exist"), achieves nothing — it arrives at "the thing we thought existed doesn't exist," which is merely the natthī extreme. The cookie-cutter analogy is used: one can break as many cookies as one likes, but the mould (avijjā, the not-knowing) is never touched.

The teacher devotes extended attention to saññā itself. A child who has never been taught "green" cannot identify green no matter how long they look; pointing must accompany the word for the association to form. The word itself contains no colour, no shape, no thing. This means recognition is strictly impossible — it has never actually been performed — and yet it appears to happen. This is why saññā is called a marīci (mirage): the mirage is not that one sees water and later finds it absent, but that the very act of "seeing water" — the recognition-event itself — was never a real event. Language, the teacher says, is the vikṛti (distortion) of saññā: it names a point where nothing can be done and makes it look as though something was accomplished.

The meditation illustration uses ānāpānasati. When one watches breath, eventually both breath and nose become non-manifest. At that point one cannot establish whether one is in feeling or non-feeling; neither statement applies, nor does "both" or "neither." The teacher warns that this is the exact juncture where ucchēda (annihilationism) captures the meditator — one unknowingly takes the non-manifest state as genuine cessation. In reality, one is still in dænīma (feeling/awareness) — it is awareness itself that makes "non-awareness" appear intelligible. Not recognising that one is in awareness, one settles into the "nothing is happening" conclusion and is led toward asaññā (perception-less) bhava. The teacher insists that nirodha is anuppāda — non-arising — not the cessation of something that arose. If "prior non-existence" truly arose even for a moment, the Buddha would not have described it as anuppāda nirodha.

The TV screen analogy ties the themes together: the screen is always on; images appearing and disappearing do not affect it; the screen has no arising-ceasing story. Our yathā svabhāvaya (actual nature) is like that screen. Not knowing the screen, we watch images and construct narratives of arising and ceasing — and that narrative-construction is itself the delusion, not the images.

The talk concludes on yoniso manasikāra. Any deliberate "wise attention" performed without seeing the not-knowing is itself ayoniso — because it takes a product of avijjā and works on it. True yoniso manasikāra is the arising of an asāmānya sihiya (extraordinary mindfulness) that sees the paṭisandhi — the background of not-knowing — from which all manasikāra springs. When one sees that background, everything that arises from it is seen as siddhi (event) rather than truth, and there is nothing to break, accept, or reject. The teacher likens this to seeing a dream as a dream while still dreaming: the events still occur, but without rævæṭīma (being deceived).
