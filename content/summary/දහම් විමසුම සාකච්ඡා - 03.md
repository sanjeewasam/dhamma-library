## Sutta referred to

- No single sutta is named by title. The Pāli verse "sabbē saṅkhārā aniccā'ti yadā paññāya passati" (when one sees with paññā that all saṅkhāra are anicca) is quoted. The paṭiccasamuppāda formula "avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā" is invoked as the root mechanism. The phrase "manō pubbangamā dhammā" (mind is the forerunner of all dhammas) is cited. The term "saññā pariññā" (full comprehension of saññā) is used, with the statement that saññā pariññā alone is the crossing-over (ōgha — flood). The phrase "nattē kāmāyānī citrāni lōkē" is mentioned briefly near the end.

## Main dhamma points

- **Saṃsāra and nibbāna are not separable.** Nopævætma (non-continuity/cessation) cannot be found separately from pævætma (continuity/existence). Nirodha means seeing within samudaya itself that nothing was ever constructed — not finding a separate "cessation" somewhere else.
- **Anicca, dukkha, anattā are labels that exist only because of mulāva (delusion).** Without nittya saññā (the perception of permanence), there is nothing to label "anicca." The ti-lakkhaṇa are not properties discovered in things; they are the names given to what becomes visible when the root delusion is seen.
- **The lamp analogy for paccaya (conditions).** A lit lamp brought near an unlit one does not transfer its flame. The jvalanaya (combustion/ignition) arises right there, at that point, when conditions converge. One cannot say it came "from that lamp" (sassata extreme) nor that it arose "without that lamp" (ucchēda extreme). Both statements are wrong. This is the meaning of paccaya — neither "cause" nor "not-cause."
- **Majjhē majjhē mantā na lippati — the middle is not a findable position.** The "middle" is not a third location between two extremes. It is seeing without either extreme operating. When both extremes are seen as wrong, what remains is not a "middle" but avabōdhaya (understanding) — which has no location.
- **Ocean/rivers analogy.** Rivers' names cannot be found in the ocean; the ocean cannot show its hetu (causes/origins) from within itself. This illustrates that hetu-phala (cause-result) never truly applies — it is a framework imposed by viññāṇa, not a discoverable reality.
- **Plate/white colour example.** A child with no prior knowledge of "white" or "plate" cannot identify either, no matter how long they look. Things do not appear because they exist independently — they appear because of prior knowing (danna kam/āsava). What we take as recognition of existing things is actually the imposition of prior associations.
- **Saṅkhāra dukkha cannot be understood by viññāṇa.** Only paññā can see it: "sabbē saṅkhārā aniccā'ti yadā paññāya passati." Viññāṇa's function is to make things intelligible; it therefore cannot see that making-intelligible is itself the problem.
- **Colour perception as proof that saññā is a marīci (mirage).** Extended demonstration: (1) Seeing alone never delivers a colour — a child shown blue and red cannot name them from looking. (2) The name alone never delivers a colour — knowing the word "blue" does not let one pick it out. (3) Neither rūpa (the seen form) nor nāma (the learned name) ever produces the colour. Colour has never been "found" (hambu vecca) through either route. Recognition is therefore strictly impossible — an act that was never performed. This is saññā as marīci.
- **The act of recognising vs. the thing recognised.** We always seize the recognised thing and analyse it ("this is delusion," "this is impermanent"), but the act of recognising — the kriyāva (action) itself — is never caught. Even concluding "delusion is understood" is viññāṇa producing another māyā. Viññāṇa can never see that its own functioning (tēreema/understanding) is the delusion, because viññāṇa is that very functioning.
- **Saññā pariññā** (full comprehension of saññā) alone is the crossing of the flood (ōgha). If saññā is fully seen — that recognition itself was never performed — that alone is enough.
- **Sleep/wakefulness (nidra/avadiya) analogy — the major pedagogical exercise.** During sleep, there is no idea "I am asleep" and no idea "I am not asleep." Sleep has never been experienced — neither as present nor as absent. It is completely beyond viññāṇa's range. Yet we say "I am awake" by defining wakefulness as the absence of sleep. But since sleep was never encountered, "absence of sleep" names the absence of something never known — a double impossibility. Wakefulness, built on this foundation, is equally unfound. Neither sleep nor wakefulness can be established. Therefore one cannot even say one is in a "moment" (mohota). Even paccuppanna (present moment) cannot be applied — it would require one of the two (sleep or wakefulness) to be established, and neither is.
- **What remains is nodanna bava (a state of not-knowing).** One does not know sleep, does not know wakefulness, and does not even know that one does not know. This very not-knowing-of-not-knowing is why thinking (hitīma) arises. This is avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā: from not-knowing, all formations arise. The entire paṭiccasamuppāda process stands on this foundation of not-knowing.
- **More knowledge = deeper delusion.** The more interpretations one accumulates about this process, the deeper the bottom gets buried. Less interpretation brings one closer to seeing. Maximum knowledge about the delusion is maximum delusion.
- **When vidyā (true knowing) arises, both sides dissolve.** The puggala (person) dissolves because puggala-bhāva was built on not-knowing. But vidyā as a "thing found" also does not remain — because the person who would claim to have found it has dissolved. No seer remains, no thing-seen remains. Like erasing letters on a board: when letters are done, the erasing is done, and the eraser too is done.
- **The tree analogy.** A tree breathes, takes nutrients, exchanges gases — but has no concept of any of these activities. No idea of "I am a tree" or "I am doing this." Yet the tree continues. Similarly, when the kriyāvaliya (process) is seen with paññā, no "process" is found. One becomes like the tree — the process continues but without a doer, without even the concept of a process.

## Summary

This is the third discussion in the "දහම් විමසුම සාකච්ඡා" series, and it is a long, layered conversation that progressively dismantles every conceptual foothold the listener might use to stabilise understanding. The teacher moves through a series of analogies and exercises, each one removing a layer of assumed ground.

The discussion opens with the relationship between saṃsāra and nibbāna. The teacher establishes that cessation (nirodha) cannot be found separately from arising (samudaya) — one must see within samudaya itself that nothing was ever constructed. From here, the standard ti-lakkhaṇa (anicca, dukkha, anattā) are reframed: they are not properties of things but labels that exist only because mulāva (root delusion) has first created the perception of permanence (nittya saññā). Without that false perception, there is nothing to call "impermanent."

The lamp analogy is then introduced to examine paccaya (conditionality). A lit lamp brought near an unlit one does not transfer its flame — the ignition arises right there when conditions converge. One cannot say the flame came from the first lamp (that would be sassata, eternalism) nor that it arose independently (that would be ucchēda, annihilationism). Both extremes are wrong, and the "middle" (majjhē) is not a third position between them but the seeing that neither extreme applies. This is avabōdhaya — understanding — and it has no findable location.

The ocean and rivers analogy extends this: rivers lose their names in the ocean, and the ocean cannot show its river-origins from within itself. The hetu-phala (cause-result) framework, so natural to viññāṇa, cannot be found in reality. It is imposed, not discovered.

The teacher then turns to direct perception with the plate/colour example. A child with no prior learning cannot identify "white" or "plate" regardless of how long they look — proving that recognition depends entirely on prior knowing (āsava), not on things existing independently. This leads into an extended examination of colour perception as proof that saññā is a marīci (mirage). Neither seeing (rūpa) nor naming (nāma) ever delivers a colour. Colour has never been "found" through either route. Recognition — the very act of identifying — is therefore something that was never actually performed. The teacher stresses that we habitually seize the recognised thing and analyse it ("this is a delusion"), but never catch the act of recognising itself. Even understanding that "this is delusion" is viññāṇa generating another māyā. Viññāṇa, whose entire function is to make things intelligible, can never see that making-things-intelligible is the problem. Only paññā — seeing that transcends viññāṇa — can comprehend this.

The culminating pedagogical exercise uses sleep and wakefulness. The teacher methodically shows that during sleep, no idea of "being asleep" or "not being asleep" exists. Sleep has never been experienced in any mode — neither as present nor absent. It is fully beyond viññāṇa's reach. Yet we define wakefulness as "sleep is absent" — but since sleep was never encountered, this "absence" names the absence of something never known. Wakefulness, built on this impossible foundation, is equally unfound. Neither can be established. This means one cannot even say one exists in a "moment" — even the concept of paccuppanna (present moment) requires at least one of the pair (sleep or wakefulness) to be established, and neither is. What remains is nodanna bava — a state of not-knowing. One does not know sleep, does not know wakefulness, and does not know that one does not know. This not-knowing-of-not-knowing is precisely why thinking arises — it is avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā laid bare. The entire paṭiccasamuppāda process rests on this foundation.

The teacher warns that more knowledge about this process does not help — it deepens the burial of the ground. As interpretations multiply, the bottom gets further away. Less interpretation, less conceptual elaboration, brings one closer. Maximum dænuvat bhāvaya (awareness/knowledge about the delusion) is maximum mōḍakama (foolishness).

The discussion concludes with two powerful images. First, when vidyā (true knowing) arises, both sides dissolve simultaneously: the person (puggala-bhāva) dissolves because it was built on not-knowing, and vidyā as a "thing attained" also cannot remain because the one who would claim it has dissolved. No seer remains, no seen remains — like erasing letters on a board where, when the letters are finished, the erasing is finished, and the eraser too is finished. Second, the tree analogy: a tree breathes, takes in nutrients, exchanges gases, but has no concept of doing any of it — no idea of "I am a tree" or "I am performing this process." Yet the tree continues. The teacher says this is what one becomes: the process continues, but without a doer, without even the concept of a process — just the natural svabhāva (own-nature), like the tree.
