## Sutta referred to

- **Oghataraṇa Sutta** (SN 1.1, Crossing the Flood) is referenced: "neither struggling (dæṅgeluvēt nǣ) nor standing still (nikan hiṭiyēt nǣ)" — the one who crossed the flood neither exerted nor remained passive.
- The verse "sabbē saṅkhārā aniccā'ti yadā paññāya passati" is invoked when discussing how saṅkhāra (the act of understanding/recognising) cannot be seen by viññāṇa.
- The raft analogy (kullūpama) from **MN 22** (Alagaddūpama Sutta) is used extensively: dhamma is a raft, needed only for the crossing; when the flood (ogha) is crossed, neither raft nor crosser remains.

## Main dhamma points

- **"Turtle's feathers" (ibbagē pihāṭu) analogy.** A turtle has no feathers — this is a fact the world accepts as true. But saying "turtle's feathers don't exist" already takes a non-existent thing (pihāṭu) and makes a statement about it. The statement "it hasn't arisen" (haṭagen nǣ) applies only where arising is a meaningful concept; where the thing itself cannot be spoken of, "hasn't arisen" is equally inapplicable. This extends to all dhamma formulations: "not arisen," "arisen and ceased," "nothing remains" — each is a statement that takes something already constructed by moha and comments on it. They are all true at the worldly level but are themselves part of delusion.
- **Understanding (tēreema) is not avabōdhaya (realisation).** Understanding is a piece of delusion itself. If one "understands" that something hasn't arisen, one has merely acquired another conceptual position. The seeing that understanding itself is wrong (varada) is what must occur — not better understanding.
- **Dhamma as prativisa (counter-poison).** If delusion is poison, dhamma is a more potent poison designed to neutralise it. It draws from the same saṅkhāra-vocabulary as the delusion it describes. Every dhamma formulation — "not arisen," "ceases without remainder," "beyond words" — is part of the same raft. Only when the counter-poison is also seen as poison does liberation become possible.
- **The feather analogy extended.** A feather has quills and a stem, but neither quills nor stem can be called "feather." Yet without them, no "feather" can be found. Both positions fail: saying quills-and-stem IS the feather is wrong (sassata); saying feather exists apart from them is also wrong (ucchēda). The feather cannot describe itself from within itself — artha śūnya (devoid of meaning from within). This parallels how any saṅkhāra, examined from within, reveals no self-referential meaning.
- **Room (kāmarē) analogy.** A room is defined by what it is not: not the kitchen, not the hall, not the dining area. Walls are placed to separate it from those others. But those "others" are never found within the room's meaning — they are hamba novena (never-encountered) elements whose absence constitutes the room's identity. An absence of something never found is taken as "not-being," and that not-being is then negated to produce "being" — this is the mechanism by which asti (existence) arises from ucchēda (the annihilation extreme). The room is a name given to a double negation of things never encountered.
- **Light/darkness (ālōkaya/andura) analogy.** Darkness has never been found in the present moment. "Light" means "darkness is absent." But darkness — never encountered — is what is being negated. So light is a name given to the absence of something never known. Even saying "darkness has never been found" is itself a statement about something that cannot be spoken of, generating yet another concept. The teacher shows that every attempt to describe or negate produces another formation — the description of delusion is itself delusion.
- **Womb/outside (kusakin piṭaṭa) analogy.** A baby in the womb never experiences "being in a womb" — the womb is only known after leaving it, by which time the experience is already past. "Outside" is defined as "not-womb," but the womb itself was never experienced. So "outside" is a name for the absence of something never known — identical in structure to the sleep/wakefulness and light/darkness analyses.
- **The blind person (andhayā) analogy.** A person blind from birth has no concept of seeing or not-seeing. Their mental state regarding vision is ēkākārī (uniform, undifferentiated) — no pulsation between "I see" and "I don't see." This state is analogous to avabōdhaya: when both seeing and not-seeing are seen as never-found, one's relationship to perception becomes like the blind person's — without kampanaya (oscillation/disturbance). The teacher states that the blind person's mental constitution regarding sight equals our yathā svabhāvaya (actual nature).
- **Kalyāṇa mitta (spiritual friend): gained and lost at the same point.** The kalyāṇa mitta is needed because of moha; the very words that create tēreema (understanding) also contain the capacity to transcend tēreema. When one sees through the understanding to the impossibility of understanding, one becomes parapratya rahita (independent of external conditions) — at which point the kalyāṇa mitta is no longer needed. But this independence arose through dependence (the kalyāṇa mitta's words). Neither "because of him" nor "without him" applies — the avabōdhaya belongs to neither extreme.
- **Medicine analogy.** The relief from dhamma-understanding is like medicine dissolving in the body: the patient does not need to be told moment by moment that pain is decreasing — the relief happens nirāyāsayeṅ (effortlessly, of itself). Similarly, when the dhamma "enters," the dissolution of suffering is self-evident and cannot be forced or confirmed by another.

## Summary

This is the fourth discussion in the "දහම් විමසුම සාකච්ඡා" series. It opens with a participant's question about what "has never arisen" (anuppāda) truly means, and the teacher uses this as the springboard for an extended examination of how every dhamma formulation — every statement about reality — is itself part of the delusion it describes.

The discussion begins with the turtle's feathers: a thing that has never existed cannot meaningfully be described as "not existing." The words "has not arisen" require the concept of arising, and the concept of arising requires a thing that could arise. Where the thing itself was never there, even the negation is baseless. The teacher extends this to all standard dhamma formulations — "arisen and ceased," "nothing remains," "beyond words" — showing that each is a statement taken from within the moha-structure, commenting on moha from moha's own resources. They are true as worldly (lōkika) facts, like "turtles have no feathers," but they are not avabōdhaya. The teacher introduces the term prativisa (counter-poison): dhamma is a more powerful poison given to neutralise the original poison of moha. But it is still poison, and it must be recognised as such.

The feather analogy deepens the analysis. A feather's components (quills, stem) are not the feather, yet without them no feather can be found. Both identification (this IS the feather) and separation (the feather exists apart) fail. From within itself, the feather is artha śūnya — meaning-empty. This parallels any saṅkhāra: examined from within, it cannot describe itself, yet it cannot be described without its conditions, and those conditions are not it. The teacher marks the point: when both extremes (asti and nāsti) are seen to fail, what remains is not a "middle" — a middle would create two more sides. Rather, the complete non-applicability of any vigraha (analysis/definition) is what dawns.

The room analogy makes this concrete. A "room" acquires its identity from what it is not — not the kitchen, not the hall. But those other spaces are never found within the room's present meaning. The room is built on the absence of things never encountered. Darkness/light follows the same structure: darkness, never experienced in this moment, is negated to produce "light." The womb/outside analogy completes the triad: the womb is never experienced while inside it; "outside" is the name given to not-womb, but since womb was never known, outside names the absence of the unknowable.

The blind person analogy introduces the soteriological dimension. A person blind from birth has no concept of either seeing or not-seeing — their mental state regarding vision is ēkākārī (uniform). This, the teacher says, is the analogue of avabōdhaya. When one sees that both seeing and not-seeing are never-found, one's relationship to the entire perceptual apparatus becomes like the blind person's relationship to sight — akampita (without oscillation). Not a "middle" between seeing and not-seeing, but the complete non-applicability of both.

The discussion concludes with the kalyāṇa mitta paradox and the medicine analogy. The spiritual friend is needed because moha exists; the friend's words contain both the understanding (tēreema) and the transcendence of understanding (the seeing that tēreema is varada — wrong). At the exact point where one sees through, the friend is both fully present (it was through his words) and fully absent (one is now parapratya rahita — independent). Neither "because of him" nor "despite him" applies. The teacher compares this to medicine: once it enters the body, the relief is nirāyāsa (effortless), and the patient needs no external confirmation. Knowledge that accumulates without this seeing only makes the raft heavier — the more one "knows" about dhamma, the further one is from avabōdhaya, because every additional piece of knowledge is another plank on a raft one cannot put down.
