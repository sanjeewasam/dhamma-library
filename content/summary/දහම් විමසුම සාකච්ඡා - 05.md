## Sutta referred to

- The **horse-hair simile** is invoked: splitting a horse hair into seven (or a hundred) and piercing one piece with another piece of the same hair — attributed to the Buddha's response to Ānanda's remark about the Licchavi princes' archery skill. Used to illustrate the extreme subtlety of svabhāva seeing itself from within itself.
- The **avyākata (undeclared) questions** about the arahant after death are cited: "exists" is wrong, "does not exist" is wrong, "both exists and does not exist" is wrong, "neither exists nor does not exist" is wrong — all four are diṭṭhi.
- The Pāli phrase **"na hidaṃ attakataṃ bimbaṃ na parakatañ aghaṃ"** (this form is neither self-made nor other-made) is quoted.
- **Majjhimā paṭipadā** is discussed: the middle way is not a findable position between two extremes but the seeing that both extremes are unfindable.
- **Idameva saccaṃ mogham aññaṃ** ("only this is true, everything else is wrong") is cited as itself a diṭṭhi.

## Main dhamma points

- **Pratyakṣa (direct realisation) is not understanding (tēreema).** Jānatō (knowing) always leaves a knower behind. Passatō (seeing) is not the same as knowing, yet it does not exist apart from knowing — like flame that is neither the matchbox nor the match, yet does not exist without them. When one says "this is avabōdhaya," both the understanding and the understood remain, perpetuating puggala-bhāva. Pratyakṣa is where no knower survives.
- **Earth's rotation analogy — the central pedagogical exercise.** Standing on Earth, one has never experienced rotation — not even a fraction of a degree. So "rotation" carries no experiential meaning. Leaving Earth's orbit does not help: there too one cannot determine whether one is in rotation or non-rotation. Neither inside nor outside delivers the experience. No middle position exists either — with both extremes unfindable, a middle is meaningless. This parallels pratyakṣa: it cannot be thought, understood, or even labelled as paññā. One cannot arrive at it by effort (āyāsa) or by non-effort. Every vigraha (analysis) attempted from within or without is a diṭṭhi.
- **Understanding (tēreema) deepens delusion.** Every concept understood is another layer burying the ground. Trying to understand is like thrashing legs in mud. When tēreema stops, one approaches avabōdhaya.
- **The horse-hair simile for subtlety.** Svabhāva must meet svabhāva — the seeing must be of the same texture as what is seen. There is no external instrument. One does not "understand" svabhāva; one becomes anugata (aligned) with it. The tree that breathes without knowing it breathes is the model.
- **Tree/body/breath analysis.** A tree exchanges CO₂ and O₂ but has no concept of doing so. Its "process" (kriyāvaliya) has no kriyā-concept. The tree is neither "a tree" nor "not a tree" to itself. Similarly, the body has never encountered breath: breathing continues day and night, yet the body has no awareness of it. We create a "knot" (gæṭaya) between process and entity — once tied, the two cannot be separated, and the knot itself becomes invisible.
- **Breath as cetanā, not vāyu.** When one thinks "breath," what arises is manāyatana — a mental moment, not wind. Within that cetanā, no oxygen, no wind, no physical air is found. Neither the body has encountered breath (it has never felt it) nor the mind (thinking produces only cetanā, not airflow). The śūnyatā of thinking itself becomes visible: thinking about breath does not deliver breath.
- **Samudaya and nirodha in the same act (udayavaya).** Finding (hamu vīma) and not-finding (hamu novīma) occur at the same point. When cetanā is seen as cetanā, one sees that "breath" was never found within it — and yet, only through thinking did the finding seem to occur. Both finding and not-finding are visible simultaneously. Neither "arose" (haṭagattā) nor "ceased" (niruddha) can be said — the words lose their applicability.
- **Oil lamp extended.** In the act of burning (dāvīma), neither oil nor wick is found. Without paccaya, no flame can be spoken of. No arising, no ceasing — the entire vocabulary of haṭagen niruddha vīma (arising-and-ceasing) is revealed as constructed narrative, not discovered reality.
- **Sensor door analogy — the major analogy.** An automatic sensor door: opens when a person approaches, closes when they leave. But the sensor is always ON — 24 hours. The person's presence or absence makes no difference to the sensor's state. Key derivations: (1) If the sensor has never been OFF, one cannot say it was ever "turned ON" — without OFF, ON has no meaning. (2) The meaning of ON resides not in ON itself but in OFF — and OFF has never been encountered. (3) This is the structure of sassata-diṭṭhi: we take perception (peṇīma) as "existing" precisely because we have never encountered its absence (ucchēda extreme). But since ucchēda has never been found, we don't know that we don't know it — and this not-knowing-of-not-knowing is avijjā. (4) From this double not-knowing, puggala-bhāva arises: "I see," "I don't see" — both positions construct a person. (5) When both ON and OFF are seen as unfound, puggala-bhāva cannot form. No one remains to have the realisation.
- **Hearing/deafness (bīra) analogy.** Hearing is always "on" — even when there is no sound, hearing persists (one notices the absence of sound through hearing). We never encounter "not-hearing." Yet we don't know we're always in hearing — we only notice hearing when sound arrives. The deaf person (bīra) knows neither hearing nor not-hearing; their mental constitution regarding sound is ēkākārī (uniform). The teacher says our actual state is identical to the deaf person's — both hearing and not-hearing are unknown to us. This parallels the blind-person analogy from the previous discussion.
- **Knowing (dænagænīma) is itself the muḷāva (delusion).** Not the known things — the act of knowing. Seeing (peṇīma) is itself moha. Hearing (æsīma) is itself moha. The delusion is not in what is perceived but in the perceiving-ness. Not understanding this is what drives kalpanā (conceptual elaboration) about perceived objects.
- **The path (mārgaya) has no time dimension.** Sotāpanna, sakadāgāmī, anāgāmī, arahant — all arise in the paccuppanna moment, in paññā. There is no temporal gap between stages. The four labels are landmarks given for worldly comprehension, not sequential attainments found by a person. No one walks the path; no path is walked upon; no destination is reached.

## Summary

This is the fifth discussion in the "දහම් විමසුම සාකච්ඡා" series, structured as a Q&A session opening with a participant's question about pratyakṣa (direct realisation). The teacher uses this question to build an extended demonstration — through the Earth's rotation, the sensor door, and the hearing/deafness analogies — that pratyakṣa is precisely what cannot be understood, and that the attempt to understand it is itself the obstacle.

The discussion begins by distinguishing jānatō (knowing) from passatō (seeing). Knowing always produces a knower — whatever is known, someone remains who knows it. Even knowing that "knowing is not avabōdhaya" leaves a knower behind. Seeing is not the same as knowing, yet it does not exist independently of knowing — like flame that is neither match nor matchbox but does not exist without them. This immediately establishes the impossibility of reaching pratyakṣa through accumulation of knowledge.

The Earth's rotation analogy occupies the first major section. Standing on Earth, one has never experienced rotation — the word "rotation" carries no experiential content. Leaving Earth's orbit does not resolve this: in space, one equally cannot determine whether one is in a state of rotation or non-rotation. Neither inside nor outside delivers the experience. No middle position is available — since both extremes are unfindable, a middle between them is meaningless. The teacher extends this to the four avyākata positions about the arahant after death (exists, does not exist, both, neither) — showing all four are diṭṭhi. Every attempt at vigraha (analysis) from any position produces another diṭṭhi. One cannot arrive at pratyakṣa by understanding, by non-understanding, or by any combination. Pratyakṣa is where manasikāra (mental attention) cannot reach.

The horse-hair simile deepens this: svabhāva must meet svabhāva — the seeing must be of the same subtlety as what is seen. One does not understand it; one becomes anugata (aligned). The tree provides the model: it breathes without any concept of breathing, lives without any concept of living. Its process continues without a process-concept. This is contrasted with our situation: we tie a "knot" between process and entity (body and breathing, eye and seeing) so tightly that the knot becomes invisible, and we take the combination as a single self-evident fact.

The breath analysis makes this concrete. When one thinks "breath," what arises is manāyatana — a mental event, a cetanā. Within that cetanā, no actual wind, oxygen, or physical air is found. The body has never encountered breath (it has no awareness of it, just as the tree has none). Thinking about breath does not deliver breath. The śūnyatā of thinking itself becomes visible. From here, the teacher shows that samudaya (finding) and nirodha (not-finding) occur at the same point: within the very cetanā that seems to find breath, breath is not found. Both hamu vīma and hamu novīma are visible simultaneously. The entire vocabulary of arising-and-ceasing (haṭagen niruddha vīma) is revealed as constructed narrative.

The sensor door analogy is the discussion's centrepiece. An automatic door's sensor is always ON. A person approaching triggers the door to open; their departure triggers it to close. But the sensor's state never changes — it operates identically whether someone is present or not. The teacher draws out the implications systematically: if the sensor has never been OFF, "ON" has no meaning — meaning requires contrast, and the contrast-state has never occurred. This is the structure of sassata-diṭṭhi: we take perception as "existing" because we have never encountered its absence. But that absence (ucchēda) has never been found — and we don't know that we don't know it. This not-knowing-of-not-knowing is avijjā. From it, puggala-bhāva is constructed: the person who sees, the person who doesn't see, the person who knows ON and OFF. When both ON and OFF are seen as unfound, puggala-bhāva cannot form. The seer dissolves with the seen.

The hearing/deafness analogy completes the demonstration. Hearing is always "on" — silence is noticed through hearing, not through its absence. We never encounter not-hearing. Yet we don't know we're always in hearing. The deaf person knows neither hearing nor not-hearing — their mental state regarding sound is ēkākārī (uniform, undifferentiated). The teacher states our actual condition is identical: both hearing and not-hearing are equally unknown to us. We are, in effect, deaf — and not knowing our deafness is what creates the illusion that we sometimes hear and sometimes don't.

The discussion concludes with two important clarifications. First, knowing (dænagænīma) itself is the muḷāva — not what is known but the knowing-ness. Seeing is itself moha; hearing is itself moha. The delusion resides in the perceiving, not in the perceived. Second, the path (mārgaya) has no time dimension. Sotāpanna, sakadāgāmī, anāgāmī, and arahant all arise in the paccuppanna moment. There is no temporal gap between stages — the four labels are landmarks for worldly communication, not sequential attainments in time. No person walks the path; no path is found; no destination is reached.
