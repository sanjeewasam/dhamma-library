## Sutta referred to

- The formula **"cakkhuṃ paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu viññāṇaṃ"** (dependent on the eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises) from the Saḷāyatana Saṃyutta is quoted and then subjected to extended vimasīma. The teacher takes it apart: eye ≠ seeing, form ≠ seeing — two "not-it" things cannot produce "it."
- **"Tinnāṃ saṅgati phasso"** (the meeting of the three is contact) is cited as the lōkiya (worldly) description of how perception arises, and then examined from the noyedīma (non-applicability) angle.
- The **diṭṭha suta muta viññāta** (seen, heard, sensed, cognised) formula is used in the closing section, mapped onto the tree analogy: "I" is the name given to these four, just as "tree" is the name given to four activities.
- The **horse-hair simile** (splitting a horse hair and piercing one piece with another) is referenced again for the subtlety required to see manasikāra from within manasikāra.

## Main dhamma points

- **Yedīma (applying) is itself noyedīmak (non-applying).** The train compartment booking analogy is extended: "booking" applies and does not apply simultaneously. When both are seen together, neither yedīma nor noyedīma can be taken as a separate truth. No vigraha (analysis) survives the simultaneous seeing. Manasikāra cannot reach beyond this point — paññā arises precisely at the point of impossibility.
- **Hearing (æsīma) as the invariant.** Sound present or sound absent — in both states, hearing capacity (æsīmē hækiyāva) is unchanged. The invariant across both states is hearing itself. But concluding "hearing always exists" is sassata-diṭṭhi — a nigamanaya (conclusion) that one has arrived at and made ættha (true). Both asti and nāsti extremes must be transcended: hearing is neither "always there" nor "sometimes absent."
- **Hearing cannot be understood (tērum gan̆na bǣ).** Every arthakathana (interpretation) applied to hearing — "it is a feeling," "it arises from avijjā," "it arises and ceases," "it has never arisen" — leaves hearing tērilā (understood), which means an understander survives. The vimasīma must arrive at the point where no arthakathana remains — not even "hearing is beyond understanding."
- **Sand-mountain analogy mapped to hearing.** Sand = sound; mountain = the concept "hearing." Sand never contains mountain, yet mountain is only spoken of through sand. Samudaya (the sand) within itself contains nirodha (no-mountain). When nuvaṇa (wisdom) is applied, one sees within sand itself that mountain was never there — this is not "mountain is false" but "mountain-ness never applied." Neither yedīma (mountain found) nor noyedīma (mountain denied) survives.
- **Nodannakam (not-knowing) is above both lābīma and nolābīma.** Finding (lābīma) is made ættha by not seeing not-finding (nolābīma). But nolābīma itself is constructed from lābīma — we can only say "not found" because we first constructed "found." Both arise from nodannakam. When this is seen, no mental passage (mānasika hāraya) to either arthakathana or nigamanaya is possible. Avijjā paccaya is visible: not-knowing creates the ættha/boru (true/false) duality.
- **Eye and form cannot produce seeing.** The Saḷāyatana formula is examined: eye ≠ seeing, form ≠ seeing. If neither is seeing, two "non-seeing" elements cannot combine to produce seeing. The teacher shows the key is phassa (contact) — the "leaning together" (hētthuva) — not the elements themselves. The matchbox/match analogy is extended: fire comes from friction (ilīma), not from matchbox or match. Contact is the cause, and contact is never found in either element.
- **Bamboo stick (baṭamiṭi) analogy — the central analogy for phassa.** Two bamboo sticks, each unable to stand alone, lean against each other and stand upright. The capacity to stand is in neither A nor B — it is in their hētthuva (leaning/support). But hētthuva belongs to neither stick. The "cause" (hētuva) is never found in either element — it is hamba novena (never-encountered). This parallels eye-and-form: what we take as "eye and form cause seeing" actually conceals phassa, which is invisible. When samudaya is rightly seen — when the cause is seen to be hamba novena — nirodha is sākṣāt (realised). The anuppāda svabhāva (nature of non-arising) is what becomes evident.
- **Sleep challenge to phassa.** A participant raises: during sleep, eye and form are present but seeing does not occur. The teacher uses this to show that eye-and-form are not the actual cause — phassa (contact/manasikāra) is. What we take as "the reason I see" (eye + form) is actually the two "ends" (koṇ) of a knot (gæṭaya) whose middle — manasikāra — we never encounter.
- **Manasikāra is the invisible hētuva.** Manasikāra ties kāya and poṭṭhabbha together (nose and breath, eye and form). But manasikāra itself is never viññāṇa-gōcara (accessible to consciousness) — because viññāṇa is the product of manasikāra. One cannot see the ground one stands on — the standing-on-feet analogy. Diṭṭhi arises from manasikāra, but manasikāra is never visible to the diṭṭhi it produces. This is why "I" appears separate from "what I perceive."
- **Tēreema (understanding) IS diṭṭhi.** The very act of understanding creates "me" on one side and "what is understood" on the other. When one understands "hearing always exists," the understander survives. Tēreema = tēreṇa kam = mam (understanding-ness = me). When tēreema is seen as itself being mam, then neither the understander nor the understood can form — the dvitvatā (duality) collapses.
- **Tree analogy — the culminating analogy.** A tree performs four activities: takes food through roots, draws water, absorbs CO₂, releases O₂. "Tree" is a name given to these four activities. None of the four = tree, yet tree cannot be found apart from them. The tree never encounters itself as "tree" — it never knows it eats, drinks, breathes in, or breathes out. These activities are not "tree's activities" — "tree" has no relationship to them. From the tree's side: tree has never found itself as tree, nor has it found itself as not-tree. Both positions are inapplicable. The teacher maps this to diṭṭha suta muta viññāta: "I" is the name given to seen, heard, sensed, cognised — exactly as "tree" is the name for four activities. When the four are seen as kriyā (activity) itself, with no entity attached, diṭṭhi has no room to form.
- **Kriyā seen as kriyā = no diṭṭhi.** When activity is seen from within activity — not as "my activity" or "activity happening to me" — then neither the doer (mam) nor the done (kriyāva) can be separated. The seer and the seen, the finder and the found, dissolve together. Hamba vīmam mam nam (if finding itself is me), then hamba veṇa dēyi hamba veṇa kenāyi (what is found and who finds) are not two. The dvitvatā (duality) between abhyantaraya (internal) and bāhira (external) ends.

## Summary

This is the sixth discussion in the "දහම් විමසුම සාකච්ඡා" series, structured as an extended Q&A. The teacher opens with a brief reference to sunātha dhāretha carātha (listen, retain, practise), then builds through a series of increasingly precise analogies — dream, sand-mountain, hearing, bamboo sticks, tree — toward the central thesis: diṭṭhi (view/self-view) arises from manasikāra (attention), and manasikāra is structurally invisible to the viññāṇa it produces.

The early section develops the dream analogy: water splashing in a dream, if taken as real, makes the dream real; if both are seen as dream, neither truth nor falsehood applies. This extends to dhamma itself — when dhamma-facts are taken as "true," they become a new diṭṭhi. The sand-mountain analogy then demonstrates non-arising (anuppāda): sand piled up is called "mountain," but mountain cannot be found in sand (sassata) nor apart from sand (ucchēda). The teacher insists that understanding "mountain hasn't arisen" is itself another tēreema — not avabōdhaya. When the vigraha (analysis) becomes "true," what looked like "delusion overcome" is actually Māra in disguise.

The train compartment booking analogy makes kriyā (action) itself inapplicable: when all seats are booked, the action "to book" loses meaning, and even "not booking" loses meaning. Where both doing and not-doing are inapplicable, manasikāra cannot reach — paññā arises at that point of impossibility. This is extended through the hearing analysis: hearing capacity is invariant whether sound is present or absent. But concluding "hearing always exists" is sassata-diṭṭhi. Hearing must be vimasīma'd (inquired into) until no arthakathana survives — not even "hearing cannot be understood."

The sand-mountain analogy is then mapped onto hearing: sand (sound) within itself contains the non-arising of mountain (hearing-concept). The teacher shows samudaya tulum̆a nirōdhayak — nirodha within samudaya itself, not samudaya discarded and nirodha found separately. Nodannakam (not-knowing) sits above both lābīma (finding) and nolābīma (not-finding): both arise from it, and seeing this prevents any nigamanaya.

A participant's question about the Saḷāyatana formula — "cakkhuṃ paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu viññāṇaṃ" — becomes the pivot for the second half. The teacher dismantles it: eye is not seeing, form is not seeing; two non-seeing things cannot produce seeing. The key is phassa (contact) — not the elements but their meeting. The bamboo stick analogy makes this concrete: two sticks that cannot stand alone lean together and appear to stand. The capacity to stand belongs to neither stick — it is in the hētthuva (support/leaning), which is never found in either. This is the structure of phassa: invisible, never viññāṇa-gōcara, yet the actual cause of everything perceived. The sleep objection is used to confirm: during sleep, eye and form are present but seeing absent — proving eye-and-form are not the cause; manasikāra (which is absent in sleep) is.

The teacher then establishes the critical insight: manasikāra is the gæṭaya (knot) between kāya and poṭṭhabbha (body and tangible). We see only the two ends (koṇ) — nose and breath, eye and form — never the knot. Because viññāṇa is itself produced by manasikāra, viññāṇa can never see manasikāra — like one can never see the ground one stands on. From this invisibility, diṭṭhi arises: "I" appears on one side, "what I perceive" on the other.

The tree analogy brings everything together. A tree performs four activities — eating, drinking, breathing in, releasing — but "tree" is merely a name imposed on these four. None of the four is "tree," yet tree cannot be found without them. The tree never encounters itself as tree. These are mapped to diṭṭha suta muta viññāta: "I" is the name given to seen, heard, sensed, and cognised, exactly as "tree" is the label for four activities. When the activities are seen as activities — kriyāva kriyāveṅma — with no entity attached, diṭṭhi cannot form. Neither "I who sees" nor "what is seen" can be separated out. The dvitvatā (duality) between finder and found collapses, and with it, the entire structure of abhyantaraya (internal) and bāhira (external).

The discussion closes with the teacher emphasising sustained vimasīma: taking a single upamā and inquiring into it until dhammatā (the nature of dhamma) surfaces on its own, rather than cycling through analogies superficially.
