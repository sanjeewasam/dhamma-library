## Main dhamma points
- A structured doctor–engineer dialogue defining right view against ignorance through the Four Truths, and arguing the title thesis — that awakening is "knowing there is nothing to be known" — with a prism/coloured-glass simile for how the whole world is a fabrication seen only through self-view.
- **Right view is the counterpart of ignorance.** Right view is the four knowledges — of suffering, its arising, its cessation, the path — while ignorance (*avijjā*) is precisely non-knowledge of those same four, so right view is the exact opposite of ignorance. The first fetter is *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, and its breaking is attaining right view; *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* is the 20-fold taking of each of the five aggregates as "I," "mine," "I in it," "it in me," and from that view suffering arises.
- **Only the arising-and-ceasing of a mind can be known; the title thesis.** Every mind that arises carries the seven universal factors, of which *phassa* (contact) is taken as the key: contact is a characteristic of an arisen-and-ceasing mind. What you can know is *only* that arising-and-ceasing; if you claim to know anything beyond it, you have *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* — "in short, awakening is to know that there is nothing to be known." Nothing can be posited in the world; when something seems to be met "outside," it is because, as the arisen mind ceases, we begin to conceive (*maññanā*) and proliferate (*papañca*), and the resulting fabricated nature — not the arisen-ceased mind — is *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*: a wholly deluded nature split into good and bad, clung to or resisted, from which craving and the defilements arise.
- **The prism simile: the world exists nowhere.** A white light (or the doctor's frangipani tree) shone through four coloured glass pieces — *vedanā, saññā, saṅkhāra, viññāṇa* — yields a mixed colour that is not in the glass, not outside it, not anywhere; it is *saṅkhata*, appearing only when looked at through the self-view, not even truly "arisen." Just so, mother, father, "me," this body, the whole world is met only through self-view's conceiving — a fabricated appearance existing nowhere, to be seen not with the eye but with wisdom.
- **Nothing to do or know; effortless mindfulness.** Since there is nothing to know and nothing to do, "effort" here does not mean doing something: as *paṭicca-samuppāda* has no doer put in front and is not a linear program in time, so the practice is not discussion-as-one-extreme nor sustained sitting-samatha-as-the-other, but attention held at the ever-present "cannot-be-fabricated" nature, effortlessly, so that defilements do not arise — like balancing a bicycle, hard at first, then effortless. One only keeps mindfulness on the arising-and-ceasing, with nothing to know; a "there is external" is posited only by *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, and when that breaks the lower problems are no longer problems, though conventional seeing continues unobstructed, as one who knows a mirage still sees it but does not go to bathe or fish in it.
- **Non-arising cessation; only a mind arises.** The *anutpāda-nirodha* spoken of is that beings, persons, substances and objects do not arise — only a mind arises, and in that mind there is nothing to recognize. This is why the Buddha told Aṅgulimāla "I have stopped, you stop" — stop at the place a mind arises-and-ceases, for it was only his hard self-view that posited the external "people to be killed." As a coin has "me" on one side and the whole external — mother, father, wife, objects — posited on the other, the arisen mind seen with right view reveals the delusion of the five clinging-aggregates.
- **Hearing the never-before-heard; both sides must be tuned.** The point is whether "the never-before-heard Dhamma" is actually heard: *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ* is not in the Dhammacakka itself but is what Koṇḍañña realized on hearing it — a resonance, seen not thought. As a radio needs both a transmitting station and a tuned, battery-powered receiver for sound to come, so the four factors — association with good persons, hearing the true Dhamma, wise attention, practice in accord with the Dhamma — set both the outer and inner side; unless one is "tuned," only something else is heard. There need be no special one-off "event," for full awakening comes only when all ten fetters are broken, the hard first entrance being *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, where against-the-stream thinking replaces with-the-stream thinking.
- **Even "I have realized it" can be consciousness's magic; the criterion is suffering.** A consciousness that can conjure the thirty-one planes, galaxies, a universe and stars can surely also deceive with "now you have understood, you have had a special experience" — so that "realization" too may be part of the illusion, everyone caught at the same knot of "the thing I was seeking, I have now got." The Buddha therefore gives a criterion: if suffering ever arises, you have not caught the right Dhamma and are still in self-view — for the six heterodox teachers, and even the Bodhisatta in the forest, had suffering-free states that, like a ball held underwater, popped back up; the requirement is a nature in which suffering can *never* arise, its causes — the wanted and unwanted things met externally — never being met. This is why the root, ignorance itself, must be struck, and why *anicca-saññā* — sensing only the arising-and-ceasing of a mind and going no further — becomes manifest, going further being possible only through self-view.

## Summary

This structured doctor–engineer dialogue defines right view as the exact counterpart of ignorance: right view is knowledge of suffering, its arising, its cessation and the path, while ignorance is non-knowledge of those same four. The first fetter is *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, the 20-fold taking of each aggregate as self, and its breaking is right view. The heart of the talk is that only the arising-and-ceasing of a mind can be known — every mind carrying the seven universal factors, with contact taken as key — and that to claim knowledge beyond this is self-view, so that "in short, awakening is to know there is nothing to be known." Nothing can be posited in the world; when something seems met "outside," it is because the arisen mind, as it ceases, is worked on by conceiving and proliferation, and the resulting fabricated nature is self-view, split into good and bad, clung to and resisted, whence craving and the defilements. The prism simile makes it vivid: white light (or the frangipani tree) through the four coloured glasses of feeling, perception, formations and consciousness yields a colour found nowhere — in the glass, outside, or anywhere — a *saṅkhata* appearance seen only through self-view, as mother, father, body and world are; a thing to be seen with wisdom, not the eye.

Since there is nothing to know and nothing to do, the teacher stresses, "effort" is not doing: dependent origination has no doer in front and is not a linear program in time, so practice is neither discussion nor sustained sitting but attention held effortlessly at the ever-present un-fabricated nature, so defilements do not arise — hard at first, like balancing a bicycle, then effortless. The *anutpāda-nirodha* is that beings, persons, substances and objects do not arise; only a mind arises, in which nothing is to be recognized — which is why the Buddha told Aṅgulimāla to stop where a mind arises-and-ceases, his hard self-view alone having posited the external "people to kill," as a coin bears "me" on one side and the whole external on the other. The question is whether the never-before-heard Dhamma is truly heard — *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ*, not in the Dhammacakka but realized by Koṇḍañña as a resonance, seen not thought — and, like a radio needing both a transmitting station and a tuned receiver, both outer and inner must be set by the four factors of stream-entry, or only something else is heard. There need be no special event, full awakening coming only when all ten fetters break, the hard entrance being self-view, where against-the-stream thought turns to with-the-stream. Finally, since a consciousness that can conjure the thirty-one planes and a universe can also deceive with "now you have realized it," even realization may be its magic; so the Buddha gives a criterion — if suffering ever arises, the right Dhamma has not been caught and self-view remains, for the heterodox teachers and even the Bodhisatta had only temporary suffering-free states that popped back up like a ball held underwater, whereas what is required is a nature in which suffering can never arise, its causes never met. Hence the root, ignorance, must be struck, and *anicca-saññā* — sensing only the arising-and-ceasing of a mind and going no further — becomes manifest, going further being possible only through self-view.
