## Main dhamma points
- A structured doctor–engineer dialogue continuing the "awakening is knowing there is nothing to be known" theme, on where ego is subtle, how to catch the fire of craving early, why the first insight cannot be un-seen, and the criterion that any arising of suffering shows self-view remains.
- **Where ego is subtle: equanimity born of delusion.** The person is built more prominently in some states (blatant ego, "ice-cream and its icing"), more subtly in others; a *moha-sagata-upekkhā* can arise where there is no lust or aversion yet still delusion and ignorance, an external is still posited, and one remains subject to the deception — so absence of like and dislike is not freedom.
- **Catch the fire early.** When mind arises with fierce lust or aversion, the chance of catching the delusion is small, for we are feeding fuel and the blaze grows; as a fire is extinguishable only in its first two or three seconds, the subtle nature is best seen when lust or aversion first arises, whereas once a conflagration ("I struck, I scolded"), it is hard, and one is caught in self-view.
- **The first insight cannot be un-seen.** Right view's dawning is called *gotrabhū-ñāṇa* — seen a first time, then a second, so it is a thing seen — and *nāma-rūpa-pariccheda-ñāṇa*, a crucial early station, is realized by holding attention on contact until the "name-dhamma" (the thinking-nature) and the "form-aggregate" split in two; once split, they cannot be "hi-fied" back together, for however one rejoins them one knows they are two, and to one for whom "name-dhammas arise dependent on a form-aggregate" is directly seen, this holds even while doing focused, critical work.
- **The criterion of suffering; the ship's-pool boat.** The Buddha's test is the Four-Truths knowledge: if, in any relevant situation, suffering arises — "this went wrong, I wasted all this time, I should have done it thus" — then one is not in the present and must accept that result. As one rowing a little boat in a swimming pool aboard a ship imagines the rowing moves the ship, and grieves when a "wrong" stroke fails to steer it, so "I" in the great system of nature is only a sub-system, a small wheel turning as the machine turns; realizing this, daily work still succeeds or fails, but the demand "because I did it, it must go my way" is exactly the difference between understanding and non-understanding. The purest place is the arising-and-ceasing of a mind; going a little below it does not defile, for no fuel is added, and the arahant, in full comprehension, is simply nourished by nature with nothing to think or do.
- **Effort only to keep mindfulness; against the stream.** With this seen, the clinging-resisting, defilement-suffering nature falls away, since what we always focus on is the Buddha's result — how suffering arises, ceases, and the path — so whenever anger or upset arises even after much meditation, the change-of-lineage or the two-split nature, once known, cannot be undone, being a right-view state in which with-the-stream thinking dwindles and against-the-stream inclination grows; the four bases — desire, mind, energy, investigation — are applied as *investigation* (*vīmaṁsā*), not thought (*vitakka-vicāra*), and energy is spent only to keep mindfulness on the arising-and-ceasing, not on doing or on one's thoughts. Tasted once like sea-salt, this nature is known in every mind thereafter, and to see it even for seconds is a glimpse of right view that keeps one from falling.
- **Wisdom, not consciousness; and the good friend.** "There is nothing to be known" is caught only by wisdom, never by consciousness, to which it is never an object; a good friend may not see it 100% but keeps openness and non-judgment — "shall we look at this too?" — and, as the texts say, senior monks may teach while junior monks realize, since the realization is not in the sound but resonates in the hearer, the other's voice being only a condition (*paratoghosa-paccaya*) alongside wise attention, no special "message" transmitted. The two classic errors in *paṭicca-samuppāda* — putting "me" in front, and reading it as a linear program in time — must be avoided.
- **Non-arising cessation; two names for one thing.** A thought arises and at once ceases, and there is nothing to know — that very "nothing to know" being what must be known, after which there is nothing to cling to or resist. What the suttas call the deluded nature is this conceiving-and-attention; the un-deluded side is the "non-arising nature," what does not arise being beings, persons, substances, objects — in short the world — because one thought, having arisen and ceased, is analyzed only by a next thought, again positing them by self-view, which the Buddha declared cannot be done, the very attempt being a cause of suffering. As with the prism's colour, what never arises appears to arise through the view; and "arising-and-ceasing" and "the non-arisen nature" are the same thing said two ways, forced by language, as one cannot speak of dark without light — *anutpāda-nirodha* meaning that to this vision nothing arises, arising-and-ceasing being posited on the path only so that the impermanence, suffering and not-self of a clinging-aggregate can be seen.

## Summary

This second part of the doctor–engineer dialogue continues the theme that awakening is knowing there is nothing to be known, beginning with where the ego is subtle: a *moha-sagata-upekkhā* can arise with no lust or aversion yet with delusion still present and an external still posited, so calm without like or dislike is not freedom. The fire of craving must be caught early — extinguishable only in its first seconds, hard once a blaze — for with fierce lust or aversion we feed fuel and are caught in self-view. Right view's dawning is *gotrabhū-ñāṇa*, a thing seen a first and second time, and the crucial early *nāma-rūpa-pariccheda-ñāṇa* is realized by holding attention on contact until the thinking-nature and the form-aggregate split in two, after which they cannot be rejoined, holding even during focused work. The criterion is the Four-Truths knowledge: if suffering ever arises — "this went wrong, I wasted my time" — one is not in the present; as a man rowing a boat in a ship's swimming pool imagines his rowing moves the ship and grieves at a "wrong" stroke, "I" is only a sub-system, a small wheel turning as the machine turns, so that daily work still succeeds or fails but the demand "because I did it, it must go my way" marks the difference between understanding and non-understanding. The purest place is the arising-and-ceasing of a mind, and the arahant is simply nourished by nature, with nothing to think or do.

With this seen, the teacher continues, the clinging and defilement fall away, since the whole aim is the Buddha's result — the arising, cessation and path of suffering — and whenever upset arises even after much meditation, the change-of-lineage nature, once known, cannot be undone, being a right-view state in which with-the-stream thinking dwindles; the four bases are applied as investigation, not thought, and energy only keeps mindfulness on the arising-and-ceasing, not on doing. Tasted once like sea-salt, this nature is known in every mind, and even a few seconds' seeing is a glimpse of right view that keeps one from woeful rebirth — for "nothing to be known" is caught by wisdom, never by consciousness. A good friend, who may not see it fully, keeps openness and non-judgment, and as senior monks teach while junior monks realize, the realization is not in the sound but resonates in the hearer, the other's voice being only a condition beside wise attention — the two errors in dependent origination, putting "me" in front and reading it as linear in time, being avoided. Finally, a thought arises and at once ceases with nothing to know, that very "nothing to know" being what must be known; what does not arise is beings, persons, substances, objects — the whole world — since one ceased thought is analyzed only by a next, again positing them by self-view, which cannot be done and is itself a cause of suffering. As with the prism's colour, the never-arisen appears to arise through the view, and "arising-and-ceasing" and "the non-arisen nature" are one thing said two ways, forced by language as one cannot speak of dark without light — *anutpāda-nirodha* meaning that to this vision nothing arises, arising-and-ceasing being posited on the path only to reveal the impermanence, suffering and not-self of a clinging-aggregate.
