## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue completing the chair-and-shadow analysis, showing that consciousness always falls into one of two views, that "I" is born through the very feeling as a shadow through a chair, and that seen exactly at the feeling both experiencer and experience end.
- **Consciousness only ever goes to one of two views.** Thinking with consciousness, one can only take it in two ways — either "the chair *is* the shadow" (identical) or "the shadow exists apart from the chair" (separate, causeless) — and it is precisely in this going-to-one-of-two that the view is built and "I" arises: taking the chair as "me," or the shadow as the chair, or the shadow as existing without the chair. Both are wrong, yet these two are all consciousness has.
- **The mechanism is never seen, so we think.** We do not notice that we are thinking in these two ways; the mind seizes the thought instantly, and the very "thinking in two ways" is not caught — and because the flaw is not seen, one keeps thinking, the whole mechanism staying invisible. That is exactly why we live in a place where "chair" and "shadow" seem separate.
- **"I" is born through feeling as a shadow through a chair.** As a shadow arises only because a chair is there — the world's own way of speaking — so it is through "feeling" (taken, for want of another word, as "chair") that "I exist" is understood: through the very feeling the "I" is born, as the shadow is born from the chair. Then who experiences? If "I" is a re-creation of the feeling itself, there is no separate experiencer standing apart; even to say "feeling is only feeling" — who says it? No one is left at the place rightly seen, that too being only a thing a consciousness "understands."
- **Even the Bāhiya point carries a subtle conceit.** "Feeling is only feeling" and "train in feeling by feeling" — as in the Bāhiya Dāruciriya instruction — still leave a slight someone who "trains" or "discerns feeling as feeling," a residual conceit (*asmi-māna*); but seen exactly at the feeling itself, the "experiencer" and the "experience" both end, for if "I" is born through the very feeling, no separate one was there to feel a separately-standing feeling.
- **The origin can never be caught while split in two.** The real "arising," the *yoni* (origin), is never caught, because whenever it arises we understand it already split in two — "I stood and it was felt by me" — so the genuine event of arising is never met, and that is its very defect. Seeing the *yoni*, no "understander" is created there; it is only by not seeing the origin, the single place that splits in two, that a separate "someone" is fabricated on the feeling-side to talk about feeling. As the fan's sound is "heard" only when one thinks of it — one sitting and thinking having grasped it — so even there it is not seen that "I" was born through the very conceiving of "the fan's sound."
- **All analyses of feeling from the split are empty.** A consciousness born of feeling arises to talk about feeling, and can call it "false," "a delusion," or "only feeling" — but every such account is spoken by the replica born of the feeling, which does not know it is itself the view, self-view, "I," born at one place split into two. Not knowing this, one supposes "I stand apart and discuss the feeling," and so, the moment one goes to the view "feeling is separate, I am separate," any analysis of feeling can be made — and all such analyses are empty.
