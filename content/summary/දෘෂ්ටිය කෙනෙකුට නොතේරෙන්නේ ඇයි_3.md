## Main dhamma points
- A short two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue completing the set, showing that even "nibbāna" gets conceived as a thing built from the distortion, that the view can never be understood by a person, and that one must therefore question from one's own stuck-point rather than analyzing the objects.
- **Even nibbāna is conceived from the distortion.** We imagine that "when nibbāna is realized, seeing will be *this* way, hearing *this* way," and this itself goes to distortion — we manufacture a "nibbāna" out of the very distortion, "this is not how it is, this is not how it is heard or seen" — which is exactly conceiving nibbāna (*nibbānaṁ maññati*), taking nibbāna too as a thing.
- **The "non-existence of this" mistaken for nibbāna.** The "I" born of the distortion — like the shadow asked about the chair — then thinks separately of a place where *this* distortion is absent, building a world of "the non-existence of this," an equanimity-tinged nothing; suppressing perception, a *asaññā*-type "place free of these," "no seeing occurs, no hearing occurs," and taking that absence for nibbāna. But that too is only another knot tied by not-knowing — nibbāna conceived as a thing by way of its supposed absence of seeing and hearing.
- **The view can never be understood by a person.** For just this reason the personal view that is built is not something a person can understand — "the view is not something anyone can understand" — however one takes it, by the sense-base method or the perception method or any *pariyāya*, one only settles on "this is the version that fits me," and any such settling is still the distortion analyzing itself.
- **Question from your own stuck-point, not from the objects.** The real question is "why, how did I fall into this?" — and it is answered, and loosened, only from the very place where "this fits me" arises, from where "I" am stuck; analyzing the objects (the seen, the heard) never frees, for after all the mental analysis "I" am still left over, and the question therefore still stands. Answering from the point where "I" am jammed dissolves the "I"; hence one must seek the Dhamma exactly from where such thoughts arise in oneself, since one tries to free the distortion while "I" remain, asking "how did I get caught," and only realizing the distortion itself to be false, an illusion, a dream, can free — yet even that realizing is never seen to be a fresh cause for the birth of view, so it yields only one more analysis.
- **The one who understands is unharmed.** Whatever way one analyzes, at the end of the analysis one grasps some point as "wrong" or "right" — but the one who has understood is not harmed in the slightest by this; that person simply remains perfectly well.

## Summary

This short third part completes the set by showing that even "nibbāna" gets conceived as a thing built from the very distortion. We imagine that when nibbāna is realized "seeing will be this way, hearing this way," which itself goes to distortion — a "nibbāna" manufactured out of the distortion, "this is not how it is seen or heard" — and that is precisely conceiving nibbāna, taking it too as a thing. The "I" born of the distortion, like the shadow asked about the chair, then thinks separately of a place where this distortion is absent, building a world of "the non-existence of this," an equanimity-tinged nothing in which perception is suppressed and "no seeing, no hearing occurs," and takes that absence for nibbāna — which is only another knot tied by not-knowing. For just this reason the personal view is not something a person can understand: however one takes it, by the sense-base or perception method or any means, one only settles on "this is the version that fits me," which is still the distortion analyzing itself. So the real question — "why, how did I fall into this?" — is loosened only from the very place where "this fits me" and "I" are stuck, never by analyzing the objects, for after all the analysis "I" am still left over and the question still stands; answering from the jammed point dissolves the "I," which is why one must seek the Dhamma exactly from where such thoughts arise in oneself. One tries to free the distortion while "I" remain, asking "how did I get caught," and only realizing the distortion itself to be false, an illusion, a dream, can free — yet even that realizing is never seen to be a fresh cause for a birth of view, so it yields only one more analysis. And whatever the analysis concludes as "wrong" or "right," the one who has understood is not harmed in the least, but simply remains well.
