## Sutta referred to
- **Kālakārāma Sutta** — named (twice): "the Buddha taught in the Kālakārāma that through not-knowing nothing can be gained" (a non-heard cannot be experienced, a non-heard thing cannot be obtained).
- **Reference (not named as a sutta):** the teaching that **wisdom and consciousness cannot be separated** (*paññā* and *viññāṇa* "conjoined, not disjoined" — the Mahāvedalla theme).
- **The two nibbāna-elements** — named: *sopādisesa-nibbāna* (with residue, the living arahant) and *anupādisesa-nibbāna* / *parinibbāna* (without residue).
- Directly referred to, unattributed: *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen-heard-sensed-cognized) as "four views"; the Buddha's saying that "the very eternalism (*śāśvata*) we stand planted in is never met by the run-of-the-mill mind."
- Terms used: *diṭṭhi* (view — the title), *maññanā* (conceiving), *hitaḷuva* (a mere notion/thought), *manasikāra* (attention), *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* (self-view), *viññāṇa* / *paññā* (consciousness / wisdom), *saṅkhata* / *asaṅkhata* (fabricated / unfabricated), *nibbāna*, *atīta / anāgata / paccuppanna* (past / future / present), *saṁsāra*. No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** a two-speaker session (the teacher addresses the questioner as "malli," younger brother); the filename means "Seeing the view *as* 'view'," this being **Part 1**. ## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue whose aim is to see the view *as* view — that "I" *is* the view — showing that "knowing" and "not-knowing" are two sides of one notion, that "I have existed since morning" is the most dangerous view, and that wisdom is nothing but seeing consciousness's own incapacity.
- **Nothing is gained through not-knowing; yet a breath is "gained."** As the Buddha taught in the Kālakārāma Sutta, nothing can be obtained through not-knowing — a non-heard thing cannot be experienced — yet from morning there was no "knowing" of breath, nose or breathing, and now "I have been breathing since morning" is thought; so a "knowing" is claimed to arise from a not-knowing, which cannot be. "Knowing" and "not-knowing" are two sides of one notion (*hitaḷuva*), both mere thoughts.
- **"I have existed since morning" is the most dangerous view.** The frightening thing is not merely "I breathed since morning" but "*I* have existed since morning": in the very thought "I breathed all morning," "I" is born through the thinking, the reflection of that thought — as the chair's shadow, or the tree's reflection falling on water. So "since morning my breath fell though I did not know, I existed" — an "I" that persisted from a place not even known to itself, by *not*-hearing; both "I breathed" and "I existed" are views, neither ever obtainable, and this is *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*.
- **The line of attention splits past from present, and both are one coin.** Not-knowing's *non-existence* is what separates a "past moment" from "this moment": attention is the dividing line; before it is placed the un-known side (the past), and the near side, the felt, is the present. One can never actually experience "I have not been feeling until now" — as, coming home, one says "I didn't see the shop," yet at the shop no thought "I don't see this shop" ever arose; the "I didn't know" is a thought made afterward, one face of a coin whose other face is "now it is felt." The vanishing of the not-known becomes "an arising" for me, never seen to be just a version of the not-known — this pattern is common to all four, seen-heard-sensed-cognized.
- **Past, present and future are all built from two extremes; "I" is the reflection.** Grasping the un-felt makes a past, grasping the felt makes a present, and "it will be felt" (*dænēvi*) makes a future — and in these three "my saṁsāra" consists: "I existed in the past, I exist now, I will exist in the future," all within that one thought. It is not that a separate "I" stands apart telling this story; "I" is the reflection of the not-knowing, born again through the "knowing," and born yet again through "it can be felt later" — so the three times are all built from the two extremes, and the "I"-reflection is established just there. Hence *the view itself is "I"*: what "I" am is the view, the seen-heard-sensed-cognized being "four views," held not from experience but with 100%-confident conviction as "a real seeing" — a saṁsāra-long habituated distortion, now so automatic that not even a thought about it arises. Looking at anything, does the thought "it is seen" arise, or only the seen? We seize "my eye sees it — I am not blind, I am a seer, a hearer, a feeler," and cling to that as identity.
- **Conceiving is thinking about the un-understandable.** "Seeing is a conceiving" is subtler still, for conceiving cannot be understood — its very nature is that it is not grasped, never once in saṁsāra has "it is seen / it is heard" truly been understood, and a thing never understood can never be thought. Remove the "I" and look: "a seeing arises, a seeing ceases" is thought, "a hearing arises, ceases" is thought — that thinking is conceiving, and the thought *is* the "I," so removing the conceiving, the thought itself is extinguished, and it is a view alone that "I" am, which crumbles when self-view crumbles.
- **Wisdom is seeing consciousness's incapacity; it is not separate from consciousness.** When foolishness is seen *as* foolishness, one is no longer a fool but wise, and consciousness itself becomes wise — for the "cannot," the incapacity of consciousness, is exactly what begins to appear to an extraordinary mindfulness, and that capacity to see consciousness's incapacity *is* wisdom. Wisdom is not a separate thing found apart from consciousness (as a sutta says the two cannot be separated); consciousness polished on its other face becomes wisdom, like the two faces of one mirror, for without a consciousness arising there is no wisdom to speak of, and it is from the very place a consciousness arises that wisdom must be drawn out. If taken as separate, one merely conceives a "nibbāna seen by a wisdom that exists apart."
- **Nibbāna is the disappearance of the view; the fabricated cooling.** Seen with extraordinary mindfulness at the consciousness-knot itself, the knot moves to where it cannot be tied — non-fabrication, the unconditioned (*asaṅkhata*) — for the fabricated (*saṅkhata*), truly looked at, turns into the unconditioned with that very seeing. Non-arising of the fabricated *is* nibbāna: there is no separate "nibbāna," only the fabricated cooling out, and since the "fabricated" was itself a thing taken by a view, its cooling is the *disappearance of the view* — the disappearing of the view is what nibbāna means. And because it is by the view that we sustain "I exist" through the seen-heard-sensed, when this incapacity is realized the sustaining cannot go on, nibbāna is not conceived, and "fabrication itself as non-fabrication" is known — the living arahant's view-turned-to-wisdom being *sopādisesa* nibbāna, and the final cooling of the fabricated *anupādisesa* nibbāna, the *parinibbāna*.
