## Sutta referred to
- **Uṇṇābha Brāhmaṇa Sutta** (SN 48.42) — named; the "what supports what" chain: the sense-bases support consciousness, and (the reverse) consciousness is supported by mindfulness (*sati*), mindfulness by liberation (*vimutti*), liberation by nibbāna — worked through in both directions.
- **Bāhiya Sutta** — named; the instruction to Bāhiya that "in the seen there is only the seen, in the thought only the thought" (thinking is only thinking, seeing only seeing — the four: seen-heard-sensed-cognized).
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: *kāyañca paṭicca poṭṭhabbe ca uppajjati kāya-viññāṇaṁ* ("dependent on body and tangibles, body-consciousness arises"), shown to be itself another fabrication of consciousness; *saṅkappa-rāgo* ("intention-passion" — *saṅkappa-rāgo purisassa kāmo*, "a man's sensuality is his intention-lust"); *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* ("the meeting of the three is contact").
- Terms used: *viññāṇa* (consciousness) and its *māyā* (illusion), *manasikāra* (attention), *maññanā* (conceiving), *saṅkalpa* (concept), *kāma-taṇhā* / *bhava-taṇhā* / *vibhava-taṇhā* (sensual / existence / non-existence craving), *poṭṭhabba* / *kāya* (tangible / body), *diṭṭhi* (view), *ayoniso-manasikāra* (unwise attention), *samudaya* / *nirodha* (arising / cessation), *śūnyatā* (emptiness), *jāty-andha* (one born blind). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** a **group** session — the teacher (Speaker 1) leads, with a questioner "mahaththaya" (Speaker 2) and a woman "nōna" (Speaker 3). This is **Part 1**; the filename means "The illusion of consciousness, shown so that the Dhamma is understood." The transcript opens with a transcriber's meta-line.

## Main dhamma points
- A group "good-friend" dialogue showing that not knowing the act of knowing is never met is what gives consciousness something to think about and sustains it, that "body and tangible" are fabricated in the very knowing, and that the three cravings and consciousness's two-sided deception all rest on this.
- **A "knowing" is never met by the body.** As the Uṇṇābha Sutta has it, the sense-bases support consciousness and consciousness the mindfulness that leads on to liberation; but examined, a "knowing" (or a tangible met by the body) is never actually met — if this body truly met tangibles, every feeling would be met all day long, which never happens. The "knowing" appears only when one *thinks*: asked "do you feel it?", one thinks, and calls that thinking "knowing"; what is "understood" is only "there is heat, there is a nose," and it is never noticed that this understanding arises by attention (*manasikāra*).
- **"I" and "body-and-tangible" are built together in the knowing.** When a "knowing" is met, a "present moment" and a "self" are added at once — "it is felt to me" arising only as the knowing arises, with no "I" and no such talk before it. Because "I" too is built through the knowing, this is never suspected; consciousness on the other side displays "my body, my nose, heat" as two things confirming the feeling, so it seems the heat strikes *this* body — yet this is a never-met, un-experienceable thing being thought, and one takes it as a bodily experience, not seeing thinking as thinking, so that a "mind" and "body-and-tangible known" are constructed. Thus *kāyañca paṭicca poṭṭhabbe ca uppajjati kāya-viññāṇaṁ* is itself only one more fabrication of consciousness, and *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* is fabricated exactly here, by our not knowing we are settled within the "knowing" itself, as one turning with the turning earth can never speak a word of "turning."
- **The three cravings; the concept comes first.** The very being-met that seems to happen is not known as a fault, so it is taken as a really-existing thing — this is *saṅkappa-rāgo*, for it is from the concept that passion arises: thinking "chair" brings lust or aversion, good or bad, near or far, and that measuring-out of "chair" is *kāma-taṇhā*, the sensual craving that is simply the act of thinking "there is heat, there is nose"; not recognizing it, one takes it as existing, which is *bhava-taṇhā*, and taking "there is no nose, no breath, no feeling" is *vibhava-taṇhā* — the middle sensual-craving generating both extremes. To see the chair as chair is to know sensual-craving as sensual-craving; lacking that, one goes straight to "it exists."
- **Consciousness deceives from both sides, both taken as "Dhamma."** Watched closely, one realizes "it was never met," but that realization is only a *realization*, consciousness itself now displaying "now I have understood" — a further lie, since the very statements "this is only attention, it never arose, it was never experienced" also come *from consciousness*. So consciousness deceives from both sides — its "there is" and its "I have realized it is false" — and both are taken as "Dhamma," by which we are deceived; for the Buddha said these things are not met, are mind-built, arise only by not-knowing, and that teaching too we grasp as a thing, the finally-understood being just one more knot struck by consciousness. Hence one must not stop at understanding: as a child cries, 200%-certain, if his toy breaks while an adult cannot even imagine crying, so watching daily until the mind can no longer accept "it exists" is what "the incapacity being directly realized" means — and even the teacher's own explaining is a copy of the delusion, to be taken as meaning only, not grasped, its very "escape this" being unwise attention.
- **Better than "emptiness"; and the born-blind who cannot see it as a view.** It is better to see this as an "un-know-able nature" than to say "emptiness," for "empty" is understood only relative to a thing — "the things I know are absent" — which is again an extreme and a view. Cessation cannot be shown on its own and must be seen *within* the arising (*samudaya*): the not-met-ness of seeing and hearing must come *with* a seeing and hearing that arise, else a born-blind man, meeting nothing anyway, would realize cessation most easily of all — but he cannot, for he holds a view, "there is seeing and I do not have it," suffering the very sensual-craving, never seeing that "seeing" is itself a view never met. So this is no mere understanding: it must be met, and the not-being seen within the meeting.
- **The knowing veils itself and lends value to thinking.** Seeing and thinking are shown knotted together, the whole process caught there, and because "cognizing" is taken as a separate thing it is never seen apart — both being built on the un-seen attention. Not knowing that "body and tangible" are never met is exactly what leaves consciousness something to think about; had it been seen, there would be no problem. Because a knowing is not seen *as* a knowing, thinking is given meaning and the two — body and tangible — spring up; then consciousness, taking its own fabricated "body and tangible were not met" as a condition, analyzes further and calls *that* "Dhamma," an analysis itself born of the un-met body-and-tangible talk. In this way the not-knowing of a knowing *as* a knowing is the very cause a mind arises, the fabricated "it was met" lending value to thinking and giving consciousness its footing.

## Summary

This group good-friend dialogue argues that not knowing the act of knowing is never met is what sustains consciousness. As the Uṇṇābha Sutta has it, the sense-bases support consciousness and consciousness the mindfulness that leads to liberation; but a "knowing" is never actually met — if the body truly met tangibles, every feeling would be met all day, which never happens — and the "knowing" appears only when one thinks, so that "there is heat, there is a nose" is called knowing without ever noticing it arises by attention. When a knowing is met, a "present moment" and a "self" are added at once, "it is felt to me" arising only with the knowing and never suspected, since "I" too is built through it; consciousness displays "my body, my nose, heat" as confirming the feeling, so it seems heat strikes this body, whereas a never-met thing is being thought and mistaken for a bodily experience — thinking not seen as thinking. Thus *kāyañca paṭicca poṭṭhabbe ca uppajjati kāya-viññāṇaṁ* is itself one more fabrication, and *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* is fabricated just here, by our not knowing we are settled within the knowing, as one turning with the earth cannot speak of "turning." The being-met not known as a fault is taken as real: this is *saṅkappa-rāgo*, for from the concept passion arises — thinking "chair" brings lust and aversion, and that is *kāma-taṇhā*, which taken as existing is *bhava-taṇhā* and taken as absent is *vibhava-taṇhā*, the middle craving generating both extremes.

Watched closely, one realizes "it was never met," but that is only a realization, consciousness now displaying "now I understand" — a further lie, since the very statements "this is only attention, it never arose" come from consciousness, which thus deceives from both sides, its "there is" and its "I have realized it is false," both taken as "Dhamma." So one must not stop at understanding: as a child is 200%-certain his toy is real and cries when it breaks while an adult cannot even imagine crying, watching daily until the mind can no longer accept "it exists" is what direct realization of the incapacity means — and even the teacher's explaining is a copy of the delusion, to be taken as meaning only, its "escape this" being unwise attention. It is better to see an "un-know-able nature" than to say "emptiness," which is grasped only relative to a thing and is again a view; cessation cannot be shown alone and must be seen within the arising, the not-met-ness of seeing coming with a seeing that arises — else a born-blind man, meeting nothing, would realize it most easily, but he cannot, holding the view "there is seeing and I lack it," suffering the very sensual-craving, never seeing that "seeing" is itself a view never met. Finally, seeing and thinking are knotted together and never seen apart because both rest on the un-seen attention; not knowing that "body and tangible" are never met is what leaves consciousness something to think about, and consciousness, taking its own fabricated "they were not met" as a condition, analyzes further and calls that "Dhamma" — so the not-knowing of a knowing *as* a knowing is the very cause a mind arises, lending value to thinking and giving consciousness its footing.
