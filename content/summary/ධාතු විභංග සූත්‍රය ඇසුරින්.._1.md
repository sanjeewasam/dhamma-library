## Sutta referred to
- **Dhātu-vibhaṅga Sutta** (MN 140) — named and central; its six elements (*paṭhavī, āpo, tejo, vāyo, ākāsa, viññāṇa*), the anatomical list of the earth-element (hair, body-hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bones, marrow, kidney, heart, liver, membranes, intestines, etc.), the refrain *n'etaṁ mama, n'eso'ham asmi, na me so attā* ("this is not mine, this I am not, this is not my self" — rendered "mine not, I not, my self not"), and the sutta's **four foundations (adhiṭṭhāna)** are all drawn on: *paññāya appamādo* (do not be negligent in wisdom), *saccam anurakkheyya* (protect the truth), *cāgaṁ anubrūheyya* (develop relinquishment), *santim eva so sikkheyya* (train in peace).
- Directly referred to, unattributed: *kalyāṇa-mitta-sampatti* — "the whole Sāsana depends 100% on the good friend" (the Upaḍḍha Sutta saying); *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*.
- Terms used: *saññā* (perception/recognition) and *saññā-vipallāsa* (perversion of perception), *dhātu* (element), *ākāsa* (space), *viññāṇa* (consciousness), *manasikāra* (attention), *cetanā* (volition), *bhāṣā* (language), *diṭṭhi* (view), *yoni* (origin), *nibbidā* / *kalakirīma* (disenchantment), *nitya-saññā* (permanence-perception), *paññā* (wisdom). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** a two-speaker session (the questioner is addressed as "mahaththaya"); the filename means "Based on the Dhātu-vibhaṅga Sutta," this being **Part 1** of three. ## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" examination based on the Dhātu-vibhaṅga Sutta, showing that the earth-element is only a perception, that language is the perversion of perception, and that the sutta's "not mine, not I, not my self" reveals the defect only to one who has seen the un-recognizability — otherwise it is a mantra carried like a raft.
- **The earth-element is only a perception, taken as a thing.** To see *paṭhavī* as *paṭhavī* is to see perception as perception, for "earth" is really only a *saññā*, a recognition — yet we meet it not as a recognition but as a *thing*, so "hardness" felt is itself a recognizing, and the moment we think "sitting?" the recognition "hard" is already complete. We knot the recognizing to a "thing," a chair, and thereby *paṭhavī* becomes a thing; but seen as perception it cannot stand, being an un-knowable nature. The sutta's own list — hair, nails, teeth, skin, flesh, sinews, bone, marrow, kidney, heart, liver, intestines — is met each as a "thing" via a recognition, an intestine being really only a *saññā*.
- **If a real body touched a real chair, hardness would be felt all day.** Were there truly a hard quality, this body's hardness would be felt constantly, for what is always in contact must always be felt; and if body and chair really existed and their contact really produced a "feeling," one would necessarily feel it all the while one sits — which one does not. So "why is it not felt?" has no answer available to the ordinary mind: it is not cognizable, and precisely because the not-being-met is not cognizable, all that *is* cognizable is the bare "there is," the recognition arising only when one thinks. Thus one is only ever met by a built-up thing born of "understanding," never knowing the actual piece.
- **The flaw of recognizing cannot be found from within recognizing.** For the defect of recognition to be seen, one must first see the un-recognizability — only one who sees that "recognizing" is a thing that cannot be done sees recognizing at all, and only such a one sees its flaw; anyone caught in the recognition, caught in the *saññā*, can never find the flaw of recognizing by recognizing. Put otherwise, the flaw of thinking, that thinking itself is the flaw, can never be understood by thinking. Sitting all this while, one has been in a recognizing not met at all, and had all the conditions been present — body, chair, everything — the thing would have been recognized, yet it is not; that un-recognizable nature is where the "cannot" stands, and only one who sees it sees recognizing as a defect.
- **"I" and "body-and-chair" arise together in the recognition, so its flaw is hidden.** The permanence-perception one dwells in, and the "it cannot be known," are alike beyond the worldling's range, which is why the arisen attention is 100%-certain, admitting no doubt — for that is all we are given as "I exist," measurable only from there, and one cannot see its flaw because it is from that very thing that "I" arise. From "there is a chair for the hardness" the "body the chair touches" is also born, body and chair arising together, and "I" is born through that recognition, arising with it — so no flaw in the recognizing can be sought, since "I" is understood in the very understanding; had one never come to understand "there is a chair and a body," the "I sitting on the chair" would never have formed, so "I" arise only through the chair, the chair-recognition itself being the problem.
- **Space too is only "not-earth" named; language is the perversion of perception.** Space (*ākāsa-dhātu*) — the mouth-cavity, the nostrils — is recognized only relative to *paṭhavī*, as "the four not being there," a fresh *saññā* built from the absence of a prior one, "nothing there," where "nothing" means the other elements, so it is a plain perversion of perception, a not-*paṭhavī* given another name and made a thing. This cannot be understood through language, for language is exactly what was made to recognize a nature that does not stand — a name given to a recognition — so the very naming veils the un-recognizability and distorts it; whatever the language, Pāli or Sinhala or English, no language can support the seeing, since a perception-perversion cannot be undone by more of the same, and the flaw of understanding can never be understood, "wisdom" being precisely what is not an understanding but a seeing beyond all words.
- **Not mine, not I, not my self — a mantra unless the un-arising is seen; and the four foundations.** When wisdom settles at the un-arising of "I," the sutta's "not mine, not I, not my self" is no longer needed as a mantra — for those three are talk bound up with "I," and once "I" does not engage they become empty sounds; but to one who has not seen the nature, they are recited like a spell in hope it will "wear off," taking a not-yet-arisen "I" and saying over it "not mine, not I, not my self," which never removes the view — carrying the raft on one's head. The Dhamma only exhibits the *defects*, the puthujjana's level, because a worldling cannot see the defect himself; hence a good friend is needed to draw out the wisdom, as a teacher draws out for the exam what the books alone cannot convey — which is why "the whole Sāsana depends 100% on the good friend." And the sutta's four foundations are: be not negligent in wisdom, protect the truth (all that is cognizable), develop relinquishment, and train in peace — so that, seeing the un-arising, disenchantment (*nibbidā*) toward the earth-element comes effortlessly and "mine, I, my self" no longer engage.

## Summary

This two-speaker examination based on the Dhātu-vibhaṅga Sutta turns on seeing the earth-element as only a perception. "Earth" is really a *saññā*, a recognition, yet we meet it as a thing, so "hardness" felt is itself a recognizing, complete the instant we think "sitting?"; we knot the recognizing to a chair, and *paṭhavī* becomes a thing, though seen as perception it cannot stand, being un-knowable — the sutta's own list of hair, teeth, flesh, bone, kidney, heart and intestines being each met as a "thing" via a recognition. Were there truly a hard quality, this body's hardness would be felt all day, and if body and chair really existed and their contact really produced feeling, one would feel it constantly while sitting, which one does not; so "why is it not felt?" has no answer for the ordinary mind, and precisely because the not-being-met is not cognizable, all that is cognizable is the bare "there is," arising only when one thinks. The flaw of recognizing cannot be found from within recognizing: only one who sees the un-recognizability sees recognizing at all, and its flaw, whereas anyone caught in the *saññā* can never find it — just as the flaw of thinking can never be understood by thinking.

Moreover "I" and "body-and-chair" arise together in the recognition, so its flaw stays hidden: from "there is a chair for the hardness" the "body the chair touches" is born, and "I" is born through that same recognition, understood in the very understanding, so no flaw can be sought there — had one never understood "there is a chair and a body," the "I sitting" would never form, and "I" arise only through the chair, the chair-recognition itself being the problem. Space too is only "not-earth" named, recognized relative to *paṭhavī* as "the four not being there," a fresh perception built from the absence of a prior one — a plain perversion of perception. This cannot be understood through language, which is exactly what was made to recognize a nature that does not stand, so naming veils the un-recognizability; no language, Pāli or Sinhala or English, can support the seeing, for a perception-perversion cannot be undone by more language, and the flaw of understanding can never be understood, "wisdom" being not an understanding but a seeing beyond words. Finally, when wisdom settles at the un-arising of "I," the sutta's "not mine, not I, not my self" is no longer needed — those three being talk bound to "I," empty once "I" does not engage — whereas to one who has not seen the nature they are only a mantra recited over a not-yet-arisen "I," never removing the view, the raft carried on the head. The Dhamma exhibits only the defects, the worldling's level, since he cannot see the defect himself, so a good friend is needed to draw out the wisdom as a teacher draws out what books cannot — "the whole Sāsana depending 100% on the good friend" — and by the sutta's four foundations (be not negligent in wisdom, protect truth, develop relinquishment, train in peace), seeing the un-arising, disenchantment toward the earth-element comes effortlessly, and "mine, I, my self" no longer engage.
