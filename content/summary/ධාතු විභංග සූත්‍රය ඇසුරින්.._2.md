## Sutta referred to
- **Dhātu-vibhaṅga Sutta** (MN 140) — named and central; **taught to King Pukkusāti**. Its **four foundations (adhiṭṭhāna)** are worked through: *paññā-adhiṭṭhāna* (be not negligent in wisdom), *sacca-adhiṭṭhāna* (protect the truth), *cāga-adhiṭṭhāna* (develop relinquishment), *upasama/santi-adhiṭṭhāna* (train for peace only). Its **consciousness-element passage** is quoted at length: the purified consciousness remains, and by it one knows pleasant, painful and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling; dependent on the contact for pleasant feeling, pleasant feeling arises, and on the cessation of that contact the feeling ceases — "then one knows it has subsided" (*santi*/*upasama*); likewise for painful and neutral feeling.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti* ("what one feels, one perceives"); *udaya-vaya(-ñāṇa)* (rise-and-fall knowledge); the six elements (*paṭhavī, āpo, tejo, vāyo, ākāsa, viññāṇa*).
- Terms used: *dhātu* (element), *saññā* (perception), *phassa* / *passa* (contact), *vedanā* (feeling), *asmi-māna* (the "I am" conceit), *asaṅkhata* (unconditioned), *bhava* (becoming), *rāga* / *dveṣa* (lust / aversion), *paññā* (wisdom), *nibbidā* / *kalakirīma* (disenchantment), *diṭṭhi* (view). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** a two-speaker session (questioner "mahaththaya"); **Part 2** of the Dhātu-vibhaṅga examination. Opens with a transcriber's meta-line and a fragmentary "sacca..." exchange; uses the matchbox-and-matchstick image. One passage (lines ~643–673) is duplicated.

## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue continuing the Dhātu-vibhaṅga examination, showing how the sutta's four foundations align effortlessly with the seen nature, how the earth-recognition births the person, and how the consciousness-element's "subsiding" is the realization of one who sees contact's arising-and-ceasing together.
- **The four foundations align effortlessly with the seen nature.** Where wisdom operates, truth is protected without effort — it simply keeps itself; where grasping is seen impossible there is nothing to let go, so relinquishment (*cāga*) grows effortlessly, the mind inclining to release because "holding" is seen undoable; and one established in the nature has no room for conflict, so trains for peace (*santi/upasama*) of itself. So the four foundations — wisdom, truth, relinquishment, peace — are not separate exercises but coincide with the nature, as the four noble truths interlock, for one who has seen it; the whole is a "non-doing," the wearing-away of the "I am" conceit (*asmi-māna*), the person dwindling, not a person cultivating a path.
- **The earth-recognition births the person; its flaw can't be seen from within.** The person is built with the elements, rooted in the *paṭhavī*-recognition by which "chair" (hard) and "water" (flowing) are separated into things; at the very naming "hard," the birth of the one to whom hardness occurs is complete — *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti* — so the person arises with the recognition, and no one can ever find the flaw of recognizing from within it, since he arises together with the *saññā*. Both "doing" and "not-doing" alike birth the person, so seeing the defect of both — "I recognize" wrong and "I do not recognize" wrong — is wisdom, not something a person understands, for one born by doing-or-not-doing cannot grasp what transcends both; this is why the path grows effortlessly, of itself.
- **Fitting suttas is not seeing; delusion cannot be seen by delusion.** To find one's own words confirmed in a sutta — "what he says is right here in the text" — moves nothing, for one's lust and aversion do not stir unless a *vision* arises; merely matching the talk to a verse or paragraph leaves the defilements unshaken, since no direct seeing came, only a grasp of the point. Delusion is never seen by delusion, madness never known as madness by the mad; the flaw is seen only when the very way the error was made appears, whereupon it is realized that in a never-occurred event a felt "it occurred" was the whole error — and to such a one there is neither a "wrong" nor a "right" thing met, since it was only by not seeing the true nature that a "world" was built as real. Seen as un-fabricated (*asaṅkhata*), no forming ever occurred, so nothing is "lost," and no "it exists" or "it is false" can even be said.
- **You cannot see impermanence by breaking a thing.** The easy but wrong move is to analyze the chair into four great elements, deem it "false," and conclude "there is no such chair, the existing thing breaks apart" — but one cannot see impermanence by breaking an existing thing, and no scientist analyzing what exists will ever reach the root a Buddha shows, for that is analyzing the very "thing" that was taken as real. To watch fabrication while holding the chair ends in "there is no chair" — a plain error, sliding unknowingly from a present chair to a chair-less absence, "the chair is an illusion" — whereas what is met is only the not-knowing of fabrication.
- **The consciousness-element's "subsiding" is the seer of arising-and-ceasing together.** The sutta's viññāṇa-passage — pleasant, painful and neutral feeling each arising with its contact and ceasing with that contact's cessation, "then one knows it has subsided" — is not the world's talk of "the pleasure that arose has now gone," but points to one who sees *both* the arising and the ceasing together: the "subsided" (*santi*) is realized only by such a seer, this being the *udaya-vaya-ñāṇa*, for seeing contact one sees that the arising-story and the ceasing-story are alike built by not seeing contact, like birth and death. As a matchbox and matchstick struck together are said to yield fire, and their parting to end it — the world's two stories of arising and ceasing — so, seeing the *striking* (contact) rightly, one sees it is neither the matchbox nor the matchstick, nor apart from the two, nor caused by the two, nor uncaused: to say "it is from the two" is wrong, "not from the two" is wrong (there being no striking without them), "apart from the two" is wrong (no contact then), and "not caused by them" wrong — all four alternatives seen as defects, so contact is no thinkable place, and the "subsiding" is the seeing that dissolves birth-and-death.

## Summary

This second part of the Dhātu-vibhaṅga examination first shows that the sutta's four foundations align effortlessly with the seen nature: where wisdom operates, truth protects itself; where grasping is seen impossible, relinquishment grows of itself; and one established in the nature, having no room for conflict, trains for peace naturally — so wisdom, truth, relinquishment and peace are not separate exercises but coincide with the nature, as the four noble truths interlock, the whole being a non-doing, the wearing-away of the "I am" conceit, the person dwindling rather than a person cultivating a path. The person, moreover, is born with the elements from the *paṭhavī*-recognition by which "chair" and "water" are split into things; at the very naming "hard," the birth of the one to whom hardness occurs is complete — *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti* — so the person arises with the recognition and can never find its flaw from within, since he arises with the *saññā*. Both doing and not-doing birth the person, so seeing the defect of both is wisdom, not a person's understanding, and the path grows of itself.

Merely finding one's words confirmed in a sutta moves nothing, the teacher stresses, for lust and aversion stir only when a vision arises; delusion is never seen by delusion, madness never known by the mad, and the flaw appears only when the way the error was made is seen — that in a never-occurred event a felt "it occurred" was the whole error, so that to such a one there is neither "wrong" nor "right," a "world" having been built as real only by not seeing the true nature; seen as unconditioned, no forming ever occurred, and no "it exists" or "it is false" can be said. Hence one cannot see impermanence by breaking a thing: analyzing the chair into elements and deeming it false slides unknowingly from a present chair to a chair-less absence, and no science reaches the root a Buddha shows, for it only analyzes the "thing" taken as real. Finally, the sutta's consciousness-passage — each feeling arising with its contact and ceasing with that contact's cessation, "then one knows it has subsided" — is not the world's "the pleasure has now gone" but points to one who sees both arising and ceasing together, the "subsided" being realized only by such a seer, the *udaya-vaya-ñāṇa*; as matchbox and matchstick struck are said to yield fire and their parting to end it, so, seeing the striking rightly, one sees contact is neither the two, nor apart from them, nor caused by them, nor uncaused — all four alternatives being defects — so that contact is no thinkable place, and the "subsiding" is the seeing that dissolves birth-and-death.
