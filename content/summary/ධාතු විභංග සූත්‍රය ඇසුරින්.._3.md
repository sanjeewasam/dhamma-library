## Sutta referred to
- **Dhātu-vibhaṅga Sutta** (MN 140) — named and central; its **two-fire-sticks simile** is quoted: as two fire-sticks rubbed together produce heat and fire, and on being drawn apart the heat born of them ceases and subsides, so the feeling dependent on a contact arises and, on that contact's cessation, ceases and subsides — the same being said of pleasant, painful and neutral feeling. The **consciousness-element passage** ("dependent on the contact for pleasant feeling, pleasant feeling arises... it subsides") is restated. The speakers note the sutta's depth lies at its very end (a *Tipiṭaka* text shared by WhatsApp).
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti* ("what one feels, one perceives"); *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* ("with ignorance as condition, formations").
- Terms used: *phassa* / *passa* (contact), *vedanā* (feeling), *saññā* (perception), *paṭhavī* (earth-element), *maññanā* (conceiving), *diṭṭhi* (view), *viññāṇa* (consciousness). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** a two-speaker session (questioner "mahaththaya"); **Part 3** — the short closing of the Dhātu-vibhaṅga examination. Ends "let us stop for today."

## Main dhamma points
- A short two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue closing the Dhātu-vibhaṅga examination, confirming that only one who has seen contact sees both the arising and ceasing of feeling, that "it is felt" is where the felt-thing and the felt-"I" arise together, and that the sutta's images are everywhere once rightly seen.
- **The two extremes both fall away; "with or without cause" cannot be said.** To say contact is "apart from the two [sticks]" is wrong, so neither "there is a cause" nor "there is no cause" stands — both extremes are seen as defects, and to one who sees contact *as* contact no question of "are there two [things] or not" engages, nor any slide from "these exist" to "these do not exist." As the sutta itself illustrates with two fire-sticks — heat and fire born of their rubbing, ceasing and subsiding when they are parted — so feeling arises with its contact and subsides with that contact's cessation, an image that, rightly seen, is found everywhere in the teaching.
- **Contact arises exactly at "it is felt"; the felt-thing and the felt-"I" arise together.** We dwell in "feeling" itself, and until "feeling" is seen *as* feeling there is no contact; contact arises just at the point one thinks "it is felt," and there, before-and-after-less, both the felt thing and the "I" who feels arise — for "the nose" is a faculty the mind imagines and "the body" is what is met, and only after seeing these two does one realize contact is "the very thinking," from which "there is a body and a breath" is understood, falling at once into "pleasant" or "painful." This arising comes only from not knowing one is settled in not-knowing — *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*, never cognizable to consciousness — so only one who has seen that un-knowable nature can see what contact is: "seeing contact" is not seeing some thing but seeing the *defect*, that thinking itself is the flaw, the "cannot," the impossibility of knowing where one recognizes.
- **Recognizing the feeling makes it a thing and births the recognizer.** It is through this very recognizing that the contact goes to the "pleasant" or "painful" side: a felt quality taken as soft is a pleasant feeling, taken as rough or hard a painful one — *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti*, the feeling recognized, and there feeling and perception knot. Feeling is not known *as* feeling; there is no "earth" talk in it, for "it is felt" seen rightly is only a conceiving with no *paṭhavī* in it — if felt, only "felt," and it is by going to think *about* the feeling that "what is felt is such-and-such" is bred, a naming. The instant it is recognized — hard, or soft, or fire, or air — it becomes a thing, and a thing means a person stands on this side, the one to whom it was felt, so that with that thing, before-and-after-less, the view arises, after which there is no escape by any means.
- **The sutta's depth is at its close; enough for now.** These same illustrations run through the whole teaching, and the Dhātu-vibhaṅga has a good deal when examined, its depth lying at the very end after the elements are set out; the discussion closes here for the day.

## Summary

This short third part closes the Dhātu-vibhaṅga examination. To say contact is "apart from the two fire-sticks" is wrong, so neither "there is a cause" nor "there is no cause" stands — both extremes are defects, and to one who sees contact as contact no question of "are there two things or not" engages, nor any slide from "these exist" to "these do not." The sutta's own two-fire-sticks simile makes it plain: heat and fire born of the rubbing cease and subside when the sticks are parted, so feeling arises with its contact and subsides with that contact's cessation — an image found everywhere once rightly seen. This comes only from not knowing one is settled in not-knowing — *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*, never cognizable to consciousness — so only one who has seen the un-knowable nature sees what contact is: seeing not a thing but the defect, that thinking itself is the flaw. It is by recognizing the feeling that contact goes to the pleasant or painful side — a felt quality taken as soft being pleasant, as hard painful — *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti*, feeling and perception knotting; feeling is not known as feeling, there being no "earth" in it, only a conceiving, and to think *about* it breeds "what is felt is such-and-such," a naming.
