## Main dhamma points
- A two-speaker "good-friend" dialogue on the name-and-form knot, showing that the truly non-existent is never met, that both "it exists" and "it is absent" are fabrications, that convention itself is the perversion of perception, and that name-and-form has no life — life being projected by us.
- **The journey is fabricated at home; the "topmost view."** Driving home from town, one arrives and thinks "I came past the bends, bridges and culverts" — yet none of them was seen on the way, no seeing occurred there and no "I" arose there; the road, the distance, the whole journey are built at home from the "influx-bag" of memory, a *bhava* manufactured backwards. "I could not be here unless I came" is the *agrama-diṭṭhi* of the Sañcetanā Sutta, for the coming was never met; and the mirage-fabricated "town" and "home" and the four-kilometre gap between them are all *saṅkhāra*, drawn from memory.
- **The truly non-existent is never met; "there is" and "there is not" are both built.** As with a chair asked after in empty space — "there is no chair" is spoken only by first conjuring a chair in the mind, a whole five-clinging-aggregate raised in order to deny it — so the "zero" (absence) is as fabricated as the "one," both being *saññā*. What is truly forgotten can never be met (a snack forgotten in a bag is met only when memory revives it, and "I forgot it" is spoken holding the memory); so the genuine non-existence is never experienced, whatever is met being a met-thing — "if it is met, it is a met-thing; if forgotten, the forgetting too is met." Hence the world only ever "uses" the there-is-not as another eternalism, never the real absence, and both eternalist and annihilationist views are the two sides of this coin.
- **Convention is the perversion of perception; the true nature can't be found from within it.** Because perception is distorted, language gives us a word like "forgotten" used not in a real forgetting but by reviving a thing — so language itself is *saññā-vipallāsa*, a distortion, and the whole of convention is a lie. One can never grasp the true nature (*prakṛti*) from within the distortion (*vikṛti*), for we live inside it and try to cross the flood while swimming in it, working only with the heaped-up perceptions; the account cannot be caught within our range of thinking, and one must get out, not analyze from within.
- **The knot the Buddha alone shows; contact never known.** Any fool sees "there is a form before the eye, a bus, I must step aside" — *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, the meeting of the three, needs no Buddha to state the obvious, being the world's nature whether or not a Buddha arises. A Buddha's distinction is to show that these three — eye, form and consciousness — *knot together by not knowing contact*, that "seeing" is a mental process not seen as a process, and that is the knot no other teacher can show. It is by not seeing this contact that "my eye," "an external form" and "a real seeing" become real, *manasikāra-sambhavā sabbe dhammā* — all things originating from attention, arising by attention, not because the things exist.
- **The concept, not the picture, carries the passion.** A painted deer at water and a real deer drinking differ only in our *holding* — "this is a picture (lifeless)," "that is alive" — for both are recognized by perception, and name-and-form carries no "alive/lifeless": *saṅkappa-rāgo purisassa kāmo, na te kāmā yāni citrāni loke*, the world's pretty things are not sensuality, the passion being in the concept. To the bare "deer"-perception nothing of "graspable, sellable, a nuisance, it can run" belongs; these we add, and it is to our own added concepts that lust and aversion attach — the five-cord sensuality being the passion in the marks *we* supplied, not in the perception. Life is projected: "my life" lends life to the mark, and perception seen *as* perception has no living or non-living perceptions.
- **Perception itself is the mirage; the five similes; nothing is obtained.** "The water isn't real" is not the point — perception *itself* is a mirage: "nose" is a perception, and if no nose is ever obtained, whence a perception "of a nose"? — as with the hen and the egg, neither is found. So recognizing is itself the mirage, un-obtainable; and this is the meaning of the five similes — form a foam-lump, feeling a water-bubble, perception a mirage, formations a heartwood-less plantain, consciousness a conjuror's trick — all sharing one meaning: they are never met, cannot be grasped or "brought" (one cannot fetch ten foam-lumps or ten bubbles), have no persistence, so are impermanent, not-self, and any persistence built on the un-met is suffering — the three characteristics standing together. Seeing this, the "mind-and-mental-object" level cannot even be thought, and only then does *dhammānupassanā* develop rightly, after body, feeling, perception and formations are each seen as they are. Finally, by the Kālakārāma formula — no thing conceived apart from seeing, no unseen, no to-be-seen, no seer persisting through seeing; and likewise for hearing (a song exists only while played, the CD is not the song, "unheard sounds out there" being held only through hearing) — the "I" is born through the three, so the hearer, the heard, and the to-be-heard are all "I," and what the world imagines it obtains the Buddha does not obtain: there is no obtainer and nothing obtained, we merely play, knotted, inside the distortion, supposing "it exists."
