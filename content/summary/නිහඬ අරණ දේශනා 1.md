- **Satipaṭṭhāna Sutta** — named/quoted: *samudaya-dhammānupassī, vaya-dhammānupassī, samudaya-vaya-dhammānupassī* (contemplating arising, passing, and both together).

## Main dhamma points
- A formal "Silent Grove" discourse showing that the Buddha's impermanence is beyond reasoning and un-cognizable to consciousness, that suppressing perception only prunes the tree, and that the true nature — dream, sleep, ignorance, the un-arising — is seen only by a wisdom that transcends knowing, through a good friend.
- **The Buddha's anicca is not the change of a thing.** Most see impermanence through perception — a thing passing, changing, dream-like — but that is understandable by anyone and needs no special knowledge; a doctor sees skin-cells changing under a microscope, and a Muslim or Christian can prove that things pass. What is *atakkāvacara* and *pubbe ananussutesu*, un-shareable with any Brahmā, Māra or deva, harder than piercing a hair split into seven, is the impermanence seen only by wisdom (Dhp 277), which is the removing of *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* — and its difficulty is exactly the mark of the never-before-heard.
- **Science merely re-names; the "teapoy"-becoming never breaks.** Analyzing the same thing as four great elements, then as electrons-protons-neutrons, then as quantum energy is only a *saññā-nānatta*, one thing variously recognized, the difference lying in language, the perversion of perception; the root is untouched, the "teapoy" *bhava* never breaks, and truth is not "updated" — the Four Truths hold across all three times. So making the recognized-thing subtle relieves nothing at the root.
- **Suppressing perception prunes the tree, not fells it.** By meditation, perception can be thinned until not even a thought arises, and by developing it one way or another one reaches the formless spheres — space-infinity, consciousness-infinity, neither-perception-nor-non-perception — but this only veils the *way perception is built*, leaving the flaw of recognizing untouched; the *anusaya* remains, and, like a ball held underwater, resurfaces when the tendency arises. Consciousness (*viññāṇa*) can suppress the recognized *thing*, but the recognizing itself is never escaped by recognizing — as a tree is pruned branch by branch but not felled; only seeing that recognizing *cannot be done* frees from the root, and there is a world of difference between "clearly seeing" and "knowing."
- **The dog's reflection; both extremes grasp a never-arisen perception.** A dog takes its water-reflection for another dog (deceived by holding the perception "dog" as existent); we see our reflection knowing it a shadow — but "there is no dog there" grasps a never-arisen perception exactly as the dog grasps an existent one, both being one perception deceived two ways, into the "there-is" and "there-is-not" extremes. "I am not deceived" is itself another knot; and the base of both is *sleep* — the dream is "real" only relative to "the dream/sleep is false," yet "sleep" is never experienced, so "this is real" rests on a false perception and is equally false; both are mere perception (*saññā-mātra*), and only by a wisdom toward "sleep" is the two-fold fabrication seen.
- **We live in a state un-cognizable to consciousness: avijjā.** "Sleep" is named though it is reached by none of the four — seen, heard, sensed, cognized — for those four never engage there; consciousness cannot cognize the not-knowing, and it is precisely because this un-cognizable state (where our feet stand) is not known that whatever consciousness sees, hears or knows becomes "real." This not-knowing, in another word *avijjā*, can never be met by a person, since a "person" is only a *sakkāya* view arising with consciousness, and what is un-cognizable to consciousness can never be cognized by a consciousness-bound self. Hence *paññā* is a separate word from knowing: the capacity to see what is un-cognizable to consciousness, which so transcends "knowing" (*jānato*) that it is called *seeing* (*passato*) — neither by knowing nor apart from it.
- **The middle is never reached by thinking the two sides.** As a tree's "growing tall" is neither the axe nor the water, nor apart from the two, nor caused by them, nor uncaused — a magic no thought can resolve — and as "rising" holds no "falling" and "falling" no "rising," so the middle is never reached by reasoning the two extremes; it is only when both are seen together (Satipaṭṭhāna's arising-and-passing contemplated *together*, not passing alone) that wisdom dawns and the middle finds no footing. Consciousness stands only by holding two sides — tall against short, waking against sleep — so its very footing dissolves when the un-arising of both sides is seen, and within that seeing the fabricated is seen *as* a non-fabricating.
- **"One perception arises, another ceases"; light and darkness.** The naive "the arisen thing itself ceases" keeps eternalism — the same one born lives, dies and is reborn — whereas Ven. Ñāṇananda's *aññam eva saññā uppajjati, aññam eva saññā nirujjhati* shows that "ceasing" is *another* perception, no mind ever seeing its own arising-and-ceasing. "Light" is only the un-arisen "no-darkness" named, a reflection of the never-met darkness: seeing that the condition (darkness) is never met, cessation is realized — no lost darkness and no gained light, birth-and-death transcended, nothing to accept or reject — for a persistence lent to light out of not-knowing is itself sought, and seeing the condition never met, that persistence turns to non-arising, *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ*.
- **Effortless, and only through the good friend; not-self and suffering.** Where no darkness is met to reject and no light to accept, there is nothing to stand on and nothing to strive for, so the path is effortless — "neither standing nor striving," the near branch and the far both let go in mid-flood, felt as danger only while a footing is still sought. This frees only through association with the good friend, never by thinking, since "I have understood" is itself a footing; and seeing self-perception (*ātma-saññā*) rightly, not-self is realized then and there, the meeting of a persistence within the un-persisting being itself the suffering — *saṅkhāra-dukkha*, a suffering un-cognizable to consciousness, which is why a Buddha alone can point it out, and why seeing it aright even "Buddha," "Dhamma" and "the Four Truths" fall away as names given only for the world's understanding.

## Summary

This formal Silent Grove discourse begins by distinguishing the Buddha's impermanence from the ordinary kind. Most see impermanence through perception — a thing changing, passing, dream-like — but that is understandable by anyone, a doctor or a scientist or another religion, and needs no special knowledge; what is beyond reasoning and never-before-heard, harder than piercing a hair split into seven, is the impermanence seen only by wisdom (Dhp 277), which is the removing of self-view. Science merely re-names the same thing — four elements, then electrons, then quantum energy — a diversity of perception whose root is untouched, so the "teapoy"-becoming never breaks and truth is never updated. Suppressing perception by meditation, even to the formless spheres, only prunes the tree, veiling how perception is built while the underlying tendency remains and resurfaces like a ball held underwater; consciousness can suppress the recognized thing but never escape recognizing by recognizing, and only seeing that recognizing cannot be done frees from the root. The dog takes its reflection for a dog by holding the perception as existent; we hold "there is no dog" — both grasping a never-arisen perception, the two extremes of one perception, "I am not deceived" being another knot, and the base of both being sleep, which is never experienced, so "this is real" rests on the false "the dream is false," and both are mere perception.

We live, the teacher shows, in a state un-cognizable to consciousness: "sleep" is named though none of the four — seen, heard, sensed, cognized — reaches it, and because this un-cognizable ground is not known, whatever consciousness meets becomes real; this not-knowing is *avijjā*, never met by a "person," since a person is only a self-view arising with consciousness, so wisdom is a separate word — the capacity to see what consciousness cannot cognize, called seeing, not knowing, neither by knowing nor apart from it. The middle is never reached by thinking the two sides: as a tree's growing is neither axe nor water nor apart from them nor caused nor uncaused, and rising holds no falling, so only when arising and passing are seen together does the middle find no footing, consciousness standing only by holding two sides and dissolving when the un-arising of both is seen, the fabricated seen within the fabricating as a non-fabricating. The naive "the arisen ceases" keeps eternalism, whereas *aññam eva saññā uppajjati, aññam eva saññā nirujjhati* shows "ceasing" is another perception; "light" is only the un-arisen "no-darkness" named, and seeing the condition never met, cessation is realized — no darkness lost, no light gained, birth-and-death transcended. Where nothing is met to accept or reject, the path is effortless, neither standing nor striving, the near and far branches let go in mid-flood; and this frees only through the good friend, never by thinking, since "I have understood" is itself a footing — so that, seeing self-perception rightly, not-self is realized, the persistence found within the un-persisting being the very suffering, un-cognizable to consciousness, which only a Buddha can point out, and before which even "Buddha," "Dhamma" and "the Four Truths" fall away as names given for the world.
