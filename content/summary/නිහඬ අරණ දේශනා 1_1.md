## Main dhamma points
- The opening of a formal Silent Grove discourse, on why the Buddha's impermanence is beyond reasoning, why science and meditation only re-name or suppress perception without touching the root, and how the dog's reflection and the dream reveal that both "it is real" and "it is false" are mere perception.
- **The Buddha's anicca is not the change of a thing.** Most see impermanence through perception — a thing passing or changing, likened to a dream — but that is graspable by anyone, better described by a doctor watching skin-cells change under a microscope or provable to a Muslim or Christian, so it needs no special knowledge. What is *atakkāvacara* and *pubbe ananussutesu*, un-shareable with any Brahmā, Māra or deva, harder than piercing a hair split into seven, is the impermanence seen only by wisdom (Dhp 277), which is the removing of *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* — its very difficulty being the mark of the never-before-heard.
- **Science merely re-names; the "becoming" never breaks.** Analyzing the same thing as four great elements, then electrons-protons-neutrons, then quantum energy is only a *saññā-nānatta*, one thing variously recognized, the difference lying in language; the root is untouched, the object's *bhava* never breaks, and truth is not "updated," the Four Truths holding across all three times.
- **Suppressing perception prunes the tree, not fells it.** By meditation, perception can be thinned until no thought arises, and developed one way or another one reaches the formless spheres — but this only veils *how* perception is built, the *anusaya* remaining and, like a ball held underwater, resurfacing when the tendency arises. Consciousness can suppress the recognized thing but never escape recognizing by recognizing — as a tree is pruned branch by branch but not felled — so only seeing that recognizing *cannot be done* frees from the root; "clearly seeing" and "knowing" are worlds apart.
- **The dog's reflection; both extremes are mere perception.** A dog takes its water-reflection for another dog, deceived by holding the perception "dog" as existent; we look and say "there is no dog," yet we grasp a never-arisen perception exactly as the dog grasps an existent one — the same one perception deceived two ways, into "there-is" and "there-is-not," neither escaping the two extremes though we sit in the same boat. "I am not deceived" is itself a further knot: this present is made "real" only relative to "the dream is false," so on what basis is the dream judged false? — on a perception, which is itself a perception, so "this is real" rests on the false "the dream is false," and both are mere perception (*saññā-mātra*). One cannot find, from within perception, whether the dream is false because this is real or the reverse; perception, however long pondered, never ends. (The transcript loops here without advancing.)

## Summary

This opening segment of the Silent Grove discourse distinguishes the Buddha's impermanence from the ordinary. Most see impermanence through perception — a thing changing, dream-like — but that is graspable by anyone, better described by a doctor under a microscope and provable to any religion, needing no special knowledge; what is beyond reasoning and never-before-heard, harder than piercing a hair split into seven, is the impermanence seen only by wisdom (Dhp 277), which is the removing of self-view. Science merely re-names the same thing — four elements, electrons, quantum energy — a diversity of perception whose root is untouched, so the object's becoming never breaks and truth is never updated. Suppressing perception by meditation, even to the formless spheres, only prunes the tree, veiling how perception is built while the underlying tendency remains and resurfaces like a ball held underwater; consciousness can suppress the recognized thing but never escape recognizing by recognizing, so only seeing that recognizing cannot be done frees from the root. Finally, the dog takes its reflection for a dog by holding the perception as existent, and we hold "there is no dog" — both grasping a never-arisen perception, the two extremes of one perception, "I am not deceived" being a further knot; for this present is "real" only relative to "the dream is false," which is itself a perception, so both are mere perception, and perception can never find its own root.
