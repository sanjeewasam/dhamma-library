## Main dhamma points
- The continuation of the Silent Grove discourse, showing that "the world is like a dream" is only swapping one perception for another, that the base of both "real" and "false" is un-experienceable sleep, that this un-cognizable state is ignorance beyond any person, and that wisdom is the seeing of what consciousness can never know.
- **"The world is a dream" merely swaps perceptions.** Because "this is real" holds only against "the dream is false," we then say "this world too is like a dream" — but that only replaces the "world" perception with the "dream" perception, projecting onto the long-held "world" the same trick, so it is not discerning; watch how the perception is merely exchanged. The account is given to a thing never experienced: we call the un-experienceable "world," and give "the false" a name only relative to "the real," two names for one thing, the non-being of the "real"-becoming named "false." Both, seen rightly, are one coin held as two, and perception, however long pondered, can never find the root.
- **The base of both is un-experienceable sleep.** What underlies both "real" and "false" is *sleep* — one can never experience the dream *as* a dream while dreaming (that comes only on later reflection, *tattha tattha vipassati* being missed), so "dream" too is a name given to something un-experienceable; and only a wisdom toward *sleep* sees how the one thing is taken two ways, "there-is" and "there-is-not," that very seeing being the transcending of duality. Seeing *how* the two are built is itself the wisdom of non-building: seeing the arising rightly, cessation is realized within it — cessation being not a thing apart from arising, but the very relinquishing of arising. Doing this "within itself" is said to be harder than piercing a hair split into seven — the removing of *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*.
- **We live in a state un-cognizable to consciousness: ignorance.** The dog's bark, its bite, its being seen — all become "real" from a place not visible to us, un-cognizable to consciousness; the *sleep*-state (*nidi-bhava*) can never be experienced, being outside the range of the four (seen-heard-sensed-cognized), and it is precisely because we do not know that our feet stand in this un-cognizable state that whatever consciousness sees, hears or knows is taken as real. We name it "sleep" though none of the four reaches it, so a perception un-discernible is turned into something cognizable, veiling its un-cognizability. This not-knowing, in another word *avijjā*, can never be met by a person, since a "person" is only a *sakkāya* view arising with consciousness, and what is un-cognizable to consciousness can never be cognized by a consciousness-bound self — so long as that state stays un-cognizable, the taking of anything seen or known as real cannot stop.
- **Wisdom sees what knowing cannot; neither by knowing nor apart from it.** Because the not-knowing can never be thought or understood, the capacity to see what is un-cognizable to consciousness is called by a separate word, *paññā*: it transcends consciousness, which is only "knowing," so it is *seeing* (*passato*), not *knowing* (*jānato*) — the two so far apart, sky from earth, that a distinct word was needed. Yet it is not that *jānato* is discarded: "of one who knows and sees" means neither by knowing alone nor apart from it, and to say "then it must be done without knowing, in some other way" is another extreme, locking one up.
- **The middle is never reached by thinking the two sides; the axe and water.** As a tree falls when axe and water are applied — the "growing tall" being neither the axe nor the water, since neither contains "growing tall," nor caused by the two, nor apart from them, nor without them — so the cause is not the fruit and not other than it, a magic no thought can resolve. Whatever the two sides, consciousness cannot reach the middle by reasoning them; only seeing the flaw of both discloses it. (The segment breaks off as a further question on "knowing and the seen" is raised.)

## Summary

This continuation of the Silent Grove discourse shows that saying "the world is like a dream" merely swaps one perception for another: since "this is real" holds only against "the dream is false," we project onto the long-held "world" the same trick, replacing the "world" perception with the "dream" perception, which is not discerning but only exchanging perceptions. The account is given to a thing never experienced — the un-experienceable is called "world," and "the false" is named only relative to "the real," two names for one thing, so both are one coin held as two, and perception can never find its own root. The base of both is un-experienceable sleep: one can never experience the dream as a dream while dreaming, so "dream" too names something un-experienceable, and only a wisdom toward sleep sees how the one thing is taken as "there-is" and "there-is-not," that seeing being the transcending of duality — seeing how the two are built being itself the wisdom of non-building, cessation realized within the seeing of arising, harder than piercing a hair split into seven, the removing of self-view.

We live, the teacher presses, in a state un-cognizable to consciousness: the dog's bark and bite become real from a place not visible to us, the sleep-state never experienced, outside the four, and because we do not know our feet stand in this un-cognizable ground, whatever consciousness meets is taken as real — we name it "sleep" though none of the four reaches it, veiling its un-cognizability. This not-knowing is ignorance, never met by a person, since a person is only a self-view arising with consciousness, so what is un-cognizable to consciousness can never be cognized by a consciousness-bound self, and the taking of anything as real cannot stop while that state stays un-cognizable. Hence wisdom is a separate word — the capacity to see what knowing cannot, "seeing" not "knowing," neither by knowing alone nor apart from it, "it must be done without knowing" being another extreme. And the middle is never reached by thinking the two sides: as a tree falls by axe and water while "growing tall" is neither of them nor caused by them nor apart from them — a magic no thought resolves — so only seeing the flaw of both extremes discloses the middle.
