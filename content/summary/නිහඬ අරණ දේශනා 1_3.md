## Sutta referred to
- Not framed on a single named sutta, but several are named or quoted:
- **Kālakārāma Sutta** — named and paraphrased: "to tell the world 'this does not exist' would be my falsehood"; "the seen, heard, sensed, cognized — that I have specially known"; "yet the Blessed One does not draw near to it."
- Directly referred to, unattributed: *piṭṭhāda nositimi, vāyāmda nokaromi* ("neither standing nor striving" — the flood-crossing); *pubbe ananussutesu* (never heard before); *tattha tattha vipassati* (discern "there and there" — the Bhaddekaratta theme); the Buddha's image of consciousness as **a magician at the four-way crossroads**.
- Terms used: *viññāṇa* / *paññā* (consciousness / wisdom), *saññā* (perception), *phassa* / *passa* (contact), *aññamañña-paccaya* (mutual conditionality), *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen-heard-sensed-cognized), *nidi-bhava* (the sleep-state), *sakkāya*, *nāma-rūpa*, *jāty-andha* (born blind), *paṭiṭṭhāna* / *pratiṣṭhā* (footing/establishment). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.

## Main dhamma points
- The continuation of the Silent Grove discourse, on the mutual-conditioning knot of knowing and seeing that contact ties, why it cannot be untied by any of the four, how the Buddha neither accepts nor rejects the world, and why only wisdom — not consciousness — can see it, through the un-experienceable sleep-state.
- **Knowing and seeing are mutually conditioned; a knot no side unties.** "Seeing" is called real only because the thing is recognized, and the thing is confirmed only because it is seen, so neither leans to one side — the glass is real because seen and the seeing real because of the glass, like the hen and the egg, each answer looping back to the other. Recognition (*saññā*) itself cannot be denied (the glass *is* met), yet neither can be found self-standing; this very mutual knotting is the *viññāṇa*-knot, contact (*phassa*), and it cannot be escaped from the recognition-side. From no side of the seen-heard-sensed-cognized can it be untied — which is why the Buddha called consciousness a magician at the four-way crossroads, and why nibbāna is "the thing that is there yet cannot be understood."
- **First one must realize it cannot be resolved.** Consciousness always seeks a solution — "is it there because it is seen, or seen because it is there?" — wanting a footing (*pratiṣṭhā*) to settle on and be at peace, but no solution comes; the very seeking of a resting-place is the bondage, and freedom is precisely the mind's being *unable* to establish a footing. So one must first have it self-evident that this knot cannot be untied by understanding, that this impermanence does not loosen at the level one can grasp — hard as it is for consciousness, habituated to demanding a resolution, to bear "it cannot be understood."
- **The Buddha neither accepts nor rejects the world.** Confronted with "is the glass there?", the Buddha would neither accept it nor reject it: "there is no seeing" told to the world would be his falsehood (Kālakārāma), for while ignorance operates the world's account is 100% real to the world, and he has full comprehension of exactly how the world knows through the four — so he does not reject it, having no quarrel with the world, yet, knowing the whole system, does not draw near to it either. We cannot stay free of both, because we do not know the system, so we must either accept ("the glass is there") or reject ("it is not"), the moment of "not" being a rejection — whereas the way is *neither standing nor striving*.
- **Only wisdom, not consciousness, can see it.** "Seeing is name-and-form," never met by consciousness, must be seen by wisdom alone, for the seeing and the seen are real only because consciousness is real, and what consciousness cannot see, wisdom must; when one realizes that the knot cannot be untied by understanding, the *pubbe ananussutesu* quality dawns, and the very "who is the Buddha" — the Buddha-nature — begins to be felt, as one sees this loosens only from beyond the consciousness-level. The two errors, taking the glass as "there" and as "not there," are escaped only by seeing rightly the cause by which both are built.
- **The un-experienceable sleep-state; "dream is false" is only a name.** Calling the dream "false" is itself the base by which this waking is made "real," but "dream" names something un-experienceable — one never experiences the dream *as* a dream while dreaming, the recognition being complete in the moment and the "flaw" appearing only on later reflection, so *tattha tattha vipassati* is missed. The name "sleep" too is given to what none of the four can reach: we say "I slept twelve hours" though sleep was never experienced, describing an un-experienceable stretch not by knowing but by not-knowing. So it is by not knowing the sleep-state — not knowing that our feet stand in a state un-cognizable to consciousness — that whatever is seen and heard is taken as real; and holding "no-sleep" as the base is what makes this "real," relative to which the dream is named false. Seeing this, the taking of "known" as real goes hollow; but a born-blind man cannot even conceive "I do not see," having never experienced seeing, and we are likewise, so the comparison itself must be transcended — only beyond comparison is the recognizing rightly discerned. (The segment breaks off pressing that "sleep" is only the held "there is no sleep.")

## Summary

This continuation of the Silent Grove discourse takes up the "knowing versus seeing" question. "Seeing" is called real only because the thing is recognized, and the thing confirmed only because it is seen, so neither leans to one side — glass real because seen, seeing real because of the glass, like hen and egg, each answer looping back. Recognition cannot be denied, yet neither is found self-standing, and this mutual knotting is the *viññāṇa*-knot, contact, which no side of the seen-heard-sensed-cognized can untie — which is why the Buddha called consciousness a magician at the crossroads, and nibbāna "the thing there yet not understandable." First one must realize it cannot be resolved: consciousness always seeks a footing to settle on, but no solution comes, and the very seeking is the bondage, freedom being the mind's inability to establish a footing — hard as it is to bear "it cannot be understood." The Buddha neither accepts nor rejects the world: "there is no seeing" would be his falsehood, for the world's account is real to the world while ignorance operates, and he has full comprehension of how the world knows, so he does not reject it, yet does not draw near either; we cannot stay free of both, not knowing the system, so we accept or reject — whereas the way is neither standing nor striving.

Only wisdom, not consciousness, can see that "seeing is name-and-form," for the seeing and seen are real only because consciousness is real; when one realizes the knot cannot be untied by understanding, the never-before-heard quality dawns and the Buddha-nature begins to be felt, as this loosens only from beyond the consciousness-level, the two errors of "the glass is there" and "not there" being escaped only by seeing the cause of both. Calling the dream "false" is the base making this waking real, but "dream" names the un-experienceable — one never experiences the dream as a dream while dreaming, the recognition complete in the moment and its flaw seen only on reflection — and "sleep" too is given to what none of the four reaches, "I slept twelve hours" describing an un-experienceable stretch by not-knowing. So it is by not knowing that our feet stand in a state un-cognizable to consciousness that whatever is seen is taken as real, "no-sleep" held as the base against which the dream is named false; seeing this, the taking of "known" as real goes hollow — but a born-blind man cannot even conceive "I do not see," and we are likewise, so the comparison itself must be transcended, the recognizing rightly discerned only beyond comparison.
