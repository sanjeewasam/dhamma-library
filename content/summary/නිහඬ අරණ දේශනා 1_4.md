## Main dhamma points
- The continuation of the Silent Grove discourse, on the born-blind and un-experienceable sleep, why "the arisen ceases" keeps eternalism, why the middle is un-thinkable, and how a suffering un-cognizable to consciousness is seen only where arising and cessation are realized together.
- **The born-blind cannot conceive "I do not see"; we live in un-experienceable sleep.** A man born blind, never having met seeing, cannot even conceive the "non-being of seeing"; told "there are forms you cannot see," he only makes a second supposition, "non-seeing," on top of the first, "seeing" — both un-experienced. And we are the born-blind here: "sleep" is a state met by none of the four (seen-heard-sensed-cognized), never experienced, so "I slept" is spoken not by knowing but by not-knowing, holding "no-sleep" as the base by which this waking is made "real." Because we do not know our feet stand in a state un-cognizable to consciousness, "I am awake now" is hollow — this waking too a piece of the not-knowing.
- **"The arisen thing ceases" keeps eternalism; one perception arises, another ceases.** Taking "the very thing that arose ceases" means the one who was born lives on and dies, and the one who dies is reborn — an eternalist story; whereas Ven. Ñāṇananda's *aññam eva saññā uppajjati, aññam eva saññā nirujjhati* shows a *different* perception ceases, no mind ever seeing its own arising-and-ceasing. As "climbing" contains no "descending" and "descending" no "climbing," a rising and a falling can never be seen together; a "ceasing" is known only to a *later* mind, so "ceases" is itself another recognition. And the Buddha's "not the very dying one is reborn, yet not another either" cannot be reached by analysis, for holding "not this one" one slides to "then another," each attempt at release hooking one on the other side.
- **The middle is never reached by thinking the two sides.** These two — arisen and ceased, tall and short, waking and sleep — can never be thought into a middle, for consciousness stands only by holding two sides and demanding a footing; only when both are seen *together* does the middle find no footing, that very seeing being the wisdom by which the middle is let go. This is Satipaṭṭhāna's point: passing alone was enough to show, yet it adds "arising-and-passing together" — for only seeing both together discloses the flaw of both, the non-arising of the two extremes realized, within which the fabricating is seen *as* a non-fabricating, harder than piercing a hair split into seven.
- **A suffering un-cognizable to consciousness; and only a Buddha shows it.** We understand "arising" only at the level consciousness can grasp — a happened-to-us arising — and *that* very understanding is the suffering; only when it dawns that arising itself cannot be understood does *saṅkhāra-dukkha* become a knowledge, for the ageing-death-sorrow-lamentation any Muslim or Christian grasps is a consciousness-cognizable suffering, whereas the Buddha's is un-cognizable to consciousness, no object to any Brahmā, Māra, deva or human — which is exactly why "Buddha" is neither Māra nor Brahmā nor deva nor man, but the vision of what consciousness cannot cognize, the *Buddha*-knowledge. Seeing this, "Buddha," "Dhamma" and even "the four noble truths" fall away as names given only for the world's understanding; the four truths named as such are consciousness-cognizable, self-made, so if the four truths "make sense" to someone, that is the trouble — the Dhamma being called a raft precisely because to grasp it is to be caught, and it must be let go.
- **Light is only the un-arisen "no-darkness" named.** "This is light" is understood by conditioning on "there is no darkness," and to describe the light one must invoke an *absent* darkness — so it is a bowing to an un-met darkness, "clean" named only relative to absent dirt. Watch how perception deceives: "darkness" is a perception not now met, and holding its non-being one names the near side "light," which is not two perceptions but one thing named twice. Rightly seen, "there is no darkness" is itself un-arisen in this moment — *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ*, an idea "light" arising from a condition (the absence of darkness), *sabbaṁ taṁ*, all of it — so that watching how the "understanding" is built, one is thrown back to where one stands, and the whole "it is real" is seen to rest on a never-met condition.

## Summary

This continuation of the Silent Grove discourse presses the born-blind analogy: a man born blind cannot even conceive the non-being of seeing, only piling a second supposition, "non-seeing," on the first, and we are likewise, living in un-experienceable sleep — a state met by none of the four, so "I slept" is spoken by not-knowing, "no-sleep" held as the base that makes this waking "real," which is therefore hollow, a piece of the not-knowing. Taking "the arisen thing ceases" keeps eternalism — the born one lives, dies and is reborn — whereas *aññam eva saññā uppajjati, aññam eva saññā nirujjhati* shows a different perception ceases, no mind seeing its own arising-and-ceasing, "climbing" holding no "descending," and "ceases" being only a later recognition; the Buddha's "not the dying one, yet not another" cannot be reached by analysis, each release hooking one on the other side. The middle is never reached by thinking the two sides, consciousness standing only by holding two and demanding a footing; only seeing both together disquiets the middle, which is Satipaṭṭhāna's point in adding "arising-and-passing together," disclosing the non-arising of both extremes and the fabricating seen as a non-fabricating, harder than piercing a hair split into seven.

We understand "arising" only at the level consciousness can grasp, and that understanding is itself the suffering, so *saṅkhāra-dukkha* becomes a knowledge only when it dawns that arising cannot be understood — the ageing-and-death any religion grasps being consciousness-cognizable, the Buddha's suffering un-cognizable, no object to Brahmā, Māra, deva or man, which is why "Buddha" is the vision of what consciousness cannot cognize. Seeing this, "Buddha," "Dhamma" and even "the four noble truths" fall away as names for the world; the four truths named as such are self-made and consciousness-cognizable, so their "making sense" is the trouble, the Dhamma called a raft because to grasp it is to be caught and it must be let go. And "light" is only the un-arisen "no-darkness" named — described only by invoking an absent darkness, a bowing to what is not met, "clean" relative to absent dirt — one thing named twice, so that rightly seen "there is no darkness" is itself un-arisen now, an idea arising from a condition, and the whole "it is real" is seen to rest on a never-met condition.
