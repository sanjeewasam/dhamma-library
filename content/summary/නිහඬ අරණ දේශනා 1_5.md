## Sutta referred to
- Not framed on a single named sutta.
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed: *yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ, sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ* ("whatever is of a nature to arise, all that is of a nature to cease" — implied throughout the light/darkness analysis as "cause and fruit, a cessation-nature"); the flood-crossing image of *piṭṭhāda nosita, vāyāmda nokara* ("neither standing nor striving") and the letting-go of the near and far branches.
- Terms used: *samudaya* / *nirodha* (arising / cessation), *paccaya* / *prratya* (condition), *sākṣāt* (realization), *anicca* (impermanence), *nirāyāsa* (effortless), *kalyāṇa-mitta* (good friend), *diṭṭhi* (view). No sutta should be inferred.
- **Notes on the source:** the **short closing segment (Part 5)** of the **"Silent Grove" (Nihaṇ Araṇa) Discourse 1** (same discourse as the file `නිහඬ අරණ දේශනා 1`), a cleaner re-transcription of that discourse's conclusion, with audience exchange. It ends mid-flow.

## Main dhamma points
- The short closing of the Silent Grove discourse, on light as the fruit of an un-met darkness, on realizing cessation within arising, and on why the path is effortless and freed only through the good friend.
- **Light is the fruit of an un-met "no-darkness."** Darkness is the "cause," and "no-darkness" the fruit born of it — a cause and fruit that are a cessation-nature (*yaṁ kiñci samudaya-dhammaṁ, sabbaṁ taṁ nirodha-dhammaṁ*). The "darkness" condition never arose at all, yet from a never-arisen thing a meaning, "light," was given; so is that meaning an arisen thing, or itself un-arisen? Looked at, nothing there arose and nothing ceased — no light "arose and persisted"; not knowing the condition, we lent light a persistence, and seeking a persistence out of that lent persistence is what "seeing impermanence" wrongly means.
- **Cessation realized within arising; not a lost darkness or a gained light.** This persistence came only from not seeing the condition, so seeing the condition, the very persistence turns to non-arising: what one takes for "light" is only another reflection of the never-met darkness, its other face. We think "darkness went, light came," but seeing the true arising, one sees a *meeting made out of a never-met thing* — a meeting fabricated by not-knowing; and seeing the condition of that meeting, the condition's un-met-ness is realized, which is cessation. Neither a met thing nor a lost thing can be spoken of, so when meeting and losing are both realized as a non-being, birth and death are alike transcended.
- **Cessation is not the absence of arising, and does not fall to either view.** While the condition is un-met, the arising is real to the world's mind, so the Buddha does not deny it (he does not say "there is no arising"); yet that same wisdom sees the condition is never met, so cessation is realized. Because cessation is seen, one does not slide to "it exists"; because arising is seen, one does not slide to "it does not exist." A fruit *is* met when the cause is seen — yet what is there to accept, when no darkness is met to reject, and what to reject, when no light is met to accept? The account ends right there.
- **Effortless, and freed only through the good friend.** With nothing to stand on and nothing to strive for, the path is effortless — for a persistence, once established, would compel effort, and no footing means no striving. As in crossing a flood, fear comes only while a footing is still sought; when both the grasped branch and the branch stood on are gone, one asks "where am I now standing?" — and the realizing that one *cannot* set a foot down is what must dawn. But that very "now I understand there is nowhere to stand" is itself a subtle footing, a place consciousness settles as if "I see nibbāna"; seeing this discourse dissolves that address, though one cannot dissolve it oneself. Hence this frees only through association with the good friend, never by thinking it out — and seen rightly, the condition discloses only non-establishment. (The segment breaks off here.)

## Summary

This short closing of the Silent Grove discourse turns on light as the fruit of an un-met darkness. Darkness is the "cause" and "no-darkness" its fruit, a cessation-nature — yet the "darkness" condition never arose, and from a never-arisen thing the meaning "light" was given, so nothing there arose and nothing ceased; not knowing the condition, we lent light a persistence, and to seek a persistence out of that lent persistence is what "seeing impermanence" wrongly means. Seeing the condition, that persistence turns to non-arising: "light" is only another face of the never-met darkness, so that where we think "darkness went, light came," rightly seen there is a meeting fabricated of a never-met thing, and seeing that condition's un-met-ness is cessation — neither a met thing nor a lost thing sayable, so that when meeting and losing are both realized as a non-being, birth and death are transcended. While the condition is un-met the arising is real to the world's mind, so the Buddha does not deny it, yet the same wisdom sees the condition never met, so cessation is realized — seeing cessation, one does not slide to "it exists"; seeing arising, one does not slide to "it does not exist" — and there is nothing to accept, no darkness met to reject, nothing to reject, no light met to accept, the account ending there. With nothing to stand on and nothing to strive for, the path is effortless, as in a flood-crossing where fear comes only while a footing is sought and, both branches gone, one asks where one stands — the realizing that one cannot set a foot down being what must dawn. But "now I understand there is nowhere to stand" is itself a subtle footing consciousness settles on as "I see nibbāna," which this discourse dissolves though one cannot dissolve it oneself — so this frees only through the good friend, never by thinking, and seen rightly the condition discloses only non-establishment.
