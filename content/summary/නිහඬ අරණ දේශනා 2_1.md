## Sutta referred to
- Not framed on a single named sutta, but several are quoted:
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ* ("consciousness is like a magic show / illusion" — the Pheṇa-piṇḍūpama, SN 22.95); *abhūtaṁ* / "the un-become" (*ahutvā sambhūtaṁ* — "having not been, it comes to be," and *bhavissati*); *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* ("with ignorance as condition, formations"); the Buddha's image of consciousness as **a magician at the four-way crossroads**.
- Directly referred to, unattributed: *kalyāṇa-mitta-sevana* — "the Sāsana rests 100% on the good friend"; *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen-heard-sensed-cognized); *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* (announced as the topic).
- Terms used: *viññāṇa* / *māyā* / *paññā* (consciousness / illusion / wisdom), *manasikāra* / *yoniso-manasikāra* / *ayoniso-manasikāra* (attention / wise / unwise attention), *upekkhā* (equanimity), *saññā*, *āyatana* (sense-base), *pañca-upādāna-skandha*, *phassa* / *sparśa* (contact), *nidi-bhava* (the sleep-state), *avijjā*, *sati* / *asati* (mindfulness / un-mindfulness). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **opening segment (Part 1)** of a **new formal discourse, "Silent Grove" (Nihaṇ Araṇa) Discourse 2** — permission taken from senior monks (the "great venerable," and the nuns), an intended talk on *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* that had been deferred from the previous day. Speaker-labelling is absent for most of this segment (a near-continuous monologue).

## Main dhamma points
- The opening of the second Silent Grove discourse, on why consciousness can never cognize the true nature (it *is* the illusion), why the "mindfulness" we cultivate is caught in that illusion, and why our real state is an un-cognizable, sleep-like equanimity veiled by consciousness's attention.
- **Consciousness is illusion, so no attention can catch reality; hence the good friend.** We try to do wise attention *with* consciousness, but the Buddha said *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ* — consciousness is like a conjuror's show — so no attention can ever catch the true nature, since everything consciousness displays is shown as a magic; the true nature can never be brought to light by a consciousness. This is why it cannot be got by reading or by anyone's mere say-so, and why "do not take it just because it is written, or because someone said it" is taught, the whole vision advancing only through a good friend — the Sāsana resting 100% on association with the wise.
- **The "mindfulness" we cultivate is caught in the illusion.** When it is clear that everything consciousness shows is magic, one sees that even the "wise attention" we try to keep belongs to *unwise* attention: holding an object and establishing mindfulness is one thing, and a mindfulness that stands where no object can be grasped is another, and seeing that difference shows the mindfulness we keep to be itself caught in consciousness's illusion — a "mindfulness" defined inside the magic. What consciousness displays we take as an object and, thinking we have understood it, call the resulting construct "wise attention," "this is its reality"; but that understanding is only a shift from one perception to another — as re-describing a chair as "four great elements" or "pure octads" is only another consciousness-built trap swapped for the chair, useful for analysis yet still a magic.
- **Our real nature is the un-understood; understanding comes only with thinking.** Ordinarily no nose, no breath, no body is understood at any moment without thinking; we live mostly in the *un*-understood, which is our very equanimity-level, un-cognizable, so that any action, thought of, is understood — and *that* thinking we define as "mindfulness." Breath is felt only with a condition, nose-and-breath together, and once felt cannot be denied; but this is a fabricating, for a body has never actually met a breath — "breath felt by the body" is never met by the body itself, and neither the body, nor the breath, nor their contact has ever really occurred, which is the *un-become* (*abhūtaṁ*, *ahutvā sambhūtaṁ*). One who sees equanimity rightly sees this non-being; but consciousness presents the three — an un-met body, an un-met breath, and a feeling born of their contact — as though the body had experienced it, "and now I feel it because my mindfulness was present," which is like waking and recounting a dream-dog: taking a never-experienced thing as experienced, then later calling it false, while the felt experience *at the moment* seems fully real.
- **Half the round is always un-understood; "sleep" names the un-cognizable.** Of a full day's 360 degrees, only 180 are ever understood; the other 180 are never understood, and we name the understood part "mindfulness" and the un-understood part "un-mindfulness" — yet even the "mindfulness" is only the un-met turned into a "known" in the moment of thinking, as we translate the un-cognizable state we sat in (like sleep) into a "mindfulness" while thinking. Sleep is the plain example: the four — seen, heard, sensed, cognized — do not operate there, so "am I, am I not" cannot arise and even the word "understand" cannot be applied; because we do not know that our feet stand in this un-cognizable, sleep-like state, the attention shown in a dream is all taken as "there is," and it is by not knowing this that whatever is seen is taken as real. When one sees that "the four operating where the four do not operate" is the flaw, *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* is realized there.
- **Only wisdom, transcending consciousness, sees the crossroads-magic; contact never cognized.** A nature in which the four do not operate can never be cognized *by* the four, and since the four are all we have to grasp with, this is exactly "the magic at the four-way crossroads" — from whichever of the four one looks, it is precisely the four that are not operating, the un-cognizable side. So this cannot be a consciousness-cognizable account, and only a wisdom transcending consciousness — *paññā* — can see the un-cognizable side; only there is the real meaning of "consciousness is illusion" directly realized. Nose and breath, once understood, are each grasped as a clinging-aggregate, so however one analyzes the built pair, contact is never cognized — for contact arose conditioned by the very not-knowing, and had the vision "a breath can never be met by this body" arisen, no "thinking" would occur; it is the un-cognizable not-knowing, veiled by consciousness's own attention as "sleep," that feeds consciousness its footing, so the way to that nature is closed precisely by this magic. (The segment breaks off restating that this is what blocks the way to that nature.)

## Summary

This opening of the second Silent Grove discourse turns on why consciousness can never cognize the true nature: we try to do wise attention with consciousness, but *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ* — consciousness is a conjuror's show — so everything it displays is magic, and no attention can catch reality; hence it cannot be got from books or hearsay, only through a good friend, the Sāsana resting 100% on association with the wise. Seen thus, even the "mindfulness" we cultivate belongs to unwise attention: holding an object is one thing, a mindfulness standing where no object can be grasped another, and the mindfulness we keep is itself caught in the illusion, a construct we take as "its reality" — only a shift from one perception to another, as re-describing a chair as "four elements" is another consciousness-built trap. Our real nature is the un-understood: no nose or breath is understood without thinking, and we live mostly in that un-cognizable equanimity, so "understanding" comes only when we think, and that thinking we call "mindfulness." Breath is felt only with a condition, yet a body has never met a breath, and neither body nor breath nor their contact ever really occurred — the un-become — so consciousness presents the three as if experienced, "felt because my mindfulness was present," like waking to recount a dream-dog, the felt experience seeming real at the moment.

Of a day's 360 degrees only 180 are ever understood, the teacher presses, and we name the understood "mindfulness" and the rest "un-mindfulness," though even the "mindfulness" is the un-met turned into a "known" while thinking, translating the un-cognizable sleep-like state into a "mindfulness." Sleep is the example: the four do not operate there, so "am I or not" cannot arise and "understand" cannot even be applied; because we do not know our feet stand in this un-cognizable state, the dream's attention is all taken as "there is," and by not knowing this whatever is seen is taken as real — and when one sees that "the four operating where the four do not operate" is the flaw, *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* is realized. A nature in which the four do not operate can never be cognized by the four, and since the four are all we grasp with, this is the magic at the four-way crossroads — from whichever of the four one looks, it is the four that are not operating — so only a wisdom transcending consciousness can see the un-cognizable side, and only there is "consciousness is illusion" directly realized. Nose and breath, once understood, are each grasped as a clinging-aggregate, so contact is never cognized, having arisen conditioned by the very not-knowing; had the vision "a breath can never be met by this body" arisen, no thinking would occur, for it is the un-cognizable not-knowing, veiled by consciousness's attention as "sleep," that feeds consciousness its footing and closes the way to that nature. (This is Part 1 of "Silent Grove Discourse 2," on *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*.)
