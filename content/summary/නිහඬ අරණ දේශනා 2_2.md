## Sutta referred to
- Named explicitly: **Kālakārāma Sutta** ("as the Buddha says in the Kālakārāma Sutta" — that he does not deny [the world], because he knows the arising); **the Majjhima Sutta** (as cited in the talk): "contact [*phassa*] is one end, the arising of contact [*phassa-samudaya*] is one end, the cessation of contact [*phassa-nirodha*] is the middle."
- Directly referred to, unattributed by title: the **Satipaṭṭhāna** phrasing *samudaya-dhammānupassī* / *vaya-dhammānupassī* (contemplating the nature of arising / of passing away, and the two seen together); the **two-reed-bundles** simile (*naḷakalāpī* — two sheaves standing only by leaning on each other, alluded to as "the two reed-bundles" and compared to the fire-drill / matchbox image).
- Terms used: *paññā* / *ñāṇa* (wisdom / knowledge), *viññāṇa* (consciousness), *diṭṭhi* (view), *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* (the meeting of the three is contact), *sakkāya* (the self-identity that is "born"), *phassa* / *sparśa* (contact), *samudaya* / *nirodha* (arising / cessation), *anuppāda* / *anutpāda* (non-arising), *ātma-saññā* / *anatta* (self-perception / not-self), *sassata* (eternalism), *anta* (extreme). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **second segment (Part 2)** of **"Silent Grove" (Nihaṇ Araṇa) Discourse 2**, continuing directly from Part 1. This segment is a genuine dialogue — the teacher and at least one venerable questioner exchange, and a physical prop (two reed-bundles / sticks) is handled in the room ("this one," "that one"), so some deixis is unrecoverable in text.

## Main dhamma points
- The second segment of the second Silent Grove discourse, on wisdom as nothing but the capacity to recognize defilement, on "taking-as-existing" being the view that is *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, and on why the real cause of the leaning pair is found in neither side — so that arising itself is realized as non-arising.
- **Wisdom is not a separate thing; it is only the recognizing of defilement as defilement.** Because we do not recognize the "filth" (*kuṇu*, the defiling views) consciousness builds, we consort with it and see no fault in it, so that nature never comes near; seeing the filth *as* filth is what purity is. Wisdom is precisely the capacity to recognize these defilements — not a separate "purity" or "knowledge" set up alongside them, for a purity held as a separate thing becomes itself another defilement, and a "knowledge" held as a separate thing becomes one more thing that exists. To recognize the lie *as* a lie is the truth; taking the lie as truth *is* the not-recognizing, and one who sees the lie as a lie thereby knows the truth — the very ability to know the false as false is what "truth" names, not a truth kept apart.
- **"Taking-as-existing" is the view — and that view is the self.** When feeling is examined, it is never felt bare but always with two sides — "there is a nose, there is a breath" — and *there*, right in the feeling, the self (*sakkāya*) is born together with the felt. There is a gulf between "it exists" (*tiyenawā*) and "taking it as existing" (*tiyena bavaṭa gannawā*): feeling arises not because these things exist but from the *view* that takes them as existing; the taking-as-existing is itself eternalism, an "is-ness" superimposed, and because we cannot see this view as a view we take what the view displays as simply "there." To recognize *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, then, is to see "I was born" — for the meeting of the three is exactly this view, and seeing it *as* a view is release from the "it exists"; not seeing it as a view, the "is-ness" is confirmed 100%.
- **The dream-dog: two sides make contact real, so contact is never cognized.** If a dog appears, we say "it is seen because the dog is there and the eye is there" — but who would believe the waking dog is as much a dream as the sleeping one? Consciousness always makes the contact real from the two sides: the dog is real from the eye's side, the eye real from the dog's side, each confirming the other, so the contact can never be shown false, since it never appears alone but only with the two. The two sides being displayed, contact is *veiled*; contact is un-cognizable to consciousness, and it is precisely because contact does not fall within consciousness that the two sides it generates become "real." From these two made-real sides the contact can never be seized — the knot is already tied — and all that is left to think about is the two sides ("did the eye arise because of the dog, or the dog appear because of the eye?"), the very predicament of the magic at the crossroads.
- **"Here and there": we only ever meet "there," never "here."** As in another discourse heard that day, we never meet "here" — only "there": the face is met in the mirror over there, the hand looked at is seen over there, not here. Logically neither "here" nor "there" could stand without the other; "there is over there because I am here." But if one sees the cause by which both "here" and "there" are made, that cause is never found from the two sides — only the two sides, with the cause hidden, are ever met; and falling into this knot is again the crossroads-magic, for however much one analyzes, no fault appears, since two sides stand conditioning each other.
- **The two reed-bundles: the cause of the leaning pair is in neither.** As with two reed-bundles that stand only by leaning (like the fire-drill), asked "why does this one stand?" the answer points to the other, and asked "why the other?" it points back — a lovely mutual existence is built, yet the cause by which that existence is built is in *neither*, so the middle can never be reached from the two sides. What lent the pair its persistence is not found in either, and seeing that the true cause of an existence is never met in the two things it is built of, that very persistence is realized as a non-persistence — existence itself as non-existence. This is why seeing the self-perception (*ātma-saññā*) rightly is itself *anatta*: not a separate self-perception negated by a separate not-self (which would only build two more extremes), for knowledge is the falling-away of both extremes.
- **The real cause is un-findable by thought, so non-arising is realized; and arising and cessation must be seen together.** The cause by which the pair persists cannot be found by saying "both exist" (another thing is then shown) nor by saying "both are absent" (no answer comes either), because the true cause of the "two-sided talk" cannot be found within that talk at all — so no consciousness can think it out. Seeing the real cause, the built existence is realized as non-existence; the cause is seen to be *un-arisen*, and the realizing of the un-arisen is *anuppāda* (non-arising) — un-cognizable to consciousness precisely because it never arose. Thus, when the cause (the *samudaya*) is rightly seen, both "a thing that was, now gone" and "a thing that never was" fall away, and cessation is realized *within* the very seeing of arising: as in Satipaṭṭhāna's *samudaya-dhammānupassī* and *vaya-dhammānupassī* held together (not a third step "stop at passing-away = cessation"), for taken apart they yield only "it is, then it is not," while both seen at once cross beyond both. As the Buddha says in the Kālakārāma Sutta, he does not say "there is not," because he knows the arising — "by such a contact such an existence is built, that is the arising, by conditions this is constructed" — yet the wisdom that sees cessation does not say "there is" either; and the Majjhima Sutta's "contact one end, the arising of contact one end, the cessation of contact the middle" shows both extremes, contact itself being one end. (The dialogue continues, still handling the two reed-bundles.)

## Summary

This second segment of the second Silent Grove discourse opens by fixing what wisdom is: not a separate thing but only the capacity to recognize the "filth" — the defiling views consciousness builds — as filth; a "purity" or a "knowledge" set up alongside them would itself become one more thing that exists, so to recognize the lie as a lie *is* the truth, taking the lie as truth being the very not-recognizing. It then turns on "taking-as-existing." Feeling is never felt bare but always with two sides — "there is a nose, there is a breath" — and there, in the feeling, the self is born with the felt; there is a gulf between "it exists" and "taking it as existing," for feeling arises not from the things but from the view that takes them as existing, a superimposed "is-ness" we cannot see as a view. So to recognize *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* is to see "I was born" — the meeting of the three is this very view, and seeing it as a view is release, not seeing it confirming the "is-ness" 100%. The dream-dog shows why: consciousness makes the contact real from both sides, the dog real from the eye's side and the eye from the dog's, so contact never appears alone and can never be shown false; contact is un-cognizable, and it is because it does not fall within consciousness that the two sides become "real," leaving only the two sides to think about — the crossroads-magic.

The teacher presses the point with "here and there": we only ever meet "there," never "here" — the face in the mirror, the hand seen over there — and though neither could stand without the other, the cause by which both are made is never found from the two sides. So too the two reed-bundles that stand only by leaning: asked why each stands, the answer points to the other, a mutual existence built, yet the cause of that existence is in neither, and the middle is never reached from the two sides; seeing that the true cause of an existence is never met in the two things it is built of, that persistence is realized as non-persistence — existence as non-existence. This is why seeing self-perception rightly is itself not-self, not a separate not-self negating a separate self, for knowledge is the falling-away of both extremes. The real cause cannot be found by "both exist" nor by "both absent," because it lies outside the two-sided talk entirely and no consciousness can think it out; seeing it, the built existence is realized as non-existence, the cause seen as un-arisen, and the realizing of the un-arisen is *anuppāda*, un-cognizable precisely because it never arose. Hence when the arising is rightly seen, both "a thing now gone" and "a thing that never was" fall away, and cessation is realized within the seeing of arising — Satipaṭṭhāna's *samudaya-* and *vaya-dhammānupassī* held together, not split into "it is, then it is not." As the Kālakārāma Sutta has it, the Buddha does not say "there is not," for he knows the arising — "by such a contact such an existence is built" — yet the wisdom seeing cessation does not say "there is" either; and the Majjhima Sutta's "contact one end, its arising one end, its cessation the middle" shows contact itself as an extreme.
