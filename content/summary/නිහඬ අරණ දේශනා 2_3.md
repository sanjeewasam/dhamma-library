## Sutta referred to
- Named explicitly: **Māluṅkyaputta** (Ven. Māluṅkyaputta) — the Buddha's questioning "toward what was not seen in the past, is not seen now, and will not be seen (nor expected to be seen) in the future, can lust or hate arise?" (the *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* teaching, SN 35.95); the **tetralemma on the Tathāgata after death** — a brahmin asks whether the Tathāgata exists after death / does not / both / neither, and the Buddha replies each time "the question is wrong," which the worldling hears as evasion ("a body-saving cunning answer"); the cryptic "**not the one who dies, yet not another**" (asked "is it the same one who dies that is reborn?").
- Named, with a transcription uncertainty: a sutta given as **"Kāraṇā Sutta"** — "one does not conceive a seen thing apart from seeing" (*na diṭṭhe diṭṭhamattaṁ maññati* — the phrasing is the **Kālakārāma / Bāhiya** formula; the title as transcribed is uncertain and should not be relied on).
- Referred to, unattributed by title: the **Satipaṭṭhāna** *samudaya-dhammānupassī* / *vaya-dhammānupassī* (the two seen together); the dependent-origination formula seen *anuloma* (forward) and *paṭiloma* (reverse); the "**seven existences**" to arahantship (a stream-enterer's at-most-seven-lives).
- Terms used: *phassa* / *sparśa* (contact), *phassa-samudaya* / *phassa-nirodha* (arising / cessation of contact), *anta* (extreme), *anuppāda-nirodha* / *anutpāda* (non-arising cessation), *hetu-phala* (cause-and-fruit), *viññāṇa-agocara* (not within consciousness's range), *paññā* (wisdom), *cakkhu-viññāṇa* (eye-consciousness), *āyatana* (sense-base), *paṭiṭṭhita* (established). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named, and "Kāraṇā Sutta" is flagged as uncertain.
- **Notes on the source:** the **third segment (Part 3)** of **"Silent Grove" (Nihaṇ Araṇa) Discourse 2**, a continued dialogue. A physical prop (the two reed-bundles / a knotted cord, later "2 and 3" written down) is handled in the room, so some deixis is unrecoverable. The segment ends mid-example.

## Main dhamma points
- The third segment of the second Silent Grove discourse, working the tetralemma of contact — not from the two, not without the two, not in both, not apart — showing every corner to be a "defilement" (extreme), so that contact is un-cognizable to consciousness and realized only by wisdom, within which existence itself shows as non-existence.
- **The cause has no birth to point to, so both birth and death become un-understandable.** Asked whether the "cause" was born from this reed-bundle or that, no birth can be shown for it; and seeing the real birth is exactly seeing that birth cannot be understood by anyone. We understand death only because we understand birth, so when the true cause is seen, one arrives where *both* birth and death cannot be understood.
- **All four corners of the tetralemma are wrong.** The real cause (contact) cannot be said to have been "in this" or "in this," so taking the two as *existing* slides to the "it exists" extreme (eternalism); yet removing both — "neither has it, so there is no cause / no contact" — slides to the "it does not exist" extreme (annihilation), for a cause standing free of both would have to hang in empty space; and concluding "no two extremes, therefore no middle" is as different from *seeing the middle as the middle* as sky from earth. Consciousness cannot tell these apart — to it, "neither this side nor that, so where is a middle?" is simply logical — which is exactly how "there is no contact" is arrived at, and why wisdom never settles on the real contact. Even the "fifty-fifty / neither-nor" answer is itself another defilement — the brahmin's tetralemma about the Tathāgata after death (exists / not / both / neither), to each of which the Buddha said "the question is wrong," and the "not the dying one, yet not another" — showing contact is neither *from* these conditions nor *without* them.
- **Contact is un-cognizable; hence it seems "evasive," and jams thought at the two sides.** Because the six bases always display a "because-of" (the condition), we only ever chew on the two strand-ends and never on the knot itself — as with a knot tied in a cord, "knot" is not either end, but the joining of the two, and yet the "joining" is found nowhere. To the worldling the Buddha's refusals sound like a cunning, body-saving evasion, precisely because consciousness, meeting the real truth, rejects it as "cunning" and settles back into the lie, refusing to let go of existence. What jams thought is not a fault to be fixed but the very sign that one is nearing the un-thinkable; the way there is that a fault appear at *every* place thinking lands — that is what wisdom is — until one is brought to where thinking cannot go.
- **Contact seen by contact is cessation; not seen, arising hardens into an extreme.** When the two are seen not to be met from either side, dwelling neither in "it is" nor "it is not," the mind that swings between the two comes to rest, and the wisdom that sees the middle as the middle arises. Seeing contact by contact, one sees that within contact the conditions do not arise (their non-arising) — and that is the cessation of contact; the very same seeing also shows that it is *by not seeing contact* that the conditions get built, so arising and cessation stand disclosed in one place, not as two separate inquiries. If wisdom is absent, the arising becomes a real arising, an extreme; taking "these two conditions alone produce it" makes *phassa-samudaya* an extreme, taking "contact stands apart, free of the two" makes contact an extreme, and taking "the two are simply absent" makes annihilation an extreme — every corner a defilement.
- **Un-cognizable to consciousness, so realized only by wisdom; it cannot be rushed.** Because the un-met cause does not *exist*, it does not fall within consciousness's range, so it can never be thought out — asked to "show me the cause," one can point to nothing, though at a glance the two seem plainly there; existence appears, and appears not to have existed at all, which is what "seeing non-existence within existence" means, harder than piercing one horsehair with another. Only the wisdom that settles at the un-thinkable sees the fault on both sides; and precisely because it cannot be reasoned month by month into stream-entry and once-returning, the Buddha spoke of up to seven existences. This is what "cause-and-fruit seen forward and backward, both together" means — the Satipaṭṭhāna *samudaya-* and *vaya-dhammānupassī* held as one — a fruit met from a cause that is never met. (The segment ends beginning a fresh illustration: writing "2 and 3," from which nothing beyond "2 and 3" can be received — a lead-in to showing how something un-thinkable arises, as eye-and-form are said to produce eye-consciousness while really only a knot is shown.)

## Summary

This third segment presses the tetralemma of contact. Asked whether the "cause" was born from this reed-bundle or that, no birth can be pointed to — and seeing the real birth is precisely seeing that birth cannot be understood, so that when the true cause is seen one arrives where both birth and death become un-understandable. Every corner is then closed off: taking the two conditions as existing slides to eternalism; removing both — "neither has it, so there is no contact" — slides to annihilation, since a cause free of both would hang in empty space; and "no two extremes, therefore no middle" is as far from seeing the middle as the middle as sky from earth. Consciousness cannot tell these apart, which is how "there is no contact" gets reached and why wisdom never settles on real contact; even the "fifty-fifty / neither-nor" answer is one more defilement — as in the brahmin's four questions about the Tathāgata after death, to each of which the Buddha said "the question is wrong," and the "not the dying one, yet not another." Because the six bases always display a "because-of," we chew only on the two strand-ends, never the knot: as "knot" is neither end but the joining of the two, and yet the joining is nowhere. To the worldling the Buddha's refusals sound like evasion, precisely because consciousness meets the truth and rejects it as "cunning," settling back into the lie; the jamming of thought is not a fault but the sign of nearing the un-thinkable, reached only when a fault appears at every place thinking lands — which is what wisdom is.

Seeing the two as un-met from either side, dwelling neither in "it is" nor "it is not," the swinging mind rests and the wisdom that sees the middle as the middle arises. Seeing contact by contact, one sees that within contact the conditions do not arise — their non-arising, the cessation of contact — while the same seeing shows that it is by *not* seeing contact that the conditions are built, so arising and cessation stand disclosed in one place, not two inquiries. Absent wisdom, arising hardens into an extreme: "these two alone produce it" makes the arising an extreme, "contact stands apart" makes contact an extreme, "the two are simply absent" makes annihilation an extreme — every corner a defilement. Because the un-met cause does not exist, it is outside consciousness's range and can never be thought out; asked to "show the cause," one points to nothing, though the two seem plainly there — existence appears, and appears never to have existed, which is "seeing non-existence within existence," harder than piercing one horsehair with another. Only the wisdom settling at the un-thinkable sees the fault on both sides, and because it cannot be reasoned month by month into the fruits, the Buddha spoke of up to seven existences — cause-and-fruit seen forward and backward together, Satipaṭṭhāna's *samudaya-* and *vaya-dhammānupassī* as one, a fruit met from a cause never met. (This is Part 3 of "Silent Grove Discourse 2"; it ends beginning a fresh "2 and 3" illustration, leading into eye-and-form producing eye-consciousness as really only a knot.)
