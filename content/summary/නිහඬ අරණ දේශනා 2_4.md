## Sutta referred to
- Named explicitly: **Māluṅkyaputta** (Ven. Māluṅkyaputta) — "not seen in the past, not seen now, not to be seen in the future," in support of the point that the word *anuppāda* (non-arising) is used precisely because the thing never arose at all (the *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* teaching, SN 35.95).
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: *evaṁ dhammo, na-y-idaṁ dhammo duppaññassa* ("such is the Dhamma; this Dhamma is not for one of poor wisdom" — the Buddha's declaration that this is hard to see, not attainable by the unwise); *pubbe ananussutesu dhammesu* ("among things not heard before" — the Dhammacakkappavattana phrase, that Buddha-knowledge is un-matchable by any deva or brahmā); *bhāvitā bahulīkatā* ("cultivated and made much of"); *paṭisotagāmī* ("going against the stream").
- Terms used: *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* (the meeting of the three is contact), *phassa* (contact), *samudaya* / *nirodha* / *anuppāda-nirodha* (arising / cessation / non-arising cessation), *cakkhu-viññāṇa* (eye-consciousness), *paccakkha* / *sākṣāt* (direct realization), *vipassanā* / *vidarśanā*, *nirāyāsa* (effortless), *paccaya* / *prratya* (condition), *viññāṇa-agocara* (not within consciousness's range), *vīriya* (energy). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **fourth and closing segment (Part 4)** of **"Silent Grove" (Nihaṇ Araṇa) Discourse 2**, a continued dialogue. A worked "2 and 3" arithmetic prop is handled and completed here; the segment concludes cleanly and ties the whole discourse back to the light/darkness analysis of Discourse 1.

## Main dhamma points
- The closing segment of the second Silent Grove discourse, completing the "2 + 3 = 5" illustration to show a fruit born of a meaning-less cause, insisting cessation is realized only within arising, defending non-arising against annihilationism, and folding the whole back into the light/darkness analysis: "light" is only the naming of a never-arisen darkness's absence.
- **A fruit from a meaning-less cause: "five" comes from neither side nor from the joining.** Five is not got from the two, nor from the three, nor is the "+" (the joining) itself equal to five — yet without the joining there is no five; so the joining that produces five has no meaning of its own ("addition" is not "five," and is nowhere in "two" or in "three"), and a meaning arising from a meaning-less cause is exactly a magic, like a magician drawing a dove from a handkerchief. This is precisely *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*: we say "eye-and-form" hold no seeing, yet "when the two meet, there it is" — and taking "it happened because of the meeting" is the very knot we fall into. A cause that cannot be given any meaning yields the "meaning" five, so cause-and-fruit *do not stand* (they find no footing) — which is where one must arrive.
- **Cessation is realized only within arising; you cannot set the knot aside.** One must take up (own) the very knot one has fallen into and untie *that* — not fetch a different knot, nor climb out by a different well than the one fallen into. So one cannot untie "eye-and-form produce seeing" by merely declaring it false and laying it aside to seek a separate cessation; not even a Buddha can show a cessation without first showing an arising, which is why "seeing the arising, cessation is realized" — a separate *nibbāna* sought by removing *samudaya* is the easy, wrong path. Seeing arising and passing together is what is so hard, harder than the split "it arose, then it ceased."
- **Non-arising is not annihilation; hence the very word *anuppāda*.** "The arisen ceases without remainder" is a worldly, consciousness-level account — a Buddha need not arise across countless aeons of perfections to announce that "what is born dies"; that is plain. The word *anuppāda* (non-arising) was given precisely because this never arose at all, and as told to Māluṅkyaputta — not seen in past, present, or future — one cannot say of such a thing "the arisen ceased without remainder"; to do so is a flat contradiction, a concession left to the world.
- **"Not for the unwise" is a bringing-down-to-earth, not scorn; then rouse energy.** The Buddha's *evaṁ dhammo, na-y-idaṁ dhammo duppaññassa* — declaring 2500 years ago that this cannot be grasped by poor wisdom — is not a rebuke but a bringing of us to earth: the first thing to be understood is that our own dull mind cannot grasp it (this is what the "fifty-fifty" talk really shows — our incapacity), and seeing that we are simply unskilled at this is enough. That recognition is what rouses the energy (*vīriya*) to get free; and it is *there* that one grasps what a Buddha is — the *pubbe ananussutesu*, the never-before-heard — for any worldly field, science, medicine, engineering, can be mastered and brought to certainty by knowledge, whereas the place the Buddha points to is where knowledge reaches zero, un-matchable by any deva or brahmā, because it does not come from knowledge at all.
- **The practice: cultivate and make much of, going against the stream.** To the question "if it is un-thinkable, is there a path?" — a path there must be, or it could be built by any mind from a book. The answer is not "however much you look, that is all," which would stop inquiry; the Buddha said *bhāvitā bahulīkatā*, cultivate and make much of it, because it is by looking and looking that direct realization is built. This is *paṭisotagāmī*, going against the stream: not "seeing appears because eye and form are there," but turning to look the other way — "even when eye and form are present, does seeing appear?" — hard to do. As a tree takes in carbon dioxide and releases oxygen without knowing how it does so — an effortless (*nirāyāsa*) nature to which one cannot put a "method" — so one cannot ask an effortless nature for its technique; one only contemplates the non-arisen conditionlessness of this very moment.
- **Folding back into light/darkness: "light" names a never-arisen darkness's absence.** What was compared to all this is *light*: the answer we reached — "the absence of a non-arisen thing" — is exactly what "light" is, differing only in the word; one who sees the condition contemplates "the absence of a never-arisen darkness." Can one even think "the absence of a non-arisen thing"? It is like seeking the surname of an unborn child: of a child not yet born one can say neither "the child exists" nor "the child does not," because talk of a "child" begins only with birth. To name a non-arisen darkness "light" — is that name a born thing or an unborn thing? Seeing the condition rightly, one sees the condition is non-arisen, and what we took as "the arising of light" is the absence of a non-arisen condition — so the "because-of" knot we build joins a *non-arisen* condition, and from a non-arisen condition no arising can ever be got. This is exactly why light (and so eye-consciousness, and contact) is un-cognizable to consciousness: consciousness named a non-arisen condition "light." Seeing this, even in this present moment non-arising itself is realized — and the discourse closes there.
