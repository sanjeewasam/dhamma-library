## Main dhamma points
- The opening of the Kandy lay discussion, laying the ground: seeing is beyond knowing, the path is effortless, both question and answer are faulty, and re-understanding the "self" as *nāma-rūpa* only swaps a gross self for a subtle one — the release lies in reaching the un-cognizable, as one must wake to see a dream is false.
- **Seeing is beyond knowing; hence the rarity, and the "beyond all religions."** There are two things — understanding/knowing and wisdom, *jānato* and *passato*, knowing and seeing — and the world lives at the level of *knowing*, where all worldly subjects and learning sit, the level the mind (consciousness) can reach. What a Buddha shows must be beyond that understood level: as the Buddha said, those who see are like the dust on a fingernail, those who cannot like the dust of the whole great earth — showing how exceedingly rare it is. This is why it is *pubbe ananussuta*, never heard before, surpassing every religion: all faiths can be understood up to their highest point, but this points past the very limit of understanding, which must be crossed to make an end of *saṁsāra*.
- **The path is effortless; drop "I must do something."** We think, as with a degree or a job, that we must *do* something over time to get a result, and so approach this backwards, starting with effort (*āyāsa*) — planting our feet the wrong way round, from the unclear. But the Buddha's basic point is that this path is *nirāyāsa*, effortless, wholly contrary to the striving we bring; only when its effortlessness is at least sensed does the very nature of the path begin to dawn. We treat it as a lesson to be understood — hearing suttas, understanding them, as if studying for an exam one could pass with long and broad answers — but a question arising and an answer given are *both* signs of a problem.
- **Both question and answer are faulty; get free of the question, not answer it.** When a question arises it is because its root was not seen, and whoever answers it has likewise not seen the root, so the two merely "balance out" — "my question got an answer, now I understand" — while the Buddha shows the fault in both. As a lion bites the source of the thrown stone while a dog chases the stone, one who has seen strikes at the root; one who has not answers the root-blind question. So the aim is not to answer these questions but to become free of the question itself — and seeing that root is where the effortlessness begins to be felt, where it dawns that there is nothing to *do* on this path.
- **The tetralemma: neither "it is" nor "it is not," and thinking cannot escape the pair.** As when a brahmin asked whether the "supreme person" (arahant) exists after death — and to "exists," "does not," "both," "neither" the Buddha each time said "the question is wrong" — any inquiry falls into one of four corners. Asked "is there then no perception?" the answer is "the question is wrong"; asked "so perception exists?" — "not that either." Can one think outside the two? One cannot: we grasp a thing and so it registers either as "it is" or "it is not," and only a mindfulness set where neither applies sees both questions as wrong. Left to ourselves we think the question, think our own answer, then with the same mind judge that answer wrong — going in circles, which is exactly the trap.
- **Re-understanding the self as *nāma-rūpa* only swaps a gross self for a subtle one.** Taking a glass first as a physical object, we then re-analyze it at the *nāma-rūpa* level — form a lump of foam, feeling a bubble, its marks and colour and shape perception, then formations, all cognized by consciousness — and say "this is just a bit of *nāma-rūpa*." But understood by whom? By *me*. So the same self that grasped "there is a real glass out there" now stands as the self that "understood it more subtly as *nāma-rūpa*" — the two are one; the demon, *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, is unchanged, only the way of looking has shifted. This is a perversion of perception (*saññā-vikṛti*): we replaced the glass with a concept "*nāma-rūpa*" and got caught in it, mistaking a fresh understanding for release. And once it is called "Dhamma," one can no longer call it false as one could the physical glass — which is why the Dhamma is a *raft*, given to cross a river and then let go, not carried on one's back, and a *snake* that, seized by the middle, bites, but taken by the head, frees; a medicine to be put down once the illness is cured, not treasured while well.
- **See what the thing *is*, right there; and only waking frees the dream.** The Buddha said to look at "the glass" itself, not at the glass's ways and qualities — *tattha tattha vipassati*, see it there and there, see *what it is* on the spot, not how it behaves. It is like a dream: only one skilled enough not to be deceived *within* the seeing has understanding, and to not be fooled by the dream one must reach "sleep" — an un-cognizable (*agocara*) nature the mind cannot grasp; an extraordinary mindfulness born right there is the only thing that is not taken in. In the dream the dog is understood as a dog, the bite felt as pain, all happening to "me" — yet is this a "me understanding," or does it happen where "I" cannot even understand? One cannot untie the dream-dog by reasoning inside the dream ("how do I see a dog with eyes shut?"); one must first wake, for only on waking is it seen to be false — the stable is locked only after the horse has bolted, and we call the glass false only after first accepting "glass," no one seeing the moment of its arising. ## Summary

This opening of the Kandy lay discussion, beginning with the refuges and precepts and led as open question-and-answer by Ven. Lasantha, lays the ground for the whole teaching. There are two things, knowing and seeing — *jānato* and *passato* — and the world lives at the knowing level, where all learning sits and which the mind can reach; what a Buddha shows lies beyond it, so rare that those who see are like the dust on a fingernail and those who cannot like the dust of the great earth, and so *pubbe ananussuta*, surpassing every religion, which can be understood only up to its highest point. We approach it backwards, thinking that as with a degree or job we must *do* something to get a result, whereas the path is *nirāyāsa*, effortless, and only sensing that begins to reveal its nature. A question arising and an answer given are both signs of a problem: a question comes because its root was unseen, and the answerer has not seen it either, so the two merely balance; the Buddha shows the fault in both, as a lion bites the source while a dog chases the stone. The aim is to get free of the question, not answer it.

Any inquiry, moreover, falls into one of four wrong corners — as the Buddha told the brahmin who asked whether the arahant exists after death, "does not," "both," "neither": each time "the question is wrong" — and thinking cannot escape the pair "it is / it is not," for only a mindfulness set where neither applies sees both as wrong, while left to ourselves we think the question, think the answer, and judge it wrong with the same mind, circling. Re-understanding the self is the same trap: taking a glass as physical, then re-analyzing it as *nāma-rūpa* — foam, bubble, marks, formations, consciousness — we say "just *nāma-rūpa*," but understood by *me*, so the gross self is merely swapped for a subtle one, *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* unchanged, a perversion of perception mistaken for release; and once called "Dhamma" it can no longer be dropped as the glass could, which is why the Dhamma is a raft to be let go after crossing and a snake that bites if seized by the middle but frees if taken by the head. So one must see *what the thing is*, right there — *tattha tattha vipassati* — not its qualities; as with a dream, only waking frees it, for one must reach the un-cognizable "sleep" the mind cannot grasp, and cannot untie the dream-dog by reasoning inside the dream, the stable being locked only after the horse has bolted.
