## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted, unattributed by title: *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā'ti, yadā paññāya passati, atha nibbindati dukkhe, esa maggo visuddhiyā* ("all formations are impermanent — when one sees this with wisdom, then one turns away from suffering; this is the path to purity," **Dhammapada 277**) — with the point that it is seen with *paññā*, not consciousness, and that *visuddhi* (purity) is a name for *nibbāna*.
- Referred to, unattributed: the standard list of the eight — *jāti, jarā, maraṇa, soka, parideva, dukkha, domanassa, upāyāsa* (birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, despair); the Buddha's image of **consciousness as a magician at the four-way crossroads**; the four-incalculables-and-a-hundred-thousand-aeons (*sārāsaṅkhya-kalpa-lakṣa*) of perfections.
- Terms used: *saṅkhāra-dukkha* (the suffering of formations), *paṭicca-samuppāda*, *pañca-upādāna-skandha* (five clinging-aggregates), *nāma-rūpa*, *samudaya* (arising), *hetu-phala* (cause-and-fruit), *taṇhā* / *bandhana* (craving / attachment), *viññāṇa* / *paññā* / *ñāṇa* (consciousness / wisdom / knowledge), *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* (self-view), *kusala* / *akusala* (wholesome / unwholesome), *paṭhavi-dhātu* (earth-element). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **second segment (Part 2)** of **"Nuwara (Kandy) Dhamma Discussion 1,"** continuing the lay question-and-answer led by Ven. Lasantha, with lay participants (one addressed as *malli*, "younger brother," and others). It carries on directly from Part 1's glass illustration.

## Main dhamma points
- The second segment of the Kandy discussion, drawing the line between the ordinary suffering everyone understands and the *saṅkhāra-dukkha* a Buddha shows, exposing the endless cause-and-fruit regress, warning that a "me trying to escape" is one side of the very problem, and closing that wisdom belongs to no one.
- **The suffering everyone understands vs the suffering a Buddha shows.** Asked "what is the suffering of the glass?" one can only say it breaks, cracks, wears out — a suffering a schoolchild grasps, needing no Dhamma; the whole world weeps at death without knowing *paṭicca-samuppāda* or the five aggregates. That understood suffering is drawn "from a thing" (*dēkin*), whereas the *saṅkhāra-dukkha* a Buddha shows cannot be grasped from a thing at all — it is not consciousness's object (*viññāṇa-agocara*). This is why the verse says *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā'ti, yadā paññāya passati* — seen *with wisdom*, not consciousness — and *atha nibbindati dukkhe, esa maggo visuddhiyā*, one turns from suffering right there, the path to *visuddhi*, which is a name for *nibbāna*. No Buddha need fulfil perfections over countless aeons merely to announce that things break and beings die.
- **The eight are "bonuses" of not seeing saṅkhāra-dukkha; the understood suffering comes from attachment.** Birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief and despair fall to us like a bonus precisely because we do not see the suffering a Buddha shows; they occur whether or not a Buddha arises — the world's own system — and need no analysis into categories to be felt. The suffering at the glass's breaking arises only from the *attachment* one had to it, from having taken it as "a glass" out of craving; that is the understood suffering, and merely re-labelling the glass "*nāma-rūpa*" cannot free one from it, for however finely it is analyzed, when it falls and breaks the plain suffering still registers.
- **The cause-and-fruit regress is endless, and the very "building" is the fault.** Trace the glass back — hard-element, then material, then particles, then electrons/protons/neutrons, then "quantum," then some "energy," then next year's update — and a cause is never finally found; each cause, given its own cause, becomes a fruit, on and on without end, like asking which came first, the hen or the egg. So one can never seize the knot from where it fell: "understanding" it means the knot is already tied. The Buddha's point is that this endless building of cause-and-fruit is *itself* the fault, and that the cause is *non-arising* — yet while one looks in this fashion a cause is always left over at the end. Told to "look at the arising (*samudaya*) as far back as you can," one finds, looking, that causes keep forming — arising from arising — so the knot cannot be resolved by thinking, and once fallen into the *nāma-rūpa* knot no one can untie it: consciousness is a magician at the four-way crossroads, more skilled than any stage conjuror, fabricating from whichever side one looks, with no escape.
- **A caution on meditation-as-escape.** To sit and look at the "found" suffering one would suppress the object and meditate, gaining a mental rest — but this can become like a drug's high, a pleasant lightness with no heavy object present; the meditator does it "according to Dhamma" and builds a wholesome result, while one taking a hit does it in a distorted, unwholesome way, yet both merely forget the problem for a while. (Flagged as a careful warning that object-suppression can slide into escapism.)
- **A "me trying to escape" is one side of the very problem; wisdom belongs to no one.** In a dream a dog is understood and feared — but is the fear owned by anyone, or does it belong to no one? "Because of the dog, I am" arises *by nature*, not by a mindful "me" sitting and understanding; and asked which arose first, the dog or the "me," both arise together, mutually conditioned, so one cannot hold one and falsify the other — to do so is again to seek the egg from the hen. We think "because I was mindful I understood the dog is there," and the fault of *that* "mindfulness" is exactly the point: right where the dog is, the "me" too arose, and there was never a separate "me" sitting to understand. Trying to escape the glass while remaining "me" is like trying to wipe out the reflection while holding the mirror, or remove one's shadow while standing — the glass and the "me who would escape it" are two sides of one problem, so no one can ever solve it; and *that very clarity* — that it cannot be untied by understanding — is what is needed. We make one side real and falsify the other, but seeing that both spring from one place, who is the seer? There is no owner — which is why wisdom (*ñāṇa*) is not anyone's wisdom, nor anything anyone understands.

## Summary

This second segment draws the crucial line between two sufferings. Asked "what is the suffering of the glass?" one can only say it breaks and wears out — a suffering a schoolchild grasps, needing no Dhamma, as the whole world weeps at death without knowing dependent origination — and that understood suffering is taken "from a thing," whereas the *saṅkhāra-dukkha* a Buddha shows is no object of consciousness at all. Hence *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā'ti, yadā paññāya passati* — seen with wisdom, not consciousness — *atha nibbindati dukkhe, esa maggo visuddhiyā*, one turns from suffering right there, on the path to *visuddhi*, a name for *nibbāna*; no Buddha need fulfil aeons of perfections merely to say things break and beings die. The eight — birth, aging, death, sorrow, lamentation, pain, grief, despair — fall to us as a "bonus" of not seeing that suffering, occurring whether or not a Buddha arises; and the pain at the glass's breaking comes only from the attachment one had to it, so re-labelling it "*nāma-rūpa*" frees no one.

Trace the glass back — element, material, particles, electrons, quantum, energy, next year's update — and no cause is ever finally found; each cause becomes a fruit, endlessly, like hen and egg, so the knot can never be seized from where it fell, and the Buddha's point is that this very building of cause-and-fruit is the fault, the cause being non-arising while a cause is always left over as one looks. Told to look at the arising as far back as possible, one finds causes forming from causes, so the knot cannot be thought out, and once fallen into the *nāma-rūpa* knot no one can untie it — consciousness a magician at the crossroads, fabricating from every side. A meditation that suppresses the object can bring a rest that slides toward escapism, wholesome for the meditator yet still only a forgetting. And a "me trying to escape" is itself one side of the problem: in a dream the dog is feared, but "because of the dog, I am" arises by nature, both arising together, so one cannot hold one and falsify the other; we think "because I was mindful I understood the dog," yet right there the "me" arose too, with no separate self sitting to understand. Escaping the glass while remaining "me" is like erasing the reflection while holding the mirror, or removing one's shadow while standing — two sides of one problem no one can solve, and that clarity is what is needed; making one side real and the other false, yet seeing both spring from one place, who is the seer? No owner — so wisdom is no one's wisdom, and nothing anyone understands. (This is Part 2 of "Nuwara [Kandy] Dhamma Discussion 1.")
