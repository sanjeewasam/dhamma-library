## Main dhamma points
- The third segment of the Kandy discussion, showing that *bhava* (existence) is present without any "understanding," that our abiding nature cannot be known from within it, that the "is-not" extreme is no one's experience, and that seeing where our existence is built dissolves the "address" we give ourselves.
- **The submerged part is the powerful one; "sleep" is un-cognizable, so the knot cannot be escaped.** As the *Titanic*'s captain trusted the small visible ice and was sunk by the far larger submerged part, so "sleep" — the un-mindful, un-cognizable side — is the powerful part the mind cannot grasp, and not knowing it we are wrecked on the knot: in a dream the dog appears and "I am" registers, each making the other real, a mutually-arisen knot no one in the world can seize. This cannot be caught by thinking in our usual way; only from a place *beyond* consciousness's range can consciousness's whole magic be seen, for caught inside the magic we merely describe the magic. This penetration is harder than splitting a horsehair sevenfold and piercing one strand with another — the Buddha's measure to Ānanda for the abandoning of *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*; and since the path is effortless, to say "there is something to *do*" is already a problem.
- **Existence (*bhava*) is present without any understanding; naming is not *bhava*.** Even a newborn, with no concept "toy" or "mine," turns its eyes to follow a dangled object, and suckles untaught — existence is there without any "I am" or "this is a glass"; a being reborn yesterday as a *preta* has the same reaction with no names attached. So one cannot free oneself from *bhava* by *understanding*: understanding frees only the understood thing, never *bhava*, which is not what we take it to be — we mistake the naming ("me," "glass") for existence, but the existence is not in the naming. In the dream one does not know one is asleep, does not truly recognize "a dog," and no dog exists, yet the sheer *appearing* is a hundred percent present; these four — seen, heard, sensed, cognized — suffice to keep *saṁsāra* turning, and no fool or genius escapes *bhava* by cleverness.
- **The "live" quality comes from the appearing, not from "me" or "the glass."** Existence is caught not in the glass but in the appearing — in the "audible" when tapped, the "felt" when touched, the "glass" when thought — a *live* quality; and that liveness came to the glass through the *appearing* (seeing), not because "I" exist or "a glass" exists. It is seeing/appearing alone that makes both the "me" and the "glass" real: in the dream "I am" is known only because the dog appears, and both are made real by the appearing, not by any owner. (An arahant too has "seeing" — but our known "seeing" must not be applied to the arahant; we speak of a "seeing" we do not actually know, and the last step, how seeing itself arises, is not to be imagined by "we see, and arahants see too.")
- **The "is-not" extreme is no one's experience: the born-blind.** To one blind from birth, neither seeing nor non-seeing is cognizable — never having experienced seeing, he cannot even conceive "non-seeing," and he takes his non-seeing itself to be "seeing." This is the Buddha's point that the annihilation extreme (*uccheda*, the "it does not exist") is not something anyone experiences or realizes. We only "understand that the blind cannot see" because *we* know seeing — yet we do not know *non-seeing*: we have never experienced it. On a journey one can list what was seen (remembered), never what was *not* seen; "non-seeing, there and there" cannot be experienced; "I didn't see it" is only relative to the seen, meaning attention was elsewhere. So asked to describe "seeing," we describe seen *things*, not seeing; non-seeing is the submerged iceberg — grasp it rightly and the whole 360 degrees comes into view, whereas we know only the seen portion, and knowing only that, its fault can never be found.
- **Our abiding nature cannot be known from within it; seeing it dissolves the "address."** As a fish born, living and dying in water never knows "water" — to know it, it must experience non-water, dry land — so our very abiding, sunk in the four (seen-heard-sensed-cognized) from birth, cannot be understood from within, which is why we abide in eternalism, an "is-ness," never once free of "seeing." We are always in one of four gears — hearing, seeing, sensing, or thinking — never outside them, and that unseen abiding is our true nature (*yathā-svabhāva*); not seeing where our existence is built is exactly why *saṁsāra* forms, and seeing it is *yathābhūta-ñāṇa*. "I have a thing, and I too exist" arises only from not seeing our true abiding. Seen rightly, the "address" dissolves: asked "where do you live?" one says "on the earth" — but everyone lives on the earth, so that is no address, and a real address (a street name, a post-office number) is a round-about fiction, a description of something that is nowhere. Not seeing the *yathābhūta* nature is precisely what gives us a place to stand — a footing for consciousness. (The segment breaks off here, mid-sentence, on that footing-for-consciousness point.)

## Summary

This third segment turns on existence being present without any understanding. As the *Titanic*'s captain trusted the visible ice and was sunk by the submerged mass, "sleep" — the un-cognizable side — is the powerful part the mind cannot grasp, so not knowing it we are wrecked on the knot: in a dream the dog appears and "I am" registers, each making the other real, a knot no one can seize by ordinary thinking; only from beyond consciousness's range can its whole magic be seen, a penetration harder than piercing a sevenfold-split horsehair, the Buddha's measure to Ānanda for abandoning *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*. Existence (*bhava*) is present without any "understanding": a newborn follows a dangled toy and suckles untaught, with no "mine" or "glass," and a being reborn yesterday reacts the same with no names — so one cannot free oneself from *bhava* by understanding, which frees only the understood, while we mistake naming for existence though existence is not in the naming. In the dream one does not know one sleeps, no dog exists, yet the appearing is a hundred percent present; these four — seen, heard, sensed, cognized — keep *saṁsāra* turning, and no cleverness escapes them.

The "live" quality is caught not in the glass but in the appearing — audible when tapped, felt when touched, "glass" when thought — and that liveness came through seeing, not because "I" or "a glass" exist; seeing alone makes both real, as in the dream "I am" is known only because the dog appears. (An arahant too has "seeing," but our known seeing must not be applied there; the last step, how seeing arises, is not to be imagined.) The "is-not" extreme is no one's experience: to the born-blind neither seeing nor non-seeing is cognizable, and he takes his non-seeing for seeing — the annihilation extreme is realized by no one. We "know the blind cannot see" only because we know seeing, yet we have never experienced *non-seeing*; one can list what was seen, never what was not, so "I didn't see it" is only relative to the seen, and asked to describe seeing we describe seen things — non-seeing being the submerged iceberg that, rightly grasped, brings the whole 360 degrees into view. As a fish never knows water until it meets dry land, our abiding — sunk from birth in the four — cannot be known from within, so we abide in eternalism, never free of "seeing," always in one of four gears; not seeing where our existence is built is why *saṁsāra* forms, and seeing it is *yathābhūta-ñāṇa*. Seen rightly, the "address" dissolves: "on the earth" is no address, and a real address is a round-about fiction describing what is nowhere; not seeing the *yathābhūta* nature is exactly what gives us a footing to stand on — a footing for consciousness.
