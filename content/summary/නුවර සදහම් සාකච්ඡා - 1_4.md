## Sutta referred to
- Referred to, unattributed by title: the **Satipaṭṭhāna** triad *samudaya-dhammānupassī viharati*, *vaya-dhammānupassī viharati*, *samudaya-vaya-dhammānupassī viharati* (dwelling contemplating the nature of arising, of passing away, and of both together); the **Noble Eightfold Path** — *sammā-diṭṭhi, sammā-vācā, sammā-kammanta, sammā-ājīva, sammā-sati, sammā-samādhi* (right view becoming the root from which the rest turn "right"); *udaya-vaya-ñāṇa* (knowledge of rise-and-fall) and *āsavakkhaya-ñāṇa* (knowledge of the destruction of the taints); the "**great person**" (*mahā-purisa*) who transcends the two extremes; the three marks *nicca-sukha-atta* vs *anicca-dukkha-anatta*.
- Terms used: *ātma-saññā* / *anatta* (self-perception / not-self), *nitya-saññā* / *anicca* (permanence-perception / impermanence), *sukha* / *dukkha*, *viññāṇa* / *paññā* / *ñāṇa* (consciousness / wisdom / knowledge), *viññāṇa-agocara* (not within consciousness's range), *nirāyāsa* / *āyāsa* (effortless / effort), *sakkāya* (self-identity). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **fourth segment (Part 4)** of **"Nuwara (Kandy) Dhamma Discussion 1,"** continuing the lay question-and-answer led by Ven. Lasantha (a participant addressed as *malli*, "younger brother," asks the closing question). It continues directly from Part 3's "where do you stand?" thread and the dream/glass illustration.

## Main dhamma points
- The fourth segment of the Kandy discussion, showing that "I" arises with each seeing, hearing and feeling; that seeing the abiding rightly *is* the realization of the three marks; that the path is not a watching of rise-and-fall but the knowledge in which both arising and ceasing fall away; and that from that alone the whole Path turns "right."
- **"I" arises with each cognition; the address is built from not seeing where one stands.** With seeing, "I" arise — the dog and the "me"; with a sound, "I am"; with the chair felt, the feeling-"me" is. The suffering we pay is the fruit of not seeing this standing-place, so we build ourselves an "address." Asked "how do you know 'I am'?" we answer "it is seen, heard, felt" — but hearing, seeing and feeling are not the "me" or the standing-place; asked to look at where we stand, we look at something else (asked what is happening, we answer "coconut in the bag"). We never actually see the abiding itself.
- **Seeing the abiding rightly IS the realization of the three marks — nothing new to seek.** Seeing the persistence rightly, where the persistence is built, non-persistence is realized; seeing the self-perception (*ātma-saññā*) form rightly, *anatta* is realized; seeing the permanence-perception (*nitya-saññā*) rightly, *anicca* is realized; seeing the "*sukha*" we hold rightly, *dukkha* is realized — one and the same move. We abide in *nitya-sukha-atta* as a fish abides in water, borne up from birth on hearing, seeing and feeling; so the task is not to go and find an "impermanence" somewhere — that is the mistake, watching rise-and-fall (*udaya-vaya*) on and on — but simply to see the standing-place rightly.
- **The path is not a watching of rise-and-fall, but the knowledge in which both fall away.** The Buddha did not say "keep watching"; when it is realized, the watching of rise-and-fall *stops* — which is why it is called *udaya-vaya-ñāṇa*, a knowledge that arises, not a watching. One watches until the knowledge dawns, but the knowledge is not the watching; watch forever and rise-and-fall only sharpens, never ends, and one never sees anything beyond it. The knowledge in which neither "arising" nor "ceasing" applies is *paññā*, not within consciousness's range, unthinkable to consciousness because without a pair it cannot think — as a fish cannot think "water" without knowing dry land, or the born-blind think "non-seeing" without knowing seeing, so without knowing the self-perception one can never see *anatta*, and without the permanence-perception, never *anicca*.
- **From that knowledge alone the whole Path turns "right."** This is *sammā-diṭṭhi*: only there do *sammā-vācā, kammanta, ājīva, sati, samādhi* all become "right," when the two — rise and fall — are transcended without before-and-after. Of Satipaṭṭhāna's three, *vaya* (passing-away) shows the "is-not" extreme and *udaya* (arising) the "is" extreme; taking the two seems like grasping both, but the knowledge that *transcends* both is *udaya-vaya-ñāṇa*, the *āsavakkhaya-ñāṇa* — not a watching one can do, but the place where there is nothing to do, effortless (*nirāyāsa*), the knowledge that dawns "without before-and-after."
- **The extraordinary mindfulness stands where we stand; it exposes the "mindfulness" we cultivate.** What we ordinarily keep as "mindfulness" is a thinking; the extraordinary mindfulness stands at the very abiding we do not know, and the knowledge shows the fault of the cultivated "mindfulness." When wisdom comes to our true abiding it is *paññā*, because consciousness cannot reach there. One with wisdom at the standing-place — the *nitya-sukha-atta* we plant our feet in — sees, through that knowledge and not through any watching of rise-and-fall, the fault of everything we had taken as permanent; the knowledge sees that the very *appearing* of "arising-and-ceasing" is itself the fault. "Arising and ceasing" is being born and dying, which any worldling can understand; the nature in which both are present and neither applies is what wisdom is, so within it no "arising" or "ceasing" is engaged, the two extremes are not handled, and this is the "great person" who transcends both extremes — the very knot to be untied. (The segment ends on the participant's question — "what is there to see as impermanent?" — answered that "rise-and-fall" is just two things held split apart, and, forgetting the word "knowledge," that splitting is exactly *udaya-vaya*: we do nothing but watch the arisen and the ceasing.)

## Summary

This fourth segment shows that "I" arises with each cognition — with seeing, the dog and the "me"; with a sound, "I am"; with the chair felt, the feeling-"me" — and the suffering we pay is the fruit of not seeing this standing-place, so we build ourselves an "address." Asked "how do you know 'I am'?" we answer "it is seen, heard, felt," but those are not the "me" or the standing-place; asked to look where we stand we look elsewhere, never seeing the abiding itself. Yet seeing the abiding rightly *is* the realization of the three marks: seeing the persistence where it is built, non-persistence is realized; seeing the self-perception form, *anatta*; seeing the permanence-perception, *anicca*; seeing the "*sukha*" we hold, *dukkha* — one and the same. We abide in *nitya-sukha-atta* as a fish in water, borne from birth on hearing, seeing and feeling, so the task is not to go find an "impermanence" somewhere — that is the mistake, watching rise-and-fall endlessly — but to see the standing-place rightly.

The Buddha did not say "keep watching"; when it is realized, the watching of rise-and-fall stops, which is why it is *udaya-vaya-ñāṇa*, a knowledge that arises, not a watching — one watches until it dawns, but the knowledge is not the watching, and watching forever only sharpens rise-and-fall. The knowledge in which neither "arising" nor "ceasing" applies is *paññā*, unthinkable to consciousness, which cannot think without a pair — as a fish cannot think "water" without dry land, or the born-blind "non-seeing" without seeing, so without the self-perception one never sees *anatta*, without the permanence-perception never *anicca*. From that knowledge alone the whole Path turns "right": only there do *sammā-vācā, kammanta, ājīva, sati, samādhi* all become right, when rise and fall are transcended without before-and-after. Of Satipaṭṭhāna's three, *vaya* shows the "is-not" extreme and *udaya* the "is"; the knowledge that transcends both is *udaya-vaya-ñāṇa*, the *āsavakkhaya-ñāṇa*, not a watching but the effortless place where there is nothing to do. The cultivated "mindfulness" is a thinking, whose fault the extraordinary mindfulness — standing at the abiding we do not know — exposes; when wisdom reaches our true abiding it is *paññā*, for consciousness cannot go there, and it sees that the very appearing of "arising-and-ceasing" is itself the fault. Being born and dying any worldling understands; the nature where both are present and neither applies is wisdom, the "great person" who transcends both extremes.
