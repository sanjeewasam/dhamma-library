## Main dhamma points
- The opening of the second Kandy discussion, showing that "arising" and "ceasing" are two separate perceptions (mirages), not one thing arising and then ceasing; that watching rise-and-fall is chasing two mirages; and that one must instead see the arising *within* the arising, at the standing-place, which realization is effortless and can be sudden.
- **The six bases are six empty villages, taught singly because the worldling could not bear the whole.** The Buddha likened the six sense-bases — eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, mind — to six empty villages, but did not declare all at once that "even seeing is false," for then killing anything, offering alms to visible beings, and "monks exist" would all be meaningless to a worldling; so he taught the six bases the worldling actually uses. Only one base arises in a moment, and two cannot knot together at a single instant — the careful point: look first at where you stand.
- **"Arising" and "ceasing" are two separate perceptions, not one thing.** We watch "the arisen thing ceasing," thinking we can see the ceasing of what arose — but a thing is never seen to cease at its arising; only when the *next* thing arises do we think "the previous is gone." The Buddha's *aññā va saññā uppajjati, aññā va saññā nirujjhati* shows that "arising" is itself one perception and "ceasing" quite another perception — both perceptions (*saññā-kkhandha*), not a thing-arising-and-that-same-thing-ceasing — and perception is *marīcikūpama*, like a mirage, never actually met. So the "arising" and the "ceasing" we watch are two mirages, and comparing two mirages endlessly, no knowledge ever dawns.
- **Ninety-nine percent watch rise-and-fall; realization is sudden, not the fruit of long watching.** Watching "it arose, it ceased" is what almost everyone does, caught in it — the dust-of-the-great-earth many against the fingernail's-dust few. When knowledge dawns and perception is seen through and through, one sees there was never an arising or a ceasing at all — that is the crossing of *saṁsāra*, and stream-entry happens *right there*, on hearing, not after long watching; Cūḷapanthaka became an arahant wiping a cloth, unable even to memorize a verse, so one must not hold "I can act only once I have first understood everything." (In the Buddha's day one sutta was one medicine for one person; now, with all the medicine-packets boiled together off the internet, there is a "Dhamma war," a pile of knowledge that cannot be applied — better to use the little one has.)
- **The walking-fish: not knowing we walk, we seek a way to stop.** As a fish cannot know water, imagine fish whose nature is to walk: told "there is such a thing as stopping," they go searching how and which way to stop — yet they are already walking, and not realizing they walk they seek a "method to stop," whereas realizing "I am walking" *is* seeing "stopping." Likewise, not knowing our abiding, we try to go and see an "impermanence" — a wasted effort — which is why it is called seeing *effortlessly*: to see it is not a doing, since we already stand *in* it, and the only thing to be done is to see the very place we stand, not to do something else to find something.
- **See the arising within the arising, at the standing-place; words can only point.** With cup and fan: I look one way and see the cup, another and see the fan, and say "I saw rise-and-fall" — but I took the cup, the fan, and the "me" all with *permanence-perception*, assuming things persist behind me when I turn away; in truth, turning to the fan the cup is gone, a "cup"-perception having arisen and ceased, then "fan." Two perceptions — "cup" recognized, then "cup ceased" recognized — both mirages. Even "I know perception is perception" is a step up but still another perception ("who knew it? — me"), the same limitation; so *pubba-paṭipadā* is known as such only *after* seeing, and *sutamaya-ñāṇa* is the view being cleared by hearing one who has seen. What we should look at is not the *constructed* thing (the cup, the fan) but the *constructing* — a subtle sketch-difference — for seeing the constructing itself, there is no "ceasing" there, whereas we try to see ceasing from the already-arisen. So one cannot look from the constructed; yet not "cannot look, so just sit" — rather, the fault of the constructing must appear, which requires first finding the place, the location, as one must find where Colombo is before going there ("can't find it, so I'll sit" is no answer). Only seeing that right place shows what the Buddha meant by "rightly seeing *samudaya*": seeing the arising *within the arising itself*, not by thinking — and how such a place is seen cannot be told in words, which only describe; the arising must be seen from within the arising, at the very covered standing-place. (The segment continues on this point.)

## Summary

This opening of the second Kandy discussion turns on the difference between watching rise-and-fall and seeing the arising itself. The Buddha likened the six sense-bases to six empty villages but taught them singly, since declaring "even seeing is false" outright would make killing, alms-giving and "monks exist" meaningless to a worldling; and only one base arises per moment, two never knotting at one instant — so look first at where you stand. We watch "the arisen ceasing," but a thing is never seen to cease at its arising; only when the next arises do we think "the previous is gone," and the Buddha's *aññā va saññā uppajjati, aññā va saññā nirujjhati* shows "arising" to be one perception and "ceasing" quite another — both perceptions, both mirages (*marīcikūpama saññā*) — so comparing two mirages endlessly, no knowledge dawns. This is what almost everyone does, the great-earth many against the fingernail few; yet when knowledge dawns and perception is seen through, there was never an arising or a ceasing, and stream-entry happens right there on hearing, not after long watching — Cūḷapanthaka became an arahant wiping a cloth — so one must not hold "I can act only after understanding everything first." (One sutta was once one medicine for one person; now all the packets are boiled together into an unusable pile — better to use the little one has.)

As a fish cannot know water, imagine fish whose nature is to walk: told of "stopping," they search how to stop, yet realizing "I am walking" *is* seeing "stopping"; so, not knowing our abiding, we try to go find an "impermanence," a wasted effort, which is why seeing it is effortless — not a doing, since we already stand in it.
