## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ* ("consciousness is like a magic show / illusion," the **Pheṇa-piṇḍūpama, SN 22.95**); *marīcikūpama saññā* ("perception is like a mirage," same sutta); the Buddha's image of consciousness as **a magician at the four-way crossroads**; *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* (the meeting of the three is contact).
- Referred to, unattributed: *kāyānupassanā* — "seeing the body through the body," with the point that the body is never actually met (the **Satipaṭṭhāna** body-contemplation); the *ariya-suññatā* (noble emptiness) that cannot be understood, distinguished from the meditative "emptiness" that can; an **extinguished-fire** image (a fire burned out without a trace — no ash, no heat — so that neither "it burned" nor "it was put out" can be met; resonant with the **Aggi-Vacchagotta Sutta, MN 72**, though not named here).
- Terms used: *āyatana* — *mano* (mind-base) and *kāya* (body-base); *phassa* (contact), *saññā* (perception), *udaya-vaya* (rise-and-fall), *nāma-rūpa*, *viññāṇa* / *ñāṇa* / *paññā* (consciousness / knowledge / wisdom), *samādhi*, *śūnyatā* / *ariya-śūnya* (emptiness / noble emptiness), *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* (self-view), *nija-bhūmi* (home-ground), *bhava* (existence), *taṇhā* / *upādāna* (craving / clinging). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **second segment (Part 2)** of **"Nuwara (Kandy) Dhamma Discussion 2,"** continuing the lay question-and-answer led by Ven. Lasantha (participants *malli*, "younger brother," and *akkā*, "elder sister"). The body-and-chair, the breath, and an extinguished fire are used as illustrations. The segment ends mid-sentence.

## Main dhamma points
- The second segment of the second Kandy discussion, showing that the body never actually meets the chair (so contact is only thought), that meditation's "emptiness" is a subtle self-view, and that our home-ground is the place where neither an arising nor a ceasing is met — like a fire burned out without a trace.
- **The body never meets the chair; contact is only thought, so consciousness is illusion.** When one thinks "am I sitting?", three things register — the chair, the body, and the two-touching-felt — which is *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*; but this was understood by *thinking* (the mind-base, *mano*), and before thinking the body (*kāya*) never actually received any such experience. This is *kāyānupassanā*, seeing the body through the body: the Buddha says the body is never met, and seeing this is why consciousness is called an illusion — it presents as "understood" a thing never experienced and not even thinkable, exactly why *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ*. The knot is that the moment we think, we assume "the chair is there, the body is there — how could I deny it, having sat here all along?", and a past is fabricated because this arises now; but seen rightly the root is never met, and when wisdom comes to that never-met place — where we were abiding as if in sleep — the magic shows itself as magic.
- **Caught in the magic, we watch the constructed thing's rise-and-fall — which never ends.** Taken in by the conjuror, we watch the handkerchief-and-dove and no knowledge comes; we label the constructed concept "arising/passing" and watch the *nāma-rūpa* of the two split sides, which never stops — like an endless mould stamping out biscuits, the mould nowhere yet stamping on. "Arose" is one perception and "ceased" another; we split the single mirage into two, take the mirage as real and then divide it — "picking up one mirage as another mirage vanished" — and the mirage-simile was given precisely to show it is never met. The thinking-perceived chair and body can no more be falsified than the dream-dog or dream-"me" from inside the dream; they are inseparable, and we attempt a separating where none can be done, making the demon and then fearing it.
- **Meditation's "emptiness" is a subtle self-view, consciousness's ceiling.** The same error runs through meditation done in ignorance: watching the breath, the breath fades from cognition into "nothing at all," and some call this "lights appearing" or "cessation," and are deceived — there is no result there, only raw ignorance (done unknowingly it goes astray; done knowingly it is the sevenfold-hard thing). Fixing the mind on one object stills it and can even bring sleep, since cognition fades; "not-felt" cannot itself be experienced, for "I don't feel it" requires recalling a felt breath, so "felt" and "not-felt" both drop from cognition — where people report "lights." When object and both feeling and non-feeling vanish one calls it "empty," but that "emptiness" is understood only relative to *other* places having things, a comparative emptiness drawn from the remembered breath; the real, noble emptiness (*ariya-suññatā*) cannot be understood, whereas this meditative emptiness can — and there too is *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*: "I experienced emptiness," an object still remaining ("empty" is itself an object), from which one cannot escape. That is consciousness's ceiling, unbreakable from within, so meditation is set aside and one returns to recognizing where one stands.
- **The home-ground: where neither arising nor ceasing is met — the extinguished fire.** Coming to a place where a fire had burned and been wholly put out — no ash, no heat, no external sign — one could not infer a fire had ever been there, and so could not think it had been "extinguished" either, for only having met a fire could one say it went out; neither "it burned" nor "it was put out" is met — in other words, neither an arising nor a ceasing is met. This is exactly like the place we stand: seeing our standing-place rightly, where neither "what arose" nor "what ceased" applies, is our home-ground (*nija-bhūmi*), our true nature. It gives us no "meaning" to grasp, precisely because there nothing is met.
- **How existence is built forward: craving fastens on the thought-built body and chair.** Before being asked about the leg, one was in a place experiencing nothing — the un-cognizable abiding — with no craving or thought about chair or body. The moment "chair-and-body, therefore feeling" registers, we come to dwell in the belief that contact yields feeling; and before that registering, the very talk "there is a body, there is a chair, their touching is felt" — *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* — stood in an *unconstructed* place, not a constructed one. Yet once the talk is built, it is exactly the thinking-perceived body and chair that we crave and cling to (*taṇhā*, *upādāna*): the craving fastens on the feeling and on the felt. (The segment breaks off here, mid-sentence, on "a body exists…")

## Summary

This second segment shows that the body never actually meets the chair. When one thinks "am I sitting?", three things register — chair, body, and the two-touching-felt, which is *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* — but this was understood by *thinking* (the mind-base), and before thinking the body never received any such experience; this is *kāyānupassanā*, and the Buddha says the body is never met, which is why consciousness is called an illusion: it presents as "understood" a thing never experienced, exactly why *māyūpamañca viññāṇaṁ*. The knot is that the moment we think, we assume "the chair and body are there, how could I deny it," and a past is fabricated because this arises now; seen rightly the root is never met, and when wisdom reaches that never-met place — where we abide as if in sleep — the magic shows as magic. Caught in it, we watch the constructed thing's rise-and-fall and no knowledge comes, labelling the concept "arising/passing" and watching the *nāma-rūpa* of two split sides — never stopping, like an endless mould stamping biscuits; "arose" and "ceased" are two perceptions, the single mirage split in two and taken as real, though the mirage-simile was given to show it is never met, and the thought-built chair and body can no more be falsified than the dream-dog from inside the dream.

The same error runs through meditation: watching the breath, it fades into "nothing at all," and some call this "lights" or "cessation" and are deceived, for there is no result there; fixing the mind on one object stills it and can bring sleep, "not-felt" cannot be experienced (it needs a recalled felt breath), so both "felt" and "not-felt" drop away, and one calls it "empty" — but that emptiness is only comparative, drawn from the remembered breath, while the noble emptiness cannot be understood; and there too is self-view, "I experienced emptiness," an object still remaining, consciousness's unbreakable ceiling — so meditation is set aside and one returns to recognizing where one stands. Coming to a place where a fire had burned and been wholly put out — no ash, no heat, no sign — one could not infer a fire had been there, and so could not call it "extinguished" either, for neither "it burned" nor "it was put out" is met — neither an arising nor a ceasing; this is exactly our standing-place, and seeing it rightly, where neither applies, is our home-ground, our true nature. Finally, how existence is built forward: before being asked about the leg one abode experiencing nothing, with no craving; the moment "chair-and-body, therefore feeling" registers, we dwell in the belief that contact yields feeling, and though that talk stood in an unconstructed place, once built it is exactly the thought-perceived body and chair that we crave and cling to.
