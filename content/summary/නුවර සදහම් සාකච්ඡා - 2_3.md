## Sutta referred to
- Referred to, unattributed by title: the aggregate-analysis pairs by which perception is measured — *hīna* / *paṇīta* (inferior / superior), *santike* / *dūre* (near / far), *ajjhatta* / *bahiddhā* (internal / external, rendered *tamā* / *bāhira*), *sukhuma* / *oḷārika* (subtle / gross), with *atīta* / *anāgata* / *paccuppanna* (past / future / present) — the standard fivefold/elevenfold *khandha* formula; the Buddha's "**having gone beyond both extremes, not smeared with the middle**" (*antā dvaya… majjhe ca na upalimpati* — the "middle" teaching, resonant with **AN 6.61 / the Pārāyana "Majjhe" Sutta**); *phassaṁ phassena passati* — "see contact by contact"; a **lamp/candle** simile (flame, wick and oil).
- Terms used: *taṇhā* / *upādāna* (craving / clinging), *phassa* / *sparśa* (contact), *kriyā* (action), *viññāṇa* / *paññā* / *ñāṇa* (consciousness / wisdom / knowledge), *kalyāṇa-mitta* (good friend), *hetu-phala* (cause-and-fruit), *nija-bhūmi* (home-ground). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **third segment (Part 3)** of **"Nuwara (Kandy) Dhamma Discussion 2,"** continuing the lay question-and-answer led by Ven. Lasantha (participants *malli*, "younger brother," *akkā*, "elder sister," and one addressed as *mahattayā*, "sir," who says he finds the "no-middle" reasoning hollow). A lamp — flame, wick and oil — and a matchstick are used as illustrations. The segment ends mid-sentence.

## Main dhamma points
- The third segment of the second Kandy discussion, tracing how recognizing spawns craving and the eightfold measuring of perception, why "there is no middle" is itself a trap, and how the lamp's "burning" shows that contact is an un-cognizable action from which cause-and-fruit is fabricated.
- **Recognizing is craving-and-clinging, and it fans out into the eightfold measuring.** To recognize "there is a body," "there is a chair," "their touching is felt" is itself *taṇhā-upādāna*: we cling to the feeling, take it as *sukha* or *dukkha*, hence *hīna* or *paṇīta* (inferior or superior); the chair and body become *near* or *far*, *internal* or *external*, *subtle* or *gross* — twice four is eight — the whole measuring apparatus of perception. And recognizing the chair, we fabricate a past ("the chair was here before"); but it is the present-registering that makes the past, not a real past making the present — this arising now is what builds "it was here all along."
- **"There is no middle" is itself a trap.** Some falsify this by reasoning "the past is dead, the future unborn — both are as good as dead, so no two extremes are met, therefore there is no middle." But concluding "there is no middle" is not at all the same as *seeing* the middle un-crossed (the Buddha's "gone beyond both extremes, not smeared with the middle"); we split the two sides and hunt for a middle, which is the very error, and "there is no middle" is then grasped as one more mirage from which one cannot escape either — "that too has died." This is no light knot: it dawns only through association with a good friend, for the teacher's words cannot show it, yet without them one cannot see it alone.
- **The lamp: "burning" is an action, and that action is contact.** When the oil-drop is burning it is not itself met — it is burning — so can a flame arise now from an already-burnt drop? We think "the flame comes from the oil," but the flame arises not because oil and wick *exist* but because it is *lit*: "burning" is an action performed. With the matchstick: we think the flame here came from the matchstick's fire, yet the matchstick's flame did not come here; saying "no" we are caught — "so this is not the flame that came" — and it was not here in the oil-and-wick before, nor does it arise "by itself," nor can one say "no cause," since the lighting and the oil (the condition — cold water will not light) are needed. So neither "the flame came from there" nor "it was here" can be said; there is only a *process*, "burning" — and that burning (*piccīma*, *dahana*) is exactly *phassa*, contact. "Burning" is not the oil (oil aids burning but is not "burning"), and burning can be described only by burning, with no reference to oil or wick; that un-referenceable action is contact, which is why contact is never cognized — as "thinking" is an action we perform yet cannot understand, and that is why we are caught in the knot.
- **Cause-and-fruit is fabricated onto the un-cognizable action; the knot cannot then be untied.** The whole flame-oil-wick account is built out of "burning": from burning a flame arises, and only because a fruit (the flame) arises do we go looking for a cause (the oil), and separating the cause we can no longer speak of it — for the cause *is* the burning, the contact, in which no oil is ever met. The Buddha said to see contact by contact (*phassaṁ phassena passati*), but our folly is to strike a "thing" onto contact — asking "what is burning?" ("what burns?") instead of seeing burning by burning — and that question builds the knot: "oil burns, that is where the flame comes from," cause and fruit fabricated together. Once this knot has fallen, it cannot be untied — "the flame here did not come from there, and the fire was not there before" — neither can be resolved by any amount of thinking, because there is no cause that "came"; the whole talk arises from a never-met place, the cause itself un-arisen. (The segment breaks off here, mid-sentence, on "the cause itself from a non-arisen place…")

## Summary

This third segment traces how recognizing spawns craving and the measuring of perception. To recognize "there is a body," "a chair," "their touching is felt" is itself craving-and-clinging: we cling to the feeling, take it as pleasant or unpleasant, hence inferior or superior; the chair and body become near or far, internal or external, subtle or gross — the eightfold measuring apparatus — and recognizing the chair we fabricate a past, though it is the present-registering that makes the past, not a real past making the present. Some falsify this by reasoning "the past is dead, the future unborn, so there is no middle," but concluding "no middle" is not the same as seeing the middle un-crossed, for we split the two sides and hunt for a middle, and "there is no middle" is grasped as one more mirage from which one cannot escape — no light knot, disclosed only through a good friend, whose words cannot show it yet without which one cannot see it alone.

The lamp shows the crux: when the oil-drop is burning it is not itself met, so no flame arises from an already-burnt drop; the flame arises not because oil and wick exist but because it is lit — "burning" is an action performed. With the matchstick, the flame here did not come from the matchstick's fire, yet it was not here before, nor arises by itself, nor is it causeless, since lighting and oil are needed; so neither "it came from there" nor "it was here" can be said — there is only a process, "burning," and that burning is exactly contact. Burning is not the oil, and can be described only by burning, with no reference to oil or wick; that un-referenceable action is contact, never cognized, as "thinking" is an action we perform yet cannot understand — which is why we are caught. The whole flame-oil account is built from burning: a flame arises, and only because a fruit arises do we seek a cause, which, separated, cannot be spoken of, for the cause *is* the contact, in which no oil is met. The Buddha said see contact by contact, but our folly is to strike a "thing" onto contact — "what burns?" instead of seeing burning by burning — which builds the knot "oil burns, hence the flame," cause and fruit fabricated together; and once fallen, it cannot be untied, for there is no cause that came, the whole talk arising from a never-met place, the cause itself un-arisen. (This is Part 3 of "Nuwara [Kandy] Dhamma Discussion 2"; it ends mid-sentence.)
