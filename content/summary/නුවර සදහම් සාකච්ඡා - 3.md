## Sutta referred to
- Not framed on a named sutta; two are clearly alluded to (neither titled aloud):
- *na idha, na huraṁ, na ubhayamantarena* — "you are neither here, nor there, nor between the two" (the **Bāhiya / Udāna 1.10** formula, rendered "ඔබ මෙහිද නැත, ඔබ එහිද නැත, අතරද නැත"); the *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen-heard-sensed-cognized) teaching, that answering "how one escapes at the arising of seeing" answers for all four (the Bāhiya "in the seen only the seen" theme).
- Carried over as a term of art from Discussion 2: *phassa* as the un-cognizable *act* of "burning" (*piccīma*), onto which a thing is knotted.
- Terms used: *pṛthagjana* (worldling), *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*, *phassa* (contact), *chāyā* / *pratibimba* (shadow / reflection), *viññāṇa-agocara* (not within consciousness's range), *vikṛti* (distortion). No sutta should be inferred beyond those alluded to.
- **Notes on the source:** **"Nuwara (Kandy) Dhamma Discussion 3"** — a single, self-contained segment of sustained question-and-answer led by Ven. Lasantha, with lay participants (one addressed as *mahattayā*, "sir," another as *akkā*, "elder sister"). It reworks the "bare seeing" and "burning = contact" points of Discussion 2 through the image of a dog and its reflection. It ends at a lunch break, the teacher noting a subtler point is still to come.

## Main dhamma points
- A sustained dialogue on the dog and its reflection, showing that we measure our folly in the wrong place — that knotting "a reflection" onto bare seeing is the same folly as the dog knotting "a dog," that both stand in the same boat, and that "seeing is only seeing" taken as a graspable fact is still standing in the lit place, not the dark.
- **The dog's reflection: same seeing, same folly, measured in the wrong place.** A dog sees its reflection in water and is deceived, fastening "a real dog" onto the seeing and barking; we see the same reflection but are not deceived, taking it as "just a shadow" — and because no real dog is met to us, we think ourselves wise and the dog foolish. But the seeing is identical, on the same plane for both, and if folly is measured rightly, the dog and we are the *same*: the dog knots "a dog" onto bare seeing, we knot "a reflection," and both alike fasten a thing onto the seeing. We call the dog foolish because for it a seeing arises *and* a dog arises (cause and fruit both), while for us a seeing arises and no dog is met — yet we still knot "a shadow," so both are in one boat, and the real folly is not "who is fooled by a dog" but the fastening of *anything* onto bare seeing.
- **The simile is to recognize one's own folly, not to become "the wise one."** It was given to see the fault of "seeing" itself — that dog and we share the same folly — but we take it and make ourselves the good one and the dog the fool, which is a distortion (*vikṛti*) of its real aim; unable to recognize our own folly through it, we simply apply another folly. "Seeing is like burning": the moment of peering-in is the seeing as "burning" is the act, and a seeing arises with the peering as a flame with burning — but even *that* understanding is itself still a knot, the very fault to be seen, for anyone can say "it is seen because of the peering," whereas the point is to see that this too cannot be grasped. When one peers into water a seeing arises, but is it "understood" the way it "arises"? — drop the dog, drop the picture, take bare "seeing"; in the peering, the "burning" and the "seeing" cannot be separated, yet even "they cannot be separated" must not harden into a held view.
- **Answering the seeing answers all four; but "understood and done" is a distortion.** If one can answer "when you peer, a seeing arises — how do you escape from there?", the same answer covers all four, *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*, and the escape from all four is seen; but we merely "understand it and it is done," taking the understanding as true, which distorts the simile's aim — which was to see the *fault* of seeing and that dog and we share one boat, not to crown ourselves the wise one. Just as the dog fancies another dog there, we, though we neither bark nor strike, still hold an acceptance in the mind — "a shadow" — so being caught by "a dog" and being caught by "a dog's shadow" come to the same thing.
- **"Neither here nor there nor between," and the fault of "seeing is only seeing."** Not taking the outer dog, "I" do not arise here; it is because the dog is taken that the seeing-"me" arises here — so "you are neither here, nor there, nor between the two." But taken merely as logic that only confirms it as a *fact*, the outer, lit version; the teacher refuses to prove it logically, because a subtler, harder place has not yet been shown. We think that if we knot neither a dog nor a shadow onto seeing, then "seeing is only seeing" is right — but it is wrong, for it settles in the lit, outer place (as a man searches under the lamp for what he lost in the dark); the complete work is to see seeing by seeing rightly, and there is more, harder, still to reach. (The segment ends at a break, the participant saying the complete answer was given and he will untie the caught pieces one by one, the teacher noting the harder place is still to come.)

## Summary

This sustained dialogue works the image of a dog and its reflection. A dog sees its reflection in water and is deceived, fastening "a real dog" onto the seeing and barking; we see the same reflection but take it as "just a shadow," and because no real dog is met to us we think ourselves wise and the dog foolish. But the seeing is identical, on one plane for both, and measured rightly the folly is the same: the dog knots "a dog" onto bare seeing, we knot "a reflection," both fastening a thing onto the seeing. We call the dog foolish because for it both a seeing and a dog arise while for us only a seeing arises with no dog met — yet we still knot "a shadow," so both are in one boat, and the real folly is not "who is fooled" but the fastening of anything onto bare seeing. The simile was given to recognize our own folly, not to make us the wise one and the dog the fool, which distorts its aim; and "seeing is like burning" — a seeing arises with the peering as a flame with burning — but even that understanding is still a knot, for anyone can say "it is seen because of the peering," whereas the point is that this too cannot be grasped.
