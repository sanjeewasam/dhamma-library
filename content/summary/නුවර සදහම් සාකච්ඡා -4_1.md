## Sutta referred to
- Referred to, unattributed by title: *diṭṭhe diṭṭha-mattaṁ* ("in the seen, only the seen" — the **Bāhiya Sutta, Udāna 1.10**), which the teacher says is still not quite the point; *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* (ignorance as condition of formations) implied throughout; the three marks *anicca / nirodha / anuppāda* (impermanence / cessation / non-arising); the **raft** (grasping the very thing given for crossing); the horsehair-split-sevenfold difficulty; *pubbe ananussutesu dhammesu* ("things not heard before").
- Terms used: *avijjā* / *vijjā* (ignorance / knowledge), *nitya-saññā* / *sassata* (permanence-perception / eternalism), *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen-heard-sensed-cognized), *saññā* / *vedanā* (perception / feeling), *pañca-upādāna-skandha* (five clinging-aggregates), *phassa* (contact), *aññam-aññā-paccaya* (mutual condition), *anutpāda* / *nirodha* (non-arising / cessation), *paccakkha* (direct realization), *viññāṇa* / *paññā* / *ñāṇa* (consciousness / wisdom / knowledge), *viññāṇa-agocara* (not within consciousness's range), the four *apāya* (woeful states). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **first segment (Part 1)** of **"Nuwara (Kandy) Dhamma Discussion 4,"** continuing the sustained question-and-answer led by Ven. Lasantha (participants *mahattayā*, "sir," and *akkā*, "elder sister"). A madman, the earth's rotation, the dog-and-reflection, a wallet in the pocket, a locked door, and a fan in the room are used as illustrations. The segment ends mid-sentence.

## Main dhamma points
- The first segment of the fourth Kandy discussion, showing that ignorance must be seen *as* ignorance (never removed by doing), that our abiding cannot be cognized from within or without — as one can never feel the earth rotate — and that seeing and the seen mutually condition so the "fan" can never be falsified by thought.
- **Ignorance must be seen as ignorance, not removed; the madman never meets his madness.** The dog fastens "a dog" onto the seeing and a "me" becomes real with it, both real as in a dream; and even *diṭṭhe diṭṭha-mattaṁ* is not quite the point, for ignorance (*avijjā*) can never be understood *by* understanding — it must be seen *as* ignorance, and only then are impermanence, cessation, non-arising realized, which no worldling can do by mind alone. Unable to see ignorance as ignorance, we take ignorance and knowledge as two separate things and try to *remove* ignorance and produce knowledge — "get rid of the folly" — which is exactly what everyone means by "what do we do?"; but removing folly is done by consciousness, and re-understanding the glass as "*nāma-rūpa*" only grabs the raft, mistaking a fresh understanding for realization. As a madman never knows he is mad — treated, he tells the doctor "you are the mad one" — even if cured he never realizes "my madness is gone," because he never met his madness; so he gained no madness and lost none, nothing was done, and where madness is not met is exactly where *nibbāna* is, not a separate thing. Seeing ignorance by ignorance, the illness heals of itself, for there is nothing to do — our very doing *is* the ignorance.
- **We think where thinking cannot apply, because our abiding is un-cognizable.** We always act *from* not-knowing, our inherited nature, and thinking at a place where thinking cannot apply is itself the folly, though it never registers as folly; what is truly here is an un-arisable, cessation-nature (*anutpāda*, *nirodha*), and not seeing it we produce a consciousness and "live" in the world it builds — a form built by thinking, "I am," a sound, "I am." If our abiding were understood we would not need to think: sitting, the body-and-chair-touching-felt, if constantly understood, would need no thought, but because it is *not* understood we begin to think when prompted — as we touch the pocket to check the wallet precisely because we do not feel it, or re-check a locked door because it did not register. The leg touching the ground is by nature an un-understood state, un-cognizable to consciousness — not because we are "not mindful," and to think "if we were mindful we would get it" is the great error, for the moment mindfulness "does" something a *can* appears in place of the *can't*, a light flashed in the dark that veils the folly, and the world assembles — "a leg, a ground, this felt nicely" — so *saṁsāra* forms, while the good place, *nibbāna*, is where one already abides, unseen.
- **We are sunk in the four, which is permanence-perception; the earth's rotation is never felt.** The Buddha says we abide in a permanent nature (*sassata*), sunk into seeing, hearing, feeling and cognizing — the four (*diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*) — and that very sinking-in is permanence-perception (*nitya-saññā*), which can never be cognized by mind; not knowing it, the "world" distortion is built. As we accept that the earth rotates yet, standing on it, have never felt it turn even a fraction — because we are *on* the rotating earth — so to see it rotate one would have to view it from elsewhere, but out in space one is *not* rotating; so rotation can never be experienced, neither here (immersed in it) nor there (no longer in it). This is exactly our abiding: un-cognizable from within (immersed) and un-cognizable from thinking outside (then not in it), so "see it there and there" means one never actually experiences it from anywhere — the direct knowledge stands at the very *can't*, an extraordinary wisdom that settles where consciousness cannot reach, and settled there, never again the four woeful states.
- **Knowledge is not consciousness's object; seeing and the seen mutually condition, so the "fan" cannot be falsified.** Knowledge (*ñāṇa*) can never come near consciousness, and when it settles, even "I know" shows itself as delusion, "this fan is, and the me who sees it is" appearing like a child playing with a toy. The "fan" is a recognition (*saññā*) made real by the *seeing* (its condition), and *saññā* conditions *vedanā* — the seeing, hearing, sensing, cognizing being themselves *experiencings*, the *vedanā* here (not the "pain"-feeling but the eye's experiencing) — this pair being the heart of the five clinging-aggregates. However false one calls the fan, one cannot falsify it, because it is *seen*: seeing is real, and seeing being real the seer arises, the two mutually conditioning (*aññam-aññā-paccaya*). This "falsifying" can be proved to no one, only to oneself, and is hard to realize directly; understanding "just *nāma-rūpa*" or "seeing is only seeing" is not direct realization (*paccakkha*) but a fairy-tale by which one only deceives oneself, for while the fan is thus proved from the seeing side, the feeling side (touch it — contact on the body), and even the smell ("that is the fan's scent"), it is established in all these ways at once — so can one think at all apart from these four modes? (The segment breaks off here, mid-sentence, on that question.)

## Summary

This first segment of the fourth Kandy discussion turns on ignorance that must be seen *as* ignorance, not removed. The dog fastens "a dog" onto the seeing and a "me" becomes real with it, both real as in a dream, and even *diṭṭhe diṭṭha-mattaṁ* is not quite the point, for ignorance can never be understood by understanding — it must be seen as ignorance, and only then are impermanence, cessation and non-arising realized. Unable to see it so, we take ignorance and knowledge as two things and try to remove the one and produce the other — "what do we do?" — but removing folly is done by consciousness, and re-understanding the glass as "*nāma-rūpa*" only grabs the raft. As a madman never knows he is mad and, even cured, never realizes "my madness is gone" because he never met it, so where madness is not met is exactly where *nibbāna* is: seeing ignorance by ignorance, the illness heals of itself, for our very doing is the ignorance. We think where thinking cannot apply because our abiding is un-cognizable: what is here is an un-arisable, cessation-nature, and not seeing it we produce a consciousness and live in its world; if our abiding were understood we would not need to think, but because the body-and-chair-touching-felt is *not* understood we think when prompted — as we touch the pocket precisely because we do not feel the wallet, or re-check a door that did not register — and to think "if we were mindful we would get it" is the great error, for the moment mindfulness "does" something a *can* appears in place of the *can't*, a light flashed in the dark, and the world assembles while *nibbāna*, where we already abide, stays unseen.

We are sunk into seeing, hearing, feeling and cognizing — the four — and that sinking-in is permanence-perception, never cognized by mind; not knowing it, the "world" distortion is built. As we accept the earth rotates yet never feel it turn because we are on it, and could see it turn only from elsewhere where we would not be rotating, rotation can never be experienced — neither here, immersed, nor there, no longer in it — and this is exactly our abiding: un-cognizable from within and from thinking outside, so "see it there and there" means it is never experienced from anywhere, the direct knowledge standing at the very *can't*, an extraordinary wisdom settling where consciousness cannot reach, and settled there, never again the woeful states. Knowledge is not consciousness's object, and when it settles even "I know" shows as delusion, "this fan is, and the me who sees it is" appearing like a child with a toy. The fan is a recognition made real by the seeing, and perception conditions feeling — the seeing itself an experiencing — so however false one calls the fan, one cannot falsify it, because it is seen: seeing is real, the seer arises, the two mutually conditioning. This can be proved to no one, only to oneself, and is hard to realize directly; "just *nāma-rūpa*" or "seeing is only seeing" is not direct realization but a fairy-tale that only deceives, for the fan is proved from seeing, from feeling (touch it), even from smell — established in all these ways at once — so can one think at all apart from these four modes? (This is Part 1 of "Nuwara [Kandy] Dhamma Discussion 4"; it ends mid-sentence.)
