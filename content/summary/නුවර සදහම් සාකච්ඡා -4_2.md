## Main dhamma points
- The second segment of the fourth Kandy discussion, showing that permanence-perception is un-cognizable and hides our strongest craving (the sky that "won't fall"), that the "un-mindful" half of experience is where wisdom must reach, and that this knowledge — not any personal doing — wears away the four views to the non-arising level.
- **Permanence-perception is un-cognizable, and it hides our strongest craving.** To escape "seeing" one must see it as never-met, which is why the earth's rotation was the example: we never feel it because we are on it, and one who does not know rotation cannot even conceive "not rotating" — just as the born-blind cannot conceive "non-seeing," so we know neither "seeing" nor "non-seeing," and this is the nature of permanence-perception (*nitya-saññā*), un-cognizable to mind, seen only by an extraordinary wisdom whose very seeing is called impermanence-perception (*anicca-saññā*). We try to remove craving for seen and heard things, assuming we have none for the sky, since it never comes to mind — yet we have *maximal* craving there: we are five-hundred-percent sure the sky will not fall, and "it will not fall" is the same as "it stays permanently," a hidden *nitya-saññā*; the walls and roof we are sure will not peel away, so everywhere a concealed permanence-perception, and the "not"-places are exactly where craving is strongest. The stone on the road we do not think about — no craving? — yet our maximal craving is precisely that it "not shift, not obstruct our journey," which shows the moment it trips us; where no object arises *saṁsāra* is being built, and while the un-seer falls into the trap "no craving here," the seer knows the un-understood place itself *is* permanence-perception.
- **The un-mindful half is where wisdom must reach.** Of a full 360 degrees only 180 are cognized — what we think and call "mindful work" — while the other 180 are the "is-not" extreme (*uccheda*), never experienced and never understood, and only seeing that annihilation extreme rightly resolves the problem of the cognized 180. The un-mindful stretches are real: walking the road, asked what you saw, you list remembered things, but the un-remembered stretches you cannot recall — "I was thinking of something else" — and at those moments you never registered "I am here in un-mindfulness," for that part is abandoned to consciousness. The extraordinary wisdom sees even that un-mindful part: a mindfulness standing where no object arises, unlike the object-grasping "mindfulness" we cultivate, and therefore called *ñāṇa* (knowledge) because it is not within consciousness's range.
- **When that knowledge settles, our "mindfulness" itself shows as un-mindfulness.** The object-grasping mindfulness the world keeps — our very "wise attention," which is only *thinking* — is then seen as un-mindfulness, and the whole thing flips upside-down; to see this, the knowledge must settle, and once it does, "understanding by thinking" itself shows as un-mindfulness. That knowledge, arisen beneath the unseen 180, alone cognizes every moment consciousness settles, working around the clock — like a black dot on a spread white cloth, visible, and visible only to a Buddha; it carries the path to arahantship, not a path one "cultivates" but a knowledge that wears away the "person"-idea, the *āsavakkhaya-ñāṇa*. Once arisen it never again steps into the *saṁsāra*-stirring, and because it settles where no object arises it cannot be done by thinking — thinking always catches an object — so to establish mindfulness where no object arises is to realize it cannot be done by thinking, which is the whole depth of it.
- **The knowledge sees every settling of consciousness as a view, and conceiving as never-met.** That wisdom sees every settling of consciousness *as* a view (*diṭṭhi*), and seeing a view as a view one cannot settle in it; where "seeing" is built a "me" is built with it, and as it is built one sees that "seeing" is never understood and "hearing" never cognized, and subtler still that this un-cognizable place cannot be thought in any way. Standing there, one sees the conceiving (*maññanā*) — we conceive "it is heard, it is seen" — and seeing conceiving *as* conceiving, it is never met when rightly seen, so truly the seeing-and-hearing nature is never met in the real nature; not knowing this, lacking wisdom, we bear the four views, seen-heard-sensed-cognized. Seeing the nature, "seeing" as a conceiving no longer occurs, a direct wisdom settling at "seeing is un-knowable," and by it the seen-and-heard conceiving is worn away to the non-arising level — not a personal-consciousness work but growth by an extraordinary knowledge, so there is no doer, no maker, nothing cultivated, and it is *effortless*; just a knowledge wearing away the four. By it, even slowly, arahantship comes — at most seven existences, the seer goes for sure, as on the highway even at forty one reaches Colombo — with the guarantee of no reverse and never again the woeful states. This wisdom sees the fault of the world's mindful/un-mindful division, for we abide in un-mindfulness without recognizing it: "un-mindfulness" is hearing, seeing, feeling and cognizing at an un-knowable place, the four abiding in an un-cognizable nature like the un-thought sky — we abide *within* seeing, yet it is never understood, and that is permanence-perception.

## Summary

This second segment shows that permanence-perception is un-cognizable and hides our strongest craving. To escape "seeing" one must see it as never-met, which is why the earth's rotation was the example: we never feel it because we are on it, and one who does not know rotation cannot conceive "not rotating," just as the born-blind cannot conceive "non-seeing" — so we know neither "seeing" nor "non-seeing," and this is permanence-perception, un-cognizable to mind, seen only by an extraordinary wisdom whose seeing is impermanence-perception. We assume no craving for the sky since it never comes to mind, yet we have maximal craving there — we are utterly sure it will not fall, which is the same as "it stays permanently," a hidden permanence-perception, as with the walls and roof we are sure will not peel away; the "not"-places are where craving is strongest, as the stone on the road, un-thought, carries our craving that it "not shift, not obstruct," shown the moment it trips us. Where no object arises *saṁsāra* is built, and while the un-seer thinks "no craving here," the seer knows the un-understood place itself is permanence-perception.

Of 360 degrees only 180 are cognized — our "mindful work" — while the other 180 are the "is-not" extreme, never experienced, and only seeing that rightly resolves the cognized half; the un-mindful stretches are real, for walking the road we cannot recall the un-remembered parts, and never registered "I am in un-mindfulness" there, that part being abandoned to consciousness. The extraordinary wisdom sees even that — a mindfulness standing where no object arises, unlike our object-grasping "mindfulness," and so called knowledge, not within consciousness's range; when it settles, our very "wise attention," which is only thinking, shows as un-mindfulness, the whole thing flipping over. That knowledge alone cognizes every moment consciousness settles, working around the clock like a black dot on white cloth visible only to a Buddha, carrying the path to arahantship — not cultivated but wearing away the "person"-idea, the knowledge of the destruction of the taints — and because it settles where no object arises it cannot be done by thinking, which is its whole depth. It sees every settling of consciousness as a view, and seeing a view as a view one cannot settle in it; it sees conceiving as conceiving, never met when rightly seen, so the seeing-and-hearing nature is never met in the real nature, and by it the four views are worn away to the non-arising level — no doer, nothing cultivated, effortless, arahantship coming even slowly, at most seven existences, with the guarantee of no reverse. We abide in un-mindfulness without recognizing it: hearing, seeing, feeling and cognizing at an un-knowable place, the four abiding in an un-cognizable nature like the un-thought sky — we abide within seeing, yet it is never understood, and that is permanence-perception.
