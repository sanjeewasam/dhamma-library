## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: *appatiṭṭhaṁ … vāyaṁ akaraṁ* — "neither standing nor striving" (the flood-crossing of the **Oghataraṇa Sutta, SN 1.1**, rendered "පිහිටාද නොසිට වෑයම් නොකර"); the Buddha's "**what is gained to rejoice at? what is lost to grieve over?**" (are you happy? — no; sad? — no); *tattha tattha vipassati* ("sees it there and there," the **Bhaddekaratta Sutta, MN 131**); the snake grasped in the middle that bites (**Alagaddūpama Sutta, MN 22**); *kalyāṇa-mitta-sevana* ranked "number one," *saddhamma-savana* and *dhammānudhamma-paṭipadā*; the turtle drawing in its limbs (**Kummopama Sutta, SN 35.240**).
- Terms used: *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (seen-heard-sensed-cognized), *avijjā-paccayā* (with ignorance as condition), *yoniso* / *ayoniso-manasikāra* (wise / unwise attention), *nirāyāsa* (effortless), *akampita* (unshakeable), *viññāṇa* / *paññā* / *ñāṇa* (consciousness / wisdom / knowledge), *paccakkha* (direct realization). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **third segment (Part 3)** of **"Nuwara (Kandy) Dhamma Discussion 4,"** a sustained question-and-answer led by Ven. Lasantha (participants *mahattayā*, "sir," *akkā*/*nōnā*, "elder sister"/"lady," and *malli*, "younger brother"). Everyday illustrations — a child's toy car, a shopkeeper's wrong change, a three-wheeler fare, a bathroom light, an unplugged rice-cooker, an exam — carry the teaching. The final line notes it is "a little hard to fit to the Dhamma."

## Main dhamma points
- The third segment of the fourth Kandy discussion, resolving the apparent contradiction between "the path is effortless" and "do wise attention," showing that "neither standing nor striving" describes a nature where consciousness never settles, and that consciousness never registers its own error in the act — the magic to be seen.
- **Recognizing the unwise is itself wise attention — a seeing, not a doing.** "A bottle seen by the eye" is like a child calling a toy a "car" — real to the child, grief when it breaks — but can the whole story be realized as never-arisen? At the root, not knowing seeing itself, all four (*diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*) unknown, is the *avijjā-paccayā* nature in which the being abides sunk, where nothing can be known; and when mindfulness settles at that root and an extraordinary wisdom comes, the consciousness-magic shows plainly, every way consciousness settles appearing to that knowledge. The very capacity to see all consciousness's constructions *as* unwise (*ayoniso*) is what *yoniso-manasikāra* means — which is why the Buddha can call the path effortless yet also say "do wise attention": wise attention *happens* when wisdom rightly settles, a seeing by extraordinary wisdom, not a doing. As the sculptor only removes the excess and the statue "emerges," one might ask "so what do I do?", but one who has caught the point would never take "do wise attention," for the doing is not wise attention; the two statements only *sound* contradictory, like pouring milk and adding a drop of dung together — the trap being our fixed "I can do something," which keeps our inquiry off-track and makes us keep loading more, when the "can't" is simply to be seen, not loaded.
- **"Neither standing nor striving": consciousness never settles, so there is nothing to escape.** Fasten "neither standing nor striving" here: sitting now, with no idea of a feeling, a chair, or a body, where did the mind settle? It did not — "not standing," the answer coming straight from the mouth — and where consciousness has not settled, why strive? The story is over; "not striving" applies only where there is a footing to escape from, and where there is none there is no striving. This nature is unshakeable (*akampita*), un-moved by any object, effortless, abiding in *nibbāna* itself — so one must not load onto it what cannot be excavated; one is already in a good place, unloaded, and the stored-up suttas only weigh like the turtle's drawn-in limbs. Could one understand from a sutta by reading, the Buddha would not rank association with the good friend number one; he would have said "read the suttas" — but the fault of "understanding" cannot be got by writing, realization being purified only through association, within which the hearing of the true Dhamma happens, like mirror and reflection together.
- **Where nothing is met, there is nothing to gain or lose.** The Buddha's "what is gained to rejoice at, what lost to grieve over?" applies exactly here: asked "are you happy?" one says no, "sad?" no, though we assume we abide in sorrow; but where no feeling is met, what chair or body is there to rejoice at gaining, and if never gained, what grief at losing? Both are absent — for one abiding by nature where chair and body are never met, gaining or losing them is simply irrelevant.
- **Consciousness never registers its own error in the act — the magic to see.** The shopkeeper's wrong change, the three-wheeler fare handed over while on a phone call, the bathroom light "turned off" while still on, the rice served from an unplugged cooker: in every case the amount or the act was never actually met by the eye or the mind, done from a mind-fabricated figure while un-mindful, and no loss ever registers. The mind never shows "I erred" at the moment of erring; only later does it do a post-mortem, "correct" it, and a fresh mind arise justifying — "I was thinking of something else" — so we always make excuses, refusing to accept that we abide, like a madman, in un-mindfulness all day, the seen and the thought having no connection there. This does not mean stepping outside consciousness; we are a hundred percent sure "the eye sees, so it is done right," yet it is not, and the "un-mindful" is precisely *where the un-mindfulness shows*. As a child writing an exam never registers "this is wrong" while writing — writing wrong answers to wrong questions as "right," and even the hundred-percent child gets the right ones "right" only as rote blindfold-darts that happen to land — consciousness never shows "wrong" in the act; and when all comes out right we think "we are clever, we understood well," which is exactly the knot the Buddha says cannot be grasped: the very "it works correctly" is what cannot be seized. Both sides must be seen, for the wrongness is caught only *after* writing, never while writing, so that everything comes as "right" and nothing as "wrong" — which means the wrong was never rightly recognized, and therefore the right was not rightly recognized either. (The segment ends noting this is "a little hard to fit to the Dhamma.")

## Summary

This third segment resolves the apparent contradiction between "the path is effortless" and "do wise attention." "A bottle seen by the eye" is like a child calling a toy a "car," real to it, grief when it breaks — but at the root, not knowing seeing itself, all four unknown, is the *avijjā-paccayā* nature in which the being abides sunk, where nothing can be known; and when wisdom settles there, the consciousness-magic shows plainly, and the very capacity to see all its constructions *as* unwise is what wise attention means. So the Buddha can call the path effortless yet say "do wise attention," for wise attention *happens* when wisdom settles — a seeing, not a doing — as the sculptor only removes the excess and the statue emerges; one who has caught the point never takes "do wise attention," the doing not being it, the two statements only sounding contradictory like milk poured with a drop of dung, the trap being our fixed "I can do something," which keeps us loading more when the "can't" is simply to be seen. "Neither standing nor striving" fastens here: sitting with no idea of feeling, chair or body, the mind did not settle, and where consciousness has not settled, why strive? The story is over — this nature is unshakeable, un-moved by any object, effortless, abiding in *nibbāna* — so one must not load onto it what cannot be excavated, and stored suttas only weigh like the turtle's limbs; could one understand from a sutta by reading, the Buddha would not rank the good friend number one, for the fault of "understanding" cannot be got by writing, realization being purified only through association.

Where nothing is met there is nothing to gain or lose — the Buddha's "what is gained to rejoice at, what lost to grieve over?" — for where no feeling is met, what chair or body to rejoice at gaining, and if never gained, what grief at losing? And consciousness never registers its own error in the act: the shopkeeper's wrong change, the fare handed over on a phone call, the light "turned off" while on, the rice from an unplugged cooker — in each the amount or act was never met by eye or mind, done from a fabricated figure while un-mindful, and no loss registers; the mind shows "I erred" only later, in a post-mortem, then a fresh mind justifies, so we make excuses, refusing to accept that we abide like a madman in un-mindfulness all day, the seen and the thought unconnected. Both sides must be seen, for the wrongness is caught only after writing, so everything comes as "right" and nothing as "wrong," meaning the wrong was never rightly recognized, and therefore neither was the right.
