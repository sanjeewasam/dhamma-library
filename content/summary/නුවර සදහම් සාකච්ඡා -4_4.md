## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: *cetanāhaṁ, bhikkhave, [kammaṁ vadāmi]* — "intention, monks, [I call kamma]" (the **Nibbedhika Sutta, AN 6.63**); the dependent-origination sequence *avijjā → saṅkhāra → viññāṇa → nāma-rūpa → saḷāyatana …* stopping when ignorance is seen; the story of **Aṅgulimāla** (the nine-hundred-and-ninety-nine, "cancelled" on awakening); **Makkhali Gosāla** tiptoeing along the road so as not to tread on creatures; the "impermanence seen with wisdom" theme.
- Referred to, unattributed: *sīla-samādhi-paññā* (virtue-concentration-wisdom) and the five precepts as restraint of the one mind; *ariya-kanta-sīla* (the virtue dear to the noble ones, naturally self-guarding); the five aggregates *rūpa-vedanā-saññā-saṅkhāra-viññāṇa*; *saddhamma-savana* and *kalyāṇa-mitta-sevana* as "fertilizer and water."
- Terms used: *avijjā* (ignorance), *cetanā* (intention), *kamma*, *phassa* / *kriyā* (contact / action), *viññāṇa* / *paññā* / *ñāṇa* (consciousness / wisdom / knowledge), *viññāṇa-agocara* (not within consciousness's range), *samādhi*, *nirāyāsa* (effortless), *anicca* (impermanence). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **fourth and closing segment (Part 4)** of **"Nuwara (Kandy) Dhamma Discussion 4,"** a sustained question-and-answer led by Ven. Lasantha (participants *mahattayā*, "sir," and *akkā*/*nōnā*, "elder sister"/"lady"). Illustrations include blindfold darts, an unplugged rice-cooker, a ring on the finger, a fallen earring, a tired puppy, a growing tree, Makkhali Gosāla's tiptoeing, and a mosquito-slap. This segment completes Discussion 4.

## Main dhamma points
- The closing segment of the fourth Kandy discussion, showing that we never know our un-mindfulness while in it, that suffering comes from the *cognizing* of loss (not the loss), how discussion and concentration are the "fertilizer" that lets wisdom settle in its time, and how seeing the un-cognizable "action" frees one from kamma of itself — the noble virtue.
- **We never know our un-mindfulness while in it; loss brings no grief until cognized.** As we do not blindfold before throwing at the pot yet blindfolded people also hit, "I hit because I saw" is false — and likewise "all my answers came right because I am clever," though the same one wrote wrong answers as "right"; the mind always says "what I am doing is right," so it is all blind hits. Leaving the rice-cooker with the switch off, one departs believing "I turned it on" — not from forgetting but stepping back a hundred percent sure the task is complete — so at that moment one is un-mindful, yet the "switch is on" was made real "mindfully," and un-mindfulness is never seen. We can never know we are un-mindful *while* un-mindful, which is why the Tathāgata calls this exceedingly hard and deep; to see "I was un-mindful" in the act is already to be mindful, yet we do not even recognize mindfulness — for if we did, we would register when it is lost, as one notices a ring fall only having first registered it was there. So gaining a thing brings no joy and losing it no grief, for a thing never met: the earring falls unnoticed, and only *after* one notices does the "it fell" and the grief arise, whereas unnoticed it could be gone seven days without trouble.
- **Suffering comes from the cognizing, not the loss.** This shows the loss-suffering to be an *un-cognized* suffering that becomes suffering only through the knowing — which is why the Buddha said "this very cognizing is suffering," a suffering not from losing but from cognizing. Going forward, when regret over a past thing arises now, the mind does not abide in the present but grabs a dead, never-met object and scatters, or else plans a not-yet-arisen tomorrow; stopping both — going neither to past nor future — the mind, like a new puppy that strains at its tether and, unable to break free, at last settles in one place, comes to rest. It cannot be forced to settle; it must settle. And as fertilizer and water do not make a tree grow tall at one's will but let it grow in its time, so this very discussion is the fertilizer — *saddhamma-savana* and *kalyāṇa-mitta-sevana* the fertilizer and water — and meditation's part is only to stop the scattering: the five precepts restrain the one mind, restraint is the mind settling in one place, which is *samādhi* (as watching the breath until it subsides settles there), and maintaining *samādhi* helps wisdom settle at our true nature — the link of *sīla-samādhi-paññā*.
- **There is no "impermanence" to watch in a met thing; the met thing is the suffering.** The Buddha said that seeing ignorance, the whole set — formations, consciousness, name-and-form, the six bases — stops; so in that abiding, is a body or a chair even met? In the met-or-un-met place, is there any "thing" to watch as impermanent? We watch the impermanence *of* a met thing, but the met thing itself is the suffering, arising only because the impermanent nature is not known — so watching the impermanence of met things is the wrong track (which is why one keeps asking "what to do"), for the real impermanence was never seen; one watches the suffering of the met thing and never sees the *act* of meeting.
- **Seeing the un-cognizable "action" frees one from kamma of itself — the noble virtue.** We abide at the peak of mere "virtue," like Makkhali Gosāla tiptoeing lest he tread on creatures — yet the Buddha walked plainly, creatures were surely trodden, and tiptoeing does not free one. "Walking" is not a creature's, but when intention forms, "walking" and "creature" are knotted so that "because I walk, the creature is trodden" — and there *kamma* forms; the action of walking is never cognizable to consciousness (it is a knowledge), and where wisdom settles "walking" is never met, so "creatures die by walking" cannot be built, and by that wisdom one never kills by one's hand. On awakening, Aṅgulimāla's nine-hundred-and-ninety-nine were *cancelled* — not deleted, but he saw that within "killing" the action was made real to the world, the killed creatures made real, "killing kills beings" made real, and there intention formed, which is *kamma* (*cetanāhaṁ, bhikkhave, kammaṁ vadāmi*). So the problem is not that creatures exist but our lack of wisdom: "killing" as an action contains no dog, man, cat or mosquito, and only where "killed" and "mosquito" are knotted does intention form — the knot one remembers as "I killed a mosquito"; an action is un-cognizable because it is not a form, and consciousness settles only where form, feeling, perception and formations assemble, making the "mosquito" real and knotting it to "hitting," each giving the other its reality (the mosquito real by the hitting, the hitting meaningful by the mosquito). We take the precept "not to kill" because it "breaks," and refraining is itself an action — which, without wisdom, one never sees; but for the wisdom in which the action is never met there is neither a "doing" nor a "not-doing," no need to formally undertake "I will not kill," because knowledge having settled one is already freed from the very "doing," and so, both absent, effortlessly free from "killing beings" — Aṅgulimāla freed from the nine-hundred-and-ninety-nine at the last, that lot cancelled once the "creature" knot was untied. This is the noble-attained virtue (*ariya-kanta-sīla*), a virtue naturally maintained and self-guarding — for to kill *or* to refrain, a "creature" must first be knotted, and with that knot untied one is freed from the action itself (whereas not-killing, unlike the reflex slap, otherwise takes great effort). (The segment ends here, closing Discussion 4.)

## Summary

This closing segment shows that we never know our un-mindfulness while in it. As blindfolded throwers also hit the pot, "I hit because I saw" is false, and so is "my answers came right because I am clever," though the same one wrote wrong answers as "right"; the mind always says "what I am doing is right," so it is all blind hits. Leaving the rice-cooker with the switch off, one departs a hundred percent sure it is on — un-mindful, yet the "switch is on" made real "mindfully" — and we can never know we are un-mindful while un-mindful, which is why the Buddha calls this exceedingly deep; to see it in the act is already to be mindful, yet we do not even recognize mindfulness, as one notices a ring fall only having registered it was there. So gaining brings no joy and losing no grief, for a thing never met — the earring falls unnoticed, grief arising only after one notices — which shows loss-suffering to be an un-cognized suffering that becomes suffering through the knowing, why the Buddha said "this very cognizing is suffering," not the losing but the cognizing. Going forward, regret grabs a dead object and the mind scatters, or plans tomorrow; stopping both, the mind, like a tired puppy at its tether, at last settles — it cannot be forced but must settle, and as fertilizer and water let a tree grow in its time, this discussion is the fertilizer, *saddhamma-savana* and *kalyāṇa-mitta-sevana* the fertilizer and water, while meditation only stops the scattering: the five precepts restrain the one mind, restraint is *samādhi*, and *samādhi* helps wisdom settle — *sīla-samādhi-paññā*.

There is no impermanence to watch in a met thing, for seeing ignorance the whole set stops and no body or chair is met; the met thing itself is the suffering, arising only because the impermanent nature is unknown, so watching the impermanence of met things is the wrong track — one never sees the act of meeting. And seeing the un-cognizable action frees one from kamma of itself: we abide at the peak of mere "virtue," like Makkhali Gosāla tiptoeing lest he tread on creatures, yet the Buddha walked plainly and tiptoeing frees no one; "walking" is not a creature's, but when intention knots "walking" and "creature," "because I walk the creature is trodden," and there kamma forms — whereas where wisdom settles "walking" is never met and "creatures die by walking" cannot be built. On awakening Aṅgulimāla's nine-hundred-and-ninety-nine were cancelled, for he saw the action made real and the creatures knotted to it, where intention — which is kamma — formed; the problem is not that creatures exist but our lack of wisdom, since "killing" contains no creature and only the knot of "killed" and "mosquito" forms intention, an action being un-cognizable because it is not a form, consciousness settling only where the five aggregates assemble to make the mosquito real and knot it to "hitting," each giving the other its reality. We take "not to kill" because it breaks, and refraining is itself an action one never sees without wisdom; but for the wisdom in which the action is never met there is neither doing nor not-doing, no need to undertake "I will not kill," knowledge having freed one from the very doing — both absent, effortlessly free from killing, the noble-attained virtue that guards itself, for to kill or refrain a "creature" must be knotted, and that knot untied one is freed from the action itself. (This is Part 4, the close of "Nuwara [Kandy] Dhamma Discussion 4.")
