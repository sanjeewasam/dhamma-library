## Sutta referred to
- Not framed on a named sutta; concepts and terms only:
- Referred to, unattributed: the two extreme views *atthi* / *natthi* — "is" (eternalism) and "is-not" (annihilation); *rūpa-kāye adhivacana-samphassa* ("designation-contact in the name-body," by which a mere set of marks is named "a deer" — the designation-contact of the **Mahānidāna Sutta, DN 15**); the five aggregates *rūpa-vedanā-saññā-saṅkhāra(-viññāṇa)*; *saññā* as the recognition-marks (*nimitta*).
- Terms used: *avijjā* (not-knowing), *yathā-svabhāva* / *yathārtha* (true nature / reality), *diṭṭhi* (view), *nitya-saññā* (permanence-perception), *taṇhā* (craving), *adhivacana* (designation), *viññāṇa* / *ñāṇa* (consciousness / knowledge), *viññāṇa-agocara* (not within consciousness's range), *āyatana* (sense-base). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **first segment (Part 1)** of a **three-part discussion titled "The Two Extremes That Become Real Only Through Not-Knowing"** (*Nodannākama nisāma attha vena anta deka*) — a sustained question-and-answer between the teacher (addressed as *mahattayā*, "sir") and a lay questioner. It refers back to an earlier discussion "with Lahiru," and turns on a picture of a deer (and a flower) drinking water. The segment ends mid-sentence.

## Main dhamma points
- The first segment of the three-part discussion, showing that the "is" and "is-not" extremes are un-cognizable views rooted in not-knowing, and that "picture," "dream" and "live scene" are all built from identical perception-marks — the difference being only our mode of taking, imposed and never recognized as our own doing.
- **The two extremes are un-cognizable views, and their root is a not-knowing that consciousness can never reach.** The "is" extreme and the "is-not" extreme are exactly what a consciousness cannot cognize, and because we cannot recognize them *as* views, we fall into them, and the true nature (*yathā-svabhāva*) is thereby veiled. Reality is not something we know — we do not even know whether such a thing exists, nor that it has been veiled — and this is the very meaning of "not-knowing not-known": the root is *avijjā*, whose nature is precisely "un-cognizable to consciousness," for the meaning of *viññāṇa* is "knowing," so a "not-knowing" can never become its object. First one would have to know that such a nature exists to know it is veiled — and since consciousness cannot reach it, there is no way even the "it is veiled" talk can register.
- **"Lifeless," "dream" and "live" are all built from identical perception-marks.** In a picture of a deer drinking water we say the deer is "lifeless," the water "does not flow" — but the marks (*nimitta*, *saññā*) by which we recognize "deer" — shape, colour, hardness — are exactly the same whether the deer is pictured, dreamt, or live, and in those marks there is no "alive/dead" quality at all. The eye never actually meets "a deer": a child at a scene of a deer and a hare could never derive "deer" from the eye, for "deer" is *designated* onto the marks by attention (*rūpa-kāye adhivacana-samphassa*). So "this deer has no life, the water does not flow" is not a fact about the deer but *our taking it in the mode of absence*, imposed on the identical marks, from which consciousness yields "a lifeless deer, non-flowing water" — which we then name "a picture." In a dream too the marks carry no "it is a dream" quality, which is exactly why a dream cannot be recognized as a dream while dreaming, the deer seeming fully real; and the live deer is recognized by the very same marks, to which we merely add "this one is real."
- **One who sees perception as perception finds no living or non-living object at all.** Since the division into "lifeless picture," "false dream" and "real live thing" has no basis whatever in the marks and can never be made from them, one who sees perception rightly finds in the world no separately "living" and no separately "non-living" object — everything is, so to speak, "just a picture," and put that way the description is correct. It is only through "there is a live world" that we are bound to the world; were it all pictures, we would not cling or collide with it — we do not try to pluck a flower in a picture, but we do a real one.
- **Craving comes with the "aliveness," and both modes of taking are our own imposition.** The act of "plucking" comes only with the "aliveness": because we take the flower in the mode of presence (*ati bavin*) we crave it — it can be plucked, smelled, touched, enjoyed through every sense-base — whereas in the mode of absence (the picture) we are firmly, hundred-percent-permanently sure it "cannot be plucked," and that very certainty is itself another permanence-perception (*nitya-saññā*). Neither mode is a feature of the object: it is *we* who take it in the mode of absence and *we* who take it in the mode of presence, yet we never notice this, so once a thing is taken in the presence-mode all subsequent attention arranges itself to fit — "can pluck, can smell, can enjoy through all the bases" — the whole apparatus assembling to match the view, while in the absence-mode not even the thought of plucking can arise, because we already abide, planted, in that view. This is the fault of not seeing perception *as* perception: the qualities we crave — scent, pluck-ability — are nowhere in the marks (which never differed at all), yet we impose them and can never recognize that we did, remaining fixed in the view. (The segment breaks off here, mid-sentence, on how the absence-taken is then taken as its being real.)

## Summary

This first segment of the three-part discussion shows that the "is" and "is-not" extremes are un-cognizable views rooted in not-knowing. The two extremes are exactly what consciousness cannot cognize, and because we cannot recognize them as views we fall into them, veiling the true nature; reality is not something we know — we do not even know whether it exists, nor that it is veiled — and its root is *avijjā*, whose nature is "un-cognizable to consciousness," for *viññāṇa* means "knowing" and can never take a "not-knowing" as its object. In a picture of a deer drinking water we call the deer "lifeless" and the water "non-flowing," but the marks by which we recognize "deer" — shape, colour, hardness — are identical whether pictured, dreamt or live, and carry no "alive/dead" quality at all; the eye never meets "a deer," for "deer" is designated onto the marks by attention (*rūpa-kāye adhivacana-samphassa*). So "this deer has no life" is not a fact about the deer but our taking it in the mode of absence, imposed on the identical marks, from which consciousness yields "a lifeless deer," which we name "a picture." In a dream too the marks carry no "it is a dream" quality, which is why a dream cannot be recognized while dreaming, the deer seeming real; and the live deer is recognized by the same marks, to which we add "this one is real."

Since the division into lifeless picture, false dream and real live thing has no basis in the marks and can never be made from them, one who sees perception rightly finds no separately living and no separately non-living object — all is "just a picture" — and it is only through "there is a live world" that we are bound to it, for we do not try to pluck a flower in a picture but do a real one. The act of "plucking" comes only with the "aliveness": taking the flower in the mode of presence we crave it, able to pluck, smell and enjoy it through every base, while in the mode of absence we are firmly, permanently sure it "cannot be plucked" — that certainty itself another permanence-perception. Neither mode is a feature of the object; it is we who take it in absence and we who take it in presence, yet we never notice this, so once taken in the presence-mode all attention arranges itself to fit — "can pluck, can smell, can enjoy" — while in the absence-mode not even the thought of plucking can arise. This is the fault of not seeing perception as perception: the craved qualities are nowhere in the marks, which never differed at all, yet we impose them and never recognize that we did, remaining fixed in the view. (This is Part 1 of "The Two Extremes That Become Real Only Through Not-Knowing"; it ends mid-sentence.)
