## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted, unattributed by title: *na te kāmā yāni citrāni loke, saṅkapparāgo purisassa kāmo, tiṭṭhanti citrāni tatheva loke* — "the lovely things in the world are not sensuality; a person's sensuality is the passion of intention; the lovely things remain just as they are in the world" (a well-known verse, e.g. **SN 1.34 / the Nibbedhika theme, AN 6.63**); the proliferation-sequence *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti, yaṁ sañjānāti taṁ vitakketi, [yaṁ vitakketi taṁ papañceti]* — "what one feels, one perceives; what one perceives, one thinks about; [what one thinks about, one proliferates]" (the **Madhupiṇḍika Sutta, MN 18**); perception as a **mirage** (*marīcikūpama saññā*, the **Pheṇa-piṇḍūpama, SN 22.95**).
- Terms used: *sassata* / *uccheda* (the "is" / "is-not" extremes, *atthi* / *natthi*), *saṅkappa-rāga* (passion of intention/concept), *kāma* / *pañca-kāma* (sensuality / fivefold sensuality), *pañca-upādāna-skandha* (five clinging-aggregates), *papañca* (proliferation), *saññā* / *vedanā* (perception / feeling), *saññā-vikṛti* (perversion of perception), *artha-śūnya* (empty of meaning), *sukha* / *dukkha*, *rāga* / *taṇhā* (passion / craving). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **second segment (Part 2)** of **"The Two Extremes That Become Real Only Through Not-Knowing,"** continuing the question-and-answer between the teacher (*mahattayā*, "sir") and a lay questioner, now with a lady (*nōnā*) also present. The deer/flower picture continues, then a chair-cushion-and-black-rock illustration of feeling. The segment ends on "[TO BE CONTINUED]."

## Main dhamma points
- The second segment, showing that even the "is-not" is grasped as an "is," that sensuality lies in the concept and not the object, that recognizing is itself a mirage and a suffering, and that "hard/soft" and "pleasure/pain" are never in the feeling but imposed by comparison.
- **Even the "is-not" is grasped as an "is"; sensuality is in the concept, not the object.** "The deer has no life" is not a mere absence but *another gaining* — a "lifeless deer" is still met, so the "is-not" (*natthi*) is itself a subtle "is" (*sassata*); however one takes it, presence or absence, it is grasped as an "is," which is why one can never separate out a real "there is" or "there is not" in the world. This is the meaning of *na te kāmā yāni citrāni loke, saṅkapparāgo purisassa kāmo, tiṭṭhanti citrāni tatheva loke* — the lovely things stand in the world just as they are; sensuality (*kāma*) is not in them but is the passion of the *concept* (*saṅkappa-rāga*), for the "aliveness" is nowhere in the recognition-marks and lives only in the concept we build, which is why we crave the real flower more than the pictured one. The fivefold sensuality (*pañca-kāma*) — "can be seen, heard, smelled, touched" — is exactly this presence-taking assembling itself, and so the concept itself is the suffering, the five clinging-aggregates; seen as perception, there is no passion at all, and it is the concept that has perverted perception (*saññā-vikṛti*).
- **"Seeing" and "the flower" contain no alive/lifeless; recognition is a mirage, empty of itself.** "Seeing" carries no "real / lifeless / live" quality, and neither does "the flower"; told merely "a flower," one cannot say whether a pictured, dreamt, or real flower is meant — so the division is inserted only because perception and feeling, entangled and unseen as perception, are knotted and stamped "this is alive, this lifeless." This is the proliferation chain: *what is felt is perceived, what is perceived is thought about* — ending in *papañca*, which does not form if there is no recognition; "it has life / no life" is a *papañca*, a separate note we bring, not in the flower at all. And the "flower" itself is recognized only through colour, shape and hardness, in which no "flower" is contained, so recognizing "flower" is a mirage, empty of itself (*artha-śūnya*): to recognize "flower" now *is* suffering, like grasping a fruit where no cause ever arose — and seeing the flower *as* flower, no cause is met, the flower dissolves into emptiness, "a mirage, nothing that came from anywhere."
- **"Hard/soft" and "pleasure/pain" are never in the feeling, but imposed by comparison.** Pressing the hand on the wooden chair-frame we call "hard," on the cushion "soft" — yet the bare *feeling* does not change by a fraction however hard one presses; the feeling is one, and "hard/soft," "yielding/not-yielding" belong to it not at all, being only a comparison we impose. Sitting in the dark, the feeling soon fades from cognition until not even "is there a feeling?" registers, and no "chair" or "rock" talk is there; we say "the black rock is painful, the cushion comfortable," but the same feeling was in both, and even the cushion turns painful in time — so "comfort" is merely *the lesser suffering* named as comfort (the easy posture a mere calming), suffering being there all along while we pick the lesser and call it pleasure. Pleasure and pain are not in the things: where feeling is not met there is no "cushion or rock" and no "pleasure or pain," the liking living in the concept, not the feeling; and sitting now, before being asked, there was no idea "rock or chair" at all, and so no "pleasure or pain" either. (The segment breaks off here, on "if not even a feeling was understood, never would 'the chair is pleasant, the rock…'" — "[TO BE CONTINUED].")

## Summary

This second segment shows that even the "is-not" is grasped as an "is": "the deer has no life" is another gaining — a "lifeless deer" still met — so the *natthi* is a subtle *atthi*, and one can never separate a real "there is" or "there is not" in the world. This is *na te kāmā yāni citrāni loke, saṅkapparāgo purisassa kāmo, tiṭṭhanti citrāni tatheva loke*: the lovely things stand just as they are; sensuality is not in them but is the passion of the concept, for the "aliveness" is nowhere in the recognition-marks and lives only in the concept we build — why we crave the real flower more than the pictured one — and the fivefold sensuality is this presence-taking assembling itself, the concept itself the suffering, the five clinging-aggregates, while seen as perception there is no passion, the concept having perverted perception. "Seeing" and "the flower" carry no alive/lifeless quality, and told merely "a flower" one cannot say which is meant, so the division is inserted only because perception and feeling, entangled and unseen, are knotted and stamped "alive/lifeless" — the proliferation chain *what is felt is perceived, what is perceived is thought about*, ending in *papañca*, which does not form without recognition; "it has life" is a *papañca* not in the flower.

The "flower" itself is recognized only through colour, shape and hardness, in which no "flower" is contained, so recognizing "flower" is a mirage empty of itself: to recognize it *is* suffering, like grasping a fruit where no cause arose, and seeing the flower as flower no cause is met, the flower dissolving into emptiness — "a mirage, nothing that came from anywhere." And "hard/soft" and "pleasure/pain" are never in the feeling: pressing on wood we say "hard," on cushion "soft," yet the bare feeling does not change by a fraction, the feeling is one, and hard/soft is only an imposed comparison. Sitting in the dark the feeling fades until not even "is there a feeling?" registers, and no "chair" or "rock" is there; we say "the rock is painful, the cushion comfortable," but the same feeling was in both, and even the cushion turns painful in time — so "comfort" is merely the lesser suffering named as comfort, suffering there all along. Pleasure and pain are not in the things: where feeling is not met there is no cushion or rock and no pleasure or pain, the liking living in the concept; and sitting now, before being asked, there was no idea "rock or chair," and so no pleasure or pain either.
