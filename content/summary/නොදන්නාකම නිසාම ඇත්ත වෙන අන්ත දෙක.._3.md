## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* ("with ignorance as condition, formations" — named as "the root of dependent origination"); the four great elements as *paññatti* (designations — *paṭhavi, āpo, tejo, vāyo* / hardness, flowing, heat, movement); the barren woman's son (*vandhyā-putra*) as an image of the un-arisen; *bhava-nirodha* / *phassa-nirodha* (cessation of existence / of contact); *anuppāda* (non-arising); the **raft** to be let go; an **extinguished-fire** image (a fire burned out without a trace — resonant with the **Aggi-Vacchagotta Sutta, MN 72**, not named).
- Terms used: *avijjā* (ignorance), *saṅkhāra* (formations), *vedanā* (feeling), *saññā* (perception), *phassa* (contact), *maññanā* (conceiving), *anuppāda* / *nūpadicca* (non-arising / the un-arisen), *paññatti* (designation), *nibbāna* / *saṁsāra*, *ñāṇa* (knowledge), *viññāṇa* (consciousness). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **third and closing segment (Part 3)** of **"The Two Extremes That Become Real Only Through Not-Knowing,"** completing the question-and-answer between the teacher (*mahattayā*, "sir") and the lay questioners (with a lady, *nōnā*, present). Illustrations include touching water, fire and wind, an air-conditioner and fan, an unborn child, the barren woman's son, and an extinguished fire. The segment closes warmly, proposing to discuss a sutta another day.

## Main dhamma points
- The closing segment, showing that the four elements are designations imposed on a single un-cognizable feeling, that we think only because feeling is not understood (*avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*), that the un-arisen cannot be put into words, and that seeing contact by contact discloses birth itself as non-birth.
- **The four elements are designations on a single un-cognizable feeling.** "Hard" carries no "rough" or "painful"; water we call "flowing," fire "hot," wind "blowing," yet touching each the feeling is one and the same, so hardness, flowing, heat and movement (*paṭhavi, āpo, tejo, vāyo*) are *paññatti*, designations, not in the feeling at all. In a strong wind, "wind blowing" soon fades from cognition; in the air-conditioner's cold, "cold, cold" fades; a fan's breeze fades — because feeling itself is un-cognizable, so the concept built upon it can no longer be knotted to it, and where feeling is not met, no "felt thing" is met either, the two having come knotted together and never separately.
- **We think only because feeling is not understood — the root of dependent origination.** Not knowing the not-knowing (which consciousness can never cognize), we take feeling in the mode of presence and so the felt thing too. If breath could be known, one would not need to keep reminding oneself to attend to it; one thinks *only* because it is not understood — feeling is un-cognizable, and that is why we think: *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*, not knowing the un-knowability, we think, and thinking, "it is felt" registers, with a bonus — the felt thing, the chair or table, knotted in. It cannot be stopped: as long as feeling is conceived (*maññanā*), the felt thing is conceived; we never register that the causes are not assembled, and consciousness can never grasp that the knotted thing does not belong to the feeling. Hence even these very words are, strictly, false words — the raft to be let go at the end.
- **The un-arisen cannot be put into words; birth is seen only at contact.** To speak of birth one must speak of a child, and there is no speaking of a child without birth: "not one child was born today" can only be said by taking up the (non-existent) child, so the non-arisen nature (*anuppāda*), like the barren woman's son, cannot be put into words. Put into language it becomes a "birth," understood only through arisen things, so understanding runs to the arisen side and lands on "the arisen things are not / cease" — which is exactly why the cessation of existence (*bhava-nirodha*) is not understood: taking the arisen and looking, one merely interprets the *word* as "the arisen is false, a mirage," and "the world is a mirage" is thus wrongly taken. We watch birth by taking up a born thing, but birth cannot be seen that way — seen through a thing, only "a thing arose and the thing ceased" is met, and to see birth itself one must see it at contact (*phassa*); only at contact does birth appear as non-birth.
- **Cessation is realized only at contact; the extinguished fire.** Asked "is it felt?", where "feeling" arises the breath and nose arise, yet "feeling" contains no "breath" or "nose" and cannot be taken as either; seeing there, within "feeling" breath and nose never arose, so "feeling" can be given no meaning and consciousness cannot settle — a cause-non-arisen nature, in which the non-arisen appears *as* non-arisen. If instead one knots a breath and nose onto feeling, one "sees non-arising" merely as "no breath, no nose," knotting a non-existent breath and nose and watching their absence — which is watching the *born thing's* absence, not the un-arisen, so that "breath and nose" become for such a one false, a mirage, and "the world is a mirage" is again wrongly taken. Contact seen *as* contact (*phassaṁ phassena*) is the only way the cessation of contact is realized, for there no cause arises and there are not two to make contact; and as with a fire burned out without a trace — where one can neither say "a fire was here" nor "it was extinguished" — so seeing *saṁsāra* one can say neither "there is a *saṁsāra*" nor "there is no *saṁsāra*"; "if it is extinguished, it cannot be known as extinguished" — and that is exactly what knowledge (*ñāṇa*) is. (The segment closes warmly, the questioners thanking the teacher and proposing to take up a sutta another day.)

## Summary

This closing segment shows the four elements to be designations on a single un-cognizable feeling: "hard" carries no "rough" or "painful," water is called "flowing," fire "hot," wind "blowing," yet touching each the feeling is one, so hardness, flowing, heat and movement are *paññatti*, not in the feeling — and in a strong wind "wind blowing" soon fades, in the air-conditioner's cold "cold" fades, a fan's breeze fades, because feeling itself is un-cognizable and the concept can no longer be knotted to it, so that where feeling is not met no "felt thing" is met either, the two having come knotted and never separately. We think only because feeling is not understood: not knowing the not-knowing, we take feeling in the mode of presence and the felt thing with it; if breath could be known one would not keep reminding oneself of it, and one thinks only because it is not understood — *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* — so that "it is felt" registers with a bonus, the chair or table knotted in, and it cannot be stopped, for as long as feeling is conceived the felt thing is conceived, and consciousness can never grasp that the knotted thing does not belong to the feeling — hence even these words are strictly false words, the raft to be let go.

The un-arisen cannot be put into words: to speak of birth one must speak of a child, and "not one child was born today" can only be said by taking up the non-existent child, so the non-arisen, like the barren woman's son, cannot be worded; put into language it becomes a "birth," understood only through arisen things, so understanding lands on "the arisen ceases," which is why the cessation of existence is not understood — one merely interprets the word as "the arisen is a mirage," and "the world is a mirage" is wrongly taken. Birth cannot be seen through a born thing, where only "a thing arose and ceased" is met; it must be seen at contact, where alone birth appears as non-birth. Where "feeling" arises breath and nose arise, yet "feeling" contains neither, so within feeling breath and nose never arose, and the non-arisen appears as non-arisen; but knotting a non-existent breath and nose and watching their absence is watching the born thing's absence, not the un-arisen. Contact seen as contact is the only way the cessation of contact is realized, for there no cause arises and there are not two to make contact; and as with a fire burned out without a trace — neither "a fire was here" nor "it was extinguished" sayable — so seeing *saṁsāra* one can say neither "there is" nor "there is not," "if extinguished it cannot be known as extinguished," which is exactly what knowledge is. (This is Part 3, the close of "The Two Extremes That Become Real Only Through Not-Knowing.")
