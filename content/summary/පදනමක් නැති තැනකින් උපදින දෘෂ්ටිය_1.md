## Sutta referred to

- The **diṭṭha suta muta viññāta** formula is the structural centre: "I" (mam) arises from and within these four — seen, heard, sensed, cognised — not from a physical body.
- The **asaṅkhata** (unconditioned) is invoked when the participant recognises that seeing/hearing has never been sakasveema'd (configured/put together) — it was never saṅkhata.
- **Samudaya and nirodha** are discussed structurally: seeing samudaya (the constructed pavattuma) correctly reveals nirodha (the nopævættuma) within itself — "samudaya tuḷinma nirōdhaya hambavenavā."
- **Sakkāya** (self-identity) is redefined: sakkāya is not the physical body but the arising of "I" within diṭṭha suta muta viññāta.
- The **kavaḷa** (morsel) analogy from earlier discussions is reused: seeing/hearing is kavaḷam — indivisible activity that cannot be vigraha'd (analysed into parts).

## Main dhamma points

- **"I" arose from peṇīma (seeing), not from a body.** Seeing is the bēs eka (foundation/base) from which "I" was born. When seeing occurs, "eye" and "form" are produced — not the other way round. We think eye + form → seeing, but the teacher reverses this: seeing → eye-concept + form-concept. Since "I" is born from seeing, "I" can never see the base — like feet can never see the ground they stand on.
- **The base of hearing/seeing is never encountered.** The teacher conducts the silence exercise: during silence, can you establish that "hearing exists"? No — there is no ættha (evidence/proof) for hearing's existence. Can you establish "non-hearing"? Also no — there is no paccaya (condition) for claiming non-hearing. Both asti and nāsti fail. The base is a nopævættuma (non-existence) — it has no demonstrable pævættuma (existence).
- **Hearing/seeing can never be demonstrated by itself.** When asked about hearing, we always resort to sound — we use śabdaya (sound) to explain æsīma (hearing). But sound ≠ hearing. When asked about seeing, we resort to form. This is "yannē kohēda mallē pol" (going where the coconut goes — missing the point). The kriyāva (activity) of hearing is never prakṛta (manifest) — it cannot be vigraha'd (analysed) or described. It is kavaḷam — an indivisible process.
- **Past, present, future — hearing never occurred in any.** The teacher states: in the past, hearing was never hamba vunē nǣ (encountered). In the present, it is not ækennē nǣ (heard). In the future, it will not ækennē nǣ (be heard). If something never occurred in any of the three times, can it ever occur? No. Yet we live as though it has always been there — this is mañññanā.
- **Mañññanā = believing in what was never experienced.** Seeing/hearing has never been at-dækpum (experientially encountered), never been dænagænum (known), never been described by itself. Yet we hold "seeing exists" and "hearing exists" as self-evident. This gap between non-experience and certainty is mañññanā — conceptual construction mistaken for reality.
- **Asaṅkhata — never configured.** When the participant asks "asaṅkhatai nē?" (it's unconditioned, right?), the teacher confirms: sakasvelam nǣ (it was never put together). And when sakasveema (configuration) is seen correctly, what is revealed is sakas noveema (non-configuration) — nirodha. Seeing pavattuma reveals nopævættuma. Seeing nittya reveals anittya. Each time, the seeing is from within — samudaya tuḷin nirōdhaya.
- **The nuvaṇa "grows" (væḍenavā) as mañññanā "wears away" (gevenavā).** As the non-existent base is repeatedly seen for what it is, the mañññanā that sustained the entire structure of "ear + sound = hearing" gradually dissolves. This is what "the path grows" means — not accumulating understanding but letting the mañññanā-sustained structure lose its grip.
- **Sakkāya = arising within diṭṭha suta muta viññāta.** "I" is not born from a mother's body — "mother" herself was hamba vunē (encountered) through diṭṭha suta muta viññāta. The physical body's limbs and head are known through peṇīma (seeing) and dæneema (sensing). So "I" arises from the four, not from a body. Sakkāya's birth (ipadeema) is the arising within these four — which themselves rest on a padanamak nǣti tǣnaka (a groundless ground).
- **Diṭṭhi arises from padanamak nǣti tǣnakin.** The title thesis: the self-view that constitutes "I" arises from a place that has no foundation. The base of seeing/hearing was never established, never experienced, never described — yet diṭṭhi was born from it. This is the fundamental structure of avijjā: a diṭṭhi born from a non-existent base, which then produces the entire world of ættha (exists) and boru (doesn't exist).

## Summary

This is Part 1 of "පදනමක් නැති තැනකින් උපදින දෘෂ්ටිය" (The diṭṭhi that arises from a place without a foundation). The discussion examines how the self-view is born from seeing and hearing — activities that have no demonstrable base — and how this groundless origin makes the diṭṭhi structurally invisible.

The teacher opens with the core observation: "I" was born from peṇīma (seeing) itself. We think that eye and form produce seeing, but the reverse is the case — seeing produces the concepts of "eye" and "form." Since "I" is born from the very base, "I" can never see that base — the feet analogy (paya gahāpu tæna pēnnē nǣ). This is why peṇīma is never recognised as a diṭṭhi (view): the one born from it cannot stand outside it to examine it.

Through the silence exercise, the teacher demonstrates that hearing's base is a nopævættuma (non-existence). During silence, hearing cannot be established — there is no ættha. But non-hearing also cannot be established — there is no paccaya. Both asti and nāsti fail at the base level. The participant is then shown that whenever we speak about hearing, we actually speak about sound — we use an element that is "not hearing" (śabdaya) to explain hearing. Hearing by hearing (æsīma æsīmēn) is never hamba venavā (encountered). This is the kavaḷa — the indivisible activity that refuses all vigraha (analysis).

The teacher extends this across all three times: past, present, future — hearing never occurred in any. Something that never occurred in all three times cannot occur at all. Yet we live as though it has always been there. This certainty-without-experience is mañññanā — and the padanama (foundation) of the entire pævættuma (existence) rests on it.

The participant recognises asaṅkhata — hearing was never sakasveema'd (configured). The teacher shows that seeing sakasveema (construction) correctly reveals sakas noveema (non-construction) — samudaya tuḷinma nirōdhaya. Seeing pavattuma reveals nopævættuma. Seeing nittya reveals anittya. The nuvaṇa "grows" as mañññanā "wears away" — this is the mārgaya (path) developing.

The discussion culminates in the sakkāya insight: "I" was not born from a mother's body. "Mother" was encountered through diṭṭha suta muta viññāta. The body's limbs are known through seeing and sensing. So sakkāya's birth is the arising within these four — which themselves have no base. Diṭṭhi arises from padanamak nǣti tǣnakin — a groundless ground. The teacher names this as the fundamental structure: a diṭṭhi born from a non-existent base, invisible to the very consciousness it produces.
