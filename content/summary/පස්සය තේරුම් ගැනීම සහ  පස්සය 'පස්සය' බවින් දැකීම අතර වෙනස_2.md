## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: *na maññati* — "one does not conceive [a feeler exists]" (the **Kālakārāma / Bāhiya** formula); *kāyañ ca paṭicca poṭṭhabbe ca uppajjati kāya-viññāṇaṁ, tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* ("dependent on the body and tangibles, body-consciousness arises; the meeting of the three is contact" — the **Saḷāyatana / Madhupiṇḍika** formula); perception as a **mirage** (*marīcikūpama saññā*, **SN 22.95**); the **two reed-bundles** leaning (*naḷakalāpī*, **SN 12.67**); the **raft** that, grasped, becomes Māra.
- Terms used: *phassa* / *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* (contact / meeting-of-three contact), *vedanā* / *saññā* (feeling / perception), *sakkāya(-diṭṭhi)* (self-identity view), *puggala* (person), *viññāṇa* / *kāya-viññāṇa* (consciousness / body-consciousness), *maññanā* / *manasikāra* (conceiving / attention), *anicca* / *anatta* / *anuppāda* (impermanence / not-self / non-arising), *sassata* / *uccheda* (eternalism / annihilation), *yathābhūta-svabhāva* (nature as it really is), *samudaya* (arising), *pṛthagjana* (worldling). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **second segment (Part 2)** of **"The Difference Between Understanding Contact and Seeing Contact as 'Contact'"** — the two-speaker dialogue (teacher and student, with a lady, *nōnā*, present). It continues the reflection-in-water image and works the body-and-tangible feeling, the breath and nose, and hearing. The segment ends mid-sentence.

## Main dhamma points
- The second segment, showing that the "feeler" is built from both extremes, that self-identity creeps in at every angle so the view is never caught by the one it produces, that understanding only ties a fresh knot, and that even "impermanence" and "not-self" cannot be applied to the non-arisen nature.
- **The "feeler" is built from both extremes; the view is never caught by the one it produces.** "One does not conceive a feeler exists," yet even where feeling never registered we take "a feeler was there" — "I did not feel it, but I was there" (the never-met annihilation extreme building a view: "the un-feeling me, and now the feeling me is"), and through feeling we also build "through the felt things I arise," and "I exist even apart from the things." The person arises exactly at the knot — *kāyañ ca paṭicca poṭṭhabbe ca uppajjati kāya-viññāṇaṁ, tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* — so "I am" registers only with body-consciousness arising, the whole meeting-of-three a perversion; but because all three mutually condition, feeling cannot be false (a felt thing is there) and the felt thing cannot be false (a feeling is there), so the knot of the two can never be seen *as* a knot, for the "me/view" born of that knot is only its shadow, one face of the very perversion, two sides of one coin — and thus the view is never caught by anyone, since the one is itself born of it.
- **Understanding only ties a fresh knot; self-identity creeps in at every angle.** Consciousness itself is born of this knot, so *phassa* can never be cognized by any consciousness; body-consciousness has meaning only because a felt thing is there, and the mind persists only while the felt thing stays knotted. Understanding "ah, body-and-tangible-and-consciousness, that is the three-knot" only ties another knot — to whom does it register? to the mind-and-object again — so even the Dhamma-point "the Buddha said *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, therefore such a thing exists" arises the mind afresh, which is why the raft too becomes Māra. The other turn is the same trap: "the knot is wrong, there is no ear, no sound" slides to "ear and sound are a mirage, a dream," merely swapping one consciousness for another (ginger given, chili taken) — and harder, since it is a Dhamma-point; "no feeling, not in the body" slides to "there is no feeling," then "feeling exists separately," and the mind arises again. Whichever way one turns, self-identity (*sakkāya*) creeps in everywhere, "I" arising from every angle, and one cannot escape by "not thinking about it," for "I abide not-thinking" is only another mind — like a restless child, whichever way one turns while thinking one is caught; only seeing that here is an un-thinkable nature can free.
- **Contact is un-cognizable, so it is realized only by seeing feeling by feeling.** Recognizing "feeling because of body and tangible" is itself a recognizing done by the mind, a mirage; feeling is not really produced by two things but is a fabrication born of the conceiving "feeling," so that "body and tangible" appear only because feeling was conceived. Hence contact can never be cognized — not within consciousness's range — and from that un-cognizable event "I" arise, so I can never doubt my mind or the Dhamma I have understood, because I myself arise from it. It is untied only by seeing contact by contact: seeing feeling *by feeling*, one cannot grasp feeling and knot the felt thing onto it, and no contact can occur; the very knotting of the two — the two reeds leaning, the thinking about "a connection" — *is* the meeting-of-three contact, and until it is seen as a thinking, an attention, not the slightest doubt about "body, tangible, feeling" can arise.
- **Our abiding is un-cognizable; even "impermanence" and "not-self" cannot be applied to the non-arisen.** A nose and an air-current passing through it are two-hundred-percent true to us, yet we do not feel "there is a nose, a breath" all day; where the two are not understood no feeling is understood, and in that span one can say neither "these are present" nor "these are absent," for the moment one thinks "breath is not felt," breath begins to be felt — so the true nature we stand in is never consciousness's object. Without the capacity to hear, "no sound" cannot register, so one abides *in* hearing, not in non-hearing; "not heard" is un-experienceable, so there one knows neither hearing nor non-hearing, and does not even know one knows neither — un-cognizable to any worldling in all of *saṁsāra* until the un-knowability itself is established as wisdom. What is truly there is a non-persistence, but "non-persistence" is not a thing either, only a way of speaking, like describing a dream; a nature un-cognizable to consciousness cannot even be conceived as "non-persistence," and to recognize it so is itself wrong, which is why, when one sees, even the Dhamma-given explanation falls away with the realization. This is spoken only from within the fallen meeting-of-three knot, not as an absence pointed to; anyone hearing "the non-arisen, impermanent nature" grasps it with the very perversion they are stuck in, so it cannot be spoken apart from *samudaya*, and the seeing too happens only from within one's *samudaya*. "These things are present" is wrong and "these things are absent" is wrong: *existence must be realized as non-existence* — being appearing directly as non-being — for "the occurred thing is absent" is only impermanence (a thing arose and ceased), whereas non-being is "nothing ever occurred," as different as sky from earth. This is *anatta*: even the recognizing "this is not-self" cannot be applied there, for a "not-self-ness" grasped is itself a standing eternalism; consciousness cannot settle where no condition ever arose, so "impermanence" is not something understood and "not-self" is not something understood — and it is precisely at the un-understandable place that, when the wisdom settles, one sees. (The segment breaks off here, mid-sentence.)

## Summary

This second segment shows that the "feeler" is built from both extremes: "one does not conceive a feeler exists," yet even where feeling never registered we take "a feeler was there," the annihilation extreme building "the un-feeling me, and now the feeling me is," while feeling also builds "through the things I arise" and "I exist apart from the things." The person arises exactly at the knot — *kāyañ ca paṭicca poṭṭhabbe ca uppajjati kāya-viññāṇaṁ, tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* — so "I am" registers only with body-consciousness, the meeting-of-three a perversion; but since all three mutually condition, feeling cannot be false and the felt thing cannot be false, so the knot is never seen as a knot, the "me" born of it being only its shadow, one face of the perversion, and the view is never caught by the one it produces. Consciousness itself is born of this knot, so contact can never be cognized, and understanding "that is the three-knot" only ties another knot — even "the Buddha said *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, therefore such a thing exists" arises the mind afresh, the raft becoming Māra; the opposite turn — "no ear, no sound, all a mirage" — merely swaps one consciousness for another, so self-identity creeps in at every angle, and one cannot escape by "not thinking," for that too is a mind.

Recognizing "feeling because of body and tangible" is itself a mirage, feeling being a fabrication born of the conceiving "feeling," so that body and tangible appear only because feeling was conceived; contact is thus un-cognizable, and from it "I" arise, so I can never doubt my mind or my understanding. It is untied only by seeing feeling by feeling, where one cannot knot the felt thing onto feeling and no contact occurs — the very knotting, the two reeds leaning, being the meeting-of-three contact, unseen as a thinking. Our abiding is un-cognizable: a nose and breath are utterly certain to us, yet we do not feel them all day, and in that span one can say neither "present" nor "absent," for the moment one thinks "breath is not felt," breath begins to be felt; without the capacity to hear, "no sound" cannot register, so one abides in hearing, not non-hearing, knowing neither, and not even knowing one knows neither — un-cognizable until the un-knowability is established as wisdom. What is there is a non-persistence, but that too is only a way of speaking, un-conceivable by consciousness, and to recognize it as "non-persistence" is wrong; so even the Dhamma-explanation falls away with the realization. Spoken only from within the fallen knot, not as an absence, it cannot be worded apart from *samudaya*; "these are present" and "these are absent" are both wrong — existence must be realized as non-existence, for "the occurred thing is absent" is mere impermanence, whereas non-being is "nothing ever occurred." This is *anatta*: even "this is not-self" cannot be applied, a grasped "not-self-ness" being itself an eternalism; consciousness cannot settle where no condition arose, so "impermanence" and "not-self" are not things understood — and it is at the un-understandable place that, when wisdom settles, one sees. (This is Part 2 of "The Difference Between Understanding Contact and Seeing Contact as 'Contact'"; it ends mid-sentence.)
