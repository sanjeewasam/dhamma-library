## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted, unattributed by title: *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā'ti, yadā paññāya passati* ("all formations are impermanent — when one sees this *with wisdom*," **Dhammapada 277**); *jānato passato* ("for one who knows and sees"); the Buddha's "**what is gained to rejoice at, what lost to grieve over?**"; the **Satipaṭṭhāna** triad *samudaya-dhammānupassī / vaya-dhammānupassī / samudaya-vaya-dhammānupassī*; *avijjā-paccayā* (with ignorance as condition, the root of dependent origination).
- Named, source uncertain to the speakers: the **Koṭṭhita Sutta** — cited for "wisdom and consciousness cannot be separated" (the speakers say they do not recall it exactly; the point matches the **Mahāvedalla Sutta, MN 43**, *paññā ca viññāṇañca … saṁsaṭṭhā no visaṁsaṭṭhā*, and the title should not be relied on).
- Referred to, unattributed: *phassaṁ phassena passati* (seeing contact by contact); *paññā-vimutti* (liberation by wisdom); the *ubhayanta* (both extremes); a **reflection** (*chāyā*) in water. Terms used: *saṅkhāra* / *saṅkhāra-dukkha* (formation / suffering of formations), *paññā* / *viññāṇa* / *ñāṇa* (wisdom / consciousness / knowledge), *phassa* (contact), *avijjā* (ignorance), *sassata* / *uccheda* (eternalism / annihilation), *nirodha* (cessation). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named, and "Koṭṭhita Sutta" is flagged as uncertain.
- **Notes on the source:** the **third and closing segment (Part 3)** of **"The Difference Between Understanding Contact and Seeing Contact as 'Contact'"** — the two-speaker dialogue (teacher and student, with a lady present). It completes the reflection image and the "both-ends" analysis and closes the series.

## Main dhamma points
- The closing segment, showing that the constructing itself is the suffering seen only by wisdom, that wisdom is neither consciousness nor a separate thing and must itself be let go, and that seeing "is" and "is-not" arising together at the root ends the whole division.
- **The constructing itself is the suffering, seen only by wisdom transcending consciousness.** Where nothing occurred, a construction forms, and that very constructing — the meeting of body, tangible and feeling — is suffering, not the suffering of a constructed thing; this is *saṅkhāra-dukkha*, which a worldling's mind cannot reach, seeing only the suffering of constructed things. Because construction is un-cognizable to consciousness — consciousness itself arising from construction, from contact — the Buddha specially said *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā'ti, yadā paññāya passati*, "seen *with wisdom*," a special vision beyond the world's seeing, transcending consciousness.
- **Wisdom is neither consciousness nor a separate thing, and must itself be let go.** Yet even *saṅkhāra-dukkha* "appears" only because there *is* a construction, so wisdom cannot be separated from consciousness: wisdom is precisely the capacity to see the constructing as suffering, one cannot speak of it apart from consciousness, and neither is it a separate entity, for a "wisdom" set apart would be one more thing, another knot — as the reflection is neither in the water nor in the tree and has no place, so wisdom too is no "thing," used only until the construction ceases (the **Koṭṭhita/Mahāvedalla** point that wisdom and consciousness are conjoined, not disjoined). While there is filth a broom is needed, but on a day of no construction no wisdom is needed, like keeping medicine after the illness is cured; so finally one is freed even from wisdom — not by anyone's doing, but on the day one is established in the non-constructed, wisdom too is released, which is why the arahant is called *paññā-vimutta*, liberated by wisdom.
- **Knowing must first be clear, then become vision; contact is transcended only by seeing contact.** *Jānato passato* — knowing (*jānato*) is the clear preliminary knowledge and seeing (*passato*) the vision; one must first get the knowledge clear so it becomes a vision, for without clarity knowledge is mistaken for vision and one is deceived, taking knowledge as insight. Knowledge is transcended only by seeing contact *by contact*, for contact itself is built on not-knowing — even "contact" is spoken of only because we do not know the not-knowing, being spoken of in dependent origination beneath *avijjā-paccayā* — so one can never escape the "because-of," the contact-knot, until the root of not-knowing is seen.
- **From the two ends one never reaches the middle; hence Satipaṭṭhāna's three.** Grasping the two ends — body and tangible — one can never untie the knot: is feeling in the body? no; in the breath alone? no; so "in neither," which slides straight to the "no feeling" end. From the two sides one never reaches the middle but only lands on another end, which is why Satipaṭṭhāna gives three — *samudaya-dhammānupassī*, *vaya-dhammānupassī*, and *both together* — for if cessation were shown by "passing-away" alone, why the third? The third is seeing both at once. The world associates "is" and "is-not" separately, giving each a separate persistence, but "feeling" is real now not because "non-feeling" exists but because "non-feeling" is absent now, so "is" and "is-not" never fit together and are always two; to see they arise together, wisdom must come to where *both* are non-arisen — to the root, the birth-place, the not-knowing in which the being abides.
- **Seeing "is" and "is-not" arise together at the root ends the division.** Seeing the root, one sees "is" and "is-not" arising together, presence a gaining and absence a gaining — and then the Buddha's "what is gained to rejoice at, what lost to grieve over?" is understood, for what "was not gained" is there, if non-gaining and gaining arise together now? "I had not got these before, now I got them," like "I did not feel the breath before, now I feel it" — but seeing that "breath not felt" and "breath felt" arise together now, the whole base of both falls away, and the moment one says "now heard" one is caught in "not heard before," whereas seeing that "not heard before" was never experienced and arises together with "heard" ends the division. Then the associating of the two ends stops, and consciousness has no ends to grasp but "not-heard / heard, not-seen / seen, not-known / known"; and since all the "not-" talks were never experienced, consciousness's grasping of "not-known" is the grasping of *non-knowing itself*, an un-cognizable, never-experienced annihilation-end — the very non-knowing we abide in from morning, now taken as "absent." Thinking one has experienced the absence of a never-met thing, nothing is gained, the "gaining" reducing to zero; yet taking that non-known's absence as a "thing experienced" fabricates an absence, and that absence appears, with the wisdom, as another presence — a shadow built from it like the reflection, consciousness's magic displaying the un-experienced non-knowing's absence as "feeling now." So is it something that persists now, or something that never persisted? Truly it never persisted — and that is to be seen. (The segment ends here, closing the series.)

## Summary

This closing segment shows that the constructing itself is the suffering. Where nothing occurred a construction forms, and that constructing — the meeting of body, tangible and feeling — is *saṅkhāra-dukkha*, which a worldling cannot reach, seeing only the suffering of constructed things; because construction is un-cognizable to consciousness, which itself arises from contact, the Buddha said *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā'ti, yadā paññāya passati*, "seen with wisdom," a vision transcending consciousness. Yet even that suffering appears only because there is a construction, so wisdom cannot be separated from consciousness — it is the capacity to see the constructing as suffering — and neither is it a separate thing, which would be one more knot, being, like the reflection, in neither water nor tree and with no place; so wisdom is no "thing," used only until the construction ceases, and finally one is freed even from wisdom, established in the non-constructed, which is why the arahant is *paññā-vimutta*. *Jānato passato*: knowing must first be clear, then become vision, for otherwise knowledge is mistaken for vision; and knowledge is transcended only by seeing contact by contact, contact itself being built on not-knowing and spoken of beneath *avijjā-paccayā*, so one never escapes the knot until the root is seen.

From the two ends one never reaches the middle: is feeling in the body? no; in the breath? no; so "in neither," which slides to the "no feeling" end — which is why Satipaṭṭhāna gives three, *samudaya-*, *vaya-*, and both together, for the third is seeing both at once. The world keeps "is" and "is-not" separate, giving each a persistence, but "feeling" is real now only because "non-feeling" is absent now, so the two never fit and are always two; to see they arise together, wisdom must reach where both are non-arisen — the root, the not-knowing in which the being abides. Seeing it, "is" and "is-not" arise together, presence a gaining and absence a gaining, and the Buddha's "what is gained to rejoice at, what lost to grieve over?" is understood, for "I had not got these, now I got them" is like "I did not feel the breath, now I feel it," and seeing that "not felt" and "felt" arise together now, the base of both falls away. Then the associating of the two ends stops, consciousness having only "not-heard / heard, not-known / known," and since the "not-" talks were never experienced, its grasping of "not-known" is the grasping of non-knowing itself, the un-cognizable annihilation-end we abide in from morning, now taken as "absent." Thinking one has experienced the absence of a never-met thing, nothing is gained; yet taking that absence as a thing experienced fabricates an absence that appears, with the wisdom, as another presence — a shadow like the reflection, consciousness's magic displaying the un-experienced non-knowing's absence as "feeling now." So is it something that persists, or that never persisted? Truly it never persisted — and that is to be seen. (This is Part 3, the close of "The Difference Between Understanding Contact and Seeing Contact as 'Contact'.")
