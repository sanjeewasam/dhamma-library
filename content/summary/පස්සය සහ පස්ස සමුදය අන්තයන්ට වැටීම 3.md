## Sutta referred to
- Named / clearly referred to: the **Ānanda Sutta (SN 44.10)** — the wanderer **Vacchagotta** asking "is there a self? / is there no self?", the Buddha's silence, and his explanation to Ānanda (that "there is a self" sides with the eternalists and does not fit the Dhamma, and "there is no self" would make Vaccha think "the self I had is now gone" — annihilation); the four **undeclared (avyākata)** questions on the Tathāgata after death, all falling to extremes; **the "Majjhe" analysis** in which **six elders** each read "one end / other end / middle" (external and internal sense-bases; name and form; past and future with the present the middle; **contact, its arising, its cessation**; etc.) — with the Buddha approving all six; the **Pārāyana-vagga** teaching to the youth **Ajita** of "contact one end, its arising one end, its cessation the middle"; *sotañ ca paṭicca sadde ca uppajjati sota-viññāṇaṁ, tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*; *saḷāyatana-paccayā phasso*; *nevasaññā-nāsaññāyatana* (as the "both/neither" shape).
- Terms used: *phassa* / *phassa-samudaya* / *phassa-nirodha*, *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, *dvayatā* (duality), *sassata* / *uccheda* (*atthi* / *natthi*, "is" / "is-not"), *anuppāda* (non-arising), *maññanā* / *manasikāra* (conceiving / attention), *viññāṇa* (consciousness), *sakkāya* (self-identity). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.

## Main dhamma points
- The closing segment, using Vacchagotta's silence and the six-fold "Majjhe" reading to show that duality is consciousness's own nature, that both contact and its arising fall to the "is/is-not" extremes, that no description ever shows contact, and that only seeing contact by contact — not the constructed event but how it arises — discloses the middle.
- **Both "is" and "is-not" are rejected, because duality is consciousness's own nature.** As the Buddha met Vacchagotta's "is there a self? / is there no self?" with silence — "there is a self" siding with eternalism and not fitting the Dhamma, "there is no self" making Vaccha think "the self I had is now gone," annihilation — so the four undeclared questions on the Tathāgata after death all fall to extremes, and "neither-perception-nor-non-perception" is the "both/neither" shape. The very *nature* of consciousness is duality (*dvayatā*), which is why the six elders' "Majjhe" analyses — external and internal bases, name and form, past and future, self-identity and its arising, contact and its arising — all express the same *sassata* and *uccheda*, "is" and "is-not," operating everywhere, and the Buddha approved all six as well-expounded.
- **Understanding "contact one end, its arising one end" is only a fact; the task is to see how one falls to the two ends.** Merely knowing "contact is one end, *phassa-samudaya* one end" changes nothing — it is a *fact*, taught briefly in the Pārāyana to the youth Ajita, to those with some realization; rote-learned like a parrot it never shows contact, and from the description one can never grasp *how* both contact and its arising fall to the "is/is-not" ends. Hearing is contact; split into "an ear, a sound" it is *phassa-samudaya* — and we fall to *samudaya* as an end by not knowing attention as attention (*maññanā* as *maññanā*): not knowing the conceiving, we take "hearing" as a thing, and with it the heard thing arises, *sotañ ca paṭicca sadde ca uppajjati sota-viññāṇaṁ*, the meeting-of-three being the whole tale, from which contact can never be separated.
- **No description shows contact; we only ever examine the constructed event, not the constructing.** The matchbox, matchstick and fire together *are* the *phassa-samudaya*, present "because of contact"; but examining that event we are again looking at the *constructed* story, not the constructing — as one who traces the hen from the egg and the egg from the hen never ends, so "fire only because of matchbox-and-stick / no fire without them" are both extremes the example can never escape, and however much cause and fruit are discussed, no fault of contact appears, for we examine only what contact produced. What must be seen is not the *happened event* but *how it happened* — the arising itself — and everyone's error is to describe the happened thing while never seeing how it comes to be, so the root is never severed, like trimming the branches while the root stays in the tree.
- **"Striking" itself falls to both ends; only seeing contact by contact discloses the middle.** Asked what "striking" (*ulanawā*, the contact) is, one can form no idea from "striking" alone; asked "is striking in the matchbox?" — wrong; "in the stick?" — wrong; and one cannot say "in neither" either — so examining "striking" one falls to an extreme too. Taking "striking is not in the two" one is caught at first glance in the "is-not" (*natthi*), and from that very statement the other extreme is caught as well: "not in the two, *but there is* a contact" lands on *both* ends at once — as the younger sister, taking "there is no class," troubled her mother and came home on that "not," a whole tangle built on an absence made real. "As a fact" it is correct that contact is not found in the two causes, yet that very correctness is the fault. So only the "striking" example, rightly seen, shows how *both* the arising and contact fall to the "is/is-not" extremes; and until contact is seen — seen *by* contact — no cessation of contact is met, and "the arising of contact" and "contact" never even register rightly, this being the death-ground where everyone is stuck. Seen through, the two ends are found *not to be gone to*, and right there arising and cessation appear together — the tale's arising seen, and its non-arising seen — which no matchbox description can ever disclose.
