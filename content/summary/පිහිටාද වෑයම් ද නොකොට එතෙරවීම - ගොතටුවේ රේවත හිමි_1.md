## Sutta referred to

- **"Yō dhammaṃ passati sō maṃ passati"** — the teacher asks what one must see to see the Dhamma, and answers: paṭicca samuppāda / paṭicca samuppanna — the principle of conditionality, which is what distinguishes the Buddha's teaching from every other system in the world.
- **Paṭicca samuppāda** is the structural centre throughout. The teacher repeatedly returns to the formula: hētu paccayēn udāvenavā vǣyavenavā (arising and ceasing through conditions). In such a place, nothing — no person, no thing — can be designated (panavanna bǣ).
- **Mūlapariyāya Sutta** — the three levels of mañññanā: pruthagjana mañññati (the ordinary person constructs), sēkha mā mañññati (the learner does not construct), asēkha na mañññati (the adept cannot construct). The teacher clarifies: the pruthagjana sees the asēkha's mind directly — that is how one becomes sēkha, not by gradually reducing mañññanā.
- **Girimananda Sutta** — referenced for the sequence: anicca saññā, anatta saññā, asubha saññā, sabba lōkē anabhirata saññā, then aniccānupassanā. The teacher stresses that aniccānupassanā can only be practised after anicca has been seen — not as a method to arrive at anicca.
- **"Vivēka nissitaṃ, virāga nissitaṃ, vossagga pariṇāmiṃ"** — the teacher redefines these: vivēka = vasagē nǣ (not under control) arises naturally; virāga = rāga venna bǣ (desire cannot form); vossagga pariṇāmī = saṃsargayak vennam bǣ (entanglement is impossible) — all three are consequences of seeing, not practices to perform.
- The **bone-without-meat simile** (aṭṭhi) — a dog gnawing a meatless bone draws only its own blood. Avijjā = believing meat existed on the bone before biting. Saṅkhāra = the gnawing effort. The teacher uses this to show the structure of āyāsa (striving) applied to what was never there.
- **Jāti pi dukkhā** and the four nutriments (satura mahā dhātu, phassa, viññāṇāhāra, manōsañcētanā) are positioned in the arising sequence: avijjā → kamma → taṇhā (place-making) → āhāra → jāti → pañca upādānakkhandha → cycle.
- **Viññāṇa as māyā** — the teacher invokes the sutta statement that viññāṇa is māyā and saññā is a mirage (miriguvak): in a place where nothing can be designated, the appearance of a designatable thing is itself the māyā.
- The **Dārucīriya Thero** story — who, upon seeing, struck immediately (at the source). Contrasted with **Visākhā Mahōpāsikā** — who saw (diṭṭhi sampanna) but proceeded gradually, still having children, not yet arahant.

## Main dhamma points

- **The flower is already taken before you "see" it.** The teacher opens with the question: was the flower already selected (tērilā) before analysis? Yes — the moment "flower" is grasped, the colour is ascribed to the flower. But colour comes through the eye, and the flower is not in the colour nor the colour in the flower. The flower drawn on the board is already varaḍi (wrong) — it was never a unity of flower-and-colour. This demonstrates how the process of perception is prior to the perceived object.
- **The perception process cannot be stopped.** Rūpa, śabda, gandha, rasa, sparśa — the five sense-objects — condition the process. The mind (manasa) is a lōkāyatana indriya: it shows worldly things by nature. The process is inherently unknowable from within — like a vomit, it cannot be halted or inspected mid-flow.
- **Avijjā = grasping "this exists" before encountering it.** The teacher defines avijjā as the prior commitment: mēka tiyenavā kiyalā darupu goṅkama (the foolishness of having already held "this exists"). From avijjā, kamma arises — something to think, say, do (hitanna kiyanna karanna). From kamma, a place (tænak) is found — taṇhā. From taṇhā, the four nutriments sustain it. This is nibandhanaya (bondage), producing jāti and then pañca upādānakkhandha.
- **The saṃsāra cycle: thing → five aggregates → thing.** The flower (thing) produces the pañcupādānakkhandha; the pañcupādānakkhandha produces a new flower (thing). This cycle never ends — anavarāgra saṃsāra. The teacher draws this graphically: malēn paha enavā, pahēn mala enavā (from flower comes five, from five comes flower).
- **In hētu-paccaya, nothing can be designated.** The teacher's central thesis: hētu paccayēn udāvena vǣyavena tænak yamek hō yamak panavanna bǣ (in a place of conditional arising and ceasing, no person or thing can be established). The milk-tea analogy: can a frog come from milk-powder, sugar, tea-leaves, hot water? No — it cannot come from the ingredients. Similarly, can "milk-tea" be found in any ingredient? No. The "tea" is māyā — it appears but cannot be located in the causes. The wall analogy reinforces: stones, sand, cement, water → wall, but "wall" exists in none of them. What appears from causes yet cannot be found in the causes is māyā.
- **Viññāṇa is māyā, saññā is mirage.** The teacher invokes the sutta: in a place where nothing can be designated (panavanna bǣ), viññāṇa shows something designatable — that showing is māyā. Saññā (e.g. "sister") arises where "sister" cannot be designated — that is mirage. Neither can be stopped — that is the pēḷīma (torment) the arahant still endures until parinibbāna.
- **The bone simile: avijjā is believing meat exists before biting.** The meatless bone (mas lē næti æṭa) parallels the groundless ground. The dog gnaws and gets only its own blood. Avijjā = holding "meat exists" (mas tiyenavā kiyalā kalim-ma darana eka). Saṅkhāra = the gnawing (hapana eka = vehesak). Without avijjā, there is no saṅkhāra. Without saṅkhāra, no blood is drawn.
- **Grasping and releasing: the fist analogy.** Something is held in the fist. Two options: ælleema (holding) or atahæreema (releasing). The Buddha does not say "atārinnavā" (you release) but "atǣrenavā" (it releases itself). The teacher asks: "ærala balahan hambavenavāda kiyalā ekak" — open and see if anything is found. Nothing is found. The "release" is not effort but discovery.
- **The wise seek the ant-hill, not the snake.** The snake (nayā) analogy: when a deadly snake appears, the fool fights the snake or runs. The wise seek the tuṃbasa (ant-hill/source). Finding the ant-hill, one is willing to let the snake bite — because the source is seen. Dārucīriya Thero saw the ant-hill and struck immediately. Visākhā Mahōpāsikā saw it but proceeded gradually (kāgena, slowly — still had children).
- **Diṭṭhi sampanna requires no further bhāvanā.** Once diṭṭhi is straightened (sṛju), vācā, kammanta, ājīva, sati follow naturally. The person stands in samādhi directly. From samādhi, sammā ñāṇa and sammā vimutti complete themselves. The only āyāsa (effort) in the teaching is satipaṭṭhāna pitiṭavanna (establishing mindfulness). The lotus-leaf analogy: water cannot rest on the arahant's citta (like a lotus leaf). The pruthagjana's citta is like kapuru (camphor cloth) — it absorbs. The diṭṭhi sampanna sees the lotus-leaf nature but their own leaf is not yet lotus — so they bear it with effort (āyāsayen mitin daragena yanavā).
- **Hētu vs paccaya distinction.** Hētu = the seed/cause (e.g. kalyāṇa mitta saṃsēvanaya is a hētu for sōvān). Paccaya = the condition that enables the next hētu (e.g. kalyāṇa mitta saṃsēvanaya is paccaya for saddhamma śravaṇaya). Neither stands without the other. In the teaching: avijjā, kamma, taṇhā, āhāra, nibandha lakkhaṇa are hētu; avijjā is paccaya for kamma, for āhāra, for saṅkhāra, for nibandha.
- **No phala (result) can be designated in hētu-paccaya.** The teacher's final point: hētu paccayēn udāvena vǣyavena tænak prathiphalayak panavanna bǣ (in conditional arising-ceasing, no result can be established). Those who do not know this designate results (prathiphala panavana-vā). When this is understood, avijjā is dispelled — and with it, the ground (tæna/pōḷava) on which taṇhā stood.

## Summary

This is Part 1 of "පිහිටාද වෑයම් ද නොකොට එතෙරවීම" (Crossing over without establishing or striving), a teaching by Ven. Rēvata of Gotatuva. The discussion demonstrates that in a world governed by hētu-paccaya (conditionality), nothing can be designated, no one can be established, and therefore no āyāsa (effort/striving) and no pitiṭīma (establishing/standing-ground) are required for liberation — or even possible.

The teacher opens with the flower exercise: the flower was already "taken" before analysis began. Colour came through the eye, but neither flower nor colour contains the other — the flower-with-colour was never a real unity. This demonstrates that the process of perception precedes and produces the perceived object, not the reverse. The process — rūpa, śabda, gandha, rasa, sparśa conditioning the mind — cannot be stopped because the mind (manasa) is a lōkāyatana indriya by nature.

The teacher then lays out the arising sequence: avijjā (grasping "this exists" before encountering it) → kamma (something to think/say/do) → taṇhā (a place to stand) → the four nutriments → jāti → pañca upādānakkhandha. From the aggregates, a new "thing" is perceived, and the cycle repeats. This is the saṃsāra cakra — malēn paha enavā, pahēn mala enavā (from flower comes five, from five comes flower) — anavarāgra, without discoverable beginning.

The central thesis emerges through the milk-tea and wall analogies: in hētu-paccaya, nothing can be designated. Milk-tea cannot be found in milk-powder, sugar, tea-leaves, or hot water — yet "tea" appears. A wall cannot be found in stones, sand, cement, or water — yet "wall" appears. What appears from causes yet cannot be found in causes is māyā. The teacher invokes the sutta: viññāṇa is māyā (showing a designatable thing where nothing can be designated), saññā is mirage (naming "sister" where "sister" cannot be established).

The bone simile crystallises the avijjā structure: a dog gnaws a meatless bone, drawing only its own blood. Avijjā = the prior belief that meat exists. Saṅkhāra = the gnawing effort. Without the prior belief, there is no effort, and no blood is drawn. The fist analogy follows: something is held in the hand. The Buddha does not say "release it" (āyāsa) but "it releases itself" (atǣrenavā) — open and see whether anything was ever there.

The snake-and-ant-hill analogy distinguishes the wise from the foolish: when the snake appears, the wise seek the tuṃbasa (source), not the snake. Dārucīriya Thero, upon seeing the source, struck immediately — arahant. Visākhā Mahōpāsikā saw it but proceeded gradually — diṭṭhi sampanna with children, path still unfolding.

The teacher then shows that diṭṭhi sampanna requires no further "bhāvanā" in the conventional sense. Once diṭṭhi is straightened, sīla, samādhi, paññā complete themselves. The only effort is establishing sati — and even that is understood as the natural consequence of seeing. The Girimananda Sutta is cited: aniccānupassanā comes after seeing anicca, not as a method to arrive at it. The Mūlapariyāya Sutta's three levels (mañññati → mā mañññati → na mañññati) show that the pruthagjana becomes sēkha by directly seeing the asēkha's mind — not by gradual reduction.

The discussion closes with the hētu-paccaya distinction: hētu is the seed, paccaya is the enabling condition. Neither stands alone. In the teaching, avijjā is paccaya for everything else. And the final point: hētu paccayēn prathiphalayak panavanna bǣ — no result can be designated in conditionality. Those who designate results are still in avijjā. When this is seen, avijjā is dispelled, the ground (pōḷava) dissolves, and taṇhā has nowhere to stand.
