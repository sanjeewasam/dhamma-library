## Sutta referred to

- **The ōgha taraṇa (flood crossing)** — the Buddha's reply to a deva: "piṭiṭāda nōsiṭimin vǣyamda nōkaramin" (without standing and without striving, I crossed the flood). The teacher builds the entire discussion around this statement, showing that both piṭiṭā hiṭīma (standing/establishing) and vǣyam kireema (striving) are responses to an ōgha (flood) — and when the ōgha is seen for what it is, neither is needed.
- **Puñña, apuñña, āneñja abhisaṅkhāra** — the three types of volitional formation. The teacher shows these are the only three responses to pēḷīma (torment/pressure), and all three require avijjā paccayā. The puñña response (running away from the blow) = meritorious action. The apuñña response (hitting back) = demeritorious action. The āneñja response ("I don't care, this kāma world is worthless") = imperturbable formation. All three perpetuate saṃsāra.
- **Aggañña Sutta** — referenced for the rebirth mechanism: the being who dies with a lōka-dheyya (worldly thing) in mind is reborn through nāmyatāva (naming tendency) and rūpaṇaya (forming). The teacher corrects the common assumption that a human who thinks like a dog becomes a dog — instead, the mechanism operates through the fundamental nāma-rūpa process, not through resemblance.
- **"Cakkhuṃ bhikkhavē aniccaṃ"** — the teacher asks whether the sutta says cakkhāyatanaṃ or cakkhuṃ, drawing the distinction: the arahant retains cakkhu (the eye organ) but not cakkhāyatana (the eye as āyatana/base of perception). Even arahants experience pēḷīma from the pañcaskandha.
- **"Sacittapariyōdapanaṃ"** (purification of one's own citta) — the teacher contrasts this with sakāya pariyōdapana (purification of body/externals). The āryā śrāvaka targets citta, not the sensory objects or sense-restraint.
- **Iccā, sukha, attā, subha saññā** — the four distorted perceptions. The teacher demonstrates that each person's version of "the car" is already unique (each person's indriya produce a unique rendition), so "my version" = iccā saññā is already embedded in the act of perceiving.

## Main dhamma points

- **The infinite swimming pool: piṭiṭā hiṭīma and vǣyam are the only two options — and both fail.** The teacher poses the riddle: thrown into an infinite pool, you cannot swim (vǣyam — striving exhausts), cannot float/stand still (piṭiṭā hiṭīma — standing requires a ground), and no rescue is coming. How do you survive? The answer is: there is no option within the ōgha. The question itself contains the answer — the problem is the ōgha, not the strategy.
- **The Buddha's statement decoded: if there is no ōgha, there is no need for piṭiṭā or vǣyam.** The teacher reframes: piṭiṭā hiṭīma and vǣyam are only necessary because there is an ōgha. Remove the water from the pool — now neither standing nor striving applies. The Buddha did not use a superior technique to cross; the Buddha saw that the ōgha (the four floods: kāma, bhava, diṭṭhi, avijjā) is not what it appears.
- **Pēḷīma (torment) cannot be "removed" — it must cease on its own.** The teacher demonstrates through repeated Socratic questioning: pēḷīma næti karanna bǣ (torment cannot be removed). Removing it is āyāsa (effort), which is itself torment. Not-removing it is also āyāsa (bearing/enduring). The only resolution: pēḷīma næti venna ōnē (torment must cease on its own). This requires seeing the hētu (cause) from which it arises, not attacking the pēḷīma itself.
- **The causal chain: pēḷīma ← balāpōrotthuva ← kæmætti ← vaṭinākama ← asatpuruṣa saṃsēvanaya ← yamek hō yamak ← avijjā.** The teacher traces the chain backwards: torment arises from expectation (balāpōrotthuva), expectation from desire (kæmætti), desire from value-assignment (vaṭinākama), value from asatpuruṣa association, asatpuruṣa from the foundational belief yamek hō yamak tiyenavā (someone or something exists). That foundational belief IS avijjā. Even a newborn child already operates from yamek hō yamak — asking "ammē, ara mokakda?" (mother, what is that?) proves the child already knows "something is there."
- **Value cannot be removed — and removing is itself a value-assignment.** The teacher shows: vaṭinākamak næti karanna yana eka (going to remove value) itself assigns value to the removing. Not-assigning-value is āyāsa (restraint/sīlavrata). The only resolution: cutting the place where value is born. That place is the foundational yamek hō yamak.
- **The car exercise: raw sense-data contain no "car."** Shown an image of a car, everyone sees "a car." But the eye only receives colour (pāṭa). The ear only receives sound. No single indriya delivers "car." The "car" is constructed by the mind using accumulated data from all five indriya plus past memory. In the raw data (rūpa, śabda, gandha, rasa, sparśa), there is no car, no sitting, no driving — these are informational constructions.
- **Each person's car is unique — iccā saññā is embedded in perceiving.** Since each person's indriya are physically unique (different eyes = different colour reception, different ears = different sound capture), the "car" that forms in each mind is ananya (unique to that person). Every perceived object is already "my version" — iccā saññā (the perception of desire/ownership) is not added later but is constitutive of the perceiving act itself.
- **Rebirth through nāmyatāva and rūpaṇaya.** At death, the pañcēndriya shuts down. Only the mind remains. The last moment of a pruthagjana's mind necessarily holds a lōka-dheyya (worldly thing) — because worldly things require pañcēndriya data, and the mind has accumulated that data. The mind, through its nāmyatāva (naming tendency), produces rūpaṇaya (forming) — and rebirth occurs. The teacher clarifies: a human does not become a dog by "thinking like a dog." The Aggañña Sutta shows the mechanism operates through nāma-rūpa itself, not through imitation.
- **Sacittapariyōdapana — the āryā śrāvaka targets citta.** The teacher distinguishes: the pruthagjana, upon hearing dhamma, tries to remove external objects ("cars don't exist outside") — removing the vāhanaya. Slightly more advanced, they try to close the indriya (bamunu bhāvanā — brahmanical meditation, sense-restraint). But the āryā śrāvaka goes to sacittapariyōdapana — purifying citta itself, not the objects or the senses.
- **Cakkhu vs cakkhāyatana — the arahant's pēḷīma.** The sutta says cakkhuṃ aniccaṃ, not cakkhāyatanaṃ aniccaṃ. The arahant still has cakkhu (eye) and still pēḷīma (torment of seeing). But without cakkhāyatana (the eye functioning as āyatana — producing yamek hō yamak), the seeing does not generate saṃsāra. Pañcaskandha itself is pēḷīma — even for the arahant until parinibbāna.

## Summary

This is Part 2 of "පිහිටාද වෑයම් ද නොකොට එතෙරවීම" (Crossing over without establishing or striving). The teacher deepens the analysis of the title theme by demonstrating through the ōgha (flood) analogy that both piṭiṭā hiṭīma (establishing/standing) and vǣyam (striving) are responses to a perceived flood — and when the flood is seen for what it is, neither is required.

The session opens with the swimming-pool riddle: thrown into an infinite pool with no rescue, you cannot swim (exhaustion) and cannot float/stand (no ground). How do you survive? The participants struggle — and the teacher reveals that the question is designed to show that within the ōgha, there is no solution. The Buddha's statement "piṭiṭāda nōsiṭimin vǣyamda nōkaramin" does not describe a superior technique but a seeing that eliminates the ōgha as an ōgha.

The teacher then traces the causal chain behind pēḷīma (torment): torment → expectation → desire → value-assignment → asatpuruṣa association → the foundational belief yamek hō yamak tiyenavā (someone or something exists). That belief IS avijjā. Through Socratic questioning, the teacher shows that each link in the chain cannot be "removed" — removing is itself āyāsa, which is itself pēḷīma. Value cannot be not-assigned (that is sīlavrata restraint, a new āyāsa). Expectation cannot be abandoned (abandoning is a new expectation). The only resolution: the foundational belief from which the entire chain springs must be seen for what it is.

The car exercise makes this concrete: an image of a car is shown. The eye receives only colour. No single indriya delivers "car." The car is a mental construction from accumulated pañcēndriya data. Moreover, since each person's indriya are physically unique, each person's "car" is unique — iccā saññā (the ownership-perception) is not added after seeing but is constitutive of the seeing act. This is where the four distortions (nicca, sukha, attā, subha saññā) operate — at the level of perceiving itself, not at the level of deliberate grasping.

The teacher addresses the rebirth mechanism: at death, pañcēndriya shuts down, only mind remains. A pruthagjana's last moment necessarily holds a worldly thing (constructed from pañcēndriya data). Through nāmyatāva (naming tendency) and rūpaṇaya (forming), rebirth occurs — not through resemblance to a specific being but through the nāma-rūpa process itself. The Aggañña Sutta is referenced.

The session then distinguishes three levels of approach: the pruthagjana removes external objects ("cars don't exist outside"), the brāhmaṇa restrains the senses (indriya vāhana), but the āryā śrāvaka goes to sacittapariyōdapana — purifying citta itself. The teacher closes by drawing the cakkhu/cakkhāyatana distinction: the arahant retains cakkhu and still experiences pēḷīma (pañcaskandha is intrinsically pēḷīma), but without cakkhāyatana functioning as āyatana, the perceiving does not generate yamek hō yamak and therefore does not generate saṃsāra.
