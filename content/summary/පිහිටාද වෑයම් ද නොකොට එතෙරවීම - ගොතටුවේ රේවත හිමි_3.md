## Sutta referred to

- **"Ruppatīti kho bhikkhavē tasmā rūpanti vuccati"** — the teacher corrects a common reversal: the Buddha said "because of ruppana (being afflicted/affected), it is called rūpa" — NOT "rūpa is afflicted." The causation runs from ruppana to the designation "rūpa," not the other way. Confusing this reverses the paṭicca samuppāda direction and entangles the practitioner.
- **Phēṇapiṇḍūpama Sutta** (foam-mass simile) — foam, water-bubble, mirage, banana trunk, viññāṇa as māyā. The teacher stresses these point to asāra: not "no essence" but "essence cannot be found" (haraya hoyannam bǣ). A seven-year-old child can see nibbāna through these, but only after saddhamma śravaṇa from a kalyāṇa mitta — the sutta is for those who have already seen the kēravala (trick), not for those trying to see through the text.
- **Ōpanayikō** — "to be brought close to oneself." The teacher emphasises: dhammānudhamma paṭipadā alone is not sufficient — saddhamma must be heard. Kalyāṇa mittas cannot be identified by appearance; one must listen to many and apply the kēravala test everywhere.
- **Aṭṭhaka Nāgara Sutta** — referenced for the aṭṭha samāpatti (eight attainments). The teacher shows that the practitioner "places the mind" sequentially toward nibbāna — but nibbāna = the nivīma (cooling/cessation) that occurs when it is seen that nothing can be designated in hētu-paccaya. No āyāsa is involved; the mind settles by the weight of what was seen.
- **"Diṭṭhēva diṭṭhaṃ bhavissati"** — the teaching given to Dārucīriya Thero. Diṭṭhēva = in the seen, only the seen. The teacher draws out: nājjhōsāya ṭiṭṭhati = "does not stand established" — meaning piṭiṭanna bǣruva yanavā (becomes unable to establish), not a volitional "I will not cling."
- **"Sabbē saṅkhārā aniccā, sabbē saṅkhārā dukkhā, sabbē dhammā anattā"** — the teacher asks why the third uses dhammā instead of saṅkhārā. Saṅkhāra (volitional formations) require avijjā. When avijjā is removed, saṅkhāra cease — hence anicca and dukkha target saṅkhāra. But anattā targets dhammā — the dhamma-manas ghaṭṭana (mind-object contact) from which avijjā itself arises. Anattā strikes at the foundation that produces saṅkhāra.
- **"Yadaniccaṃ taṃ dukkhaṃ, taṃ dukkhaṃ tadanattā"** — used to show the progression: anicca → dukkha → anattā. But the teacher clarifies that anattā is not merely anicca extended — it is a qualitatively different seeing that targets the dhamma level.
- Ven. **Sāriputta** — referenced for the balu kuṇē (dog carcass) analogy: carrying the pañcaskandha around the neck like a dog carcass. The arahant has no māna (conceit) — not "does not have conceit" but "cannot have conceit" (mānayak tiyennam bǣ). The arahant endures the pēḷīma like an aṅkēṭa icca goṇā (a bull with its horns stuck), waiting for parinibbāna.

## Main dhamma points

- **The spinning creates the circle, not the circle that spins.** The teacher uses the spinning-fire analogy: when something spins, a circle appears (ōpnæṅvenavā). But if you say "a circle appears" (raumatak ōpnæṅvenavā), you've created a rauma (circle) that can now "appear" — adding an entity. The not-knowing (paṭicca samuppanna nodanna nisā) causes "the world" to be designated. This is viññāṇayē māyāva — the māyā of consciousness.
- **Ruppana → rūpa, not rūpa → ruppana.** The sutta says: because of ruppana (affliction/affecting), it is called rūpa. We reverse this: "rūpa is afflicted" — which presupposes rūpa as an existing thing that undergoes affliction. The Buddha's formulation keeps rūpa as a designation arising from the process, not a thing undergoing a process. This single reversal is where the "pot tangles" (poṭa pæṭalenavā).
- **Phēṇapiṇḍūpama: asāra = "essence cannot be found."** The five aggregates are compared to foam, bubble, mirage, banana trunk, māyā. The teacher stresses: asāra does not mean "no essence" (haraya nǣ) but "essence cannot be found" (haraya hoyannam bǣ). The one who has seen the kēravala reads the pēḷa (text) and immediately sees "hari" (correct). The text confirms what was already seen through saddhamma śravaṇa — it does not produce the seeing.
- **Fourth jhāna: avijjā is prathyakṣa'd, not "understood."** The teacher asks: does avijjā become understood through progressively deeper jhāna? No — rāga and dveṣa are seen in earlier jhāna, but avijjā is never "understood" through meditation. Rather, the avijjā that was already understood through saddhamma śravaṇa is prathyakṣa'd (directly realized) in fourth jhāna. There is no āyāsa in this progression — the practitioner is not "developing toward" avijjā-removal but placing a mind that has already seen.
- **The pit analogy: satpuruṣa = the sun, saddhamma = the light, mūlāva = avijjā.** A person falls into a deep pit at night, seizes a root in terror, hangs for eight hours. At dawn, looking down: the ground is one foot below. The eight hours of terror were tuccā (worthless/pointless). The root (mūlāva = avijjā = the grasped belief) releases itself — the hand opens (at ǣrenavā, not atārinnavā). This is sabbā saṅkhāra samatha saññā — all formations settle when the actual distance is seen. The sun is the satpuruṣa. The light is saddhamma. The root that was gripped is avijjā.
- **Nājjhōsāya ṭiṭṭhati: "becomes unable to establish."** The teacher draws the distinction in the Dārucīriya teaching: the arahant does not "choose not to cling" — the mind becomes unable to establish (piṭiṭannam bǣruva yanavā). The arahant's citta is durvala (weak/thin) compared to the pruthagjana's — it cannot sustain a piṭiṭīma. This is why piṭiṭā hiṭīma is impossible for the arahant, not merely avoided.
- **Pañcaskandha = pēḷīma; pañcupādānaskandha = the pēḷīma acted upon.** The arahant's pañcaskandha is the balu kuṇē (dog carcass) around the neck — it cannot be removed until parinibbāna. But without upādāna, there is no pañcupādānaskandha — no kuṇu paṇuvō (maggots). The arahant lives as the aṅkēṭa icca goṇā — enduring, without ramaṇa (delighting), waiting for the māyā show to end.
- **Sabbē saṅkhārā anicca/dukkha vs sabbē dhammā anattā.** Saṅkhāra require avijjā; remove avijjā and saṅkhāra cease. So anicca and dukkha target saṅkhāra. But anattā targets dhammā — the dhamma-manas ghaṭṭana from which avijjā arises. This is deeper: it strikes at the very contact that produces the not-knowing. Na attā = āthmāyi kiyalā darannam bǣ (cannot be held as ātma/self) — not "self doesn't exist" but "self-holding becomes impossible."
- **Āyatana emptying: "the eye itself" (æsama), not "the eye's contents."** The teacher warns: "æsa his karanna" (empty the eye) is commonly misread as "empty what is in the eye" — which keeps the building (the āyatana) and empties the contents. This is sassata or ucchēda. The correct reading is æsama his karanna — the eye itself is emptied. The building does not remain. This produces 64 diṭṭhi if misunderstood, covering all 31 planes.

## Summary

This is Part 3 — the concluding segment — of "පිහිටාද වෑයම් ද නොකොට එතෙරවීම" (Crossing over without establishing or striving). The teacher brings the three-part analysis to its culmination by showing that the seeing (not the doing) produces the crossing — and that the entire apparatus of piṭiṭā hiṭīma and vǣyam dissolves when the foundational structure is seen.

The session opens with the spinning-fire analogy: spinning produces the appearance of a circle, but designating "a circle appears" creates an entity where none exists. This is paṭicca samuppanna nodanna nisā — the world is designated because the conditioned-arising principle is not known. The teacher then corrects a crucial sutta reversal: "ruppatīti kho bhikkhavē tasmā rūpanti vuccati" means ruppana produces the designation "rūpa" — not that rūpa undergoes ruppana. Reversing this presupposes rūpa as an existing thing, which is exactly the avijjā the sutta is dismantling.

The Phēṇapiṇḍūpama is discussed: foam, bubble, mirage, banana trunk, māyā. The teacher stresses that asāra means haraya hoyannam bǣ (essence cannot be found), not haraya nǣ (no essence). The sutta confirms what was already seen through kalyāṇa mitta's saddhamma — it does not produce the seeing. A seven-year-old can see nibbāna through it, but only with the prior kalyāṇa mitta foundation.

The teacher addresses the jhāna progression: in fourth jhāna, avijjānusaya is prathyakṣa'd — directly realized — not "understood for the first time." The avijjā was already understood through saddhamma śravaṇa. The Aṭṭhaka Nāgara Sutta shows the mind being placed sequentially, not developed toward nibbāna through effort. There is no āyāsa.

The pit analogy brings this to life: a person grips a root in a deep pit at night, terrified. At dawn (satpuruṣa = sun, saddhamma = light), looking down reveals the ground is one foot away. The eight hours of terror were tuccā. The hand opens by itself — the root (avijjā) releases. This is sabbā saṅkhāra samatha saññā: all formations settle when the truth is seen. No effort produced the release.

The Dārucīriya teaching is unpacked: "nājjhōsāya ṭiṭṭhati" means piṭiṭannam bǣruva yanavā — the mind becomes unable to establish, not "chooses not to cling." The arahant's citta is durvala — too thin to sustain a piṭiṭīma. The pañcaskandha remains as pēḷīma (the balu kuṇē around the neck), but without upādāna there are no kuṇu paṇuvō (maggots) — no pañcupādānaskandha. The arahant endures, waiting for parinibbāna as the māyā show ends.

The discussion closes with the anicca/dukkha vs anattā distinction: saṅkhāra are targeted by anicca and dukkha (because saṅkhāra require avijjā, and removing avijjā removes saṅkhāra). But dhammā are targeted by anattā — because anattā strikes at the dhamma-manas ghaṭṭana that produces avijjā itself. Na attā is not "no self" but "self-holding becomes impossible." The teacher's final caution: "emptying the eye" means æsama his karanna (the eye itself), not emptying the eye's contents while keeping the eye. Keeping the building and emptying the contents is sassata or ucchēda — 64 diṭṭhi covering all 31 planes.
