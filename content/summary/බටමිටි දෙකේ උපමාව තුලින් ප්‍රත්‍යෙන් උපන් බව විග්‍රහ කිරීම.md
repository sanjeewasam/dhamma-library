## Main dhamma points
- A sustained working of the two-reed-bundles simile, showing that "seeking the truth" is itself a self-view trap, that the "cause" by which the reeds stand is un-locatable (contact), and that the whole scheme — the Four Truths, the five aggregates, the twelve links, the three marks — is disclosed in one place when the reeds' being-conditioned is seen with wisdom.
- **"Seeking the truth" is itself a self-view; the magician's dove shows why.** We think our fault is that we are "searching for the truth," but the Buddha said to turn over and examine what is *false* — the five clinging-aggregates, a wholly constructed nature — for if one hunts for "a truth to be found," then on the day it is found the "me who finds the truth" still stands unchanged, the self merely switching tracks from "falsehood-and-me" to "truth-and-me." As a magician's "dove from a handkerchief" is *magic* only to one who does not know the trick, and to one who does no dove is met (the event never happened), so the wisdom that sees the delusion *as* delusion finds neither a "truth" nor a "falsehood" — nothing occurred to be called real or false; there is no witness for "true" and none for "false." That is the supramundane, un-classifiable into "true" or "deluded," because nothing was ever met.
- **The "cause" by which the reeds stand is un-locatable — this is contact.** Two reed-bundles stand upright only leaning on each other — A upright because of B, B because of A — and neither has the capacity to stand alone, yet "standing upright" is manifest, so "it stands because of the cause." But show the "cause": one can point only to A or to B, never to the cause itself, which is not in A, not in B, not in the two together, not apart from them, and findable nowhere in the world — yet a "capacity" is manifest at the joining, sure to be there and sure to be in neither, like a dream that appears though it exists nowhere. This un-locatable "because-of" is *phassa*, contact; the "capacity" and the reeds' upright-standing are speakable only each with the other, an un-severable wordless connection — "arisen from condition" (*paccayen-upan*) — whose mark is that no persistence can be spoken of it. Consciousness sees only "a hundred percent right"; only wisdom (*paññā*) sees the fault of the "basis," for the fault is one the eye can never see.
- **The manifest persistence is self-perception; seen through, it is impermanent, suffering, not-self.** The manifest "upright-standing persistence" is exactly what we take as "is" — *ātma-saññā*, the sense of existing; but wisdom sees that no persistence really exists in the cause or in the two reeds, which is *anatta*; that nothing truly arose, no real "capacity" was born, which is *anicca*; and that a persistence *appears* where no persistence can be given, which is *dukkha* — the very suffering of construction (*saṅkhāra-dukkha*), a fashioning where nothing was ever fashioned. So one can say neither "is" (for no persistence really stands) nor "is-not" (for it plainly manifests): seeing the arising (*samudaya*), one is freed from the "is-not" extreme, and its manifesting frees one from the "is" — both extremes transcended.
- **The whole scheme is disclosed in one place, and named only afterward.** Seeing this, the Four Truths stand together in one spot: the manifest persistence is *dukkha*; the constructing of the leaning reeds is *samudaya*, whose "cause," searched, is found nowhere; that un-locatedness is the un-arisen, *nirodha*; and seeing the three together is the *magga* developing — all four in one moment being the supramundane right-view knowledge (a clarity of vision, not yet stream-attainment). Only afterward are the labels applied: the assembling of colour, shape and hardness into "a reed" under a *name* is *ruppana*, the fashioning of *rūpa*, which with feeling, perception and formations, all cognized by consciousness, is the five clinging-aggregates — and the whole twelve links bloom *at once*, like flowers on a single stalk, not one after another, the moment "reed" registers: *avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā* (the reed constructed by not-knowing), consciousness ("I knew a reed"), name-and-form, the six bases (the view "eye here, reed out there"), contact (*cakkhu-samphassa*, eye and form meeting), feeling (*cakkhu-samphassajā vedanā*), craving (giving the feeling a persistence), clinging (making it "mine"), existence, birth, and the whole mass of aging, death, sorrow and despair. The Buddha named the twelve only because this tangle cannot otherwise be shown — "pulverized fine so we could swallow it" — the names carrying no reality, for craving, feeling and colour can be given no description and no "reed" is in them; and the whole work is to see the five clinging-aggregates as conditioned-arisen *with wisdom*, not to find a truth, not by adding the words "impermanent, suffering, not-self," but by seeing practically — as in the reed-bundle — that no "cause" exists apart, the seeing itself yielding the three marks wordlessly. And this is done effortlessly, "neither standing nor striving," adding nothing and removing nothing, the simile taken as foolishly as it is, never knotted to one's known Dhamma-terms.

## Summary

This long dialogue works the two-reed-bundles simile to its doctrinal end. "Seeking the truth" is itself a self-view: hunt for "a truth to be found," and on finding it the "me who finds" still stands, the self merely switching from "falsehood-and-me" to "truth-and-me." As a magician's "dove" is magic only to one who does not know the trick, the wisdom that sees the delusion *as* delusion finds neither truth nor falsehood — nothing occurred to be called real or false. The "cause" by which two leaning reeds stand upright is un-locatable: neither in A, nor in B, nor in both, nor apart, findable nowhere, yet a "capacity" is manifest at the joining, sure to be there and in neither, like a dream that appears though it exists nowhere — and this un-locatable "because-of" is contact, the "capacity" and the upright-standing speakable only each with the other, an un-severable connection whose mark is that no persistence can be spoken of it. Consciousness sees only "a hundred percent right"; only wisdom sees the fault, which the eye can never see.

The manifest persistence is what we take as "is" — self-perception; but wisdom sees no persistence really exists (not-self), that nothing truly arose (impermanent), and that a persistence appears where none can be given (suffering, the suffering of construction) — so one can say neither "is" nor "is-not," and seeing the arising frees one from "is-not" while its manifesting frees one from "is," both extremes transcended. The Four Truths then stand in one place: the manifest persistence is suffering, the constructing is its arising, the "cause" found nowhere is the un-arisen cessation, and seeing the three together is the path developing — all four in one moment being supramundane right view, a clarity of vision, not yet stream-attainment. Only afterward come the labels: assembling colour, shape and hardness into "a reed" under a name is the fashioning of *rūpa*, which with feeling, perception, formations and consciousness is the five clinging-aggregates, and the whole twelve links bloom at once like flowers on one stalk — ignorance-conditioned formations, consciousness, name-and-form, the six bases, contact, feeling, craving, clinging, existence, birth, and the mass of aging and death — the Buddha naming the twelve only because the tangle cannot otherwise be shown, the names carrying no reality. The whole work is to see the five clinging-aggregates as conditioned-arisen *with wisdom*, not to find a truth, not by adding the words "impermanent, suffering, not-self," but by seeing practically that no cause exists apart, the seeing itself yielding the three marks wordlessly — effortlessly, "neither standing nor striving," the foolish simile taken as foolishly as it is and never knotted to one's known terms.
