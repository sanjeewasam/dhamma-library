## Sutta referred to
- Named / clearly quoted: the **lute simile** (*Vīṇopama Sutta*, **SN 35.246**) — "lute-sound" is a mere convention, no sound in the lute, strings or bow; *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti, yaṁ sañjānāti taṁ vitakketi, yaṁ vitakketi taṁ papañceti, tato-nidānaṁ purisaṁ papañca-saññā-saṅkhā samudācaranti* ("what one feels, one perceives; what one perceives, one thinks about; what one thinks about, one proliferates; thence proliferation-perceptions beset the man" — the **Madhupiṇḍika Sutta, MN 18**); *idam-eva saccaṁ, mogham-aññaṁ* ("this alone is truth, all else is worthless" — the dogmatic-clinging phrase), contrasted with *idappaccayatā*; *yena yena hi maññati, tato taṁ hoti aññathā* ("in whatever way one conceives it, it becomes otherwise than that"); *aññā va saññā uppajjati, aññā va saññā nirujjhati* ("one perception arises, quite another perception ceases" — the **Poṭṭhapāda Sutta, DN 9**); the **Satipaṭṭhāna** triad *samudaya- / vaya- / samudaya-vaya-dhammānupassī*.
- Referred to, unattributed: the "**magician at the four-way crossroads**"; the **dog seeing its reflection** in water; *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*; *phassa* / *phassa-samudaya* as the two ends.
- Terms used: *phassa* (contact), *saññā* / *maññanā* (perception / conceiving), *papañca* (proliferation), *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* (self-view), *mano-dhamma* (mind-object level), *viññāṇa* / *paññā* (consciousness / wisdom), *mūla-avijjā* (root ignorance), *nirodha* / *samudaya*, *sammā-diṭṭhi* (right view). ## Main dhamma points
- The first segment, using the lute-sound to show that contact is the un-locatable "striking," that even "there is a contact / it is true" is a proliferation, that recognizing "arose/ceased" is two mirage-perceptions, and that "understanding" only re-forms the knower.
- **The lute-sound: contact is the un-locatable "striking," and both ends are built.** "Lute-sound" is a mere convention — no sound is in the lute, the strings or the bow, and no sound pre-exists and comes; sound arises through contact, the "striking" (*ulanawā*), and once the cause-and-fruit (lute-and-bow / sound) is constructed, contact can never be found in the two, for it is *by* contact that the two were met. Taking the constructed sound and hunting its cause yields "the sound is in neither lute nor bow" — the "is-not" extreme — which is not the seeing-through of contact but a wrong sliding from "is" to "is-not," and this is why *vaya* (passing-away) cannot by itself show cessation, or the Buddha would not have added the third Satipaṭṭhāna contemplation of arising-and-passing together. The "hearing" is what divides sound and ear, the hearer being hearing's very reflection, and once the two are knotted no fault can ever be found — the magician at the four-way crossroads.
- **Even "there is a contact / it is true" is a proliferation; taking contact as separate makes it an end.** Going to "escape contact," one makes contact itself permanent — "there is a striking, so…" — for contact is called an end precisely because it is a thing *not to be taken*, and taking it as "real, un-graspable and therefore persisting" is the *phassa* extreme. Whatever way one conceives it, *yena yena hi maññati, tato taṁ hoti aññathā*, it turns out other than conceived; and *idam-eva saccaṁ, mogham-aññaṁ* — "this alone is true" registering anywhere is itself only a *maññanā*, so taking "the Buddha said *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, therefore such a thing truly exists" is no different, a conceiving, the raft become a snare (the pothole pointed out to be avoided, not fallen into). And *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti … papañceti* — what is felt is perceived, thought about, proliferated, so that proliferation-perceptions beset the man and the proliferation then controls the person, angering or pleasing him, as one who takes the water's reflection for a real dog can bark at it or not, but it is the *view* "a dog exists" that afflicts.
- **"Understanding" only re-forms the knower; recognizing "arose/ceased" is two mirage-perceptions.** The person arises from the very "knowing": "I feel" becomes un-falsifiable — my body, my nose, all mine — so the knower that arises with the knowing is exactly what must *not* form, and seeing the fault of the formed knower is seeing how the view (*diṭṭhi*) arises, which is the capacity of right view; but "understanding contact" is again a work of forming the person, caught at the mind-object level, "the process registered as right." So *aññā va saññā uppajjati, aññā va saññā nirujjhati*: "arising" is one perception and "ceasing" quite another, both perceptions — one never sees the same thing arise and then cease, for the mind that thinks "the chair-thought ceased" is yet another mind, and we keep both the arising and the ceasing permanent. Thinking "chair," then "the chair-thought ceased," is a fresh recognizing, another mind, so recognizing "arose/ceased" is two mirages, never the actual arising-process; we grasp the constructed thing ("chair") as "I saw the arising," while seeing the actual arising is quite different, and "chair" and "chair-absent" are the two we discuss, both perceptions — the arising itself unseen because the root delusion, the un-met not-knowing (*mūla-avijjā*), is not seen. (The segment breaks off here, mid-sentence.)

## Summary

This first segment uses the lute-sound to expose contact. "Lute-sound" is a mere convention, in neither the lute nor the strings nor the bow, and no sound pre-exists and comes; sound arises through the "striking," contact, and once the cause-and-fruit is constructed contact can never be found in the two, for it is by contact that the two were met. Hunting the cause yields "the sound is in neither" — the "is-not" extreme — which is not the seeing-through of contact but a wrong slide from "is" to "is-not," and *vaya* alone cannot show cessation, or the Buddha would not have added the third Satipaṭṭhāna contemplation of arising-and-passing together; the hearing divides sound and ear, the hearer being hearing's reflection, and once knotted no fault is found — the magician at the crossroads. Going to escape contact one makes it permanent, for it is called an end precisely because it must not be taken, and *yena yena hi maññati, tato taṁ hoti aññathā*, however conceived it turns out otherwise, while *idam-eva saccaṁ, mogham-aññaṁ* — "this alone is true" anywhere is only a conceiving, so "the Buddha said *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, therefore it truly exists" is no different, the raft become a snare. And *yaṁ vedeti taṁ sañjānāti … papañceti* — the felt is perceived, thought about, proliferated, the proliferation then controlling the person, as the view "a dog exists" afflicts one who takes the water's reflection for a dog.
