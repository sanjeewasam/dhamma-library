## Sutta referred to
- Referred to, unattributed by title: *ariya-suññatā* (noble emptiness — the theme of the **Cūḷa-/Mahā-suññata Suttas, MN 121–122**); the **name-and-form designation-contact** (*rūpa-kāya adhivacana-samphassa*) by which a set of marks is designated "a thing" (the **Mahānidāna Sutta, DN 15**); a **Zen** "mountains are mountains again"; a child's **play-house**.
- Terms used: *saññā* (perception / recognition), *adhivacana* (designation), *diṭṭhi* (view), *avijjā* (ignorance), *viññāṇa-agocara* (not within consciousness's range), *rūpa* (form), *nimitta* (recognition-marks), *pṛthagjana* (worldling). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** the **third and closing segment (Part 3)** of **"Ignorance That Is Not Met Even While Immersed In It — Can It Be Met When Seen As Such?"** — a short two-speaker segment completing the discussion. It ends abruptly, the student asking to pause. A mountain (*kanda*) is used as the illustration.

## Main dhamma points
- The closing segment, on noble emptiness where neither a thing nor a receiver is met, and on seeing recognition as mere recognition so that no "existent" or "non-existent" thing can be spoken.
- **Both the "me" and the "thing" are one construction, seen only from the un-cognizable place — noble emptiness.** Ordinarily "I am real, and what I see is real," but we never see that the "me" is born from the very thing seen — form itself becoming "me"; both the "me" and the "thing" are seen only by one who sees from *there*, the un-cognizable not-knowing, where there is no thing and no receiver-of-the-thing at all, which is *ariya-suññatā*, noble emptiness. There, with no one and nothing met, the fault of "I understand" is plain: "I sense a feeling," "I hear a hearing" — the fault appears only when seen from that place, for from morning there is no "feeling / no-feeling" story and no "feeling-me / non-feeling-me," and only within the non-arisen nature does the fault of both "I feel" and "I do not feel" appear. As with the child's play-house seen "as that," the wisdom that saw the view and the thing arise together does not see the objects as "things met by someone," but as a single construction built within the view.
- **Recognition seen as mere recognition — the mountain — leaves nothing to call existent or non-existent.** Recognizing "a mountain," we suppose it "a real mountain," and asked to describe it we give shape, colour, "made of rock, hard"; yet in the concept "mountain" itself there is no rock, colour or shape at all, so seeing without that mode is seeing recognition *as* mere recognition (perception as perception). We take "there is a mountain" only from the marks — the name-and-form designating it "a real mountain" to the worldling's mind — but seen as only-recognition there is no person, nothing there and nothing met, and one can say neither "it exists" (there is no witness) nor "it does not" (no witness), *because it was never designated*. Were the form-body designated (*rūpa-kāya adhivacana*), the person would be directly built — shape, colour and hardness registering "to someone" making the person real, "I sense it is hard, this colour, this shape" — and knotting the recognition-marks makes the view unstoppable; but un-designated, it does not form. (The segment breaks off here, the student asking to pause.)

## Summary

This closing segment turns on noble emptiness. Ordinarily "I am real, and what I see is real," but we never see that the "me" is born from the very thing seen, form itself becoming "me"; both the "me" and the "thing" are seen only from the un-cognizable not-knowing, where there is no thing and no receiver at all — *ariya-suññatā*, noble emptiness — and there, with no one and nothing met, the fault of "I understand" is plain, for from morning there is no "feeling / no-feeling" and no "feeling-me / non-feeling-me," and only within the non-arisen nature does the fault of both "I feel" and "I do not feel" appear. As with the child's play-house seen as that, the wisdom that saw view and thing arise together does not see the objects as "things met by someone" but as one construction within the view. Recognizing "a mountain," we suppose it real, and describe it by shape, colour and rock — yet the concept "mountain" holds no rock, colour or shape, so seeing without that mode is seeing recognition as mere recognition; we take "there is a mountain" only from the marks, the name-and-form designating it real, but seen as only-recognition there is no person, nothing met, and one can say neither "it exists" nor "it does not," because it was never designated. Were the form-body designated, the person would be directly built — shape, colour and hardness registering "to someone" — and knotting the marks makes the view unstoppable; but un-designated, it does not form. (This is Part 3, the close of "Ignorance That Is Not Met Even While Immersed In It — Can It Be Met When Seen As Such?"; it ends abruptly.)
