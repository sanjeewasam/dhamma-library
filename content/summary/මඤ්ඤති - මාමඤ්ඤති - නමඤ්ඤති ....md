## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: **assāda–ādīnava–nissaraṇa** (gratification, danger, escape); **sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā, sabbe saṅkhārā dukkhā, sabbe dhammā anattā**; **diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta** (seen–heard–sensed–cognized); **cakkhuñca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu-viññāṇaṁ** (dependent on eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises); **tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso** (the meeting of three is contact); **na maññati** (of the arahant, "does not conceive"); "**kīṇaṁ purāṇaṁ, navaṁ natthi sambhavaṁ**" (the old is exhausted, no new arising — a line of the **Ratana Sutta, Sn 2.1**); *katañ-ñāṇa* / "what was to be done is done" as the arahant's utterance.
- The talk's title — *maññati* (conceives) / *mā-/mamaññati* (conceives "mine") / *na maññati* (does not conceive) — is the triad characteristic of the **Mūlapariyāya Sutta (MN 1)**; note that MN 1 is **not named** in the recording, though *na maññati* is spoken of the arahant. A flower is used as the running illustration; a child's toy-car and the lotus-in-mud also appear.
- Terms used: *anuppāda* (un-arisen nature), *vipariṇāma* / *aññathābhāva* (change / otherwise-becoming), *maññanā* (conceiving), *saṅkhāra-dukkha*, *asaṅkhata* (unconstructed — of nibbāna), *viññāṇa* / *cetanā* (consciousness / intention), *mano-dhammā* (mind-objects), *pṛthagjana* (worldling), *kilesa-parinibbāna* (final quenching of defilements). No sutta should be inferred beyond those named.
- **Notes on the source:** a single continuous two-speaker dialogue titled **"Maññati – Māmaññati – Na maññati."** It opens by recalling another teacher's talk on gratification/danger/escape and appears to be a complete recording, ending cleanly at [28:30].

## Main dhamma points
- A dialogue on why the third mark reads *sabbe dhammā anattā* and not *saṅkhārā*, tracing perception, conceiving (*maññanā*) and consciousness back to the un-met nature, and closing on how the arahant who "does not conceive" carries no memory and no re-constructed suffering.
- **Escape is where gratification and danger cannot even be met — the flower.** In the recalled talk the flower's gratification is its beauty and scent, its danger its fading, and escape is removing "flower, flower" from the mind; but if a thing were of un-arisen nature (*anuppāda*) one could not even *speak* of its gratification or danger, so one who sees that nature is simply freed of both — escape is the very place where gratification and danger are un-locatable.
- **The three marks belong to the un-arisen seeing, not to the existing flower.** The fading flower is *dukkha*, yet the word used of the existing flower is not *anicca* but *vipariṇāma* and *aññathābhāva* (change, otherwise-becoming); *anicca–dukkha–anatta* register only when the un-arisen nature is seen, whereas the worldling, not seeing "the meeting of a flower is itself suffering," grasps only the decay of the flower that is there. When the knowledge "impermanent" dawns, saṅkhāra-dukkha and the anatta-mark are taken together — *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā, dukkhā; sabbe dhammā anattā*.
- **Why "all dhammas are not-self": nibbāna and conceiving are dhammas, and every dhamma is a maññanā.** The mother is recognized through seen-heard-sensed-cognized, and at the un-cognizable place a "thinking" occurs — a *maññanā*, itself not-self; whatever we call hearing, seeing, sensing, knowing are all *dhammā*, and all are conceivings. Nibbāna too is spoken of as a *dhamma* — but as *asaṅkhata*, not condition-constructed — which is why the third line says *dhammā* (covering the unconstructed) rather than *saṅkhārā*, since *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā/dukkhā* speak only of the constructed.
- **Conceiving's own nature is "not-met"; we fancy about what was never there.** The correction offered: we suppose "*maññanā* is what I think," but it is precisely *not knowing* it is a conceiving that makes us "think" — seen rightly, a conceiving can never be understood or met at all, just as *diṭṭha* seen as *diṭṭha* has no content or cause and can only be pointed to by bringing in form, hearing by bringing in sound. So going into a conceiving by way of conceiving, there is nothing to understand, only a not-knowing nature; its very nature is non-meeting, and to take a non-met nature as met is the error — hence *maññaṁ*, fancying about what was never there.
- **Consciousness is known only through mind-objects; "seeing" is an action only a Buddha shows as viññāṇa.** Asked to show "there is a mind," we can point only to things thought about — forms, sounds, smells, tastes, touches, dhammas — so we know "there is a mind" only because we think about form. The worldling lives in "there is an eye, there is a form, seeing happens"; only a Buddha shows *cakkhuñca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu-viññāṇaṁ*, that this "action" is consciousness, is intention (*cetanā*), the very *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* — and the not-knowing "there is an eye and a form" is itself the condition by which consciousness is constructed. *Viññāta* likewise: seeing was never met by the eye nor hearing by the ear, yet not-knowing this we prize "the things I saw and heard are what I think about."
- **When wisdom settles in the middle, both sides are cut; the arahant does not conceive, so no suffering is re-constructed and no memory forms.** With wisdom rightly established, "the old is exhausted, no new arises" — past kamma finds no re-linking and no future-ripening kamma is left, no forced path even to the woeful states. The student presses: the arahant still has khandhas, seeing still occurs at the un-seeable place, and "that seeing is oppression, itself suffering" is realized — so how is there saṅkhāra-dukkha for one who *na maññati*? The reply: the arahant is *done* (*katañ-ñāṇa*, defilements finally quenched), established within the seen cessation, so nothing is re-constructed; saṅkhāra-dukkha is not *conceived* at all, and the body's continuing is no problem. Like an adult playing with a child's toy-car — doing everything yet building nothing inside as "a real car I own" — the arahant handles the world's formations un-smeared, a lotus rooted in the very mud, standing in the world's dung-heap on no separate ground, yet unable to associate the saṅkhāra-khandha because it is seen by its nature. So "I saw this person in the morning" cannot be established for the arahant: seeing is known as conceiving-by-conceiving, a non-met thing, so no memory forms, whereas the worldling, not knowing seeing is a conceiving, keeps "I saw" and hears the arahant's answer as merely one more *maññaṁ*.

## Summary

This continuous dialogue opens from a remembered talk on gratification, danger and escape, illustrated by a flower. The flower's gratification is its beauty and scent, its danger its fading, and escape is removing "flower, flower" from the mind; but if a thing were of un-arisen nature one could not even speak of its gratification or danger, so escape is really the place where both are un-locatable. The fading flower is suffering, yet the word for the existing flower is not *anicca* but *vipariṇāma* and *aññathābhāva*; *anicca–dukkha–anatta* register only when the un-arisen nature is seen, while the worldling, not seeing "the meeting of a flower is itself suffering," grasps only its decay. When "impermanent" dawns, saṅkhāra-dukkha and the not-self mark are taken together — *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā, dukkhā; sabbe dhammā anattā*.

The third line reads *dhammā*, not *saṅkhārā*, because nibbāna too is spoken of as a *dhamma* — but as *asaṅkhata*, not condition-constructed — while *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā/dukkhā* speak only of the constructed; and the mother, recognized through seen-heard-sensed-cognized, is known at the un-cognizable place through a "thinking," a *maññanā*, itself not-self, so that whatever we call hearing, seeing, sensing and knowing are all *dhammā* and all conceivings. Its very nature is non-meeting, and to take a non-met nature as met — to fancy about what was never there — is the error named *maññaṁ*. So too consciousness is known only through mind-objects: asked to show "there is a mind," we can point only to forms, sounds, smells, tastes, touches and dhammas thought about; the worldling lives in "there is an eye, a form, seeing happens," and only a Buddha shows *cakkhuñca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu-viññāṇaṁ*, that this action is consciousness, is intention, the very meeting of three called contact, the not-knowing "there is an eye and a form" being itself the condition by which consciousness is built; and *viññāta* likewise, since seeing was never met by the eye nor hearing by the ear.
