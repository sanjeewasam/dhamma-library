# මඤ්ඤති - මාමඤ්ඤති - නමඤ්ඤති ..

## Sutta referred to

- **Assāda-ādīnava-nissaraṇa** — gratification, danger, and escape: reinterpreted through the lens of anuppāda (non-arising) rather than as sequential stages of disenchantment
- **Tilakkhaṇa** — sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā, sabbe saṅkhārā dukkhā, sabbe dhammā anattāti — with specific attention to why "dhammā" (not saṅkhārā) is used for the anattā formulation
- **Aññathābhāvī / viparināma** — change and transformation as worldly descriptions distinct from the tilakkhaṇa
- **Diṭṭha / suta / muta / viññāta** — the four bases of experience, shown as maññanā
- **Cakkhuñca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu viññāṇaṃ** — dependent arising of eye-consciousness
- **Tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso** — contact as the meeting of three
- **Maññanā** — conceiving/measuring: maññati, māmaññati, namaññati (conceives, conceives as "mine", does not conceive)
- **Kīṇaṃ purāṇaṃ, navaṃ natthi sambhavaṃ** — old kamma destroyed, no new arising
- **Saṅkhāra dukkha** — dukkha of formations, shown as inapplicable to one who is na maññati
- **Asaṅkhata** — the unconditioned (nibbāna), and why it falls under dhammā anattā rather than saṅkhārā

## Main dhamma points

- **Assāda-ādīnava-nissaraṇa reinterpreted**: If a flower is anuppāda (never truly arisen from conditions), then assāda (gratification) and ādīnava (danger) cannot be spoken of. Seeing anuppāda IS nissaraṇa — escape is not an effort to get away but the dissolution of the very ground on which gratification and danger stood. The worldly person tries to escape FROM the flower; the one who sees anuppāda finds there was never anything to escape from.

- **Aññathābhāvī and viparināma vs. anicca-dukkha-anattā**: The worldly view uses aññathābhāvī (becoming otherwise) and viparināma (transformation) — the flower changes, petals fall, it decays. These are descriptions of what happens to a "thing." Anicca-dukkha-anattā operate at a different level entirely: the very encounter with the flower is what is anicca. The worldling can never grasp that meeting the flower IS dukkha — only the consequences of its change are visible to them.

- **Why "sabbe dhammā anattā" and not "sabbe saṅkhārā anattā"**: Nibbāna is a dhamma but not a saṅkhāra. It is asaṅkhata — not fabricated by conditions. So the anattā formulation must use "dhammā" to include both the conditioned and the unconditioned. This is why the three phrases are not parallel: the first two address saṅkhārā (fabrications), the third addresses dhammā (all phenomena including nibbāna). When maññanā is seen as maññanā, what remains cannot be given a pavetvima (establishment/continuity) — it is asaṅkhata, anattā.

- **Maññanā is not "thinking"**: The common assumption is that maññanā means our thinking activity. The correction: maññanā is the unseen condition that makes thinking appear possible. We think BECAUSE we don't know maññanā as maññanā. The real nature of maññanā is "not-encounterability" (hamba noveṇa svabhāvaya) — something that can never be encountered is taken as encountered. That taking IS maññanā.

- **Diṭṭha seen as diṭṭha has no hetu**: If seeing (diṭṭha) is seen purely as seeing, no cause (hētu) can be shown for it. Seeing needs rūpa to demonstrate itself; hearing needs sadda. The "thing itself" can never be independently presented. So all four — diṭṭha, suta, muta, viññāta — are maññanā: the false apprehension of encountering something that was never there to encounter.

- **Viññāṇa as cetanā, not as objective event**: The ordinary person sees eye + form = "seeing happens" — an event in the world. The Buddha reveals this as cakkhu viññāṇa: a viññāṇa arising. The ordinary person never realises that "seeing" is a cetanā/viññāṇa formation, not an objective occurrence. The ignorance of this — not knowing that eye and form are manō dhamma (mind-phenomena) — IS what conditions viññāṇa's arising.

- **Viññāta built on maññanā**: The sense that "I know" (viññāta) is constructed from taking diṭṭha/suta/muta as real encounters. When maññanā is seen as maññanā, nothing remains to think about — no past kamma generates present linking (paṭisandhi), no present action generates future result. Kīṇaṃ purāṇaṃ, navaṃ natthi sambhavaṃ: old kamma destroyed, no new arising. Paññā cuts both directions simultaneously.

- **Na maññati dissolves saṅkhāra dukkha**: For one who is na maññati, saṅkhāra dukkha cannot be spoken of. The question "does the arahant experience saṅkhāra dukkha through the khandha?" contains a structural error — saṅkhāra seen as saṅkhāra (saṅkhata seen as saṅkhata) cannot produce dukkha in the mode of maññanā, because maññanā is no longer operating.

- **Arahant's functioning — toy car analogy**: Like an adult who knows a toy car is a toy — they can still play with a child, push it, interact fully. But internally, no formation of "this is a real car I'm driving" arises. The arahant operates in the same saṅkhāra-world as the puthujjana but without māyā-knots forming. The lotus analogy: rooted in mud (the world) but untouched by it. Pariharaṇa (mere functioning) continues without kamma-formation.

- **Memory and maññanā**: The arahant does not form memories as "I saw X this morning." Because diṭṭha was never taken as a real encounter, there is nothing to store as memory. The puthujjana's memory IS maññanā not seen as maññanā — the assumption that something was really encountered, preserved as a "fact" for later thinking. For the arahant, "this morning" and "this evening" are the same nondannākama (unknowing) — no before-and-after is constructed.

## Summary

This discussion takes the terms maññati (conceives), māmaññati (conceives as "mine"), and namaññati (does not conceive) as its starting point to investigate the deep structure of maññanā — what it actually is, how it operates unseen, and what its cessation means for the arahant's mode of being.

The conversation opens with the assāda-ādīnava-nissaraṇa framework — gratification, danger, and escape — commonly taught through the example of a flower. In the standard presentation, the flower is beautiful (assāda), it withers (ādīnava), and one should let go of attachment to it (nissaraṇa). Mr. Lasantha reframes this: if the flower is anuppāda — if it never truly arose from conditions in the way we assume — then assāda and ādīnava cannot even be spoken of. The very ground on which gratification and danger stand dissolves. Seeing anuppāda IS nissaraṇa, not as an achievement of detachment but as the recognition that there was never anything to detach from.

This leads to an important distinction between the worldly descriptions of change — aññathābhāvī (becoming otherwise) and viparināma (transformation) — and the tilakkhaṇa (anicca, dukkha, anattā). The worldling can observe that a flower changes, its petals fall, it decays. These are descriptions of what happens to a "thing that exists." But anicca operates at a different level: the very encounter with the flower — its appearing as a "thing" — is what is anicca. The worldling can never grasp that meeting the flower IS dukkha; only the consequences of its change (loss, decay) register as suffering. The three characteristics belong to a seeing that has penetrated to anuppāda, not to the observation of change within existence.

A structural observation about the Pāli formulations follows. The first two statements use "saṅkhārā" — sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā, sabbe saṅkhārā dukkhā. But the third uses "dhammā" — sabbe dhammā anattā. Why? Because nibbāna is a dhamma but not a saṅkhāra. Nibbāna is asaṅkhata — unconditioned, not fabricated by causes. So the anattā declaration must encompass both the conditioned and the unconditioned. When maññanā is seen as maññanā, what remains cannot be given establishment (pavetvima) — it is neither saṅkhata nor something that maññanā can claim. This is why anattā covers dhammā: even the unconditioned is not a self, not an establishment, not something to grasp.

The heart of the discussion turns to the nature of maññanā itself. The common assumption is that maññanā means our thinking — the mental activity we engage in when we consider things. The correction offered is fundamental: maññanā is not the thinking but the unseen condition that makes thinking appear possible. We think BECAUSE we don't know maññanā as maññanā. Its real nature is "not-encounterability" (hamba noveṇa svabhāvaya) — it is the taking of something that can never be encountered as though it were encountered. This is not a mere intellectual error but the structural foundation of all experience. When maññanā is truly seen, there is nothing to understand — understanding itself was built on maññanā.

This is demonstrated through diṭṭha/suta/muta/viññāta. If seeing (diṭṭha) is seen purely as seeing, no cause can be shown for it — seeing needs rūpa to demonstrate itself, hearing needs sadda. The "thing itself" (the seeing, the hearing) cannot be independently presented. All four bases of experience are therefore maññanā: the false apprehension of having encountered something that was never there to encounter. This is why the ordinary person, asked "how do you know you have a mind?", can only point to what they think about — forms, sounds, smells, tastes, touches, ideas. Without these objects, "mind" has no evidence for its own existence. But what the Buddha revealed is that eye and form are not independent entities that come together to produce seeing — they are manō dhamma, mind-phenomena, arising as cakkhu viññāṇa in the mode of cakkhuñca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu viññāṇaṃ. The ordinary person takes this as "seeing happens out there"; the Buddha shows it as a viññāṇa-formation, a cetanā.

The implications for viññāta (the sense of "I know/knew") are drawn out. Viññāta is built on taking diṭṭha/suta/muta as real encounters. When maññanā is seen as maññanā, nothing remains to think about — no basis for "I saw that" or "I heard this" survives. At that point, kīṇaṃ purāṇaṃ — old kamma is destroyed — and navaṃ natthi sambhavaṃ — no new arising occurs. Paññā cuts both directions simultaneously: no past kamma can produce present linking (paṭisandhi), and no present action can generate future result. One cannot even "try" to go to the four apāya — the structural conditions for it are absent.

The discussion then addresses whether saṅkhāra dukkha applies to the arahant. If the arahant is na maññati — does not conceive — then saṅkhāra dukkha as commonly understood cannot apply. The khandha persist in the sense that the arahant still functions, but saṅkhāra seen as saṅkhāra (saṅkhata seen as saṅkhata) does not produce dukkha through the maññanā-mode, because maññanā has ceased. The toy-car analogy makes this vivid: an adult who knows a toy car is a toy can still play with a child — push the car, make sounds, engage fully. But internally, no formation of "this is a real car" arises. The arahant lives in the same world as the puthujjana, operates with the same saṅkhāra, but no māyā-knots form. The lotus rooted in mud, untouched by it.

Finally, the discussion of memory reveals maññanā's deepest operation. The ordinary person says "I saw him this morning" and carries that as a fact into the evening — memory IS maññanā operating as the assumption that something was really encountered, stored for later retrieval. The arahant, having seen diṭṭha as never-encountered, does not form this bridge. "This morning" and "this evening" do not stand as before-and-after, because the nondannākama (unknowing) that would construct temporal sequence is continuously seen as such. When asked "didn't you see him this morning?", what arises is not memory but the recognition that maññanā would be needed to construct such a claim — and maññanā, seen as maññanā, does not operate.
