## Main dhamma points
- The first segment arguing that "hearing" is never actually understood — only sound is — so the "ability to hear" we carry is an un-verified view, and the un-understandable nature itself can never be met by consciousness, which is a knowing-nature.
- **We understand only sound, never "hearing"; "hearing" arises only alongside "sound."** Asked what is understood, one answers "the sound"; the mind never understands "hearing" at all. Focusing on a clock's tick, one knows "the clock's sound," yet thinks "the clock's sound is now audible to me" — taking a separate "hearing" — when in truth only the sound was thought, "hearing" appearing solely as the thought "sound" arises. So the notion of hearing is never a thing grasped in its own right; it is entangled with sound being conceived.
- **The "ability to hear" is a permanent-perception never verified — see hearing by hearing.** Even in silence we carry "I have hearing," but asked "in the silent moment, are you in hearing or in not-hearing?" no answer can be given; it cannot be stated in isolation. This permanent-perception (*nitya-saññā*) was never actually understood. "Seeing hearing by hearing" is not easy, but done, it disciplines on the spot and the mind can no longer re-knot it. Ordinarily we hold "my ears are not deaf, so this sound is heard," "because I have the ability to hear, I understand sounds" — the view (*diṭṭhi*) — un-falsifiable because on one side "hearing exists" and on the other a sound stands as witness; propose that "sound is only a perception arising in the mind" and no one accepts it, everyone retorting "then what is it I am hearing now?"
- **Without the knot the mind cannot arise; "it is not there" is said only because it cannot be understood.** "Sound" is the knotting, the two fixed together; without a knot the mind cannot think, for there is then no condition (*paccaya*) and no way for a mind to arise. When one says "it is not there," this is not about a thing understood and found absent — it is said precisely *because it cannot even be understood*. But do we even know the "cannot-understand"? The mind cannot grasp it, for the mind, consciousness, is a knowing-nature, and a thing utterly un-understandable can neither be thought nor "understood," so the mind has no way to arise upon it. Across saṁsāra, past lives and now, this un-understandability is never experienced by consciousness.
- **Naming defeats the un-sayable; the tree and the deaf-born are symbols of the "cannot."** To convey the un-understandable one must give it a name, yet the moment it is named the hearer "understands" it, so it cannot be conveyed — even saying "cannot-understand" turns, in the saying, into something do-able; hence dhamma said in the fewest words is best, since the nature cannot be worded, and we, abiding view-endowed, are always waiting with a hook out to understand. A tree "taking carbon dioxide, releasing oxygen" is never an understood thing — we cannot think the tree's very existence-nature, only frame "the tree has no mind, its breathing un-understood" and build a tree fitting our frame; the tree is thus a *symbol of the "cannot."* Likewise the person deaf from birth: "to the deaf, sound is not audible" — yet being-deaf is itself never experienced by us, and we can understand neither "audible" nor "not-audible" for the deaf; the simile is meant to point at the un-understandability of hearing and non-hearing, but we end by building "a deaf person we know," analysed through "hearing / not-hearing" from our own assumed "I have hearing."
- **We and the deaf are the same; only consciousness's incapacity to hold the incapacity makes us think "I can hear."** Not knowing our own inability, we take ourselves able and analyse the deaf's condition to reinforce it, "to me it is audible, to the deaf it is not" — a foregone conclusion, "I have an ability the deaf lacks," resting on assumed knowing. But we too, like the deaf, cannot know hearing; examined by "hearing by hearing," no witness to the ability is found, and in the silent moment "am I in hearing or not-hearing?" has no answer. So we abide in a permanent-perception never understood — which is why the nature here is impermanence, and the Buddha said here is *anicca–dukkha–anatta*, un-understandable to us who think we stand in permanence, "hearing well, seeing well, sensing well." Until this knot — *nāma* and *rūpa* knotted, *vedanā* and *saññā* knotted, the condition-knot — is loosened, *anicca–dukkha–anatta* is never met.
