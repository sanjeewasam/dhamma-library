## Main dhamma points
- The second segment resolving why "suffering" belongs with impermanence and not-self, why the three marks are given as one, and — through the deaf-born and the lamp similes — why realization can only be a support-condition, never transferred by words.
- **The three marks are one because fabricating itself is the suffering, seen only with its origin.** We prize audible sounds not because we truly hear but because we hold "there is a hearing"; the un-met nature is impermanence, and seeing that un-met-ness the suffering-mark shows, for to stand in a non-met nature while thinking "I have met it" is itself the suffering. Not "there is a suffering here," but the realization, dawning with the impermanence-mark, that *fabricating* is suffering and that suffering is created *in this way*; because it is spoken together with the origin (*samudaya*), *anicca–dukkha–anatta* form a single insight, the three marks, which cannot be shown apart, since only through the fabricating is "fabricating is suffering" seen. This is the *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* — at an utterly un-knowable place a "birth" occurs, eye-form-seeing, ear-sound-hearing, a construction whose very constructedness is shown as suffering.
- **The unconstructed is not "the absence of the constructed."** That suffering is seen only when the un-fabricated nature is seen with it; yet "non-fabrication" must not be taken as "the absence of this fabricated thing." Cessation cannot be spoken, and when we try, fabrication belongs to the origin side, the five clinging-aggregates; we borrow the word "fabrication" only to point, but no such nature was ever born there — it is *anuppāda*, un-arisen, not a story of "there is no five-aggregate-clinging." To read it as a not-there tale is to first posit an existing clinging-aggregate and then negate it; fitting the unconstructed into the constructed frame corrupts it. Many understand only a *relative* impermanence — "what the constructed yields is not in nibbāna" — whereas the real nature is one where no constructed thing can be built, with no condition, no cause, no way for a mind to arise upon it.
- **We stand in not-knowing; the fabricated taken as real becomes "permanent, pleasant, self."** The impermanence-mark manifests only to a wisdom that sees "this un-knowable, un-thinkable nature — through not knowing which" the whole construction happens; we abide in not-knowing itself, and precisely because that not-knowing is not known, this "knowing," this constructed thing, this fabrication occurs. Taking the fabricated as real, a being forms, an *ātma*-ness, "I exist"; not seeing it is born of not-knowing, we grasp it as permanent-pleasant-self, holding "this constructed thing existed before, exists now, will exist forever." But the permanent-pleasant-self we built is a distortion, never seen as a distortion — because the un-knowable un-fabricated nature is not seen, the whole "permanent, pleasant, self" is taken as a hundred, even two hundred, percent true, "this can be experienced, this is permanent, this is pleasant," and one is pushed that way. Wisdom here is not a seen thing but the nature itself, which is why *paññā* too is finally liberated — no separate seer apart from the seeing.
- **Realization is only a support-condition; the deaf-born simile cannot be worded into you.** The correction from Part 1 is pressed: speaking of the person deaf from birth to hint at hearing's un-knowability, the hearer fits it to a known "hearing" and concludes "the deaf cannot hear," whereas the deaf was given only as a *symbol of hearing's un-bornness*, not a definition; the insight that settles at "the deaf-nature cannot be worded" was never in the speaker's words. Hence the Buddha said "let words and terms be released, let the meaning be grasped" — meaning does not belong to words; the understanding is honed within oneself, born of one's own merit and aspiration, given by no one, the teaching and the good friend serving only as a support-condition. That a single discourse makes some stream-enterers, some once-returners, some non-returners, some arahants, and some nothing, proves the realization is not produced by the words, which only assist.
- **The lamp lit from a lamp — free of another's condition.** Lit lamps furnished with wick and oil still need a match to kindle them; the match lights them all, yet one cannot say the flame "came down from above," and not a particle of the match's flame is in the lamp's flame — the heat merely kindled combustion from the oil and wick already present, and cold water would never ignite. One's own side must be complete, the perfections full, like combustibility; talking and talking ripens the igniting-capacity and the wisdom matures, the support helping the flame arise, but it must ignite from within, for the teacher can only bring heat near, never light it. The lamp's own fire arises — not the match's fire carbon-copied — and from the moment it arose the flame has no right to claim "I came from the match-lamp"; to say so is a fault. That is what "free of another's condition" (*aparappaccayā*) means.

## Summary

This second segment resolves why "suffering" is placed with impermanence and not-self. We prize audible sounds not because we truly hear but because we hold "there is a hearing"; the un-met nature is impermanence, and seeing that un-met-ness the suffering-mark shows, for to stand in a non-met nature while thinking "I have met it" is itself the suffering. It is not "there is a suffering here" but the realization, dawning with the impermanence-mark, that fabricating is suffering and that suffering is made in just this way; because it is spoken together with the origin, the three marks form one insight and cannot be shown apart, since only through the fabricating is "fabricating is suffering" seen — the *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, where at an un-knowable place eye-form-seeing and ear-sound-hearing are constructed and the very constructedness is shown as suffering. Yet this is seen only with the un-fabricated nature, which must not be taken as "the absence of this fabricated thing": cessation cannot be spoken, the word "fabrication" only points, and no such nature was ever born there — it is un-arisen, not a story of "there is no clinging-aggregate," for to read it so is to posit an aggregate and then negate it, corrupting the unconstructed by fitting it to the constructed frame.

We abide in not-knowing, and because that not-knowing is not known, this "knowing," this construction, this fabrication occurs; taking the fabricated as real, a being forms, an "I exist," grasped as permanent-pleasant-self on the supposition that "this existed before, exists now, will exist forever." But this permanent-pleasant-self is a distortion never seen as one, taken as wholly true because the un-knowable un-fabricated nature is not seen; wisdom here is not a seen thing but the nature itself, which is why wisdom too is finally liberated. And realization can only be a support-condition: speaking of the person deaf from birth to hint at hearing's un-knowability, the hearer fits it to a known "hearing" and concludes "the deaf cannot hear," whereas the deaf was only a symbol of hearing's un-bornness, and the insight that settles there was never in the words — hence "let words be released, let meaning be grasped," the meaning belonging not to words but honed within oneself, born of one's own merit, the teacher and good friend only a condition, as one discourse makes some stream-enterers, some once-returners, some non-returners, some arahants, and some nothing. So too the lamp lit from a lamp: the match kindles wick-and-oil already present, yet not a particle of its flame passes over, the perfections must be full like combustibility, and from the moment the lamp's own fire arises it has no right to claim "I came from the match" — which is what "free of another's condition" means.
