## Sutta referred to
- **Explicitly named:** the **Māluṅkyaputta Sutta** ("මාලුංක්‍යපුත්ත හාමුදුරුවන්ට කියන්නේ…") — the instruction that toward a thing "not seen in the past, not seeable now, not to be seen in future, nor even thinkable as future-seeable," no lust or hate can arise (the frame of SN 35.95 / the Bāhiya teaching).
- **Directly quoted in Pāli:** "**diṭṭhe diṭṭhamattaṁ bhavissati, sute sutamattaṁ bhavissati, mute mutamattaṁ bhavissati, viññāte viññātamattaṁ bhavissati**" (in the seen only the seen, in the heard only the heard… — Ud 1.10 / SN 35.95), glossed as "there is no seeing, no hearing, no sensing, no cognizing"; and "**imasmiṁ sati idaṁ hoti**" (when this exists, this comes to be — the dependent-origination formula), with its complement "when this is not, this does not come to be."

## Main dhamma points
- A multi-session dialogue building, through a phone, darkness-and-light, and above all a fish, to the point that neither "is" nor "is-not" can be located in the present moment, so the seen-heard-sensed-cognized are only conceivings (*maññanā*) — the meaning of "in the seen only the seen."
- **The impossible task makes the truth self-evident: no suffering can be found in the present.** The Buddha says "the phone is suffering," but merely holding that as received knowledge is not realization; asked to locate a suffering in the phone *right now*, one finds the breaking is future, the loss is future — no suffering exists in the present phone. Like being sent to "fetch mustard from a house where no one has died," the errand cannot be completed, and its very impossibility becomes direct realization (*pratyakṣa*); this is what "see the two extremes" means — attempting the impossible, wisdom arises and one transcends both extremes.
- **One cannot analyse a picture one is painted into — the self is built by not-seeing conditioned arising.** As a character inside a film cannot step aside to describe the film, so the "I" that tries to grasp dependent origination was itself formed precisely in not-seeing arising-by-condition; not seeing the conditioned arising, things become real and "I" become real, and that real "I" then sets out to understand — which is the error.
- **The fish and the water: "is" is found only within "is-not."** A fish born, living and dying in water can never so much as conceive "water" while immersed — it is un-cognizable, no concept forms; only pulled onto land, meeting water's *absence*, does it grasp what water is, and even then only as a remembered water (a *manasikāra*, dream-like), not a present experience. So its home-nature is grasped only by leaving it, and then it sees the past place, not the present — as we can never see the ground we stand on (like standing on our own two feet, always hidden) except by going elsewhere. Existence is disclosed only through non-existence: knowing "one" requires meeting "zero."
- **Eternalism and annihilation: we experience only half the circle.** "Hearing continues all the time," "I am awake right now, one hundred percent sure" — this ever-present, never-verified permanence (*nitya-saññā*) is the eternalist "is" half; deep sleep is the annihilationist half, un-experienceable, ungraspable even by attention, where the six sense-bases are quiescent and "sleep" is only a *manasikāra* done *after* waking. We meet only the "existence" 180° of the 360°; the "non-existence"/*uccheda* half is never experienced, disclosed only to wisdom beyond attention. By "when this exists, this comes to be," sleep would have to be known *in* sleep, at that very place — not reconstructed on waking. The person deaf from birth has no "hearing"-sign at all (neither "hears" nor "does not hear"), like true annihilation, whereas one who went deaf can say "I do not hear" only because he holds the hearing-sign; hearing works like a door-sensor, active whether or not anyone passes — so is there ever a moment genuinely without it? Only sleep, which cannot be analysed.
- **Darkness and light: describing the present by a dead sign is the error.** Because consciousness is hard to discuss directly, darkness stands in as a proxy: we call it "darkness" only through "absence of light," but that light-sign is a *past* datum, so we describe the present by a dead thing — reasoning by *kamma-phala* ("a past-life deed, a leg breaks now") instead of present *hetu-phala*. Present-moment reasoning must be "when this exists, this comes to be," the cause operating *now*; if no cause operates now, "darkness exists" cannot be posited, nor "darkness absent" — so neither "is" nor "is-not" applies, and both extremes, seen, "cannot stand." That non-standing is the knowledge of arising-and-passing, a wisdom no ordinary mind can manufacture; consciousness cannot perform this search, and that very impossibility, becoming self-evident, is wisdom — for "one who sees origination realizes cessation."
- **Self-view: when "darkness is met," a "me" is born on the existence side.** When darkness "is met," the one who meets stands on this side — "darkness is me," born from the eternalist "existence"; both "darkness exists" and "darkness is absent" are met "to me," so self-identity (*sakkāya*) operates. Where neither is met and there is no meeter, only wisdom remains and no person applies; not seeing this "non-application by wisdom" is *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, whose reverse — "seeing the application by wisdom" — is again like fetching mustard from the deathless house.
- **The phone's parts, and the resolution in "only the seen."** "Phone" is only a concept-word; glass, plastic, box, hardness, black are the parts, and not one of them is "the phone," nor is any such sign born or present within the bare concept "phone" — a small child who does not know the word gets none of those signs from it, proving they are not inside the *manasikāra*. To say "a phone exists now" needs present causes, and none operate — so "phone exists"/"phone absent" both fail, exactly as with darkness. Seeing, then, is a *perceiving* of something-seen with nothing actually met — "seeing conceives" (*diṭṭha-suta-muta maññanā*); toward what was never seen, is not seeable, and cannot be thought seeable, no craving can arise (Māluṅkyaputta), and "in the seen there will be only the seen…" means there is no seeing, no hearing, no sensing, no cognizing as things. As with one stone felling two birds, meeting one, both are met — water and land alike disclosed only *outside* the present moment, so that neither land nor water is ever truly met.

## Summary

This multi-session dialogue — "The Fish Simile," between the teacher Lasantha and a physician-student — reasons entirely at the margin of the present moment. The Buddha says "the phone is suffering," but held as received knowledge that is not realization; asked to find a suffering in the phone *now*, one sees the breaking and the loss are future, so no suffering exists in the present phone. Like being sent to fetch mustard from a house where no one has died, the errand cannot be completed, and its impossibility itself becomes direct realization — which is what "see the two extremes" means: attempting the impossible, wisdom arises and both extremes are transcended. And one cannot analyse a picture one is painted into: the "I" that tries to grasp dependent origination was itself formed in not-seeing arising-by-condition, so that things and "I" become real and the real "I" then sets out to understand — the very error.

The fish carries the weight of it. Born, living and dying in water, it can never conceive "water" while immersed — water is un-cognizable to it; only pulled onto land, meeting water's absence, does it grasp what water is, and only as a remembered, dream-like water, not present experience. So existence is disclosed only through non-existence, and we, like a fish, meet only half the circle: "hearing continues," "I am awake, one hundred percent sure" is the eternalist ever-present permanence, while deep sleep — un-experienceable, ungraspable even by attention, where "sleep" is a construct done only after waking — is the annihilationist half never met. The born-deaf, with no hearing-sign at all, images true annihilation, whereas one who went deaf can say "I do not hear" only because he keeps the sign; hearing runs like a door-sensor, active on absence too. Darkness then stands in for consciousness: we call it darkness only through "absence of light," but that light-sign is a past datum, so we describe the present by a dead thing — *kamma-phala* reasoning rather than present *hetu-phala*, whereas "when this exists, this comes to be" requires a cause operating now; and none does, so neither "darkness exists" nor "darkness absent" can stand, and that non-standing of both extremes is the knowledge of arising-and-passing, a wisdom consciousness cannot manufacture — its very inability, made self-evident, being wisdom, since one who sees origination realizes cessation.

Self-view rides on this: when darkness "is met," a "me" is born on the existence side, "darkness is me," met "to me," so *sakkāya* operates; where nothing is met and there is no meeter, only wisdom remains and no person applies, and not seeing that non-application is self-view itself. Finally the phone's parts make it plain — "phone" is only a word, and glass, plastic, hardness are not "the phone," nor present within the bare concept, as a child who does not know the word receives none of them; to say "a phone exists now" needs present causes that never operate, so "is" and "is-not" both fail. Seeing is therefore a perceiving of something-seen with nothing met — "seeing conceives," seen-heard-sensed-cognized are conceivings — and toward what was never seen, is not seeable, and cannot be thought seeable, no craving can arise (Māluṅkyaputta); "in the seen only the seen, in the heard only the heard, in the sensed only the sensed, in the cognized only the cognized" means there is no seeing, no hearing, no sensing, no cognizing as things. As one stone fells two birds, water and land are disclosed together and both outside the present moment, so neither is ever truly met.
