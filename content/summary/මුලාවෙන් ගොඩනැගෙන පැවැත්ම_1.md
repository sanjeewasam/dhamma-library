This distinction runs through the entire discussion: the problem is at the level of ippaddīma (arising), not at the level of what happens after arising.

## Main dhamma points

- **Athi bava (existence) always arrives as athi bava.** Everything encountered comes through the mode of "existing" — tiyena bavinma. There is zero room for doubt at the moment of encountering. We cannot think "this encounter is false" because the existing-ness is total. This is the deception of avijjā: it does not present itself as deception.
- **The dream is never known as a dream while dreaming.** We say "I dreamed" only after waking. During the dream, the vision was encountered as fully real. If it had been known as a dream, deception would have been impossible. The teacher stresses: the point is not that the dream disappears or changes — it is that the dream was never encountered in its own nature (hīnē baving).
- **Ayoniso manasikāra is the attempt to deconstruct experience through dhamma concepts.** When a song is heard, thinking "this is just sounds assembled by the mind" is itself ayoniso — not yoniso. The one with nuvaṇa does not do anything to the appearance. The appearance causes zero damage (daśama mātrāyak hāniyak kerenē nǣ) to the one who has seen the padanama (foundation).
- **The entire darśanaya is constructed on avijjā.** We do not know that nodannākama (not-knowing) is the reason seeing, hearing, and knowing occur. Because we do not know this, everything encountered arrives as 100% existing. The kīrīma (doing) — trying to see anicca, trying to see dhātu, trying to do something — stops naturally when the mūla svabhāvaya (root nature) is grasped.
- **Ninda (sleep) as the structural model for ucchēda.** In sleep: no hearing, no seeing, no knowing, no manasikāra. Neither "exists" nor "doesn't exist" applies. Manasikāra cannot be used to understand sleep because the moment manasikāra operates, it is no longer sleep. Sleep is hātpasinma viruddha (completely opposite) to viññāṇa. The fridge-light analogy: we can never experience the light being off — opening the fridge turns it on.
- **The dream arises on a base of total non-knowing.** Sleep = no manasikāra, no athi/nathi. This ucchēda is the base on which the dream constructs itself. If one knows this base through nuvaṇa, anything appearing within it cannot be taken as real — not even by force (balenvat). The dream vision is abhavya — it cannot be. What appears as "existing" is actually nopavættīma (non-existence).
- **"Awakeness" is built on the never-experienced.** We say "I woke up" — based on "I was asleep." But sleep was never experienced — not a biṃdu mātrāyakvat (not even a particle). We claim avadhiya (awakeness) by denying ninda (sleep) — "nidi nǣhǣ" — but we are denying something we have never known. The foundation for "this moment is real" is an at-nodækpu (never-experienced) assumption.
- **The manasikāra that begins at waking produces the sense of athi bava.** "Avadhi vuṇā" (I became awake) is itself a manasikāra — it is the first construction. From that initial manasikāra flows: seeing, hearing, feeling, the whole lōkaya. The "felt change" (dænīmē venasa) between sleep and waking is the manasikāraya's balavatkama (force/intensity), not a change in reality.
- **Data identity between dream and waking.** The teacher uses the dog-bite scenario: in the dream, one recognizes the dog, runs, feels the bite, feels pain, feels fear. While awake, identical data: recognize, run, bite, pain, fear. The datta (data) that produces sajīvī bava (sense of liveliness/reality) is siyyaṭa sīyak eka samānai — 100% identical. The difference is not in the data but in whether the base (padanama) has been seen.
- **Jāti pi dukkhā: birth itself is suffering, not suffering-after-birth.** The phone analogy: we examine the dukkha that comes from having a phone (it breaks, gets stolen). But the Buddha said ippaddīmama dukyi — the arising itself is dukkha. We look at hambavecca deyē duk ṭika (the sufferings of what was obtained) rather than seeing that the obtaining is the dukkha. This is the mūla (root) level the teacher is pointing to throughout.

## Summary

This is Part 1 of "මුලාවෙන් ගොඩනැගෙන පැවැත්ම" (Existence constructed from delusion). The teacher uses the sleep-and-dream analogy as a structural model for showing how pavættma (existence) is constructed entirely from mulāva (delusion/not-knowing), and why the construction cannot be seen from within viññāṇa's own operations.

The discussion opens by establishing that athi bava (existence) always presents itself as athi bava — there is no moment where what is encountered reveals its own constructed nature. Everything arrives through tiyena bavin (the mode of "existing"), with zero room for doubt. The teacher draws the parallel to dreaming: while dreaming, the dream is never known as a dream. We say "I dreamed" only afterward. During the dream, every encounter is fully real — if it were known as a dream, deception would be impossible. The critical point is not that the dream needs to disappear or change, but that it was never encountered as what it actually is.

The teacher then identifies a common trap: the attempt to deconstruct experience through dhamma concepts. When a song is heard and one thinks "this is just sounds, the mind assembles it into a song" — this thinking itself is ayoniso manasikāra, not yoniso. The entire samasta darśanaya (totality of the vision) is constructed on avijjā, and we do not know that nodannākama is the reason any encounter occurs. Because of this, every kīrīma (doing) — trying to see anicca, trying to see the four great elements — is itself operating within the field of avijjā. This kīrīma stops only when the mūla svabhāvaya (root nature) is directly grasped.

The teacher then builds the sleep-dream model systematically. Sleep (ninda) represents ucchēda — total non-existence. In sleep there is no hearing, no seeing, no knowing, no self, no non-self. Neither "exists" nor "doesn't exist" applies. Crucially, manasikāra cannot be used to understand sleep, because the moment manasikāra operates, sleep has already ended. Sleep is hātpasinma viruddha (completely opposite) to manasikāra. This means viññāṇa can never reach, grasp, or verify sleep — it is viññāṇa-agōcara by definition.

The dream arises within this base of total non-knowing. If one knows through nuvaṇa (wisdom) that the base is manaiskāra-less, that nothing can exist or not-exist there, then anything appearing within that base is immediately seen as abhavya (impossible/cannot-be). The one who has this nuvaṇa cannot take the dream as real even by force — not a daśama mātrāyakvat (not even fractionally).

The teacher then makes the crucial move: "awakeness" is itself constructed on the never-experienced. We claim "I woke up" based on "I was asleep" — but sleep was never experienced, never known, not even a biṃdu mātrāyak. The avadhiya (awakeness) is built on denying something (nidi nǣhǣ) that we have never known. The first manasikāra at waking — "avadhi vuṇā" — is itself the first construction, and from it flows the entire lōkaya with its seeing, hearing, feeling, and sense of reality.

The discussion culminates in the data-identity argument. The teacher compares a dog-bite scenario in dream and in waking: recognizing the dog, running, feeling the bite, pain, fear — the datta (data) producing the sajīvī bava (sense of liveliness) is 100% identical in both cases. The difference between dream and waking is not in the data but in whether the padanama (foundation) has been seen. The jāti pi dukkhā teaching is invoked: the Buddha said ippaddīmama dukyi — the arising itself is the dukkha, not what happens after arising. We look at the sufferings of what was obtained (the phone breaking) rather than seeing that the obtaining is the problem. This is the mūla level the entire discussion is driving toward.
