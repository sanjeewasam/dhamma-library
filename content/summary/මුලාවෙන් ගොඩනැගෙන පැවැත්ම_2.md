## Sutta referred to

- **"Ahutva sambhūtaṃ hutvā na bhavissati"** — "not having been, it came to be; having been, it will not be." The teacher cites this Pāli phrase to capture the abhavya (impossible) nature of what appears: it arises from a base where nothing existed, and what arose cannot persist. This is not a philosophical statement but a description of what nuvaṇa sees structurally at every encounter.
- **"Imasmiṃ sati idaṃ hoti"** — "when this is, this comes to be." The teacher uses the paṭicca samuppāda formula at the end to show the structural relationship: athi bava (existence) is taken paccayena (conditionally) from næthi bava (non-existence). But the næthi bava was itself never experienced — the condition is an at-nodækpu (never-experienced) assumption.
- **"Piṭiṭāda nosiṭimin vǣyamda nokaramin"** — the ōgha taraṇa teaching. The teacher contrasts this with the pruthagjana's approach: piṭiṭā siṭa (establish on what is encountered) then vǣyam (strive to negate it). The Buddha said: without either. When hamuvīma (encountering) itself is seen as the error, neither establishing nor striving arises.

## Main dhamma points

- **Viññāṇa cannot prove its own experience false.** Just as the dream cannot be exposed as a dream from within the dream, the current experience cannot be exposed as mulāva from within viññāṇa's operations. The datta (data) — recognizing, knowing, feeling — is identical in both states. No amount of nuvaṇa available to viññāṇa can make the experience "feel" false at the moment of encountering.
- **Ucchēda is the never-experienced base.** Sleep represents the condition where no manasikāra operates — no seeing, hearing, knowing, no athi (exists) or nathi (doesn't exist). We have never experienced this ucchēda — not a biṃdu mātrāyak (not even a particle). Yet this never-experienced ucchēda is the padanama (foundation) on which all athi bava is constructed. We do not know this padanama, and because we do not know it, we cannot find aṃśu mātrāyak varadak (not even a particle of error) in what appears.
- **"I hear now" is based on "I didn't hear before" — but that was never experienced.** The teacher uses the sound example: before the call, there was no haṃda (sound). The sound's existence is taken sāpēkṣava (relatively) against its prior absence. But that prior absence involved no manasikāra — no thought of "no sound" occurred. The athi bava of sound is constructed from a næthi bava that was itself never known. This is the paṭicca samuppāda structure: imasmiṃ sati idaṃ hoti — but the "imasmiṃ" (the condition) was never experienced.
- **Both ninda and hīna are beyond dænagænīma (knowing).** Sleep cannot be known as sleep while sleeping. Dreams cannot be known as dreams while dreaming. Both are beyond viññāṇa's reach. The teacher equates hīna (dream) with śāśvata — the "permanent/eternal" that can never be known as what it is. Just as the dream is called śāśvata (what is encountered as permanently real), the current experience is equally unknowable as what it actually is.
- **Hamuvīma (encountering) itself is the error — not what is encountered.** The pruthagjana takes the encountered thing and tries to reduce it: "this is the four great elements," "this arose from viññāṇa," "this is śūnya." This is eka ṛṇa eka (minus one) — subtracting from what was obtained. But this requires first piṭiṭā siṭīma (establishing on the encounter) — accepting the encounter as valid, then working on it. The Buddha's teaching (piṭiṭāda nosiṭimin vǣyamda nokaramin) rejects both: if the hamuvīma itself is seen as the varadda (error), there is nothing to establish on and nothing to strive against.
- **Nuvaṇa is not passive non-doing — it is more sūkṣma than doing.** The listener asks: "So there's nothing to do? Just sit passively?" The teacher corrects: the nuvaṇa that sees hamuvīma as the error must be maintained at every encounter. This is more subtle (sūkṣma) than the pruthagjana's kīrīma (doing). At every encounter — hearing, seeing, feeling — the nuvaṇa sees: "mē hamuvena kamma varadda" (the encountering itself is the error). This is not non-action but continuous, effortless seeing.
- **Ayoniso decreases naturally as nuvaṇa operates.** As the nuvaṇa repeatedly sees hamuvīma as the error, the tendency to respond to encounters (æļīma/gæṭīma — attraction/aversion) decreases on its own. The teacher stresses: this is not a practice of restraint but a natural consequence of seeing. Just as knowing the dream is a dream prevents being deceived, knowing the encounter is abhavya prevents æļīma/gæṭīma.
- **Athi bava and næthi bava are both constructed from nodannākama.** The person with nuvaṇa sees: whether something appears as "existing" or "not existing," both are constructed from the same base of not-knowing. Neither athi bava nor næthi bava can be piṭiṭā (established on). This is the meaning of piṭiṭāda nosiṭimin — not that one refuses to establish, but that there is nothing there capable of being established on.

## Summary

This is Part 2 of "මුලාවෙන් ගොඩනැගෙන පැවැත්ම" (Existence constructed from delusion). The teacher deepens the sleep/dream model from Part 1 to show the structural impossibility of detecting mulāva (delusion) from within viññāṇa, and introduces the paṭicca samuppāda structure by which athi bava (existence) is conditionally constructed from a never-experienced næthi bava (non-existence).

The session opens by completing the data-identity argument: just as the dream cannot be exposed as false from within the dream — the data (recognizing, knowing, feeling) is identical — the current experience cannot be exposed as mulāva from within viññāṇa's operations. No nuvaṇa available to viññāṇa can make the experience "feel" false. This is not a limitation of effort but a structural impossibility: the padanama (foundation) on which all athi bava rests is ucchēda — total non-existence, never experienced, not even a biṃdu mātrāyak. Because this padanama is unknown, no aṃśu mātrāyak varadak (not a particle of error) can be found in what appears.

The teacher then systematically deconstructs the relationship between knowing and not-knowing. Sleep (ninda) represents ucchēda — the state where no manasikāra operates. We say "nidi nǣhǣ" (no sleep) to define awakeness, but we are denying something we have never known. The "athi bava" of this moment is always constructed by bæhæla tiyennē (riding on) a næthi bava that was itself never experienced. Similarly, the dream (hīna) cannot be known as a dream — the teacher identifies the dream with śāśvata, the "permanent" that presents itself as undeniably real and can never be known as what it is from within viññāṇa.

The crucial move is the identification of hamuvīma (encountering) as the error itself — not what is encountered. The pruthagjana's approach is described as eka ṛṇa eka (minus one): take the encounter, accept it, then try to reduce it — "this is four great elements," "this is viññāṇa-constructed," "this is śūnya." This is piṭiṭāda siṭa (establishing) followed by vǣyam (striving). The teacher shows this is the exact structure the Buddha rejected: piṭiṭāda nosiṭimin vǣyamda nokaramin. When the hamuvīma itself is seen as the varadda, there is nothing to establish on and nothing to strive against.

The teacher then corrects the listener's inference that "nothing to do" means passive sitting. The nuvaṇa is more sūkṣma (subtle) than doing or not-doing. At every encounter, in both athi bava and næthi bava directions, the nuvaṇa operates — seeing that the encountering is the error. As this nuvaṇa continues, ayoniso manasikāra (wrong attention — the tendency to think about what is encountered and react) naturally decreases. The teacher compares this to how kuṇu (defilement/rot) decreases as the nuvaṇa "balances" against it.

The session closes with the paṭicca samuppāda demonstration using the sound example. Before the conversation began, no sound was present. The sound's "existence" is taken sāpēkṣava (relatively) against its prior absence — "there was no sound, now there is sound." But the prior absence involved no manasikāra at all — no thought of "no sound" occurred. The athi bava of sound is constructed from a næthi bava that was itself never known. The teacher invokes the formula: imasmiṃ sati idaṃ hoti — "when this is, this comes to be." But the "imasmiṃ" — the condition — was a never-experienced assumption. The paccaya (condition) from which uppanna (arising) occurs is itself nodannākama. The transcript ends at [29:00] with this structural insight set up for continuation in Part 3.
