## Sutta referred to

- **"Ahutva sambhūtaṃ hutvā na bhavissati"** — carried forward from Part 2 and referenced again. "Not having been, it came to be; having been, it will not be." The teacher uses this to show that the encounter itself is abhavya — arising from a base where nothing was experienced.
- **Satipaṭṭhāna** — the listener raises satipaṭṭhāna at the end. The teacher positions the entire discussion as dhammānupassanā level: seeing how dhammā are constructed. Kāyānupassanā, vedanānupassanā, cittānupassanā lead toward this — but dhammānupassanā is where the padanama (foundation) is seen. Once the construction of dhammā is seen, the practitioner is in dhammānupassanā.
- **Paṭicca samuppāda and pañcupādānakkhandha** — not cited as specific suttas but named as what the practitioner sees within the darśanaya (vision). The teacher stresses: once the nittya padanama (the base constructed from avijjā) is seen, paṭicca samuppāda, pañcupādānakkhandha, and caturrārya satya are all visible within the same single seeing — no separate analysis is needed.
- **Udayavaya ñāṇa** — seeing both athi bava (existence) and næthi bava (non-existence) arising together as two sides of one coin through paccaya. The teacher explicitly names this as udayavaya ñāṇa.

## Main dhamma points

- **Athi bava and næthi bava are always co-present through paccaya.** The teacher shows this with the hearing example: when sound is present, "hearing exists" is said — but this is constructed on the paccaya of "there was no hearing before" (næthi bava). When sound is absent, "hearing doesn't exist" is said — but this is constructed by darāgena (carrying/holding) the concept of hearing from a prior experience. In both directions, athi bava rides on næthi bava, and næthi bava is stated by carrying athi bava. They are eka kāsiyē depætta (two sides of one coin).
- **In the paccuppanna moment, neither hearing nor non-hearing has actually occurred.** The teacher pushes deeper: in the moment when no sound is present, has hearing actually happened? No — no śabda nimitta (sound-sign) has arisen, so "hearing" is not ucita (appropriate) to that moment. Yet we say "no hearing" while darāgena (carrying) the concept of hearing from a previous experience. The "hearing" we carry is not from the paccuppanna moment — it is from the past. So even "no hearing" is constructed from a never-present concept.
- **This co-arising is udayavaya ñāṇa.** Seeing both sides — athi bava constructed on næthi bava, and næthi bava stated through athi bava — simultaneously, is udayavaya ñāṇa. The teacher explicitly names it: "ōnna ōya depættama pēna eka tamayi anna ara udayavaya ñāṇaya."
- **Ucchēda must be seen for the varadda (error) in athi bava to become visible.** Without seeing the næthi bava side (ucchēda), the athi bava side appears error-free — no aṃśu mātrāyak varadak can be found. This is why the pruthagjana cannot detect the error: only athi bava is visible. The næthi bava is never encountered as such.
- **Nittya bava (permanence) is constructed from nodannākama (not-knowing).** Ninda (sleep) is not known — it is never experienced. Hīna (dream) is not known — it is never experienced as a dream. Both are nodannākama. When two nodannākama merge, what results is tanikara nodannākamak (pure not-knowing) = avijjā. This avijjā, when taken as "known" (danna bavata hambavīma), produces nittya bava — the sense that things permanently exist. Nittya is not a property of objects but a product of avijjā.
- **Avijjā seen as avijjā = vijjā.** The teacher states directly: avijjāva avijjāva vidihataṃa dækkot ēkaṃa tamayi kiyannē vijjāva kiyalā. When avijjā is seen as avijjā — when the not-knowing is seen as not-knowing — that seeing itself is vijjā. Nothing needs to be done to avijjā; it needs only to be seen.
- **Anicca = seeing that nittya bava is constructed from mulāva.** The teacher carefully builds to the definition: nittya bava was constructed from nodannākama. When this is seen, the nittya padanama (permanent foundation) is seen as goḍanægilla tiyennē goḍanægenna bæri deyak (what is constructed was impossible to construct). This seeing is anicca. Anicca is not "things change" — it is seeing that the nittya padanama cannot hold because it was built from mulāva.
- **The dog-and-reflection analogy.** The teacher uses the image of a dog barking at its own reflection in water — trying to chase away the "other dog." This is what we do when we try to kīrīma (do something) about the encounter: the encounter is itself the reflection, constructed from mulāva. Trying to modify it is barking at the water.
- **Samasta pēna ekē (the totality of what appears) is seen from anuttāpada (non-arising).** The practitioner with the darśanaya sees the samasta pavættma (entire existence) from the vantage of haṭa noganna svabhāvayak (a state of non-arising). This is not a rejection of what appears but a seeing from where nothing has arisen. From there, everything — puṭuva, sadda, rasa, suvaṃda, sparśa — is seen as væradi padanamēn goḍanægicca (constructed from the wrong foundation). No kaḍa kaḍa (piece-by-piece) analysis is needed.
- **Dhammānupassanā: seeing how dhammā are constructed.** The discussion is positioned at the dhammānupassanā level of satipaṭṭhāna. Once the padanama is seen, the practitioner sees dhammā being constructed — this is dhammānupassanā. Kāyānupassanā, vedanānupassanā, cittānupassanā are approaches that lead toward this level, but the direct seeing of the padanama is where all four converge.

## Summary

This is Part 3 of "මුලාවෙන් ගොඩනැගෙන පැවැත්ම" (Existence constructed from delusion). The teacher completes the structural analysis of how pavættma (existence) is constructed through the paccaya relationship between athi bava (existence) and næthi bava (non-existence), derives the meaning of anicca from this structure, and positions the entire analysis within dhammānupassanā.

The session opens by completing the hearing analysis from Part 2. The teacher shows that athi bava and næthi bava always co-arise: when sound is present, "hearing exists" is stated — but this rides on "there was no hearing before" (næthi bava as paccaya). When sound is absent, "no hearing" is stated — but this is said while darāgena (carrying) the concept of hearing from a prior experience. In both directions, each is constructed from the other. The teacher draws out a subtler point: in the moment of "no hearing," has hearing actually occurred in the paccuppanna moment? No — no śabda nimitta has arisen. The "hearing" being denied is not from the present moment but from a past experience. So even næthi bava is constructed from a never-present athi bava. This two-sided co-arising is explicitly named as udayavaya ñāṇa — the knowledge of arising-and-passing seen together.

The teacher then shows why the varadda (error) in athi bava can never be found without seeing the ucchēda side. As long as only athi bava is visible, it appears error-free. The næthi bava is never encountered as such — like the dead man who is the padanama for the living man's story: mærecca minihekgē padanameṃ inna minihek gæna vistareyak karanavā (describing a living person based on a dead person's foundation). The mærecca minihā (dead man = ucchēda) is always there, always the base, but never known.

The central conceptual achievement of this session is the derivation of anicca. The teacher builds it step by step: ninda and hīna are both nodannākama (not-knowing). Together they produce tanikara nodannākamak (pure not-knowing) = avijjā. This avijjā, when taken as "I know this" (danna bavata), produces nittya bava — the sense of permanence. Nittya bava is not a property of objects but a product of avijjā misrecognized as vijjā. When the nodannākama is seen as nodannākama — when avijjā is seen as avijjā — that seeing is vijjā. And the nittya padanama, now seen as goḍanægicca mulāveṃ (constructed from delusion), can no longer hold as nittya. This seeing is anicca: not "things change" but "the permanent foundation was itself impossible — it was built from mulāva."

The teacher uses the dog-and-reflection analogy: the dog barks at its own reflection in the water, trying to chase the other dog. We do the same when we try to kīrīma (do) about the encounter — trying to make something śūnya, or see the four elements, or apply anicca as a technique. The encounter is the reflection, and kīrīma is the barking. The one who has seen the padanama sees the samasta pavættma (entire existence) from anuttāpada (non-arising) — nothing needs to be done, piece by piece or otherwise.

The session closes with the listener raising satipaṭṭhāna. The teacher positions the discussion at the dhammānupassanā level: seeing how dhammā are constructed is dhammānupassanā. Once the padanama is seen, paṭicca samuppāda, pañcupādānakkhandha, and caturrārya satya are all visible within the same single darśanaya — no separate analysis of each is required. The practitioner who sees the foundation sees everything that is built on it, from every angle, simultaneously.
