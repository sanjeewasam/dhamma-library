## Sutta referred to

- **Sabbāsava Sutta** (MN 2) — specifically the phrase **dassanā pahātabbā** (to be abandoned by seeing). The teacher and listener identify this as the first step toward nibbāna: the kilēsa (defilements) that are pahātabba (to be abandoned) through dassana (seeing/vision) — not through kīrīma (doing) or bhāvanā (development). This seeing is the darśana ñāṇa (vision-knowledge) discussed throughout the four-part series.
- **Satipaṭṭhāna** — continued from Part 3. The teacher completes the point: dhammānupassanā is not something one "does" — the practitioner who sees the padanama (foundation) is already in dhammānupassanā. Trying to "do" dhammānupassanā as a practice produces a new nittya saññā (permanence-perception) and is itself ayoniso manasikāra.

## Main dhamma points

- **Dhammānupassanā is where one already is, not something one does.** When the goḍanægīma (construction) of dhammā is seen at the moment of arising — a chair, a sound, anything — the practitioner is already in dhammānupassanā. The teacher compares it to swimming: we can describe swimming, but when someone is swimming, the description is not needed — they are in it. "Pīnanna gattahama āye eyāṭa pīnīma kiyalā amutuven deyak hambavenavada?" (When one starts swimming, does "swimming" become something separate to acquire?)
- **Naming dhammānupassanā destroys it.** If the practitioner tries to label the experience ("this is dhammānupassanā"), that labelling itself constructs a new nittya saññā (permanence-perception). The label becomes something piṭiṭā (established on), and the seeing collapses into ayoniso manasikāra. Applying "six vijjā" or any taxonomic framework to the seeing is the same error.
- **Satipaṭṭhāna is not something to be "done" — ayoniso is something to be recognized.** The teacher draws the critical distinction: yoniso manasikāra is not a practice one performs. It is the aṃdunagænīma (recognizing) of ayoniso manasikāra — the wrong construction happening in each moment. "Menna mēværadiyaṭa goḍanægena ayoniso ṭika dænaganna kammayi, aṃdunaganna kammayi yoniso kiyalā kiyannē." (Recognizing the wrongly constructed ayoniso — that recognition itself is what is called yoniso.)
- **Vaḍanava (growing/developing) happens on its own through seeing.** The practitioner does not vaḍanna (cultivate/develop) satipaṭṭhāna. Seeing the varadda (error) at each encounter is itself the vaḍīma (growing). "Ē dakina kamama tamayi eyāgē mē satipaṭṭhānaya væḍīma." (That seeing itself is the growing of satipaṭṭhāna.)
- **Dassanā pahātabbā (Sabbāsava Sutta): the first step is seeing, not doing.** The defilements abandoned through darśana are abandoned at the moment the padanama is seen — not through subsequent practice. After this darśana, the remaining defilements (those requiring saṃvara, bhāvanā, etc.) are removed kramakramayeṃ (gradually). But the first movement — the seeing — is the satya ñāṇa (truth-knowledge) that opens the path.

## Summary

This is Part 4 — the brief concluding segment — of "මුලාවෙන් ගොඩනැගෙන පැවැත්ම" (Existence constructed from delusion). In just 36 lines, the teacher brings the four-part series to its conclusion by positioning the entire analysis within satipaṭṭhāna and connecting it to the Sabbāsava Sutta's dassanā pahātabbā.

The teacher completes the satipaṭṭhāna discussion from Part 3: the practitioner who sees goḍanægīma (construction) at the moment of arising is already in dhammānupassanā. This is not something to be "done" — like swimming, once you are in it, the description becomes unnecessary. Critically, trying to label it ("this is dhammānupassanā") or apply a taxonomic framework (six vijjā, etc.) immediately constructs a new nittya saññā and collapses the seeing into ayoniso manasikāra.

The teacher then draws the central distinction about yoniso manasikāra: it is not a practice to perform but the aṃdunagænīma (recognition) of ayoniso manasikāra — the wrong construction at each moment. Satipaṭṭhāna vaḍīma (growing) happens naturally through this recognition. No separate vaḍannavā (cultivation) is needed.

The listener connects this to the Sabbāsava Sutta: dassanā pahātabbā — defilements abandoned through seeing. The teacher confirms: this darśana is the first step toward nibbāna. After this seeing, the remaining defilements are removed gradually. But the foundation — the padanama seeing — is the satya ñāṇa that opens the path.
