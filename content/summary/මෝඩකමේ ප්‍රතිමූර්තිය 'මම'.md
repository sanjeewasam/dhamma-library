## Sutta referred to

- **Paṭicca samuppāda** — the teacher walks through the specific links: avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā, saṅkhāra paccayā viññāṇaṃ, viññāṇa paccayā nāmarūpaṃ, nāmarūpa paccayā saḷāyatanaṃ. These are cited directly to show that viññāṇa arises from the very saṅkhāra (fabrication) it tries to examine, making it structurally impossible for viññāṇa to catch the error in its own arising.
- **Pubbē ananussutēsu** — "previously unheard." The teacher explains why this teaching is pubbē ananussutēsu: every other teacher (śāstṛ) took their own existence as 100% certain and searched for truth from that position. Only a Buddha saw that the centre — the "I" — is itself fabricated from the same vikṛti (distortion) being investigated.
- **Sabbāsava Sutta** (MN 2) — referenced near the end in connection with training (hikmenna) through vedanā: the practitioner who sees vedanā vedanā-bhavin (feeling as feeling) encounters the asimāna (residual conceit) that remains even at that stage, and must go beyond it.
- **Diṭṭha suta muta viññāta** — the four modes of cognition (seen, heard, sensed, cognised). The teacher takes dæṇīma (= vedanā/viññāta, the "felt/known") and shows that diṭṭhi (= sakkāya, the "I") arises from within dæṇīma itself — not as a separate entity encountering it.
- **Musāvāda** — the teacher redefines musāvāda structurally: both "I hear" and "I don't hear" are musāvāda, because hearing (æhīma) itself is never known as hearing. The saṃsāric being is constituted by musāvāda — not as moral failure but as ontological structure.

## Main dhamma points

- **The "I" who investigates delusion IS the epitome (pratimūrti) of that delusion.** The person who asks "how did I get caught in this mōḍakama (delusion)?" does so from the certainty that "I exist" — but that certainty is precisely the delusion. The question and the questioner are the same vikṛti (distortion). This is why no śāstṛ (teacher) other than a Buddha could reveal this: every teacher searched for truth while taking the searcher as given.
- **Viññāṇa cannot catch the error in its own arising.** Since viññāṇa arises from saṅkhāra (avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā, saṅkhāra paccayā viññāṇaṃ), it is structurally part of the fabrication process. Whatever viññāṇa understands — "this is wrong," "that was delusion" — is itself another viññāṇa born from the same fabrication. The teacher uses the matchbox-and-match analogy: examining matchbox and match, one concludes "there's no fire in either" — but that conclusion itself traps one in the næthi (ucchēda) extreme without ever recognising the trap.
- **Hētu-phala dhamma itself arises from avijjā.** The cause-and-effect framework (hētu-phala dhamma) is constructed within the athi-næthi (existence-nonexistence) duality. Trying to escape through hētu-phala reasoning produces more hētu-phala reasoning — one falls back into one of the two extremes. Until the two extremes themselves are seen, no amount of rearranging cause-and-effect reveals the error.
- **The shadow (hēvanælla) analogy: "I" cannot be separated from dæṇīma.** A chair's shadow cannot exist without the chair, yet the shadow is not the chair. Similarly, "I" (the dṛṣṭi/sakkāya) arises from dæṇīma (feeling/knowing) — it is dæṇīma's pratibimba (reflection). One cannot separate "I" from dæṇīma, nor equate them. Both attempts — "dæṇīma IS me" and "dæṇīma is separate from me" — are errors. Viññāṇa always operates in one of these two modes; it cannot step outside them. This is why the vikṛti is invisible from within.
- **Both "hearing" and "not-hearing" are musāvāda.** Using hearing (æhīma) as an example: in silence, one says "I don't hear anything" — but hearing itself is still present (it is hearing that recognises silence). One never knows hearing-as-hearing. So "I hear" is musāvāda (one doesn't know hearing), and "I don't hear" is also musāvāda (non-hearing was never experienced). The being is constituted from these structural falsehoods. Both ævilla (doing) and nokaraīma (not-doing) are impossible: one cannot "do" hearing, nor can one "not do" it. At the point where both are seen as impossible, sakkāya has no ground to arise.
- **Nibbāna maññanā: conceiving nibbāna is the same vikṛti.** From within the distortion, one imagines nibbāna as "a place without seeing, hearing, etc." — but this imagination is itself constructed by the vikṛti. The teacher calls this nibbānada maññanā karanavā (conceiving nibbāna). It is the same "I" — born from the distortion — projecting a distortion-free state as an object of thought. Asaññā (perception-less states) and similar formless attainments are the same error: suppressing saññā to reach a "place without these" is another gæṭaya (knot) tied by nodannākama (not-knowing).
- **No one attains nibbāna.** If puggala diṭṭhi (person-view) arises from the duality of dæṇīma, and at the point of darśana both sides of that duality are seen as impossible to perform or experience, then the puggala cannot arise. Where puggala doesn't arise, "someone understood something" and "someone was freed from something" are both absent. Nuvaṇa (wisdom) is not something even the knower knows — because the knower-structure doesn't form. The maximum that can be said in words: "this seeing (darśanaya) results in not falling into saṃsāra." Even this is said from outside — there is no one inside who "doesn't fall."

## Summary

"මෝඩකමේ ප්‍රතිමූර්තිය 'මම'" (The epitome of delusion is "I") is a single extended discussion that tackles one question head-on: "How did I get caught in this delusion?" The teacher's answer: the question itself is the delusion, because the "I" asking it is the delusion's own pratimūrti (embodiment).

The discussion opens by noting that when someone asks "why can't I see through this mōḍakama (delusion)?", the questioner's existence is taken as 100% certain. This certainty — "I am here, searching for truth" — is precisely what makes the delusion invisible. The one searching for the error IS the error's product. The teacher traces this to why the teaching is pubbē ananussutēsu (previously unheard): every other śāstṛ searched for liberation while taking the searcher as a given. Only a Buddha saw that the kēndraya (centre) — the "I" — is itself nirmāṇa vēlā (fabricated) from the same vikṛti being investigated.

The structural impossibility is then developed through paṭicca samuppāda: viññāṇa arises from saṅkhāra, so viññāṇa can never identify the error in saṅkhāra. Whatever viññāṇa concludes — "this was wrong," "that was delusion" — is itself another viññāṇa arising from the same fabrication. The second thought that corrects the first is like "the same thief in a white cloth" — it looks different but is the same process. The matchbox analogy makes this concrete: examining matchbox and match, one concludes "no fire in either," but that conclusion itself is a fall into the næthi (ucchēda) extreme, and this fall is never recognised.

The teacher then introduces the hēvanælla (shadow) analogy, which becomes the discussion's central image. A chair's shadow cannot exist without the chair; the shadow is not the chair; yet they cannot be separated. Similarly, "I" arises from dæṇīma (feeling/knowing) as its pratibimba (reflection). Viññāṇa always thinks in one of two ways about this: either "dæṇīma IS me" (equating shadow with chair) or "dæṇīma is separate from me" (treating shadow as independent). Both are errors, and viññāṇa has no third option. This dēyākāraya (duality of modes) is viññāṇa's svabhāva — it cannot operate except through these two channels. The error in operating through two modes is never caught because viññāṇa is itself a product of these modes.

The hearing (æhīma) analogy extends this to direct experience. In silence, one says "I don't hear anything" — but hearing itself persists (it is hearing that recognises the absence of sound). So "I hear" is musāvāda: hearing-as-hearing was never known. "I don't hear" is also musāvāda: non-hearing was never actually experienced. Both statements — and the being constituted by them — are structural musāvāda: not moral lies, but ontological fabrication. The teacher draws the practical consequence: one cannot "do" hearing (it is not a volitional act), and one cannot "not do" hearing (non-hearing was never experienced). At the point where both are seen as akārya (impossible to perform), sakkāya has no ground to arise — there is no gap for "I" to insert itself.

The discussion addresses nibbāna maññanā directly: from within the vikṛti, one imagines nibbāna as a state without seeing, hearing, etc. But this imagination is constructed by the same "I" that is the vikṛti's product. Suppressing saññā to reach such a state (asaññā) is another gæṭaya (knot) tied by avijjā. The teacher is explicit: "We construct nibbāna from the distortion itself."

The concluding position: at the point of darśana, where both sides of the duality are seen as impossible to enact or experience, puggala diṭṭhi cannot arise. Where the puggala doesn't form, there is no one who "understood" anything and no one "freed" from anything. Nuvaṇa (wisdom) is not something even the wise one knows — the wise-one structure doesn't form. The maximum that language can express: "this darśanaya results in not falling into saṃsāra (sassatā novatinnē)." The teacher cautions: even this is said from outside. Inside the darśana, there is no one who "doesn't fall."

The listener acknowledges that approaching from paṭicca samuppāda structures (different āyatana pariyāya, various saññā frameworks) always left him intact — the "I" survived every analysis. The teacher confirms: that is the nature of vigraha (analysis) — it always leaves the analyser. Only when the question "how did I get caught?" is seen as itself the catching — when the pratimūrti is recognised as pratimūrti — does the structure fail to regenerate.
