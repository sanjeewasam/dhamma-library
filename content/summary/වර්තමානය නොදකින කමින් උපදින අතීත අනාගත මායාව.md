## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted in Pāli: "**nayidaṁ attakataṁ bimbaṁ, nayidaṁ parakataṁ aghaṁ**" ("this puppet is not self-made, nor is this misery made by another" — a line of the **Yakkha-saṁyutta / Vajirā verses**), used to exclude "I made it," "another made it," and "it arose on its own"; "**ṭhiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso**" (the meeting of three is contact); "**avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā**" and "**viññāṇa-paccayā nāma-rūpaṁ**" (the dependent-origination links); "**phassa-mūlikā sabbe dhammā, phassa-samudayā sabbe dhammā**" (all things are rooted in contact, originate from contact — the "rooted in contact" formula).

## Main dhamma points
- A dialogue arguing that seeing the present means being unable to speak of past or future, so that the "action" is never met, only the knot tied to it, and both the elements and the perceiver are bare conceivings — leaving "not self-made, not other-made, not spontaneous."
- **Seeing arising-by-condition means seeing non-arising; we only ever examine the made thing.** "Seeing that it arises by condition" means seeing its *can't-be-made-ness* (*anuppāda*), not seeing how the made thing was made; that impossibility must become direct realization, and then cessation is realized. Yet we only ever look at the made thing — a chair's colour, shape, hardness — and the "making" is always missed; describing a form by mental factors (colour, shape, earth-element) is name-body designation-contact, and holds it permanent. Fitting effect to causes, taking the effect as real, one ends at "there is no effect in the causes" — the wrong way of seeing origination, by which cessation is never realized.
- **Contact is where cause and effect are born together, and neither belongs to the "because."** "All things are rooted in contact, originate from contact": the causes, and the "because-of-causes," produce an effect — that is the action, the *paccayā* — and by grasping "because" we knot the causes to the effect, like two bricks leaning together. But neither cause nor effect belongs to that "because," so seeing contact, both cause and effect are seen un-arisen — which is cessation itself. As "feeling" arises, nose and breath (the two causes) are constructed with it; they arise only when attention is foremost, and knowing, nose and breath all build together through contact — the meeting of three. Without contact there is no nose, no breath, no knowing here at all.
- **All day we abide in an un-arisen nature — the action is never met, so no doer applies.** So all day we abide in an un-arisen nature — "in truth, pure nibbāna" — and construct only by not-knowing that it is un-arisen; it cannot be thought, understood, or known. Walking down the road absorbed in another thought, no "things seen" or "things not seen" form, no formations arise; going to the kitchen for a cup, "going" and "taking" are never understood by the mind, only "cup" is, so the action itself is never met — what is understood is the *knot* tied to the action, which has in it no leg-story and no ground-story. Because action is never met, no doer is met, which is why one goes all day unconcerned; and the five clinging-aggregates form precisely because action is not seen as action.
- **The knot builds saṁsāra: to "prove I went," one cites what is irrelevant to the going.** "I went to town" has no witness, so it cannot be said one did or did not go; it falls to one's account only through the knot — to prove the going, one cites a beautiful flower or shop-item seen there, and there desire (*chanda-rāga*) becomes foremost and formations are constructed. Seen as action-by-action, those things are irrelevant, no condition remains for anything to form, and consciousness finds no footing (for with consciousness as condition comes name-and-form, and the seen flower is the name-and-form that knots); un-knotted, the action never becomes kamma. So the knotting is the very cause of saṁsāra, turning action into kamma — un-arisen nature, action un-cognizable to consciousness, and not knowing we stand there, the knotting happens through our ignorance: with ignorance as condition, formations; and these temporary five clinging-aggregates built by ignorance are saṁsāra, are suffering. This suffering can never be *understood*, because the "person" who would understand is itself born of not seeing the fault in understanding — understanding is the suffering, understanding is the view, so the view is never abandoned by understanding, only by its impossibility becoming clear.
- **The ice-cube: the four elements are bare recognitions, knotted to feeling like two lies.** An ice-cube gripped is "hard" (earth), then "fluid" (water), then "warm" (fire), then "vapour" (air) — four recognitions (*saññā*), not four things met; "earth" holds no hardness of its own, and if earth truly existed as a thing it could never be destroyed, yet things burn to ash. Not knowing a recognition as a recognition, we take "earth" as a thing, and the instant we do, "it is felt" arises inseparably with it — feeling and perception born together, not one after the other. So "earth" is made real by knotting it to "feeling," and "feeling" made real by "earth," each the other's witness — two lies knotted, like hen and egg, whose origin can never be found; this is the magician at the four-way crossroads, un-graspable from any side. When "earth" becomes a thing a feeler exists and a felt thing exists, and that is the person's birth — a dog seen in a dream is real, and "I" am born with it, so that neither can be falsified without the other; the person too is a piece of the same dream.
- **"Not self-made, not other-made, not spontaneous"; and science itself is only conceiving.** So this is done by no one and met by no one: "this puppet is not self-made, nor made by another," nor spontaneous either — for "spontaneous" only means "not those two," and it too cannot be inserted where the view is right; not-knowing constructs the whole story, and where that story is absent, cessation is realized. The devas-and-walls question shows the point: we take a wall as hard because it is felt, but hardness is a recognition relative only to our gross human body — to a subtle deva-body no "wall" or "earth" is constructed, so each perceives by its own plane, and the wall is neither made nor unmade. Likewise the "science" that says light must strike the eye for vision is only our human conceiving, false for a dog, cat, bat or owl that sees in the dark; both "light" and "darkness" are conceivings, so both are unreal views, and a science built of conceivings is ignorance, unable to disclose truth. Truth is realized only by stepping out of conceiving — by seeing the conceiving *as* conceiving through mindfulness, whereupon it fades and truth, nibbāna, is disclosed.

## Summary

This two-speaker dialogue holds that seeing the present means being unable to speak of past or future: "seeing that a thing arises by condition" means seeing its can't-be-made-ness, not how it was made, and that impossibility, made direct, is cessation. Yet we only ever examine the made thing — a chair's colour, shape, hardness — missing the "making," and fitting effect to causes we end at "the effect is not in the causes," the wrong way of seeing origination. Contact is where cause and effect are born together: "all things are rooted in contact," the causes and their "because" producing the action, and by grasping "because" we knot cause to effect like two leaning bricks, though neither belongs to that "because," so seeing contact both are seen un-arisen — cessation itself. As feeling arises, nose and breath are constructed with it, only when attention is foremost, knowing and both causes building through contact, the meeting of three; without contact none of them is here.

So all day we abide in an un-arisen nature — pure nibbāna — constructing only by not knowing it, unable to think or know it. Walking absorbed, no "seen"/"not seen" forms; fetching a cup, "going" and "taking" are never understood, only "cup," so the action is never met — only the knot tied to it, which holds no leg and no ground — and because action is never met, no doer is met, and the five clinging-aggregates form precisely because action is not seen as action. "I went to town" has no witness, so it falls to one's account only through the knot: to prove the going one cites a flower seen there, where desire becomes foremost and formations build; seen as action-by-action those are irrelevant, consciousness finds no footing, and the action never becomes kamma. The knotting is thus the cause of saṁsāra — un-arisen nature, action un-cognizable, and not knowing we stand there, the knotting happens through ignorance: with ignorance as condition, formations, and these temporary clinging-aggregates are saṁsāra, are suffering, which can never be understood because the "understander" is born of not seeing that understanding is itself the suffering and the view.

The ice-cube makes it concrete: hard, fluid, warm, vapour are four recognitions, not four things — "earth" holds no hardness, and a real earth could never burn to ash; not knowing a recognition as a recognition, we take "earth" as a thing, and "it is felt" arises inseparably, each making the other real, two lies knotted like hen and egg, the magician at the crossroads un-graspable from any side. Then a feeler and a felt thing exist, which is the person's birth — like a dog seen in a dream with which "I" am born, neither falsifiable without the other. So all this is done by no one and met by no one: "not self-made, not other-made," nor spontaneous, and where the story is absent, cessation is realized. Hardness is only a recognition relative to our gross body — a subtle deva builds no wall — and the science that says light must strike the eye is only human conceiving, false for animals that see in the dark; both light and darkness are conceivings, so a science of conceivings is ignorance, and truth is realized only by seeing conceiving as conceiving through mindfulness, whereupon it fades and nibbāna is disclosed.
