## Sutta referred to

- **Dhammapada 277–279** — the teacher quotes directly: "Sabbē saṅkhārā aniccāti yadā paññāya passati, atha nibbindati dukkhē, ēsa maggō visuddhiyā." When one sees with paññā (wisdom) that all saṅkhāra are anicca, one is released from dukkha — that is the path to visuddhi (= nibbāna). The teacher emphasises paññāya passati (seeing with wisdom) as distinct from jānatō (merely knowing/understanding): tērīma (comprehension) is viññāṇa-level, but passati requires paññā — a faculty that viññāṇa cannot generate.
- **Paṭicca samuppāda** — discussed structurally rather than by sutta name. The teacher argues that hētu-phala dhamma (cause-and-effect) is itself constructed from avijjā: the paccaya (conditions) attributed to a kriyā (action) were never actually present in the kriyā. The conventional hētu-phala analysis — "these causes produce this effect; remove the causes and the effect ceases" — is not samudaya-nirodha but a fabrication-then-negation cycle.
- **Caturrārya satya** — the four noble truths structure the entire discussion, particularly samudaya and nirodha. The teacher's central claim: samudaya pahāṇa vena tænamayī nirodha sākṣāt vennē — nirodha is realised at the very moment samudaya is abandoned, not after a period of observing samudaya.
- **Diṭṭha suta muta viññāta** — the four modes of cognition. The teacher asserts: diṭṭhayama, sutayama, mutayama, viññātayama dukkhak — the very act of cognising (not the object cognised) is dukkha. This is visible only through paññā, never through viññāṇa.

## Main dhamma points

- **Samudaya and nirodha are simultaneous, not sequential.** Many practitioners assume samudaya is observed over time and nirodha eventually appears. The teacher is explicit: samudaya pahāṇa vena tænamayī nirodha sākṣāt vennē — nirodha is realised at the same moment samudaya is abandoned. They are not two events separated by time. Seeing samudaya for years and then expecting nirodha to arrive is not the path. "Pera pasu novī ekaṭamayī vennē" (it happens together, not in sequence).
- **Two approaches to the middle: only one works.** Most practitioners try to dismantle the two extremes (anta deka) to arrive at the middle — "if I remove both athi and næthi, the middle remains." The teacher says this is wrong. The correct approach: seeing the middle (majjhē) directly causes the two extremes to dissolve. The mahā puruṣa (great being) is the one who sees the two extremes and thereby stands in the middle — not the one who eliminates them. Trying to reach the middle by deconstructing the extremes always falls into one or the other.
- **The breath-and-nose meditation trap.** When dæṇīma (feeling) is explored through breath-and-nose meditation, deep concentration can make both breath and nose imperceptible. The practitioner believes dæṇīma has been transcended — "I have gone beyond feeling." But what actually happened: the two saññā (recognitions — breath and nose) were suppressed, so dæṇīma became non-manifest. This is falling into ucchēda (annihilation extreme), not nirodha. The error: not knowing dæṇīma as dæṇīma (the middle), so the two sides (breath and nose) were constructed and then destroyed, leaving the practitioner in "næthi bhava" (non-existence state) — which is indistinguishable from nirodha and therefore deceptive. This is the pathway to brahma-lōka (formless realms), not nibbāna.
- **The birth (ipadīma) analogy: language itself constructs vikṛti.** If no child is born today, the word "birth" has no application. Yet to say "no birth occurred today" requires invoking the concept of birth — the non-occurred is expressed through the language of occurring. "Novēchcha ekak kiyanna giyāma hamuvīmak venavā" (trying to express what didn't happen creates an encountering). The teacher generalises: all dhamma statements ("pañcupādānakkhandha has not arisen," "paṭiccasamuppāda has not arisen") are ĩgi (pointers) to an unoccurred nature — but if taken as true, they become mana dhamma (mental objects) and sakkāya diṭṭhi forms beneath them undetected. This is dhamma becoming māra — "mārayā dharma vēśayen enavā" (māra comes in the guise of dhamma).
- **The matchbox-and-match (ginipēṭṭiya/ginikūra) analogy: hētu-phala is constructed.** The teacher develops this analogy extensively. Fire is conventionally attributed to matchbox and match as hētu (causes). But the actual hētu is ilīma/ulānava (striking) — and striking belongs to neither matchbox nor match, nor to fire. Neither matchbox alone, nor match alone, can perform striking. Therefore the hētu attributed to them were never actually present. Conventional samudaya analysis takes these false hētu, removes them, and concludes "no fire" — but this is ucchēda, not nirodha. "Mōḍakamin hadala āyē ekama nǣ kiyalā balanava" (fabricated by delusion, then looking at the same thing and saying "it's not there").
- **Ilīma (striking) is śūnya of itself.** When the teacher examines ilīma closely, it belongs to neither matchbox, match, nor fire. It cannot be defined independently of them, yet it is not any of them. Looking at ilīma through paññā, one sees it is śūnya (empty) of hētu — "ĩlīma ĩlīmen̆ma śūnyayi." There is no vigraha (analysis) possible that doesn't collapse into one of two extremes: "because of these hētu" or "not because of these hētu." Both are anta (extremes). The point where neither mode applies, where viññāṇa cannot construct a manasikarā — that is nirodha.
- **Tērīma (comprehension) itself is dukkha.** The teacher makes a striking claim: tērīma (understanding/comprehension) is itself aśihiya (un-mindfulness). The world calls tērīma "sihiya" (mindfulness) — knowing what one is doing, being aware. But tērīmama dukkhak — comprehension itself is suffering. Comprehension itself is the mulāva (delusion). Seeing that comprehension is mulāva cannot be done through viññāṇa, because viññāṇa IS the comprehension faculty. "Tērena bavama dukkhak. Tērena bavamayī aśihiya." This can only be seen through paññā — yadā paññāya passati.
- **The weaving shuttle (ṣaṭal) analogy: arahant's operation.** A loom has upper thread and shuttle thread (lower). The puthujjana's every action weaves both threads — the upper (parihāraṇa, functional operation) and the lower (saṅkhāra, fabrication that creates vipāka). The arahant's loom runs only the upper thread. The kriyā (action) happens — going, coming, eating, drinking — but the saṅkhāra thread doesn't engage. "Vissaṅkhāra maṭṭamen parihāraṇaya venavā" (operation at the dis-fabrication level). Like a father helping build a child's sand house — the house gets built, but no saṅkhāra forms in the father's mind that he is building something real.

## Summary

"විද්‍යා උදපාදි සාකච්ඡා - 01" (Vidya Udapadi Discussion 01) is the first in a series of group discussions (sākacchā) structured as interactive sessions where participants ask questions and the teacher (Mr. Lasantha) responds. The session opens with a format discussion — the teacher encourages participants to ask questions rather than passively listen, noting that the sākacchā format exists precisely because dēśanā (sermons) alone are insufficient for this understanding.

The session's core question: what is the difference between seeing samudaya-and-nirodha simultaneously versus seeing samudaya first and nirodha later? The teacher establishes the principle immediately: samudaya pahāṇa vena tænamayī nirodha sākṣāt vennē — they are simultaneous, not sequential. Someone who observes samudaya for years waiting for nirodha to appear is not on the path described by the Buddha.

The teacher then identifies two approaches to the "middle" (majjhē): dismantling the two extremes to reach it, versus seeing the middle directly and watching the extremes dissolve. Most practitioners take the first approach — examining athi (existence) and næthi (non-existence), removing both, hoping the middle remains. The teacher says this always fails because removing the extremes collapses into one extreme (typically ucchēda).

This is illustrated through the breath-and-nose meditation example. Deep concentration on breath makes both breath and nose imperceptible. The practitioner experiences this as nirodha — "dæṇīma has ceased." But the teacher shows this is ucchēda: the two saññā (breath-recognition and nose-recognition) were suppressed, making dæṇīma non-manifest. The practitioner moved from "tiyena bavin" (existing state) to "næti bavata" (non-existing state) — which is structurally identical to nirodha and therefore maximally deceptive. Contrast this with seeing dæṇīma as dæṇīma (the middle), which makes it impossible for athi bava to form in the first place, so there is nothing to negate.

The birth analogy deepens the point about language: saying "no birth occurred" requires invoking birth as a concept, so the non-occurrence is expressed through the language of occurrence, creating hamuvīma (encountering) of what never happened. The teacher generalises: all dhamma formulations — "pañcupādānakkhandha has not arisen," "saḷāyatana has not arisen" — are ĩgi (pointers) to an unoccurred nature. If taken as ættha (true), they become mana dhamma and sakkāya diṭṭhi forms beneath them invisibly. The dhamma formulations themselves are saṅkhāra — descriptions of mulāva (delusion) expressed through the same language that constitutes mulāva. The pāruva (raft) must eventually be released.

The matchbox-and-match analogy occupies the discussion's longest section. Fire is conventionally caused by matchbox and match — but the actual operative factor is ilīma (striking), which belongs to neither. Neither can perform striking alone; striking is not fire; yet without them, striking has no context. The conventional samudaya analysis removes the hētu (matchbox and match) and concludes "no fire" — this is ucchēda, not nirodha. The teacher calls this "yakā hadala āyē yakā boruyi kiyanavā" (creating the demon, then declaring the demon false). True samudaya is seeing how the hētu were attributed in the first place — through nodannākama (not-knowing) of the kriyā (action/ilīma).

When ilīma is seen through paññā: it is śūnya of the hētu attributed to it. Viññāṇa cannot construct a manasikarā at this point — there is no paccaya to grasp. This is vissaṅkhāra (dis-fabrication). The teacher is careful: nirodha is not a separate state that appears when samudaya disappears. Nirodha is the name given to the recognition that samudaya never actually occurred — "tiyena samudayak duru vēlā nirodhayak pēnavā nemēyi. Tiyena samudayak duru venna nam samudayak tiyenna epæyi." (It's not that an existing samudaya is removed and nirodha appears. For samudaya to be removed, there would need to be a samudaya — but there wasn't one.)

The session concludes with the weaving shuttle analogy: the arahant's kriyā operates without the saṅkhāra thread. Parihāraṇa (functional operation) continues — actions happen — but saṅkhāra (fabrication creating vipāka) does not engage. The father building a child's sand castle illustrates this: he performs every action of building, but no saṅkhāra forms in his mind. The kriyā and the saṅkhāra separate — or rather, the saṅkhāra is seen to have never been inherent in the kriyā.
