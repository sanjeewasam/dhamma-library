## Main dhamma points

- Nibbāna is not the annihilation of what exists. A Buddha is not needed to point out that things arise and cease — any fool knows that the born die and the healthy fall ill. The Buddha came to show the paṭiccuppanna dhamma — the conditioned present-moment nature — to free beings trapped in the two extremes.
- Viññāṇa can only operate in two modes: "exists" or "doesn't exist." What is being pointed to is where comprehension itself ends. Anyone hearing from within the sassata position will inevitably interpret the teaching as "these things don't exist" — that is viññāṇa's structural limitation.
- The fish-in-water analogy: a fish born, living, and dying in water has no vantage point from which to measure water. Likewise, a being embedded in sakkāya has no external reference from which to see the condition it is in.
- The two extremes (sassata and ucchēda) are carried from birth itself through diṭṭha, suta, muta, viññāṇāta. No one teaches a child to see or hear — these are borne innately. What needs to be taught is not these faculties but the unknowing (avijjā) within which they operate.
- Majjhimā paṭipadā is not a third position located between two extremes. If you grasp a "middle," you automatically carry two sides — majjhē mantā na lippati means the middle itself does not adhere in the seeing. The majjhimā is not found by looking from within the two extremes; it is the point from which both extremes are simultaneously seen for what they are.
- The Buddha did not teach from a "middle" that exists between two sides. There is no way to name or describe where the Buddha stood when teaching — any label (middle, beyond, neither) gets recaptured by viññāṇa into one of the two extremes.
- The chicken-and-egg demonstration: neither came first. The arising of conditions (paccaya) has no base, no beginning — pubbe koṭi na paññāyati. Seeing this baselessness is what it means to transcend birth and death. There is nothing to seek as an "end" where no "beginning" existed.
- Aṅgulimāla: having killed 999, the moment he saw the paccuppanna dhamma, all past kamma lost its capacity to bear fruit — because if ippedīma (birth) ends here, there is no one to receive vipāka. Past kamma is not "worked off" sequentially; the structure that receives results simply does not form.
- The matchbox analogy (ginipeṭṭiya-ginikūra), extensively developed: fire is neither in the matchbox nor in the matchstick, yet it cannot arise without both. The critical insight is the striking (ilīma/ælillīma) — this action belongs to neither hētu nor phala. From the striking alone, you cannot derive any concept of matchbox, matchstick, or fire.
- Hētu and phala arise simultaneously through the kriyā of contact. They are not sequential — the matchbox and matchstick become "causes" and fire becomes "effect" only through the striking, not before it. Seeing the striking as striking (phassa as phassa) = seeing that hētu never actually arose as a separate entity. This is anutpāda (non-arising), which is nirodha and anicca seen together.
- Paccuppannaṁ yō dhammaṁ tattha tattha vipassati — "there, there" means at the point of contact itself, not at the resultant extremes. We habitually look at the "two ends of the rope" (the kōn dekak) — the hētu and the phala — while the knot (gæṭēya = phassa) goes unseen. Seeing the knot is what transcends both ends.
- Nicca saññā operates invisibly: things taken as permanent (the chair one sits on, breathing, cowdung by the roadside) drop below awareness precisely because of their permanence. "Not thinking about X" is itself nicca saññā — and this permanence is identical to the sense that something "exists." One cannot see anicca by thinking or by not-thinking — both are anta. Only paññā sees the unknowing nature (avijjā) as unknowing (nondannakama nondanna bavin).
- The fire analogy applied to phassa: when striking is seen as striking, no hētu is found within it — therefore ñāṇa does not need to check whether phala exists or has ceased. Udayabbaya is seen simultaneously — samudaya and nirodha are not sequential observations but a single seeing. The saṁsāric construction (hētu-phala connection) and its non-arising are seen in the same moment.
- Sōtāpanna ≠ arahant. Seeing the darśanaya means seeing the baselessness — but rāga and dvēsha continue from habitual momentum (puruddha). The path develops as maññanā gradually wears away, and with it rāga-dvēsha diminish proportionally. Maximum seven bhava to completion.
- The darśanaya is not restricted to monastics. The Dīghajānu/Vyagghapajja Sutta shows a layperson (living with gold, silver, perfumes) being taught udayatthagāminī paññā and attaining sōtāpatti. Sakkāya-nirodha is unique to Buddha-dhamma — no other teacher has taught it — but it is a seeing (dækma), not a lifestyle requirement.
- Aṭṭha purisa puggalā defines the ariya-sāvaka saṅgha: four pairs on the path and fruit. Until one enters these, one is puthujjana — regardless of robes. Claiming that seeing the darśanaya requires monastic life is itself another diṭṭhi — another pair of extremes (gihī vs pavidi) being constructed.

## Summary

The second Vidya Udapadi discussion opens with a common misunderstanding about nibbāna: the assumption that it means the disappearance or negation of what exists. Mr. Lasantha addresses this head-on. A Buddha does not appear in the world to announce that things arise and cease — that is obvious to anyone. The born die, the healthy fall ill, hair turns white. No special teaching is needed for that. What a Buddha reveals is the paṭiccuppanna dhamma — the present-moment conditioned structure — that beings are unknowingly trapped within, and the two extremes (sassata and ucchēda) through which that trap operates.

He explains viññāṇa's fundamental limitation: it can only process reality in two modes — "this exists" or "this doesn't exist." There is no third register available to viññāṇa. Consequently, anyone hearing this teaching while operating from the sassata position (which is everyone's default, since diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta are carried from birth) will inevitably interpret it as "these things don't exist" — and thereby fall into the ucchēda extreme. The fish-in-water analogy illustrates this structural blindness: a fish has no vantage point from which to know water as water, just as a being embedded in sakkāya cannot see sakkāya from within it.

The discussion then turns to majjhimā paṭipadā, which participants initially understand as a "middle" position between two extremes. Mr. Lasantha systematically dismantles this: if you grasp a middle, you necessarily carry two sides — a middle only has meaning relative to two ends. The verse majjhē mantā na lippati shows that the avabōdhaya does not even adhere to the concept of "middle." He asks: if the Buddha abandoned the two extremes and came to a middle, does that not mean the two extremes are still held as real? Can you cut a stick at both ends and keep only the middle? The entire structure — two ends and middle — must be transcended together. This is why the dhamma is described as a raft (pāru) to be abandoned after crossing.

The chicken-and-egg analogy demonstrates the baselessness of hētu-phala relations. Which came first, the chicken or the egg? Neither — conditionality has no starting point. Pubbe koṭi na paññāyati — no first point can be established. Seeing this baselessness is what it means to transcend birth and death. There is nothing to seek as an "ending" because no "beginning" ever existed as such. This is why Aṅgulimāla, despite having killed 999 people, could see the paccuppanna dhamma in one moment and bring saṃsāra to an end — if the structure that receives vipāka does not form, no amount of past kamma can produce results.

The matchbox analogy receives the most extended treatment and becomes the centrepiece of the discussion. Fire (gindara) is neither in the matchbox (ginipeṭṭiya) nor in the matchstick (ginikūra). Yet fire cannot arise without both. The striking (ilīma) is the critical action — and it belongs to none of the three: not to the matchbox, not to the matchstick, not to the fire. From the concept "striking" alone, one cannot derive any concept of matchbox, matchstick, or fire. This demonstrates that hētu and phala are not sequential — they arise simultaneously through the kriyā of contact. Before the striking, there is no "cause" and no "effect"; these designations are retrospective constructions imposed after the contact event.

Mr. Lasantha then pushes deeper: if hētu cannot be found within phala, and phala cannot be found within hētu, and yet neither can exist without the other — what is actually happening? The answer is phassa (contact/striking) seen as phassa. When striking is seen in its own nature (tattha tattha — "there, there"), no hētu arises within it and therefore no phala needs to be checked. This is anutpāda — non-arising. The seer does not observe arising-and-ceasing sequentially (udaya-vaya in the conventional sense); rather, samudaya and nirodha are seen simultaneously because the baselessness of the hētu-phala structure is seen in the very act of contact. This is paccuppannaṁ yō dhammaṁ tattha tattha vipassati.

The discussion addresses nicca saññā as the invisible medium within which beings operate. Things taken as permanent drop below awareness — one does not think about the chair one sits on, or about breathing, or about cowdung by the roadside, precisely because these have been rendered permanent (nicca) in one's cognitive structure. This imperceptibility IS nicca saññā. Crucially, "not thinking about X" is structurally identical to "X permanently exists" — both are the same nicca condition expressed in different words. One cannot escape this by trying to think about it (that reinforces one extreme) or by trying not to think (that falls into the other extreme). Only paññā — which sees both the thought-mode and the non-thought-mode from a single vantage — can penetrate the unknowing.

A participant raises the question of whether seeing this darśanaya means becoming an arahant, and whether it is therefore incompatible with lay life. Mr. Lasantha clarifies: sōtāpanna is not arahant. The darśanaya is a seeing of the baselessness — but rāga and dvēsha continue from habitual momentum. The path develops as maññanā gradually wears away; rāgakkhayō, dvēsakkhayō proceed proportionally. Maximum seven existences remain. The Dīghajānu Sutta is cited as direct evidence: a layperson living with gold, silver, and perfumes was taught udayatthagāminī paññā and attained sōtāpatti. To claim that the darśanaya requires monasticism is itself another diṭṭhi — another pair of extremes being constructed (lay vs. monastic), with all the same structural problems just analysed. The ariya-sāvaka saṅgha is defined by aṭṭha purisa puggalā — four pairs on path and fruit — not by robes.

The session closes with a reminder about dāna for the Karandeniye hermitage under Ratpaskaṭiyē Vimalaratana Thēro, and aspirations for all participants to realise nibbāna through caturrārya satya avabōdhaya.
