# විද්‍යා උදපාදි සාකච්ඡා - 03

## Sutta referred to

- **Paṭicca samuppāda** — the central structural framework: nāmarūpa paccayā viññāṇaṁ / viññāṇa paccayā nāmarūpaṁ; the full chain (saḷāyatana → phassa → vedanā → taṇhā → upādāna → bhava → jāti → jarā-maraṇa) is cited in its nirodha direction
- **Nicca saññā / anicca** — the permanent-perception that renders itself invisible
- **Sakkāya diṭṭhi** — self-identity view, shown to be structurally unseeable from within
- **Ucchēdānta / śāśvatānta** — the two extremes (annihilationism / eternalism), shown to be co-dependent constructions
- **Avyākata pañhā / catuskōṭika** — the undeclared questions and the tetralemma (exists / doesn't exist / both / neither), all four positions shown to be wrongly formed
- **Diṭṭha, suta, muta, viññāta** — the four bases through which "I exist" is constructed
- **Kriyā citta** — functional consciousness of the arahant, referenced in the chair/drama analogy
- **Vicikicchā** — doubt, shown to dissolve when the question itself is recognised as ill-formed
- **Anicca, dukkha, anattā (tilakkhaṇa)** — referenced as what is truly understood when the paṭicca samuppāda chain ceases
- **Samudaya / nirodha** — cessation is seen within arising, not by setting arising aside
- **Yathābhūta ñāṇadassana** — seeing things as they actually are, arising when both extremes are released

## Main dhamma points

- Everything stated in the teaching is an "iguiya" (hint/pointer) toward a seeing that lies beyond the words; whatever the listener "understands" is necessarily a copy of the very delusion being pointed at (mulāvēma kōpiyak).
- Paṭicca samuppāda was given to show what *cannot* stand or be encountered by a person — not to describe an existing process. The "person" is constructed from nāmarūpa/viññāṇa/saḷāyatana, so a person can never step outside and encounter these components.
- Dream analogy: describing a dream after waking produces a duplicate of the dream, not knowledge of it. The dream state is presented as *more* revealing of our actual condition than waking life, because in the dream the total constructedness of experience is on full display.
- Nicca saññā (permanent-perception) operates by invisibility: what is permanently present is never noticed. The mind (hita) sits permanently, so we investigate everything FROM it without ever registering it. Correctly seeing nicca saññā *is* the seeing of anicca.
- Taṇhā is present even where nothing is "desired": the black stone on the road that we never think about is held in place by the certainty "nothing will happen from it" — that certainty is itself taṇhā yielding nicca bhāva. The ucchēdānta (annihilationist extreme — "not-existing") is never experienced by viññāṇa, yet it functions as the hidden support for every śāśvata (eternalist) position.
- Sleep/wakefulness analogy (central, extensively developed): Sleep has never been experienced by anyone. "Wakefulness" is constructed entirely from "sleep is absent" — it is the non-occurrence of the unknown dressed up as a positive attainment. The nondannākama (unknowing) that makes sleep un-experienceable is the same nondannākama from which the entire waking world is built.
- The analogy is then turned against itself: the statement "sleep is absent" already smuggles in sleep as something real that could be absent. Every attempt to state the ucchēdānta produces a śāśvatānta. This is shown to be the mechanism of sakkāya diṭṭhi: we never see the constructing because we ARE the constructing.
- Chair/perceiver mutual arising: "The chair is there because I'm here" and "I'm here because the chair is there" are shown to be the same statement. Neither the chair nor the perceiver is prior; both arise in a single stroke of viññāṇa. This is sakkāya — invisible because it is the very lens through which everything is seen.
- TV drama analogy (extensively developed): When absorbed in a drama, both "watching" and "eating" (if eating simultaneously) go unregistered. The viewer becomes the characters. Only after the drama ends does the conclusion "I watched" arise. During absorption, neither kriyā (action) nor kartṛ (agent) is present, yet pariharaṇa (functioning/use) has occurred. This is used to show that the question "did functioning occur or not?" falls into the catuskōṭika — all four positions are avyākata.
- The screen (tira) itself: the claim "nothing happened to the screen" is itself "kuṇu" (refuse) — it imports a screen as something that could have things happen or not happen to it. The teaching is pointing to a place where even that claim cannot be made.
- Māyā: the world is neither existent nor non-existent, yet māyā "exists." This "existing" of māyā is the mæda tævarilīma (middle-smearing) — the constructedness presenting itself as something stable. A film, a mirage — cannot be called real, cannot be called unreal, yet they are there.
- The catuskōṭika (tetralemma) is applied to the arahant's post-mortem status: exists? doesn't exist? both? neither? All four are avyākata (undeclared) because the question presupposes a kriyā (action/functioning) that was never encountered in the first place.
- No method (kriyāvaliya) can be prescribed for reaching what is nondannā (unknowable), because in a space where even the four tetralemma positions do not apply, the very concept of "doing" or "not doing" is inapplicable. Recognising this dissolves vicikicchā (doubt), because the question "how do I get there?" is itself formed from not seeing the nondannākama clearly.
- 360-degree wisdom: "seeing every kuṇu (refuse/flaw)" IS the nuwaṇa (wisdom). There is no separate vantage point from which wisdom sees — the capacity to see all kuṇu from every angle is itself what is meant by wisdom. This is not a "thing" that can be pointed to; the belief that there is a separate "place" of wisdom is itself the final kuṇu.
- Both extremes seen together: when ucchēdānta is truly seen (through nondannākama seeing itself), and śāśvatānta is truly seen, both reveal themselves as lābhīma (mere acquisitions). The division between them — the very act of dividing "exists" from "doesn't exist" — is itself the paccaya (condition). Seeing the division as paccaya is what is meant by "seeing both extremes together," and it is at that point that both become unable to sustain themselves. This is described as yathābhūta ñāṇadassana.

## Summary

Discussion 03 opens with a critical meta-teaching that governs everything that follows: whatever is said in these discussions is an iguiya — a hint, a pointer — not the seeing itself. The listener's understanding, however subtle, is necessarily a "copy of the delusion" (mulāvēma kōpiyak), because understanding is an operation of viññāṇa, and viññāṇa is precisely the mechanism being pointed beyond. This framing is not a disclaimer; it is itself a teaching. Paṭicca samuppāda, Lasantha explains, was given not to describe how things arise but to show what *cannot* be encountered: the "person" is assembled from nāmarūpa, viññāṇa, and saḷāyatana, so no person can step outside these components and observe them. A dream analogy makes the point: describing a dream after waking is just another dream. The dream state, Lasantha argues, is actually more honest about our condition than waking life, because in the dream the total fabrication is undisguised.

The discussion then takes up nicca saññā — the perception of permanence — and locates it not in what we notice but in what we never notice. The roof overhead, the road underfoot, the black stone at the roadside: these are never thought about, and that very absence of thought is the signature of nicca saññā. What is permanently present becomes invisible. The mind (hita) is permanently operative, so everything is investigated *from* it, and it itself is never registered. Correctly seeing this nicca saññā, Lasantha says, IS the seeing of anicca — not a separate insight but the same recognition turned around.

A participant asks whether things we never think about are free of taṇhā. Lasantha's answer is emphatic: taṇhā is fully present there. The certainty that "nothing will happen from the black stone" is itself a taṇhā that produces nicca bhāva (permanence-state). This leads into a structural analysis of the ucchēdānta (annihilationist extreme). The "not-existing" pole is never experienced — viññāṇa cannot register it — yet it silently supports every claim of "existing." This is the hidden mechanism: what cannot be experienced functions as the unacknowledged ground of what is experienced.

The centrepiece of the discussion is a sustained analogy of sleep and wakefulness. Sleep, Lasantha argues, has never been experienced by anyone — ever. After falling asleep, there is no registration of being asleep, of having a body, of being alive or dead. Nothing. "Wakefulness" is then shown to be entirely parasitic on this un-experienced sleep: what we call "awake" is simply "sleep is not present." The nondannākama (unknowing) that makes sleep un-experienceable is the very same unknowing from which the waking world is constructed. The analogy is then methodically turned against itself. The statement "sleep is absent" already treats sleep as something real whose absence can be noted — and that is a śāśvata (eternalist) move. Every attempt to express the ucchēdānta (that which is never encountered) immediately generates a śāśvatānta (a permanent something). The analogy itself — the very words "sleep" and "wakefulness" — is shown to be a raft that must be let go.

A participant (Roshānārā) raises the concern that since everything being discussed is within avijjā, the discussion itself cannot yield anything. Lasantha's response is precise: yes, every example, every analogy is saṅkhāra built from nondannākama — there are no "lokōttara examples." But the point is not to extract artha (meaning) from the examples; it is to see the artha śūnya bava (meaninglessness) that runs through every example. If the investigation of the analogy reveals that every point within it fails to land — that every word dissolves on contact — then the analogy has done its work. This is what is meant by "samudaya within which nirodha is seen": not setting the arising aside, but seeing through the arising until it cannot sustain itself.

The discussion then turns to the chair/perceiver mutual arising. Lasantha demonstrates through dialogue: "How do you know the chair exists?" The answer always refers back to the perceiver ("I see it, I feel it"). "How do you know you exist?" The answer refers back to perceived objects ("Because I see the chair"). The two are shown to be the same statement rotated. Neither is prior; both arise in a single stroke. This is sakkāya diṭṭhi — invisible because it *is* the seeing.

A TV drama analogy extends this further. When absorbed in a drama while eating, neither the watching nor the eating is registered. The viewer becomes the characters — kriyā (action) and kartṛ (agent) are both absent, yet pariharaṇa (functioning) has occurred. Only afterwards does the viewer conclude "I watched the drama, I ate the food." The screen itself, Lasantha insists, cannot be brought into the analysis: to say "nothing happened to the screen" is already to import the screen as a something — more "kuṇu" (refuse). The teaching is pointing to where even that claim cannot be made.

A participant (Vipul) asks about viññāṇa nirodha — how to "know" the non-arising of viññāṇa. Lasantha applies the catuskōṭika: did functioning occur? All four answers (yes, no, both, neither) are avyākata (undeclared), because the question presupposes a kriyā that was never encountered. No method can be prescribed for reaching what is nondannā, because in a space where even the tetralemma does not apply, the concept of a method has no footing. Recognising this, Lasantha says, dissolves vicikicchā — not by answering the doubt, but by revealing that the question is built from not seeing the nondannākama. The "360-degree wisdom" image closes this thread: seeing every kuṇu from every angle IS the nuwaṇa. There is no separate vantage point.

The discussion closes with the observation that when ucchēdānta and śāśvatānta are both truly seen — through nondannākama seeing itself — both reveal themselves as mere lābhīma (acquisitions). The division between "exists" and "doesn't exist" is itself the paccaya. Seeing that division as paccaya is what collapses both extremes simultaneously and is what the tradition calls yathābhūta ñāṇadassana.
