# විද්‍යා උදපාදි සාකච්ඡා - 04

## Sutta referred to

- **Paṭicca samuppāda** — the full chain from avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā through to vedanā, taṇhā, upādāna — cited explicitly to show how the entire structure arises as a single fabrication from not-knowing, not sequentially
- **Tilakkhaṇa** — uppādo paññāyati, vayo paññāyati, ṭhitassa aññathattaṃ paññāyati — arising, decay, and change-while-standing as the three designated marks
- **Catuskōṭika / avyākata** — the four positions (is, is not, both, neither) applied to puñña-pāpa, hetu-phala, and the nature of kriyā
- **Satipaṭṭhāna** — kāyānupassanā, vedanānupassanā, cittānupassanā, dhammānupassanā — used to demonstrate why there is no separate saññānupassanā
- **Mahānidāna Sutta** — nāmarūpa conditions viññāṇa and viññāṇa conditions nāmarūpa: mutual conditioning with no prior beginning (pubbe koṭi na paññāyati)
- **Pheṇapiṇḍūpama** — vedanā as water-bubble (non-establishment), saññā as mirage (marīci); rūpa as foam, viññāṇa as magician at a crossroads
- **Diṭṭha / suta / muta / viññāta** — the four bases of experience, all shown as non-establishing
- **Maññanā** — conceiving/measuring, the unseen root that makes vedanā appear as a bodily "thing"
- **Manasikāra sambhava sabbe dhammā** — all dhammas arise through manasikāra (attention)
- **Puñña-pāpa pahīnassa / āsavakkhaya** — one who has abandoned merit and demerit; the ending of āsava

## Main dhamma points

- A teacher's silence in response to a question does not imply agreement. Some questions are built on wrong foundations (dēyak darāgen) and cannot be answered without reinforcing the opposite extreme. The dhamma is atakkāvacara — beyond logic and debate.

- **Bicycle balance analogy for moha**: Balance itself — the middle where you actually ride — is moha/nondannākama. Within balance, there is nothing to understand: no left, no right. This is precisely why moha is never seen. Neither "correct" nor "incorrect" can reach the middle. The inability to distinguish right from wrong IS the condition we inhabit (hariyī varadiyī dēka haduṇē api inna svabhāvaya notēreṇa nisā).

- **Flower-picking analogy**: The action of picking (kadanavā) carries neither puñña nor pāpa. The kriyā is free of both. This is the catuskōṭika applied: all four positions are avyākata. Puñña-pāpa pahīnassa = āsavakkhaya. Wisdom IS the inability itself (bæri bavamayī nuwaṇa). Pariharaṇa (mere functioning) occurs without kamma formation.

- **Sewing-machine shuttle analogy**: The upper thread represents pariharaṇa (surface functioning). The shuttle's lower thread represents moha creating copies/traces (kōpiya vadiNavā). Without moha, the upper thread runs without knots — no saṅkhāra form. In kriyā citta, one knows flower as flower, chair as chair, but the "I did it" diṭṭhi-knot does not tie.

- **Vijānāti vs. pajānāti**: Vijānāti = knowing through viññāṇa. Pajānāti = knowing through paññā. If they were the same, the Buddha would not have used two words. The Ānanda example: dhamma bhāṇḍāgārika (treasurer of the dhamma) who heard everything yet became arahant only at the very end, in a posture between postures — neither lying down nor in any of the four iriyāpatha — when even "I will become arahant" was released.

- **Colour-recognition analogy (child learning "blue")**: The eye cannot identify colour by itself. The ear cannot. Mind-thinking cannot. The finger-pointing (ægilla tivīma) creates the association-knot. But the finger-pointing is itself seen through the eye. So three impossibilities are hidden by a fourth impossibility. The child can never find WHERE blue is located — there is no "place" for colour. Āsrava is itself śūnya — constructed from nondannākama, not a separate real entity.

- **Kakkuma (headache/pain) analogy**: Pain is always experienced with a body part — never as pain-ness alone. The question about the Buddha's pain is formed from our structural limitation. Seeing pain as pain-ness itself (kakkuma bawin dakinna), without the body-part knot, is what ñāṇa does. With ñāṇa, no hētu-chain knots form.

- **Vedanā / water-bubble reinterpretation**: The Buddha's point is NOT about arising-passing (the common interpretation). The bubble belongs neither to rain-water nor ground-water — non-establishment (nopihiṭīma). The bubble's inability to claim belonging/establishment anywhere is the real teaching, not impermanence. Observing udayavaya endlessly is āyāsa (strain), not liberation.

- **Non-establishment dissolves both samudaya and nirodha**: When wisdom sees that arising has no findable place of arising, there is no need to separately observe cessation. The one who sees nopihiṭīma transcends both the "arising" story and the "ceasing" story. This is nirāyāsa (effortless) — because the very concept of arising-and-ceasing is itself seen as avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā.

- **Why there is no saññānupassanā**: Fever/thermometer analogy — saññā is like the thermometer reading (105°, 110°). Vedanā is the fever itself. When the fever is cured, the reading disappears automatically. No separate treatment needed for the number. Vedanānupassanā, rightly practised, dissolves saññā without any additional anupassanā. If vedanā is seen as non-establishing, saññā has nothing to label. If diṭṭha/suta/muta/viññāta are all seen as non-establishing, the maññanā (conceiving) built upon them collapses without separate effort.

- **Mirage (marīci) as deeper deception than the bubble**: The mirage shows something appearing where it cannot be found. The deceptive power is greater — it makes you see water where none exists. We use saññā (the mirage itself) to identify things, then label the identified thing "mirage." Only when seeing the conditionally-arisen (paccayena uppadina) nature does the term "mirage" yield its real meaning.

- **Vedanā and body as mutually establishing**: Vedanā becomes "real" because of the head (body part). The head becomes "real" because of vedanā. Each proves the other — neither has independent establishment. This is the chicken-and-egg structure. Viññāṇa is the magician at the crossroads (hataraman handiyē māyākāraya) — from whichever direction you look, you cannot catch the trick, because A is always proven real by B and B by A.

- **Pubbe koṭi na paññāyati**: No prior beginning can be designated. The inability to find a beginning or end IS wisdom (bæri kam nuwaṇa). This is not a failure of analysis but the nature of the structure itself.

- **Hetu-phala arise together, not sequentially**: Matchbox and match are only "causes" when striking produces fire. Without the striking, they are not causes. We forget the striking (phassa) and fixate on cause-and-effect as permanent truths. Mother and father come into being at the moment of the child's birth — not before. From the Mahānidāna Sutta: nāmarūpa conditions viññāṇa and viññāṇa conditions nāmarūpa. The question "did viññāṇa exist before?" is itself wrong — the question dissolves, not an answer arrives. This is how vicikicchā is abandoned.

- **Phassa as the pivot**: Seeing phassa properly means seeing samudaya and nirodha simultaneously. One who sees phassa sees that hetu-phala arise from not-knowing (nondannākama). At that point, the entire paṭicca samuppāda chain is seen as a single fabrication, not as sequential stages. Kriyā rightly seen becomes the bridge to nibbāna; kriyā unseen becomes the gateway to saṃsāra. The two are separated by a hair's breadth — wisdom is that sharp.

- **Kāyika vedanā vs. manasikāra**: We experience vedanā as bodily only because we don't know it arises through manasikāra. Because we cannot see manasikāra as manasikāra, we take vedanā as something real located in the body. Maññanā not seen as maññanā is the root cause of this misattribution. Manasikāra sambhava sabbe dhammā — all dhammas have manasikāra as their origin.

## Summary

Discussion 04 opens with a methodological point about the nature of questions in dhamma inquiry. Mr. Lasantha explains that his silence after a question should never be taken as agreement — some questions are built on foundations that are already wrong (dēyak darāgen), and answering them directly would reinforce the very delusion the teaching aims to dissolve. The dhamma is atakkāvacara: it cannot be reached through logical debate or argumentation. This sets the frame for a discussion that proceeds through a remarkable series of analogies, each layering upon the previous to reveal deeper structural features of experience.

The bicycle balance analogy introduces the central theme: moha (delusion) or nondannākama (unknowing) is like the balance point when riding. Within balance, there is no experience of left or right — balance is never encountered as an object. This is precisely why moha is invisible. The key insight is that neither "correct understanding" nor "incorrect understanding" can reach this middle, because the middle is not a position at all. We inhabit a condition defined by the inability to distinguish right from wrong — and that inability IS the condition (hariyī varadiyī dēka haduṇē api inna svabhāvaya notēreṇa nisā).

The flower-picking analogy applies this to action (kamma). The act of picking a flower carries neither puñña nor pāpa in itself — the kriyā is free of both. All four positions of the catuskōṭika are avyākata (undeclared). What the tradition calls puñña-pāpa pahīnassa (abandonment of merit and demerit) is āsavakkhaya — the ending of āsava. The radical claim here is that wisdom IS the inability itself (bæri bavamayī nuwaṇa). Pariharaṇa — the mere functioning of life — continues without kamma-formation.

The sewing-machine shuttle analogy gives this a mechanical clarity. The upper thread is pariharaṇa — the visible surface of action. The shuttle's hidden lower thread is moha, which creates copies and traces (kōpiya) underneath every action. When moha operates, every stitch forms a knot — saṅkhāra. Without the shuttle thread, the upper thread runs freely: action occurs but no knot ties. This is kriyā citta — one knows flower as flower, chair as chair, but the diṭṭhi-knot of "I did it" does not form.

A critical distinction is drawn between vijānāti (knowing through viññāṇa) and pajānāti (knowing through paññā). These are not synonyms; had they meant the same, the Buddha would not have needed two words. The Ānanda example makes this vivid: Ānanda was the dhamma bhāṇḍāgārika, the treasurer who heard and retained every teaching, yet his arahantship came only at the very end, in a posture between postures — neither lying down nor standing nor sitting nor walking — at the moment when even "I will become arahant" was released. Vijānāti (the viññāṇa-mode of knowing) carried him through decades of learning; pajānāti (the paññā-mode) required the dissolution of even that intention.

The colour-recognition analogy is developed at length and reveals the structure of āsrava. A child learning "blue" cannot identify the colour through the eye alone, the ear alone, or thinking alone. The finger-pointing (ægilla tivīma) creates the association-knot — but the finger-pointing is itself seen through the eye. So three impossibilities (eye, ear, mind each failing to reach colour independently) are hidden by a fourth impossibility (the pointing gesture, which is itself sense-dependent). The child can never locate WHERE blue resides because there is no "place" for colour. This structure shows that āsrava (influx, defilement) is itself śūnya — it is not a separate real entity but a construction arising from nondannākama. It has no independent existence to be found or removed.

The kakkuma (headache/pain) analogy reveals the same structure in vedanā. Pain is always experienced with a body part — never as pain-ness alone, floating in empty space. When we ask about the Buddha's pain, the question is formed from within our own structural limitation: we cannot separate pain from location. Seeing pain as pain-ness itself (kakkuma bawin dakinna) without the body-part knot is what ñāṇa accomplishes. With ñāṇa, no hētu-chain knots form — the eye didn't bring it, the ear didn't bring it, yet there it is.

The vedanā/water-bubble teaching from the Pheṇapiṇḍūpama Sutta is then reinterpreted against the common reading. The standard interpretation takes the bubble as illustrating impermanence — arising and passing. Mr. Lasantha argues the Buddha's point is different: the bubble belongs neither to the rain-water nor to the ground-water. It cannot establish itself anywhere. The teaching is about non-establishment (nopihiṭīma), not about observing change. Endlessly watching udayavaya (arising-and-vanishing) is āyāsa — strain, not liberation. When wisdom sees that arising has no findable place of arising, there is no need to separately observe cessation. Both the "arising" story and the "ceasing" story are transcended simultaneously. This is nirāyāsa — effortless.

This leads to a striking structural observation about the satipaṭṭhāna: there are four anupassanā for the five khandha — kāyānupassanā for rūpa, vedanānupassanā for vedanā, cittānupassanā for saṅkhāra, dhammānupassanā for viññāṇa — but there is no saññānupassanā. Why? The fever/thermometer analogy makes it clear. Saññā is like the thermometer reading: it measures and labels vedanā. When the fever (vedanā) is treated, the reading (saññā) disappears automatically. There is no need to treat the number separately. If vedanā is seen as non-establishing — the water-bubble insight — then saññā has nothing left to label. And the mirage (marīci) analogy for saññā makes the deception even deeper: a mirage shows something appearing where it cannot be found. We use saññā to identify things, then label the identified thing "a mirage." But if peeling back through paccaya (condition), the conditionally-arisen nature is seen, then both the labeling and what was labeled dissolve together.

The discussion then reveals the mutual-establishment structure between vedanā and the body. Vedanā becomes "real" because the head exists. The head becomes "real" because vedanā is felt there. Each proves the other; neither has independent ground. This is the chicken-and-egg (kikiḷi-bittara) structure, and it is why viññāṇa is compared to a magician at a crossroads — from whichever of the four directions you approach, you cannot catch the trick, because A is always validated by B and B by A. The inability to find a beginning — pubbe koṭi na paññāyati — is not a failure of analysis but the nature of the fabrication itself. That inability IS wisdom.

The matchbox-and-match analogy extends this to hetu-phala (cause and effect). A matchbox and match lying side by side are not "causes" of anything. They become causes only when striking produces fire. But we forget the striking — the phassa — and fixate on the cause-effect pair as permanent truths. Mother and father come into being at the same moment as the child — not before. From the Mahānidāna Sutta, nāmarūpa conditions viññāṇa and viññāṇa conditions nāmarūpa; the question "which came first?" dissolves. No answer arrives — the question itself departs. This is how vicikicchā (doubt) is abandoned.

The discussion concludes with the pivot-point of phassa. One who sees phassa properly sees samudaya and nirodha simultaneously. Seeing that hetu-phala arise from not-knowing means seeing the entire paṭicca samuppāda chain as a single fabrication, not as sequential stages unfolding in time. At this point, kriyā rightly comprehended becomes the bridge to nibbāna; kriyā not comprehended remains the gateway to saṃsāra. The two are separated by a kṣaṇa mātra — a hair's breadth of an instant. Wisdom is that sharp. The final clarification: we experience vedanā as "bodily" only because we cannot see manasikāra as manasikāra. Maññanā not recognised as maññanā is the root of all misattribution. Manasikāra sambhava sabbe dhammā — all dhammas arise through attention, and it is the blindness to this that makes the constructed appear as the found.
