# විද්‍යා උදපාදි සාකච්ඡා - 06

## Sutta referred to

- **Nate kāmāyānī citrāni tateva loke saṅkappa rāgo purisassa kāmo** — pictures in the world hold no desire; desire is in the conceiving (saṅkappa), not in the forms
- **Tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso** — the contact formula: three-fold meeting of base, object, and consciousness
- **Cakkhuṃ paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu viññāṇaṃ** — dependent arising of eye-consciousness
- **Manasikāra sambhavā sabbē dhammā** — all dhammas originate from attention (manasikāra)
- **Chanda mūlikā sabbē dhammā** — all dhammas have desire/intention as their root
- **Anto jaṭā bahi jaṭā, jaṭāya jaṭitā pajā** — the inner tangle, the outer tangle, the generation entangled in tangles
- **Sabbē saṅkhārā aniccā** — all saṅkhāra are impermanent (seen only when all dharma-holdings are recognised as refuse)

## Main dhamma points

- **Whatever dhamma one holds becomes māra**: Any dharma understanding retained as "correct" or "true" is itself saṅkhāra — built from the same language, the same conceptual apparatus that was used to describe the delusion. Holding dharma and holding the world produce sakkāya equally. The dharma was taught to reveal the error in understanding, not to give something new to hold.

- **Sammā diṭṭhi is not a correct viewpoint**: It is not seeing from a special angle (kōṇa) but seeing that seeing-from-angles is itself the error. As long as one maintains "the dharma perspective" as distinct from "the worldly perspective," one has merely added another angle. The world seen as the world IS the dhamma; the problem is not seeing the world but not seeing the root (mūla) of how the world was constructed.

- **Activity (kriyākārīttva) is never encountered by viññāṇa**: This is the central thesis. Viññāṇa registers only nāma-rūpa (name-and-form). The activity or process that makes things appear alive and real is never accessible to viññāṇa. Because this activity cannot be encountered, viññāṇa constructs a living world from what is actually inert.

- **River analogy — flowing cannot be located**: Water in a glass does not flow. A river flows. But "flowing" cannot be found in the water, in the riverbed, or anywhere. Flowing is an un-encounterable activity (kriyā) that gives the river its living quality. We mistake this for a property of the river itself because we cannot see manasikāra as manasikāra.

- **Film projector analogy — activity arises from none of the four**: The negative holds only still images. The projector merely passes them through. The light adds no movement. The screen is static. Yet walking, talking, drinking tea — all these activities appear. None of the four components produces movement. The activity that makes the story coherent never arose from any of them. This is exactly how viññāṇa constructs a living world: the activity (kriyā) that ties still frames into a narrative has no source, yet our rāga and dosa arise entirely in response to it. Nate kāmāyānī citrāni — the pictures themselves are innocent.

- **Individual frames vs. story**: If you show separate still images (man, road, shop, tea cup), no story emerges. The story requires the appearance of activity — movement connecting the frames. Similarly, rūpa alone (isolated sense-data) cannot generate rāga or dosa. It is the saṅkappa (conceiving of activity/continuity) that generates desire and aversion.

- **Pañcupādānakkhandha are five activities, not five things**: The five aggregates were shown to reveal the error in understanding, not to be understood. Trying to understand them creates another instance of the same structure. They are kriyāvan (activities) — recognising, feeling, perceiving, constructing, knowing — but because kriyā is never accessible to viññāṇa, they appear as five things to be grasped and examined.

- **Manasikāra unseen = world constructed**: When a dog is recognised (aṳdunagænīma), that recognition is a manasikāra, not an encounter with a real dog. But because manasikāra is never seen as manasikāra, the recognition solidifies into "a living dog exists." Simultaneously, on the subject side, "seeing" (another manasikāra) solidifies into a diṭṭhi: "I can see." The dog validates seeing; seeing validates the dog. This mutual validation is tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso — the three-fold contact that can never be undone by examining either side separately.

- **The four diṭṭhi we are born with**: Diṭṭha (seen), suta (heard), muta (sensed), viññāta (cognised) — these four are not learned; they are the default operating system, installed at birth. No child needs to be taught how to see or hear. This is why diṭṭhi is so intractable: it is not a wrong belief acquired later but the foundational structure of all experience.

- **Bamboo-stick (baṭamiṭi) analogy for paccayā**: Two sticks leaning against each other stand upright. The leaning (hētthuva/paccayā) cannot be found in either stick. It is the unfindable middle that sustains both sides. We think the two sticks caused the leaning, but it is the leaning that created the appearance of two sticks standing. Seeing samudaya correctly means seeing that the middle (paccayā) was never established — and that seeing IS nirodha, right there. Nothing that existed is destroyed; nothing that existed was there to be destroyed.

- **Saññā not seen as saññā = sakkāya**: When "dog" is recognised, the recognition (saññā) is never seen as mere recognition. Colours, shapes, textures were never truly "recognised" — a child cannot identify colours without being given names, yet we believe we "know" colours. This non-recognition of recognition is exactly what makes the dog real and simultaneously creates the "I" who recognises it. Saññā pariññā (circumscribed seeing of saññā) would dissolve both sides.

- **Dream structure = waking structure — the knot**: In a dream: dog known, barking heard, biting felt — but the dreamer, the seeing, and the seen all arise within the same single event. There is no separate knower. Waking experience has exactly the same structure. The knot (gæṭaya) is that manasikāra is never seen as manasikāra, so two sides appear (subject and object) and mutually validate each other. Trying to undo the knot by examining either side fails because the knot created both sides.

- **Sensor-door analogy for always-on hearing**: A sensor door's sensor is always on. Person approaches: door opens. Person leaves: door closes. But the sensor itself never turned on or off — it was always on. Hearing is like the sensor: always on. We have never experienced non-hearing (jāti bīri = born deaf is the only true non-hearing). What we call "not hearing" is actually hearing-with-no-sound, which is still hearing. Because we don't know we are always in hearing, we fabricate: "Now I hear" (arising) and "Now I don't hear" (ceasing). This fabrication of arising and ceasing, projected onto the always-on base, is the genesis of birth and death (upata-maraṇa).

- **On/off never happened to the sensor**: If the sensor has never been off, "on" has no meaning either. If hearing has never ceased, "hearing arises" has no meaning. If off never happened, on cannot be known. This is why the always-on nature (nitya svabhāva) can never be understood by viññāṇa — viññāṇa requires duality (paccaya) to operate, and a nirapekkha (absolute/non-relative) state provides no paccaya.

- **Kiriīma and nokiriīma both inapplicable**: If one is already in hearing, one cannot "do" hearing (it's already done). But one cannot "not do" hearing either (not-hearing was never encountered). Both doing and not-doing are kriyā (activities); seeing them both as kriyā renders both meaningless. This is the train-ticket analogy: if the ticket is already fully booked, "booking" has no meaning and "not booking" has no meaning either. The kriyā-space collapses.

- **Ucchedānta and sassatānta from the same not-knowing**: Not knowing that one is always in hearing creates the ucchedānta (annihilationist extreme): "I wasn't hearing, now I am." From that same not-knowing, the sassatānta (eternalist extreme) is constructed: "I have the permanent capacity to hear." Both extremes rest on the same base of not-knowing. Seeing the base as it is — neither on nor off, neither arisen nor ceased — transcends both extremes without establishing a middle position.

## Summary

Discussion 06 opens with a sustained warning: whatever dhamma understanding one has accumulated is itself saṅkhāra, built from the same conceptual material as the delusion it describes. Holding dharma knowledge as "true" produces sakkāya just as holding worldly knowledge does — the dharma itself becomes māra. This sets the frame for the entire discussion: the problem is not what is understood but the act of understanding itself.

The central thesis is that activity (kriyākārīttva) — the process-character of experience — is never accessible to viññāṇa. Viññāṇa registers only nāma-rūpa: static name-and-form. The living quality of experience, what makes things appear to move, change, and matter, comes from an activity that has no location and no source. This thesis is developed through three progressively precise analogies.

The river analogy shows that "flowing" cannot be found in the water. Water in a glass does not flow; water in a river does. But the flowing is not a property of the water — it is an un-encounterable process that gives the river its apparent aliveness. We bind the flowing to the river (as we bind kriyā to the world) because we cannot see manasikāra as manasikāra.

The film projector analogy provides the most detailed analysis. A film negative holds only still images. The projector passes them through. Light carries them to a static screen. None of these four components — negative, projector, light, screen — contains or produces movement. Yet a coherent story appears: a man walks, sits, drinks tea. The story runs entirely on an activity that arose from none of its apparent components. Our rāga and dosa are responses not to the still images (which are innocent — nate kāmāyānī citrāni) but to the conceived activity (saṅkappa rāga). We like the peaceful character because of his peaceful actions; we dislike the violent character because of his violent actions. The actions never existed in any frame, yet they alone generate our emotional responses.

This leads to a restatement of pañcupādānakkhandha: the five aggregates are not five things but five activities (kriyāvan). They were taught to reveal the error in understanding, not to be understood. Because kriyā is invisible to viññāṇa, the five always appear as things to be grasped and examined — which is itself another instance of upādāna.

The discussion then turns to the knot structure. When a dog is recognised, the recognition (saññā) is a manasikāra that never discloses itself as manasikāra. Result: the dog becomes real. Simultaneously, "seeing" — another manasikāra — becomes real as a diṭṭhi. The dog validates seeing (I know the dog exists because I see it), and seeing validates the dog (I know seeing is real because there is a dog to see). This is tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso: three things (seer, seeing, seen) appearing from a single undisclosed manasikāra. Neither side can be falsified because the knot (paccayā) created both sides. The bamboo-stick analogy crystallises this: two sticks lean on each other; the leaning cannot be found in either stick; the unfindable leaning sustains both. We think the sticks caused the leaning, but the leaning created the appearance of two upright sticks. Seeing samudaya (the leaning/paccayā) correctly reveals that it was never established — and that seeing IS nirodha, without anything being destroyed.

The final section introduces the sensor-door analogy to show how arising and ceasing (and therefore birth and death) are fabricated from not-knowing. A sensor is always on. When a person approaches, the door opens; when the person leaves, it closes. But the sensor itself never turned on or off. Hearing is the same: always on. We have never experienced true non-hearing (only someone born deaf has no hearing — jāti bīri). What we call "not hearing" is actually hearing-with-no-sound, which is still a function of hearing. We use past sounds (āsrava/memory) to conclude "I'm not hearing now" — but this conclusion is drawn from within hearing itself. Because we don't know we are always in hearing, we fabricate: "now hearing arises" and "now hearing ceases." This on-off fabrication, projected onto an always-on base, is the genesis of birth and death.

The critical insight is that doing and not-doing both become inapplicable. If one is already in hearing, one cannot "do" hearing. But one cannot "not do" hearing either, because not-hearing was never encountered. Both doing and not-doing are kriyā; seeing them both as kriyā renders the entire action-space meaningless. The ucchedānta ("I wasn't hearing, now I am") and the sassatānta ("I have the permanent capacity to hear") both arise from the same not-knowing. Seeing the always-on base as it is — neither arisen nor ceased, providing no paccaya for viññāṇa to operate on — is what transcends both extremes. But this cannot be "understood" by viññāṇa because it is nirapekkha (non-relative), and viññāṇa requires duality.
