# විද්‍යා උදපාදි සාකච්ඡා - 07

## Sutta referred to

- **Tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso** — three-fold meeting constituting contact
- **Avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā** — from not-knowing, constructions arise
- **Manasikāra sambhavā sabbē dhammā** — all dhammas originate from attention
- **Sabbē saṅkhārā aniccāti yadā paññāya passati** — when one sees with wisdom that all constructions are impermanent
- **Viññāṇa paccayā nāmarūpaṃ, nāmarūpa paccayā viññāṇaṃ** — consciousness conditions name-and-form, name-and-form conditions consciousness (mutual conditionality)
- **Upādāna paccayā bhavo** — from clinging, becoming arises
- **Bhava nirodho nibbānaṃ** — cessation of becoming is nibbāna
- **Chapāṇaka Sutta** — six animals tied together; one dominant, others on standby

## Main dhamma points

- **Dream and waking use the same evidence for opposite conclusions**: When a dog chases you in a dream and you wake up, the experience of "dog chasing" serves as evidence both for "that was a dream" (it was unreal) and "this is waking" (I can now identify the unreal, therefore I am awake). The same saṅkhata material is used twice — once to validate the dream as real during it, once to validate waking as real after it. The "waking" moment is not a separate event from the dream-analysis; the act of thinking "that was a dream" IS what we call being awake.

- **Not-happening (novechcha bava) can never be known by viññāṇa**: Viññāṇa registers only what happened (vechcha bava). The not-happened nature of experience is structurally invisible to viññāṇa. When we say "the dog was unreal," we are taking something that registered as having-happened and labelling it "didn't happen" — but this is still an operation within having-happened. True not-happening is never accessed.

- **Ordinary suññatā vs ārya suññatā**: Ordinary emptiness is recognising that something which appeared to exist doesn't really exist — "the dog was a māyā." This is merely moving from "exists" to "doesn't exist," both within viññāṇa's register. Ārya suññatā cannot be accessed by viññāṇa at all because viññāṇa requires duality (paccaya) to operate, and the nirapekkha (non-relative) nature of true emptiness provides no paccaya. If one correctly sees the middle — not the "exists" extreme nor the "doesn't exist" extreme — neither can be constructed.

- **Pañcupādānakkhandha examination is a post-mortem on what was never encountered**: We take the already-formed dog (a saṅkhata never encountered as saṅkhata) and analyse its four great elements. We conclude "no dog, only elements." But we never address the root error: the recognition (haṃdunāgænīma) that made the dog appear in the first place. That recognition was a manasikāra never seen as manasikāra. Analysing the constructed dog's components cannot reveal the unconstructed nature because the analysis itself uses the same unconstructed material.

- **Awakening is not a separate event from dreaming**: We believe that first the dream is identified as dream, then we wake up — two stages. But the identification and the awakening are the same act. There is no waking-state independent of the dream-interpretation. The "awake" label is applied by taking the dream-saṅkhata and reclassifying it as "false" — the reclassification itself is what we call being awake. One manasikāra produces both the dream-world and the waking-judgement about it.

- **Cause and effect co-arise — the mirror analogy**: A mirror is just glass until a reflection appears. The moment a person stands before it, three things arise simultaneously: (1) the glass becomes "a mirror," (2) the reflection appears, (3) the "I who sees my reflection" is born. The cause (mirror) and the effect (reflection) do not precede one another — they arise through aññamañña paccaya (mutual conditionality). The person's arrival is like manasikāra: it produces both sides at once but is never seen as the producer. You cannot say the reflection is within you (it's in the mirror), nor that it's separate from you (without you, no reflection). Neither "within" nor "without" nor "both" nor "neither" applies — this is the nature of paccaya-samuppanna.

- **Tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso: all three arise in the same moment**: Eye, form, and seeing do not arise sequentially — eye existing before form, form before seeing. All three are co-arising in the present moment. If one sees this co-arising as co-arising, one does not fall into the bhava of "eye existed, then form came, then seeing happened." The bhava (sense of existence) that is consumed by this seeing is the only thing that changes.

- **Sensor-door analogy — bæri bava (impossibility)**: The sensor is always on. It recognises both the person's arrival (door opens) and the person's departure (door closes). We never encounter the sensor itself — we only see "person came, door opened." We attribute the door-opening to the person, not to the sensor. The sensor represents recognition (haṃdunāgænīma/saññā) — always on, never encountered as such. Because it has never been off, it can never be said to be "on." Because "on" cannot be said, doing and not-doing both become meaningless. This structural impossibility (bæri bava) is what we don't know, and not knowing it is what makes the entire world of "possible" (puluvanu bava) appear.

- **Screen analogy for the six sense-bases**: Five CCTV cameras feed one screen. When one camera is active, the other four show static/nothing — but the screen itself has not changed. No camera turning on changes the screen. No camera turning off changes the screen. If one sees the screen (the base), questions about which āyatana is on or off become ayoniso manasikāra. The screen is the nondiscriminative base; the six āyatana are six camera-feeds, not six separate events.

- **Hearing/not-hearing: nōæsīma never encountered, therefore æsīma never arose**: We've always been in hearing. Not knowing this, we fabricate: "earlier I wasn't hearing, now I am." The fabricated not-hearing (nōæsīma) is projected from not-knowing, and relative to that fabricated not-hearing, we claim "hearing has arisen." Both hearing and not-hearing are maññanā (conceivings) visible only when the base (nondiscriminative awareness) is seen. If asāmāñña sati (extraordinary mindfulness) touches the place where both hearing and not-hearing are both maññanā, neither "hearing exists" nor "hearing doesn't exist" can be established. This is puggala bhāvayē avasānaya — the end of personhood.

- **Sati practice is itself saṅkhāra**: Establishing mindfulness, practising meditation, maintaining sati — all are saṅkhāra arising from avijjā. Kusala saṅkhāra (wholesome constructions) are still saṅkhāra, still anicca, still leading to bhava. Seeing that the effort to achieve nibbāna is itself māyā, itself paccaya-samuppanna, itself hētu-phala-dhamma — this seeing is what ends upādāna. When upādāna ceases, bhava ceases; when bhava ceases, that is nibbāna. Until then, one is simply rotating within the wheel — sometimes through akusala, sometimes through kusala, but rotating.

- **Nāma-rūpa are not truly separable**: Separating nāma and rūpa is an āramabhaka (beginner) exercise. In paramārtha terms, nāma-rūpa are like two sides of one coin — aññamañña paccaya. Viññāṇa conditions nāmarūpa; nāmarūpa conditions viññāṇa. Rūpa is recognised only through nāma; nāma operates only based on rūpa. Both share the same suññatā, both are paṭiccasamuppanna.

## Summary

Discussion 07 opens by returning to the dream-waking analysis with a new precision. The same saṅkhata material — "a dog chased me" — serves two opposite functions: during the dream it validates the dream-world as real; after waking it validates the waking-world as real by providing the evidence that "a dream occurred." The teacher shows that there is no separate waking-event. What we call "waking up" is simply the act of reclassifying the dream-saṅkhata as false. This reclassification is itself a manasikāra that uses the same unencounterable material, producing a new label ("awake") that we treat as fundamentally different from the dream-label. But both labels are drawn from the same well of not-knowing.

This leads to a critical distinction between ordinary suññatā and ārya suññatā. When we examine the dream-dog and conclude "no dog existed," we experience a sense of emptiness — but this is merely moving from "exists" to "doesn't exist," both poles accessible to viññāṇa. Ārya suññatā cannot be accessed by viññāṇa because viññāṇa requires paccaya (conditionality/duality) to function, and the nirapekkha (non-relative) nature of genuine emptiness provides no paccaya. If one correctly sees the middle — the bæri bava (impossibility) — neither the "exists" extreme nor the "doesn't exist" extreme can be constructed. This is not a third position between two poles; it is seeing that neither pole was ever established.

The pañcupādānakkhandha examination is shown to be structurally flawed when performed as a post-mortem. We take the already-formed dog — a saṅkhata that was never encountered as saṅkhata at the moment of its formation — and analyse it into elements, concluding "the dog was empty." But we never address the root error: the recognition (haṃdunāgænīma) that made the dog appear. That recognition was a manasikāra never seen as manasikāra. The analysis takes the formed-thing and declares it unreal, but the forming-act remains unexamined because it was never accessible to viññāṇa.

The mirror analogy then illustrates how cause and effect co-arise rather than following sequentially. A mirror is glass until a reflection appears; the reflection's appearance simultaneously transforms the glass into "mirror" and creates "the I who sees." Neither cause nor effect precedes the other — they arise through aññamañña paccaya. You cannot locate the reflection within yourself (it's in the mirror) or outside yourself (without you, no reflection). The conventional analysis — "the mirror was there, then I came, then the reflection formed" — misses that all three emerged in the same instant. This is exactly tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso: eye, form, and seeing co-arising, not following each other.

The sensor-door analogy is then developed to its fullest extent. The sensor represents recognition — always on, never encountered. It detects both arrival and departure, both "hearing" and "not-hearing." Because it has never been off, saying it is "on" is meaningless. This creates bæri bava (impossibility): neither doing nor not-doing applies. We cannot "do" hearing (it's always been on) and we cannot "not do" hearing (we've never experienced not-hearing). This structural impossibility — where both kiriīma and nōkiriīma collapse — is what we don't know, and that not-knowing is what generates the entire architecture of the possible. Not knowing the impossibility, we fabricate: "earlier I wasn't hearing, now I am." The fabricated not-hearing creates the appearance of hearing arising. Both are maññanā drawn from the same not-knowing.

The screen analogy ties the sense-bases together. Five CCTV cameras feed one screen. When one camera is active, the screen doesn't change. When it goes inactive, the screen doesn't change. The screen is the base — always on, undifferentiated. If one sees the screen rather than the individual feeds, questions about which āyatana is on or off dissolve into ayoniso manasikāra. The Chapāṇaka Sutta's image of six animals tied together is invoked: one animal is dominant and the others are on "standby" — but the standby state and the active state are both functions of the base, not of the individual animals.

The discussion closes with two practical questions from listeners. To the question of how sati (mindfulness) should be established in daily life, the teacher responds that sati-practice is itself saṅkhāra, arising from avijjā. Kusala saṅkhāra (including meditation and mindfulness) are still saṅkhāra, still anicca, still generating bhava. Seeing that the effort to achieve nibbāna is itself māyā, itself conditioned, itself subject to hētu-phala — this seeing is what ends upādāna. When upādāna ceases, bhava ceases; bhava nirodho nibbānaṃ. To the question of nāma-rūpa separation, the teacher clarifies that separating them is a beginner exercise. Paramārtha-wise, they are aññamañña paccaya — viññāṇa conditions nāmarūpa and nāmarūpa conditions viññāṇa — inseparable as two sides of one coin, both paṭiccasamuppanna, both sharing the same suññatā.
