# විද්‍යා උදපාදි සාකච්ඡා - 08

## Sutta referred to

- Saññā pariññā vitareyya oghaṃ (circumscribing saññā to cross the flood)
- Nahidaṃ attakataṃ bimbaṃ nahidaṃ parakataṃ aghaṃ (neither self-made nor other-made)
- Avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā (dependent on ignorance, formations arise)
- Tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso (contact is the meeting of three)
- Pañcupādānakkhandha (five aggregates of clinging)
- Chapāṇaka Sutta reference (implicit, through the sensor-door framework)

## Main dhamma points

- Saññā (recognition/perception) is the pivotal aggregate to investigate. "Saññā pariññā vitareyya oghaṃ" — the flood is crossed by circumscribing saññā, not by abandoning it or replacing it with something superior.

- Colour as paradigm for saññā's emptiness: the word "red" produces no actual colour; a child without language cannot identify "red." Recognition is always empty of what it claims to recognise — the word and the quality never meet.

- The four mahābhūta (paṭhavī, āpo, tejo, vāyo) are four saññā, not four substances. Each is defined solely by the absence of the other three: "hardness" = not-āpo, not-tejo, not-vāyo. When one attempts to produce any of these four through cetanā, no actual quality (gati) arises. The supposed element is nothing but the act of exclusion.

- Mirage analogy reinterpreted: not "a mirage that looks like water but has none," but "a water-appearance where water can never arise." The act of recognition itself is the mirage, and the mirage is empty of mirage-ness. Saññā is the mirageness of the mirage.

- Bullseye (ilakkaya) analogy: the centre of a target exists only relative to the surrounding area (vaṭa parisaraya). Seen from itself (taththa taththa), no paccaya exists to call it "bullseye." The name and recognition are self-referential with no external grounding — an analogy for how every recognised object depends on a frame that is itself unrecognised.

- Anutpāda (non-arising): not "a thing existed but was empty" but "nothing ever arose in recognition itself." The object that recognition claims to have found was never produced.

- Nitya saññā: thinking "crow sound" produces no actual sound, but when actually hearing a crow, it is 100% convincing. Saññā is so permanently (nitya) embedded that the manasikāra generating it is invisible. This is why saññā cannot be falsified from within saññā.

- The unfalsifiable nature of saññā comes from its sajīvī bava (living quality). Because experience feels alive, recognition seems self-evidently true. No position within viññāṇa can step outside this to test it.

- TV/drama analogy extended: while absorbed in a drama, three things are lost (aprakaṭa) — the screen, the viewer, and the act of viewing. When one "comes to senses," all three appear simultaneously. But this very "coming to senses" is what we call sati (mindfulness) — and this sati is itself ayoniso manasikāra. The three that were supposedly "not found" are now taken as real, and that taking-as-real is the delusion named "mindfulness."

- Tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso — the meeting of three (eye + form + seeing, or nose + breath + feeling) was never actually encountered. No encounter happened, yet thinking makes it appear as though it did. What we call sati is the fabrication of a meeting that never took place.

- The hamba novecca (unfound) nature: throughout the day, neither "feeling the breath" nor "not feeling the breath" was present. Neither concept was operative. This absence is not "asihiya" (unmindfulness) — it is a state where the categories themselves had not arisen. Labelling it asihiya (unmindfulness) retroactively fabricates a past that never existed, creating an ucchedānta (annihilationist extreme) where "I existed but was unaware." The label closes the door on the actual not-knowing.

- Avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā demonstrated through breathing: when asked "do you feel your breath?", the three (nose + breath + feeling) co-arise instantly. This "feeling the breath" is taken as sati, but it is actually mano + dhamma (mind + mental object), not kāya + poṭṭhabba (body + tangible). Not knowing that mano + dhamma is what's happening, one takes it as kāya + poṭṭhabba — this is ayoniso manasikāra.

- Mano + dhamma itself arises from not knowing the unfound nature. The unfound nature (that kāya + poṭṭhabba never met) is not known as unfound, so thinking (manasikāra) is triggered. Not knowing the not-knowing = avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā.

- Nirāyāsa (effortlessness): true sati cannot be "established" (piṭiṭuvana) because establishing is itself saṅkhāra. Real sati is the wisdom that arises at the impossible-to-do (bæri) place — seeing that every act of "establishing sati" is itself asihiya. This seeing requires no effort (vehesa) and cannot be maintained through effort.

- Yoniso manasikāra is impossible within pañcupādānakkhandha. Since experience is entirely constructed from "impurity" (kuṇu), one can only recognise the impurity — never purify from within. Seeing ayoniso as ayoniso IS the only yoniso available; it is not a correction but a recognition.

- Even dhamma (the teaching itself) becomes māra: when āyatana are seen as fabricated and one grasps "these don't really arise," that very understanding is still mano + dhamma. The dhamma-concept is grasped as a real thing, and the cycle continues at a subtler level. This too can only be seen from the not-knowing of not-knowing.

## Summary

This discussion takes saññā (recognition/perception) as its central subject, guided by the principle "saññā pariññā vitareyya oghaṃ" — circumscribing saññā to cross the flood. Rather than treating saññā as one aggregate among five to be examined sequentially, the teacher presents it as the very mechanism through which all experience acquires its convincing, self-evident quality.

The investigation begins with colour. The word "red" produces no actual colour — one can think the word endlessly without any redness appearing. A child who has not learned the word cannot identify red among colours. Yet when one looks at a red object, the redness is utterly convincing, immediate, beyond doubt. This gap between the emptiness of the word and the fullness of the experience is precisely what saññā is: recognition that is always empty of what it claims to recognise, yet operates with total authority.

This principle is then applied to the four mahābhūta. Paṭhavī, āpo, tejo, and vāyo are not four elements but four saññā — four acts of recognition, each defined solely by the absence of the other three. "Hardness" has no positive content; it means only "not-fluidity, not-heat, not-motion." When one tries to produce any of these through thought (cetanā), no actual quality arises. The supposed element is nothing but an act of exclusion dressed as discovery.

Two analogies deepen this. The mirage analogy is reinterpreted: conventionally understood as "it looks like water but there is no water," the teacher reframes it as "a water-appearance where water can never arise." The mirage is not a container with missing content — the act of recognition itself is the mirage, and the mirage is empty of its own mirage-ness. Similarly, the bullseye analogy: the centre of a target exists only relative to its surrounding area. Seen from within itself, no condition (paccaya) exists to call it "centre." The name is groundless, the recognition self-referential. This is the structure of every recognised object: it depends on a frame that is itself unrecognised.

The discussion then addresses why this cannot be seen. Saññā is nitya — not "permanent" in the metaphysical sense, but so continuously operative that the manasikāra generating it is invisible. Thinking "crow sound" produces no actual sound, but hearing a crow is 100% convincing — so convincing that the act of recognition disappears into its own product. This is sajīvī bava, the "living quality" of experience that makes saññā unfalsifiable from within. No position inside viññāṇa can step outside to test whether recognition corresponds to anything.

The TV/drama analogy brings this into practical focus. While absorbed in a drama, three things are aprakaṭa (not manifest): the screen, the viewer, and the act of viewing. When one "comes to senses," all three appear simultaneously, and one declares: "Now I have sati — I know there is a screen, there is a viewer, and viewing is happening." But the teacher asks: was this "coming to senses" the arrival of something real, or the fabrication of something that was never actually lost? The three were "not found" (hamba novecca) during the drama, and "finding" them is itself a manasikāra that creates them. What we call sati is the birth of tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso — the "meeting of three" — not its discovery.

This analysis is then applied to ānāpānasati. Before someone asks "do you feel your breath?", neither "feeling" nor "not-feeling" the breath was operative. No breath-awareness, no breath-unawareness. Not even the concepts existed. This is not asihiya (unmindfulness) — it is a condition where the categories themselves had not arisen. But when the question is asked, instantly nose + breath + feeling co-arise, and one declares: "Yes, I feel my breath — now sati is established." The teacher shows that this labelling of the prior state as "asihiya" retroactively fabricates a past that never existed. It creates an ucchedānta — "I existed but was unaware" — closing the door on the actual not-knowing (avijjā) that was the condition.

What actually happened in that moment of "establishing sati"? Mano + dhamma (mind + mental object) arose. One experienced a mental event — not kāya + poṭṭhabba (body + tangible). But not knowing it as mano + dhamma, one takes it as kāya + poṭṭhabba: "my nose is here, the breath is touching it, I feel it." This is ayoniso manasikāra — taking a mental fabrication as a physical encounter. And mano + dhamma itself arose because the unfound nature (that kāya and poṭṭhabba never actually met) was not known as unfound. The not-knowing of not-knowing is avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā.

The teacher then addresses nirāyāsa (effortlessness). True sati cannot be "established" because establishing is itself saṅkhāra — a doing born of not-knowing. Real sati is the wisdom (nuvaṇa) that arises at the bæri (impossible-to-do) place: the recognition that every attempt to establish sati is itself asihiya. This recognition requires no effort, no maintaining, no duration. It is not a doing but a seeing-through of doing. When one sees that sati-as-doing always produces asihiya, the effort to maintain sati ceases — not through abandonment but through understanding its futility. This is nirāyāsa in its exact sense: not laziness, not passive acceptance, but the ending of the illusion that something can be done.

The discussion concludes with a crucial warning about dhamma itself becoming māra. When the āyatana are seen as fabricated — when one recognises that "eye and form don't really meet" — this recognition is still mano + dhamma. The teaching (dhamma) is grasped as a real finding, a true understanding, and this grasping perpetuates the very structure it claims to have seen through. The only exit is seeing the not-knowing of not-knowing: avijjā seeing itself as avijjā, which is not another act of knowing but the collapse of the entire framework within which knowing and not-knowing are distinguished.
