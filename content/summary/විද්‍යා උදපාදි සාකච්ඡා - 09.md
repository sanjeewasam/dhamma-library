# විද්‍යා උදපාදි සාකච්ඡා - 09

## Sutta referred to

- Avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā (dependent on ignorance, formations arise)
- Saṅkhāra paccayā viññāṇaṃ, viññāṇa paccayā nāmarūpaṃ (the paṭiccasamuppāda sequence)
- Imasmiṃ sati idaṃ hoti (this being, that comes to be)
- Paccuppanna yodhammaṃ tattha tattha vipassati (one who sees the present-arisen dhamma right there, right there)
- Saṅkappa rāgo purissassa kāmo, nate kāmā yāni citrāni tatte loke (intention-born desire is a person's sensuality, not the diverse things in the world)
- Nahidaṃ attakataṃ bimbaṃ nahidaṃ parakataṃ aghaṃ (neither self-made nor other-made)
- Sakkāya diṭṭhi, vicikicchā, sīlabbata parāmāsa (the three fetters)
- Aṅgulimāla Sutta (the Buddha's "I have stopped" while walking)
- Chapāṇaka Sutta (implicit, through the sensor-door framework)

## Main dhamma points

- Samudaya and nirodha are not separate domains. The world is constructed from not-knowing (nodannākamin sakas vecca lōkayak). Seeing this construction as construction IS nirodha. There is no nirodha apart from, or beyond, the world. A Buddha can only show how the samudaya-world works — not transport anyone to a separate "nirodha-place."

- Delusion (mūlāva) cannot be removed as an object. Saying "this is delusion" immediately generates "a non-deluded state also exists" — two extremes fabricated from one act of understanding. The one who understands delusion is also born from delusion. Delusion must be seen as delusion from within delusion (mūlāva mūlāven dakinavā), not from an imagined position outside it.

- Understanding (tērum gænīma) as a trap: when one grasps "this is nāma-rūpa, not a tree," the understanding becomes nitya saññā — permanently embedded, invisible. The "tree" thought stops arising, and one takes this as pariññā. But when the actual tree is cut down, instantly "where's the tree that was here?" arises — proving the saññā was yaṭapat (suppressed/submerged), not circumscribed. Like a ball pushed underwater, it resurfaces when conditions change. This is anusaya, not liberation.

- The matchbox-matchstick analogy for paṭiccasamuppāda: matchbox and matchstick are hetu (causes), fire is phala (result), and striking (ulanava) is phassa (contact). Contact cannot be found in either cause, nor in the result. It is not a "thing" (deyak nemey) — therefore viññāṇa cannot apprehend it. Causes and result co-arise (pera pasu novī); there are no causes "before" the result, just as there is no "mother and father" without a child. The relationship between cause and effect is established by contact, yet contact itself is unfindable — this structural invisibility is what generates the knowable world.

- Samudaya can only be seen at the point of phassa, not by examining what has already arisen. Examining arisen phenomena (vecca dē arageṇa bælīma) is post-mortem — like investigating after the horse has bolted. One always finds "this is false" or "this is constructed," but never sees the constructing. The constructing (samudaya vena tæna) is always already past by the time viññāṇa examines it.

- The hearing analysis deepened: when no sound is present, can one verify that "hearing" exists as a capability? No — thinking "crow sound" produces no hearing. Hearing cannot be known through thought (hitakaṭa gōcara vena ekak nemey). Yet when a crow calls, hearing is utterly convincing. The gap: thinking cannot produce hearing, yet hearing is self-evident when sound is present. This is because hearing IS the diṭṭhi (view) — one is embedded in it, and from within that diṭṭhi, both ear and sound appear as real. Diṭṭhi produces sakkāya: "I who hears" is born from the diṭṭhi of hearing itself, not from ear or sound.

- Hearing and not-hearing are structurally identical to the deaf person's condition: the deaf person cannot know hearing or not-hearing; neither can we, when examined at the base. The difference we assume ("we hear, the deaf person doesn't") is itself constructed from the diṭṭhi of hearing. Our "base state" and the deaf person's state are the same with respect to ēsīma (hearing): both are unknowable.

- The journey example for nitya saññā and upekkhā: driving home, some stretches have no memory — neither "I saw something" nor "I didn't see something" was operative. One cannot say seeing existed (no seen-thing to report) nor that not-seeing occurred (one didn't crash). Both pēnīma (seeing) and nopēnīma (not-seeing) are inapplicable. This is upekkhā — the base state of mūla mōha (fundamental delusion). Sati and asati both fail to describe it. Yet the journey was completed. The kriyā (action) of travelling was never found, yet one arrived.

- Nitya saññā demonstrated practically: chopping vegetables while thinking about something else — the entire task is completed without any sati about chopping. This isn't wisdom — it's mōha operating through nitya saññā. But it proves that action doesn't require the conscious engagement we assume. The action of chopping is never "found" during the doing.

- Paccuppanna (the present moment) cannot be accessed retrospectively. Comparing a remembered moment of upekkhā to one's current understanding is not paccuppanna dhamma — it is mano + dhamma examining a reconstruction. The wisdom that sees the present must arise tattha tattha (right there, right there), not through later analysis.

- Saṅkappa rāgo purissassa kāmo: desire is in the saṅkappa (intention/conceptualization), not in the things. The "knot" (gæṭaya) is the kriyā — eating/not-eating, seeing/not-seeing. Without this knot, things can be used (pariharaṇa) without rāga or dosa. A child puts a lizard in its mouth because no kriyā-knot of "edible/inedible" has been tied. The things themselves have no rāga or dosa.

- The Aṅgulimāla parable reinterpreted: the Buddha said "I have stopped" while walking. Nuvaṇa (wisdom) IS the stopped place. Walking continues as pariharaṇa, but the grasping that constructs "walking" — foot and ground knotted together — doesn't arise. Going (yæma) becomes meaningless (artha śūnya) when maññanā is seen as maññanā. The world sees movement; wisdom is the non-arising of the story of movement.

- Vicikicchā (doubt) cannot be removed by answering questions. The questions ("how does one act without kriyā?", "is there still pavættma?") persist until nuvaṇa arises. They are not questions that have answers — they are symptoms of the absence of nuvaṇa. When nuvaṇa arises, the questions dissolve, not because they are answered but because the framework generating them collapses.

## Summary

This discussion opens with a foundational clarification: samudaya and nirodha are not two separate domains. The world is a construction born of not-knowing (nodannākamin sakas vecca lōkayak), and seeing it as such — from within the construction — is itself nirodha. There is no nirodha beyond the world, no place to reach by escaping saṃsāra. Even a Buddha can only show how the samudaya-world works; he cannot transport anyone to a nirodha-realm that exists independently.

The teacher immediately identifies a trap in this understanding. When one grasps "this is delusion," a counter-position automatically arises: "then non-delusion also exists." The grasper is born alongside the grasped. This cannot be stopped — it is inherent in the structure of understanding itself. The way forward is not to remove delusion but to see delusion as delusion from within delusion (mūlāva mūlāven dakinavā). Any attempt to step outside generates another inside.

This is illustrated through the phenomenon of yaṭapat vīma (suppression) versus pariññā (circumscribing). When one contemplates a tree as nāma-rūpa long enough, the understanding "this is not really a tree" becomes nitya saññā — so permanently embedded that the tree-thought no longer arises. One may take this as liberation. But when the actual tree is cut down, instantly "where's the tree that was here?" surfaces — the saññā was submerged like a ball underwater, not dissolved. This is anusaya, latent tendency, and it reveals that understanding-as-suppression, however deep, is not seeing.

The central teaching vehicle of this discussion is the matchbox-matchstick analogy for paṭiccasamuppāda. Matchbox and matchstick are hetu (causes), fire is phala (result), and striking is phassa (contact). The teacher works through this systematically. Contact cannot be found in the matchbox, cannot be found in the matchstick, and is not the fire. If asked "what is striking?", answering "the matchstick" or "the matchbox" is wrong — those are not the striking. Yet without them, "striking" cannot even be spoken of. And striking cannot exist as a separate thing in empty space either. Contact is not findable within the causes, not findable apart from the causes, and not findable in the result — yet the entire relationship of cause and effect depends on it.

This structural unfindability is the key point. Contact is not a thing (deyak nemey), therefore viññāṇa — which only apprehends things — cannot know it. Furthermore, causes and result co-arise: there are no causes "before" the result, just as "mother and father" has no meaning without a child. The fire's arising is the moment when matchbox and matchstick become "causes." Before that, they are just objects on a table. This means examining causes after the fact (vecca dē arageṇa bælīma) is always post-mortem — one finds "this is constructed" but never catches the constructing. Samudaya can only be seen at the point of phassa, which is the point viññāṇa cannot reach.

The teacher then applies this framework to hearing. When no sound is present, one cannot verify that "hearing" exists as a capability. Thinking "crow sound" produces no hearing — no gati (quality) of hearing arises from thought. Yet when a crow actually calls, hearing is utterly self-evident, requiring no proof. The teacher poses this as a gæṭaya (knot) for the listeners to work through: why does thinking not produce hearing, while "actual" sound does? The answer reveals that hearing is itself the diṭṭhi (view). One is always already embedded in hearing-as-diṭṭhi, and from within it, both ear and sound appear as real things. The "I who hears" is not separate from hearing — it is born from the diṭṭhi of hearing. This is sakkāya diṭṭhi arising from the āyatana, not from reasoning about them.

The analysis extends to seeing through the journey example. Driving home, certain stretches have no memory at all. One cannot say "I saw something" (no seen-thing to report) nor "I didn't see" (one navigated safely). Neither pēnīma (seeing) nor nopēnīma (not-seeing) applies. This state — where both categories fail — is not asihiya (unmindfulness) either, because one arrived safely, which would be impossible in actual unmindfulness. It is upekkhā, the base condition of mūla mōha, invisible to all categories of sati and asati. The teacher calls it the nitya saññā that is so permanent it cannot even register as permanent — like water to a fish.

This leads to a practical demonstration. One chops vegetables while completely absorbed in a different thought — the chopping is completed without any sati about chopping. This proves that the kriyā (action) is never "found" during the doing, yet the doing happens. The teacher clarifies: this is not wisdom, it is mōha operating through nitya saññā. But it demonstrates the crucial point that action does not require the conscious engagement we assume. The architecture of experience handles pariharaṇa (use) through nitya saññā, not through deliberate sati.

The discussion addresses what changes when nuvaṇa arises at this unfindable base. The teacher draws on the Aṅgulimāla parable: the Buddha said "I have stopped" while walking. Walking continues — feet move, ground is traversed — but the grasping that constructs "walking" (foot and ground knotted together as cause and effect) doesn't arise. Maññanā is seen as maññanā, so "going" becomes artha śūnya (meaningless) — not absent, but without the story that normally accompanies it. This is pariharaṇa without saṅkhāra: use without construction.

The teacher also addresses the saṅkappa rāgo sutta directly. Desire is not in things but in the saṅkappa — the conceptual knot that ties kriyā (eating/not-eating, seeing/not-seeing) to objects. A child puts a lizard in its mouth because no kriyā-knot of "edible/inedible" has been tied. The lizard itself has no piḷikula (disgust) or priya (attraction). When the knot is not tied, things can be used without rāga or dosa.

A listener raises the question that naturally follows: if pēnīma and nopēnīma are both unknowable at the base, and kriyā is never found, how does one live? The teacher responds that this question (vicikicchā) cannot be answered — it persists until nuvaṇa arises, at which point it dissolves not because it receives an answer but because the framework generating it collapses. The nuvaṇa that arises at the bæri (impossible) place — where pēnīma and nopēnīma are both inapplicable — is itself the paccuppanna seeing. It cannot be achieved retrospectively by analyzing remembered states.

The discussion closes with the three fetters: sakkāya diṭṭhi, vicikicchā, and sīlabbata parāmāsa are removed together when nuvaṇa arises at the point of phassa. The doubt (vicikicchā) is not a deficiency to be corrected but a structural feature of viññāṇa trying to understand what viññāṇa cannot reach. It ceases when the unreachable is seen as unreachable — not as a thought about unreachability, but as the nuvaṇa that IS the unreachable place.
