# විශේෂ සාකච්ඡා - මූල අවිද්‍යාව [2023 12 14] පානදුර

## Sutta referred to

- Diṭṭha, suta, muta, viññāta (the four bases of experience — seen, heard, sensed, cognized)
- Paṭiccasamuppāda (dependent co-arising — implicit throughout, especially hetu-phala and phassa analysis)
- Anicca as encountered through the non-applicability of both extremes
- Sammā diṭṭhi (right view — seeing that nothing can be grasped from any angle)
- "Dhamma too becomes māra" — the raft analogy (implicit: kullūpama, the simile of the raft)
- Paccuppanna dhamma (the present-arisen)
- Imasmiṃ sati idaṃ hoti (this being, that comes to be — implicit through hetu-phala analysis)

## Main dhamma points

- "Yamak yamek" (something/someone) is itself mūla avijjā (root ignorance). The four — diṭṭha, suta, muta, viññāta — held together constitute puggala bhāva (personhood). Diṭṭhi (view) is the holding (daragænīma) of these four. "Something exists, someone exists" arises only with these four — in sleep, neither something nor someone is encountered.

- The white-paper analogy for pēnīma (seeing): drawings are made on a white sheet — cars, buses, people — but the white is never affected. Drawing something doesn't create the white, erasing doesn't restore it. The white was never "there" as a findable thing, yet all drawings depend on it. Similarly, pēnīma (seeing) is like the white base — we only ever encounter what is "drawn" (pēna dē — things seen), never pēnīma itself. It has no on/off, no arising or ceasing.

- The CCTV screen analogy extends the same point: the screen is always "on" — images appearing on it don't turn it on, images disappearing don't turn it off. Nothing that happens in the images affects the "on" state. Yet we only attend to the images, never to the "on" state. This "on" state (pēnīma) is the base we can never find, yet without which nothing could appear.

- Anicca properly seen versus anicca improperly applied: taking a cup and declaring "the cup is impermanent" (hambavecca eka aniccayi) is not seeing anicca — it is examining what has already been encountered and labelling it. True anicca is encountered through the non-applicability (noyedīma) of both pēnīma and nopēnīma. When both seeing and not-seeing are seen as non-applicable, encounter itself is encountered as anicca — not the thing "being" impermanent, but the encounter-ness (hambavīma) itself revealing anicca. This is darśanaya (vision).

- The cup demonstration: the cup is placed — "I see the cup." The cup is removed — "the cup is gone." But pēnīma never started when the cup appeared and never stopped when it disappeared. No beginning or end of pēnīma can be found. When both pēnīma and nopēnīma are seen as having no point of application, the two extremes (anta deka) are surpassed. This is what "seeing anicca" actually means — it transcends both arising and ceasing.

- Dænīma (feeling/touch) analysed through holding a cup: while holding the cup during conversation, no feeling-awareness arises — one is absorbed in thought. The moment one directs attention to the cup, "I feel it" appears. But dænīma was the base all along — the cup and hand were fabricated on top of it, like drawings on white paper. The base (dænīma) is never found as having turned on or off. It has no ōn vecca vēlāvak (time of turning on) and no ōf vecca vēlāvak (time of turning off).

- Hetu-phala (cause-result) analysis applied to dænīma: cup and hand are assigned as causes of feeling. But if they were actual causes, feeling should persist whenever both are present — it doesn't (one forgets the cup while talking). Yet without them, feeling doesn't arise from elsewhere. Feeling cannot be said to come from these causes, nor from somewhere else, nor from both, nor from neither. The connection (sambandha) cannot be established, and disconnection (asambandha) cannot be established either. The gæṭaya (knot) cannot be tied — this is the structural unfindability at the heart of experience.

- Sammā diṭṭhi defined: when it is seen from every angle that nothing can be grasped (ganna bæri) — not "this is true" nor "this is false," not "this exists" nor "this doesn't exist" — this IS sammā diṭṭhi. Even dhamma, if held as a karuṇa (fact/point), becomes diṭṭhi (view). Even the insight "nothing can be grasped" becomes another grasped position if taken as a fact. The dhamma must release even itself — like the raft that must be abandoned after crossing. Every dhamma point (haṭagatté nǣ, ipadilā nǣ, pera notibīma) is itself a "middle" (mæda) fabricated between two extremes. Seeing this is the siyum bava (subtlety) and dakṣa bava (skill) required.

- Paccuppanna (the present moment) is always constructed from what is absent: saying "Panadura is bad" while in Panadura is constructed from the absent "Mīṭiya goḍa is good." The "good" is not present — it is hambanovecca (not-encountered). From this not-encountered "good," the present "bad" is built. Both sides are constructed from what has not been encountered. Nothing (mokavat) has actually been encountered when one examines this carefully.

- The white-paper base is commonly mistaken for nibbāna — but it is upekkhā, the tērenné næti maṭṭama (level of not-understanding). It is mūla mōha (root delusion). It has never been found (hambavela nǣ koyi vēlāvakvat). Taking it as a findable "state" or as the "truth" behind appearances is itself diṭṭhi.

- Maññanā (conceiving) dissolves when the hambanovena bava (unfindability) is seen again and again. Not by working on things (dēval valata kochchara monavā karat vædak nǣ) — but by seeing pēnīma as pēnīma (the "on" state as just the "on" state, without making it into a thing). The conceiving-tendency (maññanā krana gatiya) gradually wears away (gevenaвā) as the unfindability is seen repeatedly.

## Summary

This special discussion, held in Panadura on 14 December 2023, takes mūla avijjā (root ignorance) as its central theme and works through it with sustained analytical precision, using the white-paper analogy, the CCTV screen metaphor, and a live cup-demonstration to show how pēnīma (seeing), dænīma (feeling), and the entire structure of hetu-phala operate at a level that is structurally unfindable yet functionally operative.

The discussion opens by identifying yamak yamek (something/someone) as itself mūla avijjā. The four modes of encounter — diṭṭha, suta, muta, viññāta (seen, heard, sensed, cognized) — held together constitute puggala bhāva (personhood). The holding (daragænīma) of these four is diṭṭhi. The teacher points out that in deep sleep, neither "something" nor "someone" arises — the entire world of things and persons depends on these four modes being operative, and when they are not, neither yamak nor yamek exists.

The white-paper analogy is introduced to illuminate the invisible base underlying all experience. On a white sheet, one draws a car, then erases it and draws a bus, then a person. Through all these changes, the white is never affected. Drawing doesn't create the white, erasing doesn't restore it. The teacher asks: has the white ever been encountered as a thing? The answer is no — we have always only seen the drawings. The white was never found as a findable presence, yet without it, no drawing could appear. This is pēnīma — the "seeing" that is always "on" but never encountered. Like the CCTV screen that remains on regardless of what images appear or disappear on it, pēnīma is not turned on by the appearance of a cup and not turned off by its removal. It has no discoverable beginning (paṭan gattu vēlāvak) or end.

The teacher then draws out the critical distinction between two relationships to anicca. What everyone does is take something already encountered — a cup, a person, a sensation — and declare it impermanent (hambavecca eka aniccayi kiyalā balanaвā). This is examining what has already arisen and labelling it. It never reaches anicca because it operates within the framework of pēnīma that has already been taken for granted. The teacher demonstrates the alternative: when both pēnīma and nopēnīma are seen as non-applicable (noyedīma), the encounter itself (hambavīma) is encountered as anicca. Not the thing being impermanent, but the encounter-ness — the very fact of "something being found" — is revealed as anicca. This is darśanaya (vision), and it is what "seeing anicca" actually means. It transcends both anta (extremes) — arising and ceasing — because in pēnīma, no beginning and no end can be located.

The cup demonstration makes this concrete. The cup is placed before the listener: "the cup is here." The cup is removed: "the cup is gone." The teacher asks: did pēnīma start when the cup appeared? No — the listener was already looking. Did pēnīma stop when the cup was removed? No — the listener is still looking. No point of arising and no point of ceasing can be found for pēnīma. When this is seen, the categories "exists" and "doesn't exist" both lose their footing, because both depend on pēnīma having a locatable beginning or end.

The analysis extends to dænīma (feeling/touch). The listener holds a cup throughout the conversation but has no awareness of holding it until attention is directed there. The moment the teacher says "you're holding a cup," dænīma "appears" — but it didn't arrive from the cup or the hand. The teacher demonstrates: if cup and hand were the actual causes (hetu) of dænīma, feeling should persist as long as both are present. It doesn't — one forgets the cup entirely while talking. Yet without cup and hand, dænīma doesn't arise from elsewhere. So dænīma is neither from the hetu (cup+hand) nor from tora (apart from them). Neither sambandha (connected) nor asambandha (not-connected) can be established. The gæṭaya (knot) cannot be tied. This is the same structural unfindability shown through the matchbox analogy in earlier discussions, now applied to the touch-door.

The teacher then brings the discussion to sammā diṭṭhi. When one sees from every angle that nothing can be grasped — this IS sammā diṭṭhi. But even this insight, if held as a karuṇa (fact), becomes diṭṭhi. Even "nothing can be grasped" becomes a grasped position. The teacher traces how this happens with every dhamma point: "haṭagatté nǣ" (nothing arose), "pera notibīma haṭagannava" (what was not before, arises) — each of these, intended as pointers to the middle way, is immediately fabricated into a mæda (middle) between two extremes. The teacher identifies this as the fundamental pattern: every "middle" generates its own two sides. The dhamma itself, if held, becomes māra. Even ganna bærikama (impossibility-of-grasping), if taken as "the truth," is another grasped object. The teacher insists this process continues to the very end — there is no stable landing.

The discussion on paccuppanna (present moment) reveals how good/bad, pleasant/unpleasant are constructed entirely from what is absent. The teacher uses the example of thinking "Mīṭiya goḍa is better" while in Panadura. Mīṭiya goḍa is not present — it is hambanovecca (not-encountered). From this absent "good," the present "bad" of Panadura is constructed. Both poles are fabricated from what has not been encountered. When this is examined carefully, nothing (mokavat) has actually been found at this moment — the entire evaluative structure is built on absences.

The teacher warns against a common error: taking the white-paper base (the "on" state of pēnīma) as nibbāna. It is not — it is upekkhā, mūla mōha (root delusion), the level that is not understood (tērenné næti maṭṭama). It has never been found as a findable state, and taking it as "the truth behind appearances" is itself diṭṭhi. The dissolution of maññanā (conceiving) happens not by working on things but by seeing pēnīma as pēnīma — the conceiving-tendency gradually wears away as the unfindability is seen repeatedly, not through force or suppression but through the sustained seeing that has no foothold.
