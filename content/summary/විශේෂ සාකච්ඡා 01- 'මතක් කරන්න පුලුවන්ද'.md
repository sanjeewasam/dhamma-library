# විශේෂ සාකච්ඡා 01 — මතක් කරන්න පුලුවන්ද?

## Sutta referred to

- Avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā (dependent on not-knowing, formations arise)
- Manasikāra sambhavō sabbē dhammā (all dhammas arise from attention)
- Manō pubbaṅgamā dhammā (mind is the forerunner of all dhammas)
- Paṭiccasamuppāda (the dependent co-arising sequence — implicit throughout)
- Anutpāda / ahutthā (the non-arising, the never-happened)
- Trividyā (the three knowledges — seeing the three planes of existence simultaneously)

## Main dhamma points

- Memory (matakaya) is not something one does — it happens (kerena ekak, not karana ekak). If one could actively remember, one would do it before the memory arises. But memory arises when conditions converge, not by volition. Trying to "make" a memory is a structural impossibility.

- Memory and forgetting co-arise in the same moment. When "I remembered" (matak vunā) arises, "it was forgotten" (amatak vela tibunā) arises simultaneously — manasikāra sambhavō sabbē dhammā. We split what is a single event into two and assign temporal sequence: forgetting to the past, remembering to the present. But both are available (dēpættama pēnavā) only in that single moment of manasikāra.

- Forgetting (amatak vīma) is never found (hamba venna nē). It is ucchēda — the annihilation extreme. Something that was never encountered cannot be "found now." What we call remembering is: taking something never found (the forgotten thing) and declaring it present. The nāt bhavin (from non-existence) is used to construct the "now found."

- What we call "remembering" is actually phassa (contact/sparśaya). It is manasikāra, not a volitional act. Not knowing it as phassa, we label it "I remembered." The label instantly generates its counter: "it was forgotten before." This is avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā operating in real time.

- Ahutthā means not "previously not-existing" but "such a thing could never happen" — it is asambhāvya (impossible). Remembering is impossible because forgetting is never found as a findable thing. At this recognition, kriyā (the action of remembering) is surpassed. Phassa nirōdhaya — contact ceases because manasikāra cannot engage with what was never found. This is the bæri tæna (the impossible-place) where insight arises.

- When the impossibility of kriyā is seen, thinking itself cannot proceed as before. It is not that thinking stops — but the muḷāva (delusion) of "I am doing this" within thinking becomes visible. The avabōdhaya (realization) resides at the nohitana svabhāvayak (non-thinkable nature) — it cannot be reached by manasikāra.

- Seeing (dækīma) analysed through a child who has never encountered "chair" via any of the five sense-doors. Chair-ness never arrived through eye, ear, nose, tongue, or body. Yet others teach the child "this is a chair" — tying a gaṭaya (knot) of nimitta (signs): shape, hardness, colour, labelled "chair." Those who teach also never saw chair-ness — they received the same knot from others. The knot is passed from generation to generation. When the grown-up child "sees a chair," what is seen is the knot, not the thing.

- Saññā (recognition) not known as saññā generates the entire perceptual world. Sound: the æhena gatiya (hearing-quality) has never been captured by cetanā (intention/will). In "sound" there is no hearing. Not knowing saññā as saññā, one fabricates: "a crow's sound" → then "a crow" → then "my ear" → then "my body" → then "outside world with crow, inside body with ear." Upādānakkhandha is built entirely from not-knowing (nodanna kamin).

- The perceptual knot (gaṭaya) extends across all five senses. Once "crow sound" is fabricated through hearing, it becomes available to all five āyatana — one can go look at the crow (seeing), touch it (body), smell it, taste it. This cross-modal availability is what builds the ghana saññā (solid perception) of a "crow existing out there."

- If saññā is seen as saññā at the base, the five-door engagement doesn't arise. Not because the crow disappears, but because the yāntrṇaya (mechanism) of how it was built is seen. There is then nothing to nirodha (cease) because no samudaya (arising) was found. Nuppāda (non-arising) is seen — and from that seeing, neither existence nor non-existence can be maintained.

- Trividyā (three knowledges) arises when the entire mechanism is seen simultaneously: how ghana saññā (solid/sense-sphere perception), rūpa saññā (form-sphere perception), and arūpa saññā (formless-sphere perception) are all constructed from avijjā. All three are seen in a single moment — not step by step but as a whole. This is what "penetrating the three worlds in a single citta" means.

## Summary

This special discussion takes a single, deceptively simple question — "Can you remember?" (matak karanna puluvanda?) — and uses it to expose the entire mechanism of avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā, from the most immediate act of recollection to the construction of upādānakkhandha and the three planes of existence.

The teacher begins with memory. If remembering were something one does, one would have to do it before the memory arises — which is impossible, since one doesn't know what to remember until it appears. Memory happens (kerena ekak) — it is not made (karana ekak). This is demonstrated: the listener is asked to say something they forgot. Whatever they say is already something they've remembered — they cannot produce the forgotten thing as forgotten. The act of "remembering" and the state of "having been forgotten" arise simultaneously, in the same moment of manasikāra. They are not two events separated in time.

The teacher draws this out carefully. We habitually split this single event into a temporal sequence: "it was forgotten (past) → now it's remembered (present)." But both are available only within the current manasikāra — neither the forgetting nor the remembering existed independently before this moment. The forgetting is constructed retroactively by the remembering, and the remembering depends on the forgetting for its meaning. They are ekinekata pratya vemin (mutually dependent), arising in the same moment.

This leads to the critical insight about amatak vīma (forgetting). Forgetting is never found (hamba venna nē). It is the ucchēda extreme — annihilation — that is never actually encountered. One cannot point to a moment of forgetting; one can only, from within the moment of remembering, construct the story "it was forgotten before." Since the forgotten thing was never found as forgotten, how can it be found now? What we call "remembering" is: taking something that was never encountered (the "forgotten thing") and declaring it present. The "nāt bava" (non-existence) is used to build the "present finding."

The teacher identifies what is actually happening: this is phassa (contact), not a volitional act. It is manasikāra — attention-arising. Not knowing it as phassa, we label it "I remembered." The label instantly generates its opposite: "it was forgotten before this." This is avijjā paccayā saṅkhārā operating in real time, visible in ordinary experience.

The discussion then reaches ahutthā — which the teacher clarifies does not mean "previously not-existing" but rather "such a thing could never happen" — it is asambhāvya (impossible). Remembering is impossible because forgetting is never found. At this point, the kriyā (action) of "remembering" is surpassed — one cannot engage with it as an action. Phassa nirōdhaya: contact ceases, not through suppression but because manasikāra cannot find what it needs to engage. The avabōdhaya (realization) arises at this bæri tæna (impossible-place) — the place where thinking cannot reach.

The teacher then extends the same analysis to seeing (dækīma). A child has never encountered "chair" through any of the five sense-doors. No colour, shape, sound, smell, taste, or touch has ever delivered "chair-ness." Yet the child is taught: "when something has this shape, this hardness, this size — that is a chair." The teacher points out that this teaching is itself a gaṭaya (knot) — a tying together of nimitta (signs) under a label. The teacher who taught the child also never saw chair-ness — they received the same knot from their own teachers. The entire chain is knot-upon-knot, with no one ever having encountered the thing itself.

The grown-up child looks at an object and says "there's a chair." The teacher asks: is the child seeing something that's there, or thinking (hitanam hitanavā) — "conceiving by conceiving"? The answer is the latter. The five sense-doors never delivered chair-ness. What was delivered were nimitta — signs. The word "chair" knots these signs together, and from that moment, the knotted-together thing is "seen" as chair. The teacher demonstrates: in a dream, dogs appear, chase you, bite — and you feel the bite. "Seeing" in the dream is hitāgatta ekak (something conceived). Waking "seeing" operates identically — but because we don't recognize saññā as saññā, we take waking seeing as real seeing.

The analysis is then applied to hearing. The æhena gatiya (hearing-quality) has never been captured by cetanā. In "sound" there is no hearing — sound is itself a saññā, a recognition, not a raw sense-datum. Not knowing this, one fabricates: "a crow's sound" → "a crow" → "my ear" → "my body." Each step adds another layer of upādānakkhandha, built from not-knowing (nodanna kamin). The teacher shows: once "crow's sound" is fabricated, all five āyatana become available — one can go look, touch, smell, taste the crow. This cross-modal availability is what builds ghana saññā (solid perception). But if saññā is seen as saññā at the base, this five-door engagement doesn't arise — not because the crow disappears, but because the yāntrṇaya (mechanism) is seen.

The teacher connects this to the broader framework. When the mechanism is seen, there is nothing to nirodha (cease) because no samudaya (arising) was found. Nuppāda (non-arising) is the seeing — and from that seeing, neither "exists" nor "doesn't exist" can be maintained. The teacher warns against the common approach of taking something as "existing" and then trying to make it "not exist" (nirodha it) — labelling things as "illusion" or "māyā" or "boruvak" without seeing how they were constructed. This is not pariññā — it is another gaṭaya, another label applied from within the same structure.

The discussion closes with trividyā — the three knowledges. When the entire process is seen simultaneously — how ghana saññā (sense-sphere/solid perception), rūpa saññā (form-sphere perception), and arūpa saññā (formless-sphere perception) are all constructed from avijjā — all three planes are penetrated in a single thought-moment. This is not a step-by-step journey through jhāna levels but a seeing of the samasta kriyāvaliya (entire process) at once. The teacher describes: nuppāda dhammak — a never-arisen dhamma — begins to be seen. At that point, attachment to the siddhi (attainment) itself becomes impossible because there is nothing to attach to.
