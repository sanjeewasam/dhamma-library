# විශේෂ සාකච්ඡා 02 — "එකතු වීම" (Coming Together / Addition)

## Sutta referred to

- **Majjhē Sutta** — referenced toward the end in the context of not finding the middle from the two sides
- **Tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phassō** — the coming together of three (eye, form, viññāṇa) is phassa; cited as the canonical formulation of the discussion's central point
- **Manañca paṭicca dhammē ca** — mind and dhammā as condition; cited regarding manasikāra generating dhamma-constructs

## Main dhamma points

- **Ekatu vīma (coming together) as the hidden middle**: Phassa is not one of the two sides (anta deka) it generates — it is the unseen middle. We always encounter the two sides (dæníma/nodæníma, pēnīma/nopēnīma, tæbīma/notæbīma) but never the phassa that generated them.

- **The error is in phassa, not in the two sides**: The two sides themselves are not the problem. The problem is not seeing phassa — the contact-event that constructed them.

- **Tea-making analogy**: Tea-leaves, sugar, and hot water are hetu (causes); tea is phala (result); but kavalama (mixing/stirring) is the paccaya that can never be found in either the causes or the result. Phassa functions like kavalama.

- **2+3=5 analogy (extended through the discussion)**: The value of 5 comes not from 2 or 3 but from ekatu kīrīma (addition). Addition is not 2, not 3, not 5 — yet without it, 5 has no connection to 2 or 3. Seeing ekatu kīrīma as ekatu kīrīma = seeing that 5 never arose and never ceased. This analogy is then mapped onto tæbīma/notæbīma, dæníma/nodæníma, and pavattma/nopavattma.

- **Walking analysis (tæbīma/notæbīma)**: Placing the foot and not-placing the foot both receive value from the same source — the unseen phassa (kāya-viññāṇa contact). The difference between placing and not-placing is not found in the two sides; it is found in phassa.

- **Dæníma (knowing/feeling) as the constructed ground**: Tæbīma is grounded in dæníma — "I placed the foot because I felt it." Notæbīma is grounded in nodæníma. But dæníma and nodæníma themselves arose because ekatu vīma (the coming together) was not seen. Therefore the entire chain — dæníma → tæbīma → kriyā (action) → puggala (person) — is constructed from the unseen phassa.

- **Manasikāra cannot reach the mūla (root)**: Manasikāra itself arose from not knowing the mūla svabhāvaya (root nature). Therefore manasikāra, no matter how it is turned, can never catch the mūla. Breaking manasikāra apart only proves that what manasikāra constructed was false — nothing more.

- **Ayoniso vs. yoniso manasikāra**: Ayoniso manasikāra is the condition where manasikāra's own error cannot be caught by manasikāra. Yoniso manasikāra is not something one "does" — it is the non-manasikāra nature (manasikāra novaṇa svabhāvaya). It is nuvaṇa (wisdom), which is not manasikāra. Yoniso manasikāra = seeing from the mūla itself. One cannot separate yoniso from ayoniso — any attempt to "do" yoniso is itself ayoniso.

- **Yoniso manasikāra and viññāṇa non-establishment**: Yoniso manasikāra = the non-establishment (nopihiṭana) of viññāṇa — conditions for viññāṇa's arising not applying. This is connected to the ekatu kīrīma / kavalama never being accessible to manasikāra.

- **Pavattma seen from the mūla = nopavattma**: When existence/continuity (pavattma) is seen all the way to its root, it reveals itself as non-existence (nopavattma). Seeing saṃsāra IS nibbāna — they are not separate achievements.

- **Samudaya and nirodha co-arise**: Samudaya seen fully, all the way to the mūla, is nirodha realized at the same point. There is no path from samudaya to nirodha — they are simultaneous at the binduva (zero-point).

- **Binduva (zero-point) is also constructed**: When "one" (ekak) is seen as constructed, binduva (zero) itself appears as yet another "one" — binduva is also something that cannot truly arise (goḍanægennam bæri ekak). Even the concept of non-arising is constructed.

- **Nibbāna is not a lābīma (acquisition)**: Death appears as a "getting," non-existence appears as a "getting" — both sides are acquisitions. Nibbāna is released from the getting/not-getting framework entirely.

- **Majjhē Sutta and the impossibility of the middle**: The middle cannot be found from the two sides. One cannot even cling to the middle (tævarennaṭa mædak hambavenné næhæ). The stories of "the two sides exist" and "the two sides don't exist" — both stories end.

## Summary

This special discussion takes "ekatu vīma" (coming together / addition) as its central theme and uses it to illuminate the nature of phassa as the unseen middle that constructs all dualities.

The discussion opens with the observation that we always encounter two sides — seeing and not-seeing, placing the foot and not-placing the foot, knowing and not-knowing — but never the phassa that generated both. The error is not in the two sides; it is in the unseen contact-event. A tea-making analogy is introduced: tea-leaves, sugar, and hot water are the hetu (causes), tea is the phala (result), but kavalama (the mixing/stirring) is the paccaya that can never be found in either the causes or the result. The canonical formulation tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phassō (the coming-together of three is contact) is applied: the eye, form, and viññāṇa come together, and this coming-together is the phassa that is never seen — not seeing it generates the two extremes of pēnīma/nopēnīma.

The 2+3=5 analogy is then developed at length. The value of 5 does not come from 2 or from 3 — it comes from ekatu kīrīma (addition), which is not 2, not 3, not 5. If one sees ekatu kīrīma as ekatu kīrīma, one sees that 5 never arose and never ceased — this is nuppāda (non-arising). The analogy is mapped onto walking: placing the foot (tæbīma) and not placing it (notæbīma) both receive their value from the unseen phassa (kāya sparśaya). The difference between the two is not found in the two sides; it is found in phassa. This is described as nuvañin avabōdha karagata yutu tænak — a place that must be realized through wisdom, not through words or the two sides themselves.

The discussion then goes deeper. Tæbīma is grounded in dæníma (feeling/knowing) — one knows one stepped because one felt it. Notæbīma is grounded in nodæníma. But dæníma and nodæníma themselves arose because the ekatu vīma was not seen. The entire architecture — feeling, action, person, internal/external, abhyantara/bāhira — is traced back to the single unseen mūla (root): the phassa that was not recognized as phassa.

A critical section follows on manasikāra. Manasikāra arose from not knowing the mūla svabhāvaya. Therefore, no matter how manasikāra is deployed — even if one breaks apart what manasikāra constructed — manasikāra can never reach the mūla. Breaking manasikāra only proves that what it constructed was boruvak (false); it does not reveal the root. This is the meaning of ayoniso manasikāra: manasikāra's error cannot be caught by manasikāra. Yoniso manasikāra, then, is not a type of manasikāra one performs. It is the non-manasikāra nature — nuvaṇa (wisdom) that is not manasikāra. Yoniso manasikāra means seeing from the mūla itself. Any attempt to "do" yoniso is automatically ayoniso. This connects to viññāṇa's non-establishment: yoniso manasikāra is the condition where viññāṇa's arising conditions do not apply — the ekatu kīrīma / kavalama that can never be reached by manasikāra.

The discussion culminates in several convergent insights. Pavattma (existence/continuity), when seen all the way to its root, reveals itself as nopavattma (non-existence). Seeing saṃsāra is itself nibbāna — they are not separate. Samudaya seen fully, to the mūla, is simultaneously nirodha realized. There is no journey from samudaya to nirodha; they co-arise at the same point — the binduva (zero-point). But even binduva is then shown to be constructed: when "one" is seen as constructed, "zero" appears as yet another "one." Binduva too is goḍanægennam bæri ekak (something that cannot truly arise). Death, non-existence, and even nibbāna as an "attainment" — all appear as lābīma (acquisition) from within the constructed framework. Nibbāna is released from the getting/not-getting framework entirely.

The discussion closes with the Majjhē Sutta: the middle can never be found from the two sides. One cannot even cling to the middle. The dual stories — "the two sides exist" and "the two sides don't exist" — both end. All the Buddha's examples point to the mūla, but practitioners typically break apart the constructed sides instead of seeing the root. The analogy is given of searching for a lost ring under a streetlight rather than where it was actually lost — seeking the hetu from the constructed two sides rather than from the unseen phassa that generated them.
