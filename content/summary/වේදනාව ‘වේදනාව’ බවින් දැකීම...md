## Main dhamma points
- A dialogue arguing that feeling and its felt-object arise together as a knot never before-and-after, that the "I" is born within the very attention "feeling," and that feeling seen as feeling — like a stone recognized as mere recognition — leaves no doer, no view, no suffering-in-the-thing but only in the constructing.
- **Feeling and the felt-thing arise together as one knot, not before-and-after.** Not knowing that "feeling" is only an attention, we take it as a thing, and a thing knots with things — feeling cannot arise alone but needs a nose and a breath, and with it the pleasant, painful and neutral tones are posited. Feeling and perception knot together the instant "it is felt" is thought; the sensation comes alive already knotted to nose-and-breath, not one arising after the other but simultaneously. Un-skilled at seeing this co-arising, we understand only "from an existing nose and breath, a sensation arose," and the five clinging-aggregates are built — after which no scrutiny of the "arisen" nose or breath can ever catch the non-arisen, since even "the two did not arise" still grasps the two posited things.
- **The "I" is born within the attention "feeling"; action arisen is doer arisen.** Attention is itself a consciousness that cannot arise without grasping two things, so "I exist" is understood together with feeling: from morning there is no "I feel" nor "I do not feel," and the "I" is posited by the very attention "feeling," both together in one act — action arisen being doer arisen. The doer arises not by consciousness alone; as the view "an I exists" forms, the needed other side, a breath and a nose to feel, is fabricated to match, and the whole is complete, so feeling cannot be falsified — feeling is real, therefore "I" is real, feeling becoming "me," with form, feeling and perception all made "I." No one posits this puppet, and because it is not understood as no-one's it births a "someone"; a phenomenon caught from every angle, like the magician at the crossroads, whose fault can be found only from beyond the whole phenomenon, never by consciousness — the thief set to search itself. This, the speaker notes, is why it is extremely rare and the Buddha's own hesitation reasonable: a system-flaw shown to minds whose understanding-system is itself the flaw, ingrained over aeons until feeling is worn concrete and a hundred lies have become truth.
- **Impermanence and not-self: no persistence was ever met, so the constructing itself is the suffering.** This is not-self: no abiding existence ever was, no "feeling" persisted, so no persisting nose-and-breath story holds; "feeling does not persist" means that in feeling itself feeling is never found — the nature of permanent-perception — and being immersed we never meet a "not-feeling" either. The impermanence-mark shows just here: in a place where nothing is met, what persisted? To meet "existence" or "persistence" where there is none is the suffering — not that the met thing is suffering, as most suppose; the meeting, the origination itself, never seen as a fault, is the suffering. The two ends, nose-and-breath and feeling, mutually condition, and not seeing that mutual condition, consciousness grasps only the post-knot graspables; consciousness ties the knot not knowing it un-knottable, its very duty being to knot, so using it to find its own fault is the thief's search, and every route — self-made, other-made, or spontaneous — starts from two constructed ends and never finds a middle.
- **Feeling seen as feeling — like a stone as mere recognition — leaves no doer and no view.** In contemplation, feeling must finally be seen *as* a view: the feeling-made-"me," from which "I" am born. Yet feeling is no problem in itself, or arahants could not function — they too see when walking; we say "seeing" while knotted to seen-things, supposing we avoid collisions because things are seen, but if wisdom sees that the seen does not belong to seeing, one walks the road freely. A stone is recognized as a stone — the awakened one too recognizes it — but for the worldling the recognized stone becomes an existing, "seen" stone knotted to the intention "seen," while for the wise the stone is met as stone, recognition known as mere recognition; and even "recognizing stone as stone" is not yet enough, for the concept "stone" holds no colour and no hardness, so seen as mere perception, feeling seen as feeling, and attention seen as attention, wisdom settles at the un-knowable where "seeing" cannot even be known. The worldling thinks about an un-knowable seeing and makes a conceiving; the wise, wisdom settled there, has only the "cannot," no "I saw" born — so no view and no "I" arise, recognized things do not knot, and walking one can say neither "I saw the tree" nor "I did not see it," since un-knotted to the tree no "did-not-see" thought can form and the tree is instantly met. Seeing is empty of seeing, nothing to understand, only the "cannot"; where action, contact, is never met is the true nature, so the view "a doer, an I, exists" can never arise.

## Summary

This dialogue on "seeing feeling as feeling" holds that feeling and its felt-object arise together as one knot, never before-and-after. Not knowing "feeling" is only an attention, we take it as a thing, and a thing knots with things — feeling needs a nose and a breath, and with it the pleasant, painful and neutral tones are posited; feeling and perception knot the instant "it is felt" is thought, the sensation alive already knotted, arising simultaneously. Un-skilled at seeing this, we understand only "from an existing nose and breath a sensation arose," and the five clinging-aggregates are built — after which no scrutiny of the arisen nose or breath catches the non-arisen, since even "the two did not arise" still grasps the two posited things. Attention is itself a consciousness needing two things to arise, so "I exist" is understood with feeling: from morning there is no "I feel" or "I do not feel," and the "I" is posited by the very attention "feeling," both in one act — action arisen is doer arisen. As "an I exists" forms, the matching breath and nose are fabricated, and feeling cannot be falsified: feeling is real, so "I" is real, feeling becoming "me." No one posits this puppet, and not understood as no-one's it births a "someone," caught from every angle like the magician at the crossroads, its fault findable only from beyond, never by the consciousness that is the thief set to search itself — which is why it is rare and the Buddha's hesitation reasonable.

It is not-self: no abiding existence ever was, no feeling persisted, so no persisting nose-and-breath holds; "feeling does not persist" means feeling is never found in feeling, and immersed we never meet a "not-feeling." The impermanence-mark shows here — in a place nothing is met, what persisted? — and to meet "existence" where there is none is the suffering, not the met thing but the meeting, the origination itself, never seen as a fault. The two ends mutually condition, and not seeing this, consciousness grasps only post-knot graspables and ties the knot it cannot un-tie, so every route — self-made, other-made, spontaneous — starts from two constructed ends and finds no middle. Finally feeling must be seen as a view, the feeling-made-"me"; yet feeling is no problem, or arahants could not walk and see. A stone is recognized as a stone by worldling and awakened alike, but the worldling's becomes an existing "seen" stone knotted to the intention "seen," while the wise meets stone as stone, recognition as mere recognition — and even that is not enough, for "stone" holds no colour or hardness, so seen as mere perception, feeling as feeling, attention as attention, wisdom settles at the un-knowable where "seeing" cannot be known. The worldling conceives an un-knowable seeing; the wise has only the "cannot," no "I saw" born, so no view and no "I" arise, and walking one can say neither "I saw the tree" nor "I did not see it." Seeing is empty of seeing, only the "cannot" remaining, and where contact is never met is the true nature, so no "doer, I" can ever arise.
