# සංඥාව — හඳුනාගැනීම — වැයම් නොකොට පිහිටාද නොසිට එතෙර වීම (Part 1)

## Sutta referred to

- No specific sutta cited by name. The Buddha's comparison of saññā to a mirage (marīcikā) is referenced as a well-known simile. The pañca upādānakkhandha and the anattā/anicca/dukkha framework are invoked as doctrinal context.
- The distinction between **nirodha** (non-arising nature) and **niruddha** (arising-and-ceasing) is discussed as a critical terminological point.

## Main dhamma points

- **Avijjā is hidden within the meanings of words**: The surface meaning of words can never capture what needs to be seen. Investigating dhamma through the meanings we already assign to words traps us in the very avijjā we are trying to see through.

- **The mirage analogy and its trap**: The Buddha compared saññā to a mirage — not to establish the mirage as "a type of thing," but to point at nopavattma (non-existence, anattā). However, when we say "saññā is like a mirage," we tend to make the mirage itself into a thing (deyak), giving it existence. The very analogy meant to show non-existence gets co-opted to establish a subtle existence: "recognition is a thing that is like a mirage."

- **Nimitta (sign) is internal, not external**: Why does the mirage look like water? Because of the āpo nimitta (sign of fluidity — the flowing, liquid quality) already stored within us. A one-year-old child, lacking this nimitta, would not recognize "water" at all. The recognition does not come from something external being recognized — it comes from the internal nimitta entangling with what is encountered. The whole pañca upādānakkhandha gets constructed at that point.

- **Haṇdunā gænīma (recognition) has no external basis**: We assume recognition works because something "out there" was recognized. But the flowing-liquid quality (āpo gati) was never "out there" — it was constructed internally as a nimitta. There is nothing external that was truly recognized. Recognition appears to happen, just as water appears to be in the mirage — but nothing was actually there to be recognized.

- **Nirodha vs. niruddha — a critical distinction**: Nirodha dhamma means the non-arising nature (nohaṭagatthu svabhāvaya). Niruddha means arising-and-ceasing — something arises and then perishes. Confusing the two leads listeners to take nirodha as "things arise and then vanish," which is the ucchēda (annihilationist) extreme. Many teachers inadvertently foster this confusion.

- **Cannot reject recognition (the natthi extreme)**: If one says "recognition doesn't happen" or "recognition never occurred," one goes to the natthi (nihilistic) extreme. This amounts to rejecting the constructed world using a dhamma-model as the weapon. But the dhamma-model itself then becomes another construction — dhamma becomes māra.

- **Wisdom sees delusion from within delusion**: Paññā (wisdom) does not reject the muḷāva (delusion). It sees the muḷāva as muḷāva while remaining within it. If wisdom tried to say "hearing doesn't happen, seeing doesn't happen," the arahant could not function in the world at all — which is absurd. Wisdom does not create a separate model and use it to reject experience.

- **The labelling trap**: Saying "recognition is an illusion," "recognition is false," or "recognition never happened" creates a new dhamma-model that is then used to reject the existing experience. This is falling into a rāmuva (framework) — using the Buddha's teaching as a fixed reference point from which to negate experience. The teaching becomes nittya (permanent/fixed), and from that fixed position one rejects the world. This is dhamma becoming māra — one thing is made true (the dhamma-model) to reject another.

## Summary

This is part one of a multi-part discussion on saññā (perception/recognition), haṇdunā gænīma (recognition), and "crossing over without effort, without establishing, without standing" (væyam nokoṭa pihiṭādā nosiṭa eter vīma).

The discussion opens with the principle that avijjā is hidden within the meanings of words. The surface meanings we assign to dhamma terms are themselves carriers of the very ignorance we seek to penetrate. This sets up the central warning: investigating dhamma through the pre-assigned meanings of words traps one in avijjā from the start.

The mirage analogy — the Buddha's own comparison for saññā — is then examined with great care. The standard interpretation is straightforward: a mirage looks like water, but there is no water there; similarly, saññā appears to recognize something, but nothing is truly recognized. Mr. Lasantha points out that this interpretation, while correct on one level, contains a subtle trap. When we say "the mirage looks like water but there is no water," we have already made the mirage into a thing — a type of phenomenon, something that exists in a certain way. But the mirage analogy was given to point at nopavattma (non-existence, anattā). The moment we make it into "a thing that is like this," we have given it pavattma (existence) using the very analogy meant to show non-existence.

The discussion then probes deeper into recognition itself. Why does the mirage look like water at all? Because the āpo nimitta — the sign of fluidity, the flowing-liquid quality — is already stored within us. A one-year-old child, lacking this nimitta, would see something but would not recognize "water." The recognition comes not from something external being identified, but from the internal nimitta entangling with what is encountered. What gets constructed at that point is a full pañca upādānakkhandha — form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness — all assembled around the internal nimitta. The "water" was never out there. The flowing-liquid quality was never out there. Both were internal constructions.

This leads to the question: did haṇdunā gænīma (recognition) actually happen? The discussion treats this question with extreme care. One cannot simply say "recognition didn't happen" — that would be the natthi (nihilistic) extreme. Saying "recognition is an illusion" or "recognition is false" creates a dhamma-model (a new construction) and uses it to reject the experienced world. This is described as dhamma becoming māra: one holds the Buddha's teaching as nittya (permanent/fixed truth) and from that position rejects experience. The teaching has become a rāmuva (framework) — another form of clinging.

The correct approach, the discussion indicates, is that paññā (wisdom) sees the muḷāva (delusion) as muḷāva from within the delusion itself. It does not step outside and reject. It does not create a separate vantage point from which to negate. It does not say "this never happened." It sees the non-arising nature (nirodha) of what appears to arise — without rejecting the appearance. This is distinguished sharply from niruddha (arising-and-ceasing), which many teachers conflate with nirodha. Nirodha is the non-arising nature; niruddha is the impermanence of what arose. Confusing them leads to the ucchēda (annihilationist) view.
