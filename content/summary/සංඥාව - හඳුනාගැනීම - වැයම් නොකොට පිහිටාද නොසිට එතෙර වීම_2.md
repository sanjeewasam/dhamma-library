# සංඥාව — හඳුනාගැනීම — වැයම් නොකොට පිහිටාද නොසිට එතෙර වීම (Part 2)

## Sutta referred to

- **Majjha Sutta** — cited directly with the Pāli phrase "majjhē mantā nalippati" (the wise one does not cling even to the middle)
- The **nēvasaññānāsaññāyatana** (sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception) is referenced as where those who reject saññā end up reborn — an outcome the Buddha warned against
- The mirage analogy for saññā (from Part 1) is further developed

## Main dhamma points

- **Cannot reject the appearing (vēyam = āyāsa, effort)**: If one tries to reject hearing, seeing, or feeling through some dhamma-based understanding, that very effort (āyāsa) constructs a new self — "the one who doesn't hear," "the one who has transcended seeing." This is sakkāya arising through rejection. Even a Buddha cannot stop the hearing/seeing nature — any effort toward that is an impossible, pointless task.

- **Nēvasaññāyatana as the destination of rejection**: Those who conclude "saññā doesn't truly happen" and hold that as their position are described as reborn in the nēvasaññānāsaññāyatana brahma realm — the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. The Buddha's teaching fits precisely: calling hearing a maññanā and then rejecting it lands one exactly there.

- **Natthi paccaya — building a world from the natthi extreme**: When one rejects the appearing (says "it doesn't happen"), one unknowingly moves from the atthi (existence) extreme to the natthi (non-existence) extreme and constructs a new world from there. The "I who doesn't hear" is a new sakkāya built from the natthi side.

- **Nothing can be done to the appearing**: The pēna svabhāvaya (appearing nature), the æhena svabhāvaya (hearing nature) — not even a dasama mātraya (decimal fraction) of change can be made to these. This must be established (established) first. If one expects the appearing to change, blank out, or transform after understanding, that expectation is itself diṭṭhi.

- **Pirisinda dakīma (penetrating seeing) is the only possibility**: The pañca upādānakkhandha — the entire appearing world — cannot be rejected, modified, or escaped. The only thing possible is pirisinda dakīma (seeing through, penetrating). This is not rejecting, not escaping — it is seeing the yathā svabhāvaya (the nature as it is).

- **The title decoded — "vēyam nokoṭa pihiṭādā nosiṭa eter vīma"**: (1) Vēyam nokoṭa = not making effort to reject the appearing. (2) Pihiṭādā nosiṭa = not establishing/standing on what appeared (not giving it pavattma/existence, not making it nittya). (3) Eter vīma = crossing over — which happens when neither effort nor establishment occurs.

- **Tāvulā demonstration (sound experiment)**: The word "tāvulā" is spoken. Hearing happened — that cannot be rejected. But what was gained from the hearing? Nothing. The sound was heard, but nothing was found within it. Therefore the sound is śūnya from within itself (śabdayen śūnyayi). This is not rejection of hearing (vēyam), nor establishment of what was heard (pihiṭīma). It is seeing the saññā mātraya — the mere recognition — as empty from within.

- **Saññā as kriyā mātraya (mere activity)**: Saññā is a happening, an activity, a recognition-event. But inside it, nothing is found (hambavenné næhæ). It is like the mirage: water appears, but no water is found. Similarly, a sound is heard and recognition occurs, but nothing is obtained. The recognition is śūnya from within itself — not because it was rejected, but because penetrating investigation found nothing inside.

- **Majjhē mantā nalippati**: When both extremes (rejecting hearing / establishing what was heard) are seen as errors and neither is engaged, does a "middle" remain? No — when both sides don't apply, the middle also doesn't apply. There is nothing to cling to as "middle." This is the meaning of "the wise one does not cling even to the middle."

## Summary

This second part continues the investigation of saññā (perception/recognition) and directly addresses the title's three phrases: vēyam nokoṭa (without effort), pihiṭādā nosiṭa (without establishing or standing), and eter vīma (crossing over).

The discussion opens by recapping the trap identified in Part 1: using dhamma to reject the appearing world falls to the natthi (nihilistic) extreme. This is now developed further. If one says "hearing doesn't happen" or "seeing is a maññanā and therefore not real," one constructs a new self — the one who doesn't hear, the one who has transcended. This is sakkāya arising from the natthi side. The destination of such a position, the discussion notes, is the nēvasaññānāsaññāyatana brahma realm — neither-perception-nor-non-perception — which is precisely where the Buddha located those who reject saññā while still clinging to a subtle selfhood.

A critical point is then established: even a Buddha cannot stop the hearing or seeing nature. Not a decimal fraction of change can be made to the appearing. This is not a limitation but a fact of the hāṭagatta (arisen) world. Any effort to modify, escape, or nullify the appearing is therefore an impossible task — and recognizing this impossibility is essential. If one expects that understanding will cause the appearing to change, blank out, or transform, that expectation is itself diṭṭhi.

The only possibility is pirisinda dakīma — seeing through, penetrating the appearing without rejecting or modifying it. The pañca upādānakkhandha is not to be escaped but to be seen through. This is the meaning of "vēyam nokoṭa" (without effort): not making effort to reject the appearing.

The discussion then turns to a live demonstration using the word "tāvulā." The teacher speaks the word. Hearing happens — the participant confirms this cannot be rejected. But what was obtained from the hearing? Nothing. The sound was heard as a śabda mātraya (mere sound), but no deyak (thing) was found within it. The sound is śūnya (empty) from within itself — not because hearing was denied, not because something was extracted, but because penetrating investigation found nothing inside the sound-event.

This is then connected to the mirage analogy from Part 1. The mirage shows water — but no water is found. Similarly, the sound "tāvulā" is heard — recognition happens as an activity (kriyā mātraya) — but nothing is found within the recognition. Saññā is a happening without content. It is not a deyak (thing) because nothing is found inside it. The haṇdunā gænīma (recognition) is itself śūnya from within — not empty because something was removed, but empty because there was never anything there to find.

The title's second phrase — "pihiṭādā nosiṭa" (without establishing or standing) — is demonstrated through the tāvulā experiment. The participant neither rejects the hearing (vēyam nokoṭa) nor claims to have gained something from it (pihiṭādā nosiṭa). There is nothing to stand on, nothing to establish. And this is not a special achievement or a cultivated state — it is simply what is found when one investigates without pre-assigned meanings.

The Majjha Sutta phrase "majjhē mantā nalippati" is then explained. When both extremes don't apply, the middle also doesn't apply — because "middle" is defined relative to two sides. When the two sides are seen as errors and neither is engaged, there is no middle to cling to. The wise one doesn't cling to the middle not through effort but because the middle simply doesn't arise when the two sides don't arise.

The discussion closes with the participant beginning to probe deeper into how hearing itself arises — noting that if nothing was found within the hearing, then saying "hearing happened" (haṭagatta) is also problematic. This thread is presumably continued in Part 3.
