# සංඥාව — හඳුනාගැනීම — වැයම් නොකොට පිහිටාද නොසිට එතෙර වීම (Part 3)

## Sutta referred to

- The Buddha's instruction to **Kisā Gotamī** — "bring mustard seeds from a house where no one has died" — is cited as an example of how the Buddha taught through pratyakṣa (direct experiential proof) rather than through verbal explanation or sutta recitation.
- The Buddha's statement that **lōka samudaya and lōka nirodha** are both found within this "bamba-sized body" (bambayak pamaṇa śarīraya) is referenced to show that samudaya is entirely internal.
- The phrase "æsīma æsīmen suññayi" (hearing is empty from within hearing itself) is mentioned as something said repeatedly in dēsanā but never truly penetrated by most listeners.

## Main dhamma points

- **Applying śūnyatā across all five khandha**: The participant extends the insight from Part 2 (sound is śūnya from within itself) across all five upādānakkhandha. If hearing is śūnya from within hearing, then: recognition (saññā) is śūnya from within recognition, feeling (vēdanā) is śūnya from within feeling, formations (saṅkhāra) are śūnya from within formations, and consciousness (viññāṇa) is śūnya from within consciousness. Each khandha, examined from within, yields nothing — no deyak (thing) is found inside.

- **The tāvulā/puṭuva contrast**: Two examples are used side by side. "Tāvulā" (an Italian word for "table" unknown to the participant) — hearing happens but no nimitta arises, so nothing is found. "Puṭuva" (chair, a known word) — hearing happens and nimitta immediately arise (shape, colour, sitting-function). The teacher explains: with tāvulā, the emptiness is forced to be seen because no nimitta can activate. With puṭuva, the same emptiness is present but hidden because nimitta activate instantly. The teaching point: the sound itself carried nothing in either case — the nimitta came from within.

- **Sound carries nothing**: When "puṭuva" is heard, the listener thinks the sound delivered the chair. But the shape, colour, and paṭhavī quality (solidity) did not come through the sound — they arose from the listener's own stored nimitta. A one-year-old child hearing the same sound would get nothing from it. Therefore the sound, even in the familiar case, is śūnya from within itself. What appears to be "recognition of a chair" is the entanglement of internal nimitta with the sound-event — not something the sound carried.

- **Samudaya is entirely within the bamba-sized body**: Since the nimitta that generate the entire recognized world arise from within, the samudaya (arising/origin) is entirely internal. This confirms the Buddha's statement that lōka samudaya is found within this body — not externally.

- **No nimitta to grasp — nimitta anunimitta**: If the appearing is seen as śūnya from within, no nimitta are found to grasp. The instruction "do not grasp nimitta and anunimitta" (nimitta anunimitta ganna epā) is not an effortful restraint — it is the natural outcome of seeing the śūnyatā. There is nothing to grasp because penetrating investigation found nothing inside.

- **Saṅkhāra cannot form**: When neither the "hearing didn't happen" extreme nor the "hearing gave me something" extreme applies, there is no material for saṅkhāra to construct from. No saṅkhāra form from either side — this is the natural cessation of construction, not a suppression.

- **Pratyakṣa over verbal teaching**: The teacher explains why an unknown foreign word (tāvulā) was used rather than a familiar one: verbal explanations and dēsanā repetitions ("hearing is śūnya from within") do not penetrate to the blood-and-bone level. Only pratyakṣa (direct experiential proof) does. The Kisā Gotamī story is cited: the Buddha did not give a sutta — he sent her to find a house where no one had died, knowing she would discover the truth herself.

- **Nimitta are maññanā (conceivings)**: The stored nimitta that activate on hearing a familiar word are described as maññanā — mere conceivings, not discoveries of external reality. The teacher prepares to demonstrate this practically.

## Summary

This shorter third part continues directly from Part 2's tāvulā sound experiment and extends the insight across all five khandha before introducing the contrasting puṭuva (chair) example.

The participant opens by articulating the insight from Part 2: if nothing was found within the hearing of "tāvulā," then hearing itself cannot be said to have haṭagatta (arisen) in any meaningful sense — yet it also cannot be rejected. Therefore hearing is śūnya from within hearing itself. The participant then extends this to all five upādānakkhandha: recognition is śūnya from within recognition, feeling from within feeling, formations from within formations, consciousness from within consciousness. The teacher confirms this extension.

The teacher then restates the core position with precision: hearing was not rejected (ǣhīma pratikṣēpa karannēt næhæ), and nothing was found to establish or stand on (pihiṭa hiṭinna deyak hambavennet næhæ). When both sides are seen clearly, no saṅkhāra can form — there is no material to construct from in either direction. The instruction to "not grasp nimitta" is not effortful restraint but the natural state when nothing is found to grasp.

The puṭuva (chair) example is then introduced to address a natural objection: with tāvulā, of course nothing was found — the word was unknown. But what about a known word? The teacher speaks "puṭuva" and asks what the participant experienced. The participant reports: shape, colour, the chair-image arose. The teacher then asks the critical question: did those qualities come through the sound? The participant recognizes they did not — a one-year-old hearing the same sound would get nothing. The shape, colour, and solidity came from the participant's own stored nimitta, not from the sound. Therefore the sound of "puṭuva" is equally śūnya from within — the same emptiness as tāvulā, but hidden by the instant activation of internal nimitta.

This leads to the confirmation that samudaya is entirely internal — within the bamba-sized body, as the Buddha stated. The nimitta that construct the entire recognized world arise from within, not from external sources. Lōka samudaya and lōka nirodha are both found here.

The teacher explains the pedagogical method: the tāvulā example works like the Buddha's instruction to Kisā Gotamī. Verbal explanations ("hearing is śūnya") do not penetrate. Only pratyakṣa — direct experiential proof where the person is forced to confront the truth themselves — reaches the level of genuine insight. The session ends with the teacher preparing to demonstrate that the stored nimitta are themselves maññanā (mere conceivings).
