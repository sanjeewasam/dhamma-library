## Sutta referred to
- **Explicitly named:** the **Kālāma Sutta**, cited for the principle that to declare "there are no such five things" would be "**my lie**" to a world in which they do arise; and the line "**saññaṁ pariññā vitareyya oghaṁ**" (fully comprehending perception, one crosses the flood — **Suttanipāta 779**, quoted as "සංඥා පරිඤ්ඤා විතරෙය්‍ය ඕඝං").
- Referred to, unattributed: a modern **"Paṭiccasamuppāda" book** stating that even the four elements are four "because of a diversity of sensation." Terms used: *rūpa–śabda–gandha–rasa–poṭṭhabba(–dhamma) saññā* (the perceptions of form, sound, smell, taste, touch, mind-object), *nānatva* (diversity), *āyatana* (the six sense-bases), *avijjā*, *anuppāda / nirodha* (non-arising / cessation), *samudaya*, *sakkāya-diṭṭhi* (self-view), *upādāna* (clinging), the four *gati / mahābhūta*, *vidarśanā / vipassanā* vs *paññā*, *nirāyāsa* (effortless). Similes: jam-bun and fish-bun all made of flour; a mother arising only with a child; a mirage's "water"; a horse bolting before the stable is shut.
- **Notes on the source:** titled **"The Diversity of Perception and the Delusion of Experience,"** a two-speaker dialogue (a teacher addressed *mahattaya*, and a questioner). It appears to be a complete single recording, ending cleanly at ~[26:00].

## Main dhamma points
- A dialogue arguing that the five perceptions are "five" only through a supposed diversity that is never actually experienced, that this is the true meaning of mirage, and that fully comprehending perception dissolves the six sense-bases and crosses the flood.
- **The five are "five" only through a supposed diversity, and the wrong understanding must be corrected before the division, not after.** Form, sound, smell, taste and touch are five recognitions because we suppose a diversity among them — as jam-bun and fish-bun, seen as mere flour, lose their separateness; they carry meaning only because we "know a thing" through them, and that supposed knowing makes each a "thought," splitting into five sense-bases, the wrong understanding "five things reach five bases" being the very cause of the mind's birth. Once divided, calling them "thoughts" or "a mind" is useless; the correction must come *before* the division, for there alone is non-arising — since then nothing arises at all. To take the arisen five and say "it did not happen to them" is a lie, for while ignorance operates they do arise; the Buddha will not take the arisen and negate it, saying that to declare "no such five" would be his lie to the world, nor will he go to the "it exists" extreme — and whatever analysis one makes, the persisting not-knowing is itself the evidence that not-knowing still operates.
- **Each perception is named for a felt-quality that the very knowing does not contain.** "Taste," "smell," "sound" have meaning each from some felt-quality — smell named for a quality unlike taste, sound for one found in neither taste nor smell — yet does the concept "a crow's sound" hold an iota of audible quality, or "jasmine-flower smell" the smell-specific quality absent in sound? Had we ever truly experienced that quality we would not need the flower to see the smell, holding it already in the knowing; but the two knowings hold no such quality, for we never experienced it, and we can experience a thing only if the mind knows it, which it never did. This is why they are called *perceptions* — form-perception, sound-perception and the rest, the mind's object too — named so precisely because never actually known.
- **Supposing a never-met thing "recognized" is the true meaning of mirage.** To hold "I recognized" a thing the mind can never meet is the real meaning of a mirage: a never-happened thing supposed known, like the mirage's "water" recognized from afar, approached, and found absent — after which negating "there is no water," once already deceived, is useless, the horse having bolted. The delusion is not removed by analysing the deceived thing however finely, down to CO₂ or H₂O; deception ends only by becoming non-deception at the deceiving-point itself, for the very "thinking it is water" is the fault, and one must be freed from the deceiving *there*.
- **Insight exposes the trusted "I knew"; seeing thought as thought is not yet enough.** Even seeing "it is a thinking" does not free, for it is recognized as thinking only because it is thought, the diversity's association still present, and "I know a thinking" is one more origination, caught in the "there is a mind" knot — like shutting the stable after the horse has bolted. To escape, one must see that these smell- and sound-knowings hold no intrinsic quality and that the taste was never met — a view of "it is met" established over saṁsāra; seeing "not-met" a hundred-million times is *vidarśanā*, which is not wisdom but its cause, working by exposing the crookedness of the hundred-percent-trusted "I knew," until one realizes one never in life knew a "smell," that no smell ever arose within — not as an experience but in the very thinking-nature, whose base is the direct wisdom that the smell-quality and sound-quality can never be experienced or known.
- **What is released is the six sense-bases; comprehending perception crosses the flood.** What falls away is vast: the diversity becomes zero, and the five one used with total conviction become zero; self-view falls anyway (taking "a form exists" builds "seeing" and the "me" seen through it), but larger still are the **six sense-bases** that arise with the six — "the world is the six bases" — for as no birth means no "child" and so no "mother," while a child's birth brings "mother" with it, so "smell" gives rise to "nose" and "sound" to "ear"; seen through to there, there is nothing to do. Our clinging to the organs — a two-hundred-percent belief that "sound can be known," hence "an ear is needed to know sounds" — cannot form once understanding is corrected, since "did an un-known sound come through the ear?" can never be; and asked to think of anything apart from form, sound, smell, taste, touch and mind-object, one cannot, for whatever is thought falls into these six perceptions. Thus, fully comprehending perception, saṁsāra's building can no longer be forced — it stops, and one crosses the flood.

## Summary

This dialogue holds that form, sound, smell, taste and touch are five recognitions only because we suppose a diversity among them — as jam-bun and fish-bun, seen as mere flour, lose their separateness; they mean something only because we "know a thing" through them, and that supposed knowing makes each a thought, splitting into five sense-bases, the wrong understanding being the cause of the mind's birth. Once divided, calling them thoughts or a mind is useless; correction must come before the division, for there alone is non-arising. To take the arisen five and say "it did not happen" is a lie, for while ignorance operates they do arise; the Buddha will not negate the arisen — saying "no such five" would be his lie to the world — nor affirm "it exists," and whatever analysis one makes, the persisting not-knowing itself proves not-knowing still operates. Each perception is named for a felt-quality the very knowing does not contain: does "a crow's sound" hold any audible quality, or "jasmine smell" the smell-quality absent in sound? Had we truly experienced it we would not need the flower, holding it in the knowing; but the knowings hold no such quality, never experienced, and we can experience a thing only if the mind knows it, which it never did — which is why they are called perceptions, named so because never known.

To suppose a never-met thing "recognized" is the true mirage: a never-happened thing supposed known, like the mirage's water approached and found absent, after which negating "no water" is useless, the horse having bolted; the delusion is not removed by analysing the deceived thing down to CO₂ or H₂O, but only by becoming non-deception at the deceiving-point, since the very "thinking it is water" is the fault. Even seeing "it is a thinking" does not free, for it is recognized as thinking only because thought, and "I know a thinking" is one more origination — the stable shut after the horse bolts. One must see that these knowings hold no intrinsic quality and the taste was never met; seeing "not-met" a hundred-million times is insight, not wisdom but its cause, exposing the crookedness of the trusted "I knew," until one realizes no smell was ever known or arose within — not an experience but a matter of the thinking-nature, whose base is the direct wisdom that smell-quality and sound-quality can never be known. What is released is vast: the diversity becomes zero, the five become zero, self-view falls, and larger still the six sense-bases that arise with the six — for as a child's birth brings "mother," "smell" gives rise to "nose" and "sound" to "ear"; the clinging to organs cannot form once understanding is corrected, since "did an un-known sound come through the ear?" can never be, and apart from the six perceptions nothing can even be thought. So, fully comprehending perception, saṁsāra's building can no longer be forced — it stops, and one crosses the flood.
