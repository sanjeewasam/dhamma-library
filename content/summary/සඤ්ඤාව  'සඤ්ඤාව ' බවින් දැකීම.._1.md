## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted in Pāli: the fourfold formula "**na saññasaññī, na visaññasaññī, no pi asaññī, na vibhūtasaññī**" (not one who perceives perception, not one who perceives distortedly, not without perception, not one with perception dissolved), glossed as: recognition is not a recognizer / nor a distorted-recognizer / not "no recognition" / not "recognition does-not-occur." This fourfold negation is the description characteristic of the **Kalahavivāda Sutta (Suttanipāta 4.11)** and the arahant / formless attainment; **note it is not named by title in the talk.**

## Main dhamma points
- The first segment reading the fourfold "neither-perception-nor-non-perception" formula through the mountain analysis, to show recognition is un-doable, so that no recognizer, no distorted-recognizer, and neither "no recognition" nor "recognition occurs" can stand.
- **Recognition is not a recognizer; seeing it un-doable, no "recognizer" view forms.** "Not one who perceives perception" means recognition is an action, not a person — we suppose "someone recognizes," and because it seems do-able a doer is matched to it; but only when recognition is realized as un-doable does the view "a recognizer exists" fail to form, so this must be seen subtly, or it slips past.
- **Nor a distorted-recognizer: no one converts one perception into another.** "Not one who perceives distortedly" means no one stands who recognizes the nature in a substituted way — replacing the perception "mountain" with "four great elements" or "pure octads," which are themselves only perceptions; since recognition itself cannot be done, neither can this distorted re-recognizing, so no such recognizer is created.
- **Neither "no recognition" nor "recognition occurs."** "Not without perception" does not mean there is no recognition — it refuses the slide to "no recognition happens"; and "not one with perception dissolved" refuses "recognition does-not-occur," which would itself imply "so it does occur." Thus all four extremes fail — no recognizer, no distorted-recognizer, not "no recognition," not "recognition occurs" — which is the arahant's level, as the Buddha, asked whether he inquired "gladly" or "in suffering," was to be answered by neither.
- **The mountain: its meaning lives in non-mountain items, with no gap to escape through.** At the worldling's level "mountain" is met as an existing mountain, water as water; by analysis "mountain" is only earth-element, shape and colour — all perceptions — and describing the mountain by these name-factors is name-body designation. The mountain's meaning comes not from the mountain but from these name-factors, things that are *not* the mountain ("hard" is not called mountain, nor colour, nor shape), so the mountain arises from non-mountain items; and reversing, the shape, colour and hardness we take as real seem recognized because they were "in the mountain," while really "mountain" was understood only through them — inseparable and mutually conditioning, like hen and egg, a house from cement-stone-sand, a body from blood-flesh-sinew-bone, with no beginning or end and no gap, the name-factors un-deniable because a mountain stands on the other side and the mountain seeming to hold them, so there is no escape.
- **Recognition seems a recognition only because not known as such; language is a distortion of perception.** The concept "mountain" holds no description within itself, and the description given is what birthed "mountain," from which we conclude "recognition is do-able" — for language is a distortion of perception. "Mountain" has no meaning in itself; its meaning sits elsewhere, in the irrelevant earth, colour and shape, and "brown," "hard" have no location, no "thing" for them to reside in, a mere name birthing no form — so these are not recognized from anywhere, and "such a thing existed and I recognized this" is impossible too. Could "mountain" recognize a mountain from the bare concept? If the concept held the mountain's nature, a child taught the word could recognize a mountain, but cannot; name gives no footing, so there is an inability in the very nature — one cannot recognize a mountain from "mountain." Of the three (no recognition / recognition exists / recognition un-doable), it is un-doable; and seeing the un-doable *as* un-doable, it can no longer become an action, no "action"-name fits, "recognize" does not apply, and no "recognizer" view can arise — matching "not one who perceives perception," and the distorted re-recognizing failing with it.

## Summary

This first segment reads the fourfold formula — not one who perceives perception, not one who perceives distortedly, not without perception, not one with perception dissolved — through the mountain. "Not a perceiver" means recognition is an action, not a person: we suppose "someone recognizes" because it seems do-able and match a doer to it, but only when recognition is realized as un-doable does the "recognizer" view fail to form. "Not a distorted-recognizer" means no one stands who substitutes one perception for another — replacing "mountain" with "four elements" or "pure octads," themselves only perceptions — since recognition itself cannot be done. "Not without perception" refuses "no recognition happens," and "not with perception dissolved" refuses "recognition does-not-occur," which would imply "so it occurs"; thus all four extremes fail, which is the arahant's level, answered by neither "gladly" nor "in suffering."

The mountain shows it. At the worldling's level "mountain" is met as an existing mountain, but by analysis it is only earth-element, shape and colour, all perceptions, described by name-factors — name-body designation — so its meaning comes not from the mountain but from things that are not the mountain, and reversing, the shape, colour and hardness seem recognized because "in the mountain," while "mountain" was understood only through them: inseparable and mutually conditioning, like hen and egg, a house from cement and stone, a body from blood and bone, with no gap and no escape, the name-factors un-deniable because a mountain stands opposite. Recognition seems a recognition only because not known as such, for language is a distortion of perception: "mountain" holds no meaning in itself, its meaning sitting in the irrelevant earth, colour and shape, while "brown" and "hard" have no location and a mere name births no form. Could "mountain" recognize a mountain from the bare concept? A child taught the word cannot; name gives no footing, so there is an inability in the nature itself — of the three, recognition is un-doable, and seen as un-doable it can no longer become an action, no "action"-name fits, and no "recognizer" arises, matching "not one who perceives perception," the distorted re-recognizing failing with it.
