## Main dhamma points
- The third segment using a demonstration of silence and a mirror to show that "not audible" grabs a never-understood hearing, that we abide in a hearing and a seeing we can never know, and that consciousness, like an illusion, cannot stand once no condition is found.
- **"Not audible" is said only because of an un-understood hearing.** Wisdom at "the inability" helps end consciousness, which arises abiding in the view "this is do-able"; the inability realized, the conceivings wear away and hearing, seeing and sensing lose their appeal without desire. In ten seconds of silence, "did you hear?" is answered "no" — but was there hearing though nothing was heard? We call a quiet place "peaceful," meaning "nothing audible," yet we understood "no sound" only because of the hearing-capacity, as a person deaf from birth could not; and the "sound" we deny is a non-arisen sound taken from memory, not one present. Seeing the present clearly: if no sound of mine was there, there is no ground even to say "no sound" — the "no" comes only from a hearing-capacity on my side that, examined, was never actually understood in that moment.
- **We abide in a hearing and a seeing we can never know; "I" am that very hearing.** Asked whether, in the silent moment, hearing "exists," one can say neither "yes" (it is not understood) nor "no"; we hold a hearing-capacity that is assumed, not understood, so we grab a never-understood thing and say "not audible," the "no" springing from not knowing the capacity — for hearing is "me," and not knowing hearing as un-knowable, one cannot even understand "I have this capacity." Really it was hearing, not non-hearing, that persisted, "not heard" itself implying hearing, while "non-hearing" was never experienced; so we abide in hearing, the eternalist permanence, never knowing it, and were we truly to know "hearing exists" no "not audible" could ever be spoken. The four views run on because "hearing can never be known" is never realized; not seeing acceptance as a view, "sound and ear exist" seems true, and it cannot be falsified because "I" am born in the hearing-view and, asked to prove "I exist," can only cite "I see, I hear, I sense" — so the four views *are* me, no person apart, and mindfulness must settle at the un-knowable hearing and seeing, not "understand" them.
- **Sitting, the "leg and floor" arise only knotted with feeling, confirming "I exist."** Sitting is not understood as sitting; only with the feeling comes "there is a chair, I am sitting here," and eyes closed, no notion of "leg" or "floor" arises until thought, for the three arise together as the meeting-of-three called contact and no single piece comes alone. "Leg and floor," unfelt, do not prompt thought, arising only knotted, and once knotted "the feeling is me," unseen because we are immersed and lack wisdom; not knowing, we knot the two, whose fault cannot be found since they are mutually conditioned, and the feeling proves "I sit on the floor," confirming "I exist" through the knotted things.
- **The two objects come not from existence but from thinking, through a contact in the mind.** Reversed: does feeling arise because leg and floor exist, or do they seem to exist because thought? Because thought — for unfelt they prompt no thought — yet to think, an object must come, and it comes through contact; but the body and chair did not contact, the contact happened in the mind, and the two objects are themselves a contact, the meeting-of-three by which consciousness forms, dependent on body and touch. For these three to form in the mind, where did the two objects come from? The question is clear, the answer not: we take the five or six sense-bases as witnesses — body-touch by body, eye-form by eye, ear-sound by ear — but when these witnesses vanish consciousness has nowhere to draw its condition, and when no such condition is ever found the mind's food runs out.
- **Consciousness is an illusion that cannot stand; the mirror shows why "seeing and I" resolve neither as two nor as one.** The mind grabs the sense-bases as "something heard or seen before," not from present things, so consciousness is an illusion — as a magician draws a dove from a handkerchief and no search shows how, a never-happened thing whose displaying mind cannot exist — and so consciousness cannot stand, object and consciousness both breaking when one comes to this place, since consciousness stands only till a condition is found and none is met through eye, ear or nose. The view was that "a seeing, a hearing, a sensing was met," and from that assumption the mind arises rooted in not-knowing. The mirror makes it plain: a reflection appears separate and a mirror exists, yet remove the object and there is no reflection, while glass becomes "mirror" only by its reflecting — so the two, once arisen, cannot be separated (no reflection, no "mirror"; no mirror, no reflection) nor called one (the reflection is not the mirror, nor the mirror the reflection), and apart from these two modes one cannot even think. So too "seeing and I": neither two nor one, seeing is not me and I am not seeing, yet without seeing "I" do not stand and without me there is no seeing, both established only in thinking — the knot we cannot see. (The segment ends here.)

## Summary

This third segment turns on a demonstration of silence. Wisdom at "the inability" ends consciousness, which arises abiding in "this is do-able"; the inability realized, the conceivings wear away. In ten seconds of silence "did you hear?" is answered "no" — but was there hearing though nothing was heard? We call quiet "peaceful," meaning "nothing audible," yet understood "no sound" only through the hearing-capacity, as a born-deaf could not, and the sound we deny is a non-arisen one from memory; if no sound of mine was there, there is no ground to say "no sound," the "no" coming only from a capacity that, examined, was never understood in that moment. Asked whether hearing "exists" in the silence, one can say neither yes nor no; we grab a never-understood thing and say "not audible," for hearing is "me," and not knowing hearing as un-knowable one cannot understand "I have this capacity." It was hearing, not non-hearing, that persisted, "not heard" implying hearing, while non-hearing was never experienced; so we abide in hearing, the eternalist permanence, never knowing it — and were we truly to know it, no "not audible" could be spoken. The four views run on because "hearing can never be known" is never realized, and cannot be falsified because "I" am born in the hearing-view, citing "I see, I hear" to prove "I exist"; so the four views are me, and mindfulness must settle at the un-knowable, not understand it.

Sitting is not understood as sitting; only with feeling comes "I am sitting here," and eyes closed no "leg" or "floor" arises until thought, the three arising together as the meeting-of-three, no piece alone; unfelt they prompt no thought, arising only knotted, and once knotted "the feeling is me," the feeling proving "I sit on the floor" and confirming "I exist." Reversed, the leg and floor come not from existence but from thinking, through a contact that happened in the mind, the two objects being themselves a contact by which consciousness forms; for these to form, where did the objects come from? We take the sense-bases as witnesses, but when they vanish consciousness has nowhere to draw its condition, and finding none the mind's food runs out. So consciousness is an illusion — as a magician's dove from a handkerchief, a never-happened thing whose mind cannot exist — and cannot stand, object and consciousness breaking together, for the view was that "a seeing, a hearing was met," from which the mind arises rooted in not-knowing. The mirror shows why: reflection and mirror, once arisen, are neither separable (no reflection, no mirror) nor one (the reflection is not the mirror), and apart from both modes one cannot even think — so too "seeing and I," neither two nor one, seeing not me and I not seeing, yet neither standing without the other, both established only in thinking, the knot we cannot see.
