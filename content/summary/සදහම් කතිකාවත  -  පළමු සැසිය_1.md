## Sutta referred to
- Directly referred to, unattributed by title: the account of a wanderer who asks the Buddha "**is there a self?**" and receives no answer, then "**is there no self?**" and again no answer, the Buddha sitting silent, after which **Ven. Ānanda asks why** — with the explanation that either answer would have confirmed the man in one extreme (this is the **Ānanda / Vacchagotta episode, SN 44.10**); these are called the **undeclared questions** (*avyākṛta*). Also the **horse-hair split sevenfold and pierced with another strand**, said to be easier than removing self-view (the *vāla* simile, SN 56.45).
- Referred to, unattributed: a **Zen simile** of three stages — mountains are mountains and water is water; then mountains are no longer mountains nor water water; then, at arahantship, mountains are again mountains and water water. Terms: *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*, *sakkāya / sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, *pævætma* (existence), *samādhi / bhāvanā*, *yoniso-manasikāra* (wise attention), *svabhāva-dharma* (nature), *rūpa-śabda-gandha-rasa-poṭṭhabba-dhamma*, *rūpa-vedanā-saññā-saṅkhāra*, *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*. Similes: a tree that never plans to take in carbon dioxide; a plant grown only by watering, not by pulling; a horse bolting before the stable is shut; a pesticide handled with care.
- **Notes on the source:** **Part 1** of **"Dhamma Discussion — First Session,"** a group dialogue (a teacher, *mahattaya*; a lady "Anti"; others named "Śrī" and "Chāndanī Miss"). It reads as a continuing section (the Zen simile it opens at the close is developed in Part 2).

## Main dhamma points
- The first session arguing that realization is un-doable and self-built from not-seeing the nature, that these very words are wrong-understandings to be seen through and not grasped, and that seeing the method by which saṁsāra is built — not escaping an existing saṁsāra — is the correctness.
- **Whatever is grasped without seeing the nature builds the self; realization cannot be done.** Wherever "I must grasp" is thought, an "I sitting and doing it" forms, and even building concentration is watched to be the not-seeing of the nature that builds this existence — "I exist and the world exists"; taking a worldly thing or a samādhi as "mine" is the same, so "my samādhi" is built, and self arises per object. Grasping any built thing one can never see the fault of the building; realization is not something I can do but must be realized by nature itself, un-doable even for the Buddha to force, who can only point "it is like this" — as a tree has no plan to take in carbon dioxide, its nature un-doable, and a plant is grown only by watering, the "growing" belonging to no one.
- **These very words are wrong-understandings; grasp none of them.** At the seen and heard places one need not prove "the meeting-of-three-contact does not happen"; seeing the very idea "it happens" as built by not-knowing is enough — and to say "understand it is wrong" is a worse error, for one then grabs something else in its place or thinks "if I removed the meeting-of-three, good," which is why the Buddha left the four forms of the question undeclared, any answer building a view. Everything spoken here is about wrong-understandings, not the nature — like groping the sea's surface while standing on the bottom, which cannot be shown, so no one can place the mind there. Hence these words are a poison to kill a poison, handled like a pesticide and kept close by none; grasping them as "a nice fact" only lets one demon replace another, as a listener grabs "the real follies" and leaves, the horse bolting before the stable is shut.
- **Understanding must not be the aim; the mind only hardens by it.** The fault is in this very thinking, and it cannot be grasped by thinking, only seen: understanding is knowing, and the system that builds "understanding" must be seen, not the understood thing. Examining a stool's form-feeling-perception-formations and concluding "no teapoy," the "no" still holds "there is," the teapoy met again by way of absence, and "I understood, I examined, I concluded it wrong" — so with self-view seated this never ends, the analyses only sticking to the mind, concepts fixing harder with no escape while understanding continues. "Am I in that nature?" cannot be answered either way, the question itself wrong, since from morning one neither knows a breathing nor a not-breathing — nothing to do there, and that un-arisen place is where the built self-view begins to dissolve, the extraordinary mindfulness settling at the root.
- **"Pure" is only the absence of filth; cleaning filth as filth, one day nothing is left to understand.** There is no separate "pure" nature; "pure" means only the absence of filth, the pure nature covered by filth, and cleaning the filth day by day, seeing filth as filth, one day — un-noticed — it is pure, no "now it is pure" registering, for finally nothing is left to understand, no filth-story and no "I am free of filth." The thought "I must do this to become pure" arises only from the filth-thoughts, and seeing the fault in each arising thought one loses the "address"; recognizing filth by filth, the view can no longer form, all faults are seen, nothing remains to call "pure," and asked "what became pure?" one can answer only by recalling filth — so the understanding itself ends, and the fault of "understand-and-do" appears, until one can fearlessly say "it cannot be understood-and-done."
- **See how saṁsāra is built, not escape an existing saṁsāra; "I am freed" is itself a wrong view.** "Pure" has meaning only while filth exists, as nibbāna's meaning exists only while saṁsāra exists; the Buddha shows how saṁsāra was built, and seeing saṁsāra as saṁsāra no separate "no-saṁsāra" is found — one sees the fault in the method by which it was built, and seeing that fault *is* the correctness, not being "freed from an existing saṁsāra." Taking "there is a saṁsāra" makes one's mouth jump to "I am freed from this," which is wrong too, for then there would be one caught and one freed — a duality that arises only with the view, both talk of "I am caught" and "I am freed by this understood bit" falling away when the view is abandoned. Not seeing this, one sits in the end like a stone however much one hears; and one should not say "this is easy," for the Buddha calls removing self-view harder than piercing a sevenfold-split horse-hair — those who teach it without the right view only fill up demerit and trivialize its depth, so it is better to stay silent than to speak without understanding, as the Buddha stayed silent to the self-question rather than confirm the man in either extreme.

## Summary

This first session holds that realization is un-doable and self-built. Wherever "I must grasp" is thought, an "I doing it" forms, and even building concentration is the not-seeing of the nature that builds "I exist and the world exists"; taking a thing or a samādhi as "mine" is the same, self arising per object, so grasping any built thing one never sees the building's fault. Realization cannot be done but must be realized by nature, un-doable even for the Buddha to force — as a tree never plans to take in carbon dioxide and a plant grows only by watering, the growing belonging to no one. So at the seen and heard places one need not prove "the meeting-of-three does not happen"; seeing the idea "it happens" as built by not-knowing suffices, while "understand it is wrong" is a worse error that grabs a replacement — which is why the Buddha left the fourfold question undeclared. These very words are wrong-understandings about the nature, not the nature, like groping the sea's surface from the bottom that cannot be shown; a poison to kill a poison, kept close by none, lest one demon replace another.

Understanding must not be the aim, for the fault is in this thinking, graspable not by thinking but by seeing: the system that builds "understanding" must be seen, not the understood thing. Examining a stool and concluding "no teapoy," the "no" still holds "there is," and "I understood, I examined, I concluded it wrong" — so with self-view seated it never ends, concepts only hardening. "Am I in that nature?" cannot be answered, the question wrong, since one knows neither a breathing nor a not-breathing from morning — nothing to do there, that un-arisen place being where self-view begins to dissolve. "Pure" is only the absence of filth; cleaning filth as filth, one day it is pure un-noticed, nothing left to understand, no filth-story and no "I am free"; the understanding itself ends, and the fault of "understand-and-do" appears. Finally one sees how saṁsāra is built, not escapes an existing one: seeing saṁsāra as saṁsāra finds no separate "no-saṁsāra," and seeing the fault in its method is the correctness, whereas "I am freed" builds a caught-one and a freed-one, a duality gone when the view is abandoned. Not seeing this, one sits like a stone however much one hears; and one must not call it easy, for the Buddha makes removing self-view harder than piercing a sevenfold-split horse-hair, better silence than teaching it without the right view — as he stayed silent to "is there a self?" rather than confirm either extreme.
