## Sutta referred to
- Directly referred to, unattributed by title: the Buddha's questioning "**is the eye permanent or impermanent?**" (the pattern of the Anattalakkhaṇa / *cakkhu aniccaṁ* teaching), noted as posed to one already with realization, not to a worldling who only parrots it; the **Four Noble Truths** (*dukkha–samudaya–nirodha–magga*) settling in one instant as the **noble mind** (*ariya-citta*); the **horse-hair pierced with a horse-hair** as an image of what is un-doable by thinking; *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* and *pañca-upādāna-skandha* cited as what "the Buddha said," used in argument.
- Referred to and quoted: a **Zen saying** (from an English text translated by "an Australian") in three stages — "before studying Zen, mountains are mountains and water is water; on a first insight, mountains are no longer mountains nor water water; after awakening to nibbāna, once again mountains are mountains and water is water," the last differing from the first. Terms: *catu-mahā-bhūta / śuddha-āyatana*, *viññāṇa māyā* (consciousness as illusion), *paññā / sammā-paññā*, *sammā-diṭṭhi*, *samudaya / nirodha / magga*, *anicca*, *saṅkhāra-dukkha*, *sakkāya / sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, *āyatana*. Similes: a university's years 1–3 and the degree; a hospital prescribing per illness; a duplicate; boarding a bus without knowing the destination.

## Main dhamma points
- The second segment resolving the Zen three-stage simile, warning that "understanding" only devalues one consciousness with another, and pressing the pivotal question: why the impermanence of a *thing* can never be seen, so that origination and cessation are seen only together.
- **The Zen third stage is un-understandable; "the value is gone" is still only the second.** Anti's answer — "the value was there, now it is gone" — fits the second stage, taking away the value of an existing thing, which is all understandable talk; the third stage, "mountains are again mountains," cannot be given by any understandable answer, for it is knowing the mountain's non-existence while it is met as mountain. The quoted Zen saying's last line differs from its first even in thought, and the second-level error is the "mind-made attitude" — fitting the Dhamma as four elements or a "mind-concept" and re-seeing "mountain" by the mind, which merely leaves the *mind* over as another trap, since "consciousness is an illusion" is said of the mind, not an acceptance of it; so this is reached by wisdom alone, sayable by no story, while "mind's surroundings," "a concept," and the rest are only labels, not realization.
- **"Understanding it wrong" only sets one consciousness to devalue another — endlessly.** Everything "understood" is another consciousness devaluing the prior one, a duplicate that falls back into the consciousness-illusion, so however much one does, "understand it is wrong" is just one more mind understanding differently — like going through university years one, two and three hoping the degree, cessation, comes at the fourth, when the degree and the first year must be got together. Doing it with a consciousness step by step never ends in seeing cessation; only when the fault in "understanding it wrong" is itself seen is the degree got, and origination and cessation seen together. Otherwise one keeps "watching origination" for years hoping cessation dawns later, whereas the Four Noble Truths arise as one, not year-one and then year-five — and endlessly watching origination, however much one hears, one merely dies unchanged.
- **A thing's impermanence can never be seen, because it is grasped through the sense-bases.** The pivotal question: can the origination of a *thing* be watched? A thing never persists, yet a persisting thing seems always there, and grabbing it one finally concludes "no value" — which means origination was not rightly seen, for rightly seen there would be no "teapoy" talk at all. It cannot be done, because to see origination one must first see impermanence, and impermanence is grasped only by first taking a thing through the sense-bases, as people say "the chair is impermanent" taken through the eye; so grabbing a thing one can never see impermanence — a sting that must land, "have I all this while been seeing impermanence in *things*?" Seeing this, the whole picture of "watch origination, wrong upon wrong, get the degree at last" is false, and one's system turns upstream against the current; not seeing it, one goes downstream, thinking "watching origination I am still bound for saṁsāra."
- **Seen together, origination and cessation settle the Four Truths in one instant; the aim must be grasped.** When impermanence can no longer be seen by grabbing a thing, origination and cessation are seen together, there is "something to be seen," the path is right, and in one instant the four — suffering, origination, cessation, path — settle as the noble mind, a seeing extremely rare, like piercing a horse-hair with a horse-hair, un-doable by thinking; this is "the path develops," not a separate thing but that one-moment seeing of constructing-origination-cessation, to which the one who sees it, formations-suffering shows. Without grasping the aim of the question, doing this is meaningless, like boarding a bus without knowing its destination merely because others board; and the eye's "constructing" has no "eye" as a thing, so the Buddha asked "is the eye impermanent?" of one with realization who can lion-roar it, while a worldling only parrots it, still carrying the eye as a thing — for the suttas were each prescribed to a person's level, as a hospital writes medicine per illness, and drinking every prescription only breeds the next illness.
- **Even the Dhamma, taken as knowledge, is fertilizer for self-view.** Anti's "origination is not there" is itself the view: to understand "origination is not there / cannot be understood" is the error, and that very arising must be seen as a view, self arising from it. So too these teachings — if they become "real" and a knowledge-system builds in the mind, that system is this same self-view, fertilizer added for the self to form; a teaching given to remove self-view, taken as knowledge and met with "now I understood well," is seen to be adding fertilizer for self-identity — yet its fault cannot be caught, because it has been labelled "Dhamma," and when its fault is shown one retorts "how can the Buddha's word be wrong — he spoke of the five clinging-aggregates, the meeting-of-three-contact," the very argument that makes it real, whereas the Buddha spoke all this only of the fallen saṁsāra.

## Summary

This second segment resolves the Zen three-stage simile. Anti's "the value was there, now it is gone" fits only the second stage, taking away an existing thing's value — all understandable talk — whereas the third, "mountains are again mountains," cannot be given by any understandable answer, being the knowing of the mountain's non-existence while met as mountain; the quoted Zen saying's last line differs from its first even in thought. The second-level error is the "mind-made attitude" — fitting the Dhamma as four elements or a "mind-concept" and re-seeing "mountain" by the mind — which leaves the mind over as another trap, since "consciousness is an illusion" is said of the mind; so this is reached by wisdom alone, while all labels are not realization. Everything "understood" is one consciousness devaluing another, a duplicate falling back into the illusion, like university years hoping the degree comes at the fourth when it must be got with the first — so step-by-step it never ends, until the fault in "understanding it wrong" is itself seen and origination and cessation are seen together, the Four Truths arising as one, not year-one then year-five.

The pivot is that a thing's origination cannot be watched: a thing never persists, yet grabbing it one concludes "no value," which means origination was not rightly seen, for rightly seen there would be no "teapoy" talk. It cannot be done, because impermanence, needed to see origination, is grasped only by first taking a thing through the sense-bases — so grabbing a thing one can never see impermanence, a sting that must land: "have I all this while seen impermanence in things?" Seeing this, the "watch origination, wrong upon wrong, degree at last" picture is false, and one turns upstream; not seeing it, one goes downstream thinking "still bound for saṁsāra." When impermanence can no longer be seen by grabbing a thing, origination and cessation are seen together, the path is right, and in one instant the four — suffering, origination, cessation, path — settle as the noble mind, rare as piercing a horse-hair with a horse-hair, to which formations-suffering shows; without grasping the aim, doing this is like boarding a bus not knowing its destination, and the eye has no "eye" as a thing, so the Buddha asked "is the eye impermanent?" of one with realization, not the worldling who parrots it, the suttas prescribed per person as a hospital writes per illness. Finally, even the Dhamma taken as knowledge is fertilizer for self-view: "origination is not there" is itself the view, and if these teachings build a knowledge-system met with "now I understood well," that is fertilizer for self-identity — its fault uncatchable because labelled "Dhamma," and defended with "how can the Buddha's word be wrong," though he spoke it all only of the fallen saṁsāra.
