## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted in Pāli: "**na āgatiṁ na cutiṁ … na cando na sūriyo**" (no coming, no going … no moon, no sun — the Nibbāna verse, **Udāna 8.1** / the Nibbāna-paṭisaṁyutta); the term **jānato passato** (for one who knows and sees).
- Referred to, unattributed: the **Dhamma likened to a raft** (*kullūpama*, MN 22), released when the crossing is seen; the **"I am" conceit** (*asmi-māna*) worn away up to arahantship; *sacca-ñāṇa* (truth-knowledge) / *sammā-diṭṭhi* as the abandoning of self-view; *svabhāva-dharma* (Nature); *sakkāya / sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, *samudaya / nirodha*, *magga* (the path). The recurring simile of a **tree that takes in carbon dioxide and releases oxygen** with no concept, used as a symbol of the un-understandable / of wisdom; rebirth in a deva-world, brahma-world or hell.
- **Notes on the source:** **Part 3** of **"Dhamma Discussion — First Session,"** a group dialogue continuing directly from Part 2; it ends cleanly ("hari" / "all right").

## Main dhamma points
- The third segment holding that the vision, not the method, must be grasped; that the path is not developed by a person but one by which the person is dissolved; and that our concepts drift ever farther from the tree-like nature they claim to describe.
- **The vision must be grasped before all else, or the Dhamma itself becomes Māra.** The view is only shown, with nothing to grasp, and the very grasping re-traps and re-forms the self, so that without this realization even the Dhamma becomes Māra — which is why the Buddha likened it to a raft, released when the crossing is seen. When "some fact's origination cannot be understood" settles rightly as wisdom, the fault in the very Dhamma-facts one had spoken is seen; but to one without this, told it, he goes straight to argument from his view — "the Buddha said this, these are true" — for whether a chair or a Dhamma-fact is understood as "truly there," it is the same, self arising alike from either.
- **"No answer" means the fault in one's origination-watching is not yet caught.** So subtle is this that the vision, not the method, must be grasped; if that realization comes, half the dark film lifts and a clear vision arises, "so this is what I must do," and one goes to a quiet corner to watch, rather than asking here and there and on YouTube till death without repair. That the small question — "can a thing's origination be watched?" — finds no answer means the fault in one's origination-method is still uncaught, so cessation cannot yet be realized; and even five minutes' reflection, unforgettable for life, shows the fault is one's own, that consciousness cannot handle this Dhamma-method, for "to one who knows and sees" it is visible, whereas we get caught in knowing and try to build a vision within understanding — but vision and wisdom cannot be thought-got.
- **The path is not developed by a person but one by which the person is dissolved.** "One who develops the path" is not found, only "one settled in the vision"; whoever says "the path develops well for me" develops a path built from mind-based concepts within his self, whereas the path is the extraordinary mindfulness-wisdom arising where self-view is abandoned — a wisdom whose "I have wisdom" never leaves his mouth, for with it the fault of "I" is seen a hundred percent, only a vision, and that truth-knowledge, the right vision, is the abandoning of self-view, seeing which as a view the "I am" conceit begins to wear away up to arahantship. To the one arrived, the path opens further, and he sees even "a person develops the path" as wrong: not a path a person develops but one by which the person is dissolved, the wisdom dissolving the rooted "I am." So it cannot be understood; one must never think "wisdom will dawn on me," and seeing the fault of understanding a hundred percent, "who am I?" gets an answer while the "address is lost" — the path realized as not done by me, no nibbāna a person sees, the person dissolved into coincidence with Nature, where there is no one who goes, "no coming, no going, no moon, no sun."
- **The tree has existence by no concept; our "I" is built of concepts drifting ever farther from the nature.** Even these words are utterly hollow, put only to show that the tree too holds no such "nature" as a plan — so they must not be grasped, nor "the tree has no such idea" understood, for the tree is pointed to only to expose the error of our assumed nature, "I breathe in and out." The tree exists yet by no concept, with no witness in it that "I am a tree" or "not a tree," so that very existence is seen by the wise as non-existence; whereas our "existence" is built of concepts — "I breathe, eat, drink, all I do" — and in that "I do" error is a nature where no concept operates, un-understandable, unseen across saṁsāra, whose not-seeing let us adopt the system "I run this, my kidneys, taking oxygen," a made-up story that is the root of saṁsāra. A tree dying in a drought has no idea "where will I be reborn?" — a question relevant only while alive, irrelevant after death — and could we die like the tree, with no "I was a tree" and no "I died," we too, someday, would be like it. But one must not settle at "I am there" with a "sādhu"; the filth itself is the dividing — making the tree non-living and mindless and the "I" living and minded — for grabbing concepts that drift ever farther from the nature to view the nature, coincidence with it never happens, not even in a dream.

## Summary

This third segment holds that the vision, not the method, must be grasped, or the Dhamma itself becomes Māra — which is why it is likened to a raft, released when the crossing is seen. When "some fact's origination cannot be understood" settles as wisdom, the fault in the very Dhamma-facts one spoke is seen; but one without this goes straight to argument from his view, for whether a chair or a Dhamma-fact is understood as "truly there," self arises alike. That the small question — "can a thing's origination be watched?" — finds no answer means the fault in one's origination-method is uncaught, so cessation cannot be realized; even five minutes' reflection shows the fault is one's own and that consciousness cannot handle this method, for "to one who knows and sees" it is visible, whereas we get caught in knowing and try to build a vision within understanding, though vision and wisdom cannot be thought-got.

So "one who develops the path" is not found, only "one settled in the vision": whoever says "the path develops for me" develops a path built from concepts within his self, whereas the path is the wisdom arising where self-view is abandoned, whose "I have wisdom" never leaves his mouth, for with it the fault of "I" is seen wholly — that truth-knowledge, the right vision, being the abandoning of self-view, from which the "I am" conceit wears away up to arahantship. To the one arrived, even "a person develops the path" is wrong: not a path developed by a person but one by which the person is dissolved, the wisdom dissolving the rooted "I am," so that one never thinks "wisdom will dawn on me," and "who am I?" gets an answer while the address is lost — the person dissolved into coincidence with Nature, where there is no one who goes, no coming, no going, no moon, no sun. Even these words are hollow, put only to show the tree too holds no such "nature," so they must not be grasped: the tree exists by no concept, with no witness that "I am a tree," and that existence is seen by the wise as non-existence, whereas our "existence" is built of concepts — "I breathe, eat, all I do" — a made-up story that is the root of saṁsāra. A tree dying in drought has no "where will I be reborn," and could we die so, with no "I was" and no "I died," we too would be like it; but the filth is the very dividing — tree non-living and mindless, "I" living and minded — for grabbing concepts that drift ever farther from the nature to view the nature, coincidence never happens, not even in a dream.
