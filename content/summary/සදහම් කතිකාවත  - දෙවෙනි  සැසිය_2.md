## Sutta referred to
- **Not explicitly named.** No sutta is cited by title.
- Referred to, unattributed: the phrase "**see with right wisdom / sammā-paññā**" and "**do wise attention (yoniso-manasikāra)**," criticized as instructions that never say *how*; *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*; *phassa / passa-nirodha* (contact / contact-cessation); *anicca* / *nicca* and the extremes of "existence" and "absence"; the **annihilation extreme** (*uccheda-anta*) distinguished from **noble emptiness** (*ariya-śūnya*); *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, *asmi-māna* (the "I am" conceit), *nirāyāsa* (effortless), *taṇhā* (craving); the Zen "mountains as mountains, water as water." Similes: oil and wick and the flame's un-seeable "burning"; a bud becoming a flower; a fully-booked train-carriage; a tree taking in carbon dioxide; health un-registered until illness comes; the forest by the road.
- **Notes on the source:** **Part 2** of **"Dhamma Discussion — Second Session,"** a group dialogue (a teacher, a lady "Anti," and "kamani") continuing directly from Part 1. It reads as a continuing section (the health-and-illness point is carried into Part 3).

## Main dhamma points
- The second segment on why "right wisdom" must not be taken as a thing, why understanding a simile does not apply it, and why the craving on the "absence" side — unseen because un-thought — is the health we hold and can never abandon.
- **A simile understood is not a simile applied.** "See these as impermanent by right view," but between the oil and the wick the "burning" cannot be seen, the oil is not shown, and when the connection is shown "contact" is not shown either — so "impermanent" names such an un-seeable nature; as a bud becoming a flower is a whole process never seen, appearing "permanent." One must be skilled at fitting the simile to its meaning by finding one's own lack: where in life is this "burning" that can never be seen? Merely understanding "the action cannot be seen, so impermanent means an un-seeable thing" is still a consciousness-fact that does not fit one's life; only if one can say how it applies to *feeling* is the simile truly applied. A hundred similes equal one; one grasped rightly is the work, for the Buddha gave a single meditation-subject and a single sutta per person, and if a hundred do not get one across, eighty-four thousand will not either.
- **Right wisdom must not be taken as a thing; the inability itself, settled in, is wisdom.** If "right wisdom" is taken as a tool — "consciousness cannot see the action, but right wisdom sees it" — one is in trouble, hunting "some special right wisdom," boarding another bus, never ending, told "do wise attention" without knowing how. The truth is that if the action cannot be seen, the very extraordinary mindfulness settling at that inability *is* wisdom, a further knowing at the inability — not a "me who has wisdom" and a "thing wisdom sees," which is duality again; for seen by right wisdom, "who looks?" is "me," so there is an inability, and only because the inability is unseen does it "appear to right wisdom." Where the inability is realized and wisdom-mindfulness settles, no person-status is met and no doer can arise, so no "one who understands the action" arises; and if that side cannot arise, this side's "I now see by right wisdom" cannot either — so even wisdom must not be made a thing, nor one caught on the word, lest one dwell inside the cage and ask "how does the arahant see by right wisdom," peeling view with view.
- **Feeling and the felt-"me" arise together; wisdom finds not a feeling but a not-met-ness.** From morning until now, had not even a sensation arisen — breath and nose set aside — no "sensing me" would arise; "I feel" arises only if "there is a feeling" arises, the two together, so "there is a nose, an air-current" arises with feeling, the meeting-of-three-contact forming there unseen as separate. Seeing feeling by right wisdom, is a "feeling" met or a not-met-ness? A not-met-ness — so wisdom has nothing to see, the arisen wisdom's capacity-to-see being what "wisdom" names, and one can no more think "I must do the impossible" than "I will not," as a fully-booked carriage can be neither re-booked nor un-booked; the extraordinary mindfulness settling at that inability is wisdom.
- **No developer of the path; the person is dissolved, effortlessly.** Seeing the action, "I" arise through it and the "my-I" view, and the non-arising of "I" is the realization; the nature being an inability, no "right-wisdom seer" is left, for a seer left would be a piece of self-view — "one with right wisdom" and "a thing wisdom sees" — the Dhamma understood-and-made-real caught by the very understanding, two things forming and "I" arising again, an origination growing. So there is no "seer's" story, hence "no one develops the path," "no one goes by right wisdom": where that nature settles it is merely labelled wisdom, ownerless, no "mine," no "I do" and no "I do not do," so no "developing" is even said — effortless, no one's doing, as the tree takes in carbon dioxide with no one doing it; a wisdom by which the person is dissolved, ending, at arahantship, the "I am" conceit, which is why "no one sees nibbāna."
- **The craving on the "absence" side, unseen because un-thought, is the "health" we hold.** "Impermanent" is thought a thing "seen by wisdom," said only because some things seem permanent; but it does not mean "absence" — it shows the fault of *both* existence and absence. We hold the graspable bits as permanent and try to see them as impermanent, while the un-understood — the ground never thought of, the sky, the concrete un-thought the whole talk — arouse no *apparent* craving because they do not come to mind, yet the ground's never troubling us is itself a craving that it not give way; and that "absence" is never within consciousness's range, so "no" cannot be understood or seen through a thing, our "no" being only a thing's absence, not the annihilation extreme, whereas the "no" that is noble emptiness is no understandable "no." So, as health does not register until illness comes — with no illness now, "I am well" does not register, and an un-met illness cannot be understood, so "is there illness or not?" does not even occur — the very absence-of-illness is what we call, by a separate word, "healthy." We hold "I am in health," holding the un-met absence-of-illness inside, with a craving that "this will never fail," always present but never registering; and because we do not know that craving is there, its portion never wanes, for one cannot find a way to abandon what one does not know one holds — first one must know "there is craving here."

## Summary

This second segment turns on applying, not merely understanding. "See these as impermanent," but between oil and wick the "burning" cannot be seen, and when the connection is shown "contact" is not — so "impermanent" names an un-seeable nature, as a bud becoming a flower is a process never seen. One must fit the simile to one's own lack: where is this un-seeable "burning" in life? Merely understanding "the action cannot be seen" is a consciousness-fact that does not fit one's life; only saying how it applies to *feeling* applies it — a hundred similes equalling one, the Buddha giving one meditation-subject per person, for if a hundred fail, eighty-four thousand will too. And "right wisdom" must not be taken as a thing: taken as a tool that "sees the action consciousness cannot," one hunts a special wisdom endlessly; the truth is that the very mindfulness settling at the inability is wisdom, not a "me who has wisdom" and a "thing wisdom sees," which is duality — for seen by wisdom, "who looks?" is "me," and only because the inability is unseen does it "appear to wisdom." Where the inability is realized, no doer arises, so "I now see by right wisdom" cannot either, and one must not dwell inside the word's cage asking "how does the arahant see."

Feeling and the felt-"me" arise together — from morning, had no sensation arisen, no "sensing me" would — so "there is a nose, an air-current" arises with feeling, the meeting-of-three-contact forming there; and seeing feeling by wisdom finds not a feeling but a not-met-ness, so wisdom has nothing to see, the fully-booked carriage neither re-booked nor un-booked. Seeing the action, "I" arise with the "my-I" view, and its non-arising is the realization; no "right-wisdom seer" is left, for a seer left is a piece of self-view — "one with wisdom" and "a thing wisdom sees" — the Dhamma made-real caught by understanding, "I" arising again, an origination growing. So there is no developer of the path, no one going by wisdom: where that nature settles it is merely labelled wisdom, ownerless, no "I do" or "I do not do," effortless, as the tree takes in carbon dioxide with no one doing it — a wisdom by which the person is dissolved, ending the "I am" conceit at arahantship, which is why "no one sees nibbāna." Finally, "impermanent" shows the fault of both existence and absence, not "absence"; we hold the graspable as permanent, while the un-thought ground and sky arouse no apparent craving yet the ground's never troubling us is a craving that it not give way — an absence never within consciousness's range, so our "no" is only a thing's absence, not noble emptiness. As health does not register until illness comes, the very absence-of-illness is what we call, separately, "healthy"; we hold the un-met absence-of-illness inside with a craving "this will never fail," always present but never registering, its portion never waning because we do not know it is there — first one must know "there is craving here."
