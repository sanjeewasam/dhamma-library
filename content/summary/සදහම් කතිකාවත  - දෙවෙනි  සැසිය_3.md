## Sutta referred to
- Directly referred to, unattributed by title: the **Aṅgulimāla episode** — the Buddha, walking, says "I have stopped, you stop," and the running Aṅgulimāla, having wisdom, sees the recluse does not lie and asks why he says "stopped" while walking (**Aṅgulimāla Sutta, MN 86**); "**cetanāhaṁ bhikkhave kammaṁ vadāmi**" (intention, monks, I call kamma — AN 6.63); the Buddha's "**I neither strove nor stood still, and so crossed the flood**" (**Oghataraṇa Sutta, SN 1.1**); "**manasikāra-sambhavā sabbe dhammā**" (all things originate from attention); *sīlabbata-parāmāsa* falling with *sakkāya-diṭṭhi*, and the **ten fetters** (*saṁyojana*) not to be learned by rote.
- Referred to, unattributed: the **annihilation extreme** (*uccheda-anta*) as what never registers to consciousness; *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso*; *phassa* (contact); *cakkhu / sota / ghāna / jivhā / kāya-viññāṇa*; the Dhamma as **beyond reasoning** (*atakkāvacara*); consciousness as a magic-show (SN 22.95); *nirāyāsa / āyāsa* (effortless / effortful), *bhava*, *ātma*, *sīla*. ## Main dhamma points
- The third segment on why the "absence" we hold is not the un-meetable annihilation-extreme but a gaining, why the "I" born of the three can never reason its own falsity, and why both doing and not-doing are effort, virtue standing only with self-view.
- **Our "absence" is a gaining grabbed from "existence," not the un-meetable annihilation-extreme.** To say "there is no concrete here" or "there is" is madness, for "no" grabs "there is"; we split "concrete here, no concrete in the sky," never seeing that the very absence *is* existence, because the annihilation-extreme never registers to consciousness, and from that un-meetable "no" we also hold the "existence." "No chair here" gains a thing within the idea — the chair — so "no" grabs "chair," a truth to me, and this is the fault: we speak not the real absence but an understandable one within consciousness's range, whereas the true absence, the nature's own absence, cannot be understood through a thing. Only one who sees both extremes transcends them; we, holding "chair exists / chair absent," never can, poking sticks at the is-group and the not-group — and not seeing across saṁsāra the un-meetable absence is why we got "an absence and an existence."
- **The "I" born of the three can never reason its own falsity.** Received now, "I feel the breath" is taken as arisen — a body met, a touch, a breath-feeling — and not seen as fault, for these three understood is what "I" now name myself, the very evidence of "I exist," so existence is from the three, and its fault never registers, "I" having arisen through the feeling-attention. Did the two ends produce the middle, or the middle the two? The middle the two — two ends knotted, which do not arise until the middle does — yet all say "look from the two ends to the middle," which can never be done, for not seeing the middle is why "two ends" exists, the is / is-not extremes built from not seeing it. Because the knot cannot be seen as a knot the meeting-of-three becomes real, un-graspable from four sides — felt here, so breath real, "breath hits nose" registering — and who registers "a delusion here"? "Me," a piece of it; so if my birth was from nose-feeling-breath, that being "me," its fault never registers, and I argue "without feeling how say the breath exists — I feel it clearly," never seeing "me" arose from grabbing the three. Hence it cannot be reasoned, the Dhamma being beyond reasoning: reasoning is consciousness-done, so a consciousness-built reason can never break a consciousness-tangle, and the born "I," trying to see its own falsity, is itself born of the three.
- **What is "understood after" is contact, un-cognizable, so the meeting-of-three becomes wholly real.** However reasoned, what is "understood after" is the three-being-there registering — contact; and contact is never caught, being un-cognizable, so consciousness arises *because* contact is un-cognizable, and with it eye-, ear-, nose-, tongue- and body-consciousness arise without our seeing they arise from a contact, so the story becomes a hundred-percent real — "there is a world, an eye, a form, I exist" — and we never see a delusion is made. "Thinking" too is un-cognizable — no one understands "thinking" — yet "I suddenly feel" is understood, and through this understanding the five are split, eye-and-form, ear-and-sound and the rest, never registering that this is only a thinking-understanding, which registers only when thinking, for all things originate from attention; and consciousness too is merely another understanding arising from this understanding, so that "it cannot be understood" cannot itself be put as an understood statement, always returning to the root knot — which is why doubt must arise, "this is an impossible task," and the arising thoughts stop bit by bit only by realization.
- **Cessation happens effortlessly, by nature, not by force.** Meditation forces the mind onto an object, pressing others down like holding a thing underwater, whereas here it stops by seeing the inability, by nature — meditation being effortful and this effortless, samādhi settling here without effort, the effort-built samādhi soon lost and the whole world back on rising, while to one who watches this there is no "abiding in meditation" and no "my samādhi breaks," it being always so. This is why the Buddha called the path effortless — "I neither strove nor stood still" — while we do both, grasping and calling false, making and breaking cookies with the mould still there; the stopping is no done-stopping, no one stopping it by force, but always stopped by nature. So the Buddha, walking, told Aṅgulimāla "I have stopped, you stop," and Aṅgulimāla, with wisdom, saw the recluse does not lie and asked why "stopped" while walking — for rightly seen it is always stopped, with no doing, no not-doing, no abiding, no striving.
- **Both doing and not-doing are effort; virtue stands only with self-view.** "Doing" is so real that one can kill, lie, drink; and because we think "we can do," we effortfully take precepts, promising "not to do" — but which is easier, doing or not-doing? Doing is easy, not-doing hard: killing a mosquito needs no thought, done instantly, while sitting unmoved as it drinks blood takes a strong intention "however it bites, I will not slap," so not-doing is the effortful action, both being effort and both intention-led — "intention, monks, I call kamma" — though the slap's intention goes unnoticed, as one walks to a place absorbed and arrives without knowing. The worldling's view is "killing incurs demerit; abstaining, I send myself to a good place, avoid hell, protect the self," so the guarding guards "me," and while self-view stands virtue is bound with it, virtue helping guard "me"; hence when self-view is abandoned, clinging to rites and vows necessarily falls — to be realized by insight, not by rote-listing the ten fetters, which is useless when one does not know what actually happens.

## Summary

This third segment shows our "absence" is a gaining, not the un-meetable annihilation-extreme. "There is no concrete here" is madness, for "no" grabs "there is"; we split "concrete here, none in the sky," never seeing the very absence *is* existence, since the annihilation-extreme never registers to consciousness, and from that un-meetable "no" we hold the "existence" too. "No chair" gains the chair within the idea, a truth to me — the fault — so we speak an understandable absence within consciousness's range, not the nature's own absence, which no thing can understand; and only one who sees both extremes transcends them, whereas we, holding "chair exists / chair absent," never can. The "I" born of the three can never reason its own falsity: "I feel the breath" is taken as arisen, the three understood being what I name myself, the evidence of "I exist," and I arose through the feeling-attention; the middle produces the two ends, which do not arise until it does, so "look from the two ends to the middle" can never be done — the is / is-not extremes built from not seeing the middle. The knot un-seen, the meeting-of-three becomes real, un-graspable from four sides, and "me," a piece of the delusion, argues "without feeling how say the breath exists?", never seeing "me" arose from grabbing the three; so it cannot be reasoned, a consciousness-built reason never breaking a consciousness-tangle.

What is "understood after" is contact, un-cognizable, so consciousness arises because contact is un-cognizable, and the story becomes wholly real — "there is a world, an eye, I exist" — the delusion never seen; "thinking" too is un-cognizable, yet "I suddenly feel" is understood, and through it the five are split, never registering it is only a thinking-understanding, which registers only when thinking, all things originating from attention, consciousness itself another understanding arising from understanding — so "it cannot be understood" cannot be put as an understood statement, always returning to the root knot, and doubt must arise, "this is impossible," the thoughts stopping only by realization. Cessation happens effortlessly, by nature: meditation forces the mind like holding a thing underwater, while here it stops by seeing the inability, samādhi settling without effort, and the Buddha called the path effortless — "I neither strove nor stood still" — while we grasp and call false, the stopping no done-stopping; so, walking, he told Aṅgulimāla "I have stopped, you stop," and Aṅgulimāla, with wisdom, saw the recluse does not lie. Finally both doing and not-doing are effort: killing a mosquito needs no thought, but not-slapping as it drinks takes a strong intention, both intention-led — "intention I call kamma" — and virtue, aimed at protecting "me" for a good rebirth, stands only with self-view, so when self-view is abandoned clinging to rites and vows necessarily falls, realized by insight, not by rote-listing the ten fetters.
