## Sutta referred to
- Directly quoted / referred to, unattributed by title: "**sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā … yadā paññāya passati**" (all formations are impermanent — when one sees with wisdom, **Dhammapada 277**); the suffering-formula "**saṅkhittena pañcupādānakkhandhā dukkhā**" with **piya-vippayoga** (separation from the dear) and **appiya-sampayoga** (union with the disliked) — the second noble truth of the **Dhammacakkappavattana Sutta, SN 56.11**; the dependent-origination links "**saṅkhāra-paccayā viññāṇaṁ, viññāṇa-paccayā nāma-rūpaṁ**" and "because of name-and-form, contact"; *rūpa-kāya adhivacana-samphassa* (name-and-form designation-contact, the **Mahānidāna** theme); formations as a **banana-trunk** (Pheṇapiṇḍūpama SN 22.95); the deathless-house / "no children" errand (Kisāgotamī).
- Referred to by name: **Ven. Ñāṇananda of Kaṭukurunda** (*Kaṭukurunde Ñāṇananda*) and his booklet **"Nivīmē Ekoḷaha" (The Eleven of Cooling)**, from which the questioner reads that "contact is because of name-and-form." Terms: *avijjā*, *phassa* (contact = "thinking"), *nāma-dhamma / nimitta*, *paṭhavi*, *pañca-upādāna-skandha*, *vedanā-saññā*, *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta*, *saṅkhāra-dukkha*, *upādāna*, *yoniso-manasikāra*, *anicca-dukkha-anatta / ti-lakkhaṇa*, *samudaya / nirodha*, *paññā vs viññāṇa*. Similes: a mountain vs a chair; the nonsense word "sahatōla"; the Italian word for table; a horse bolting before the stable is shut; illness un-registered.
- **Notes on the source:** titled **"Can the Cause of Origination Be Within the Mind's Range,"** a group dialogue (a teacher and questioners named "Dimutu," "Andi" and "mahattaya"). It closes cleanly on the three marks.

## Main dhamma points
- A dialogue holding that no formation has a root of its own, that "I see a mountain" is a name-and-form knot registering as a thing, and that wise attention is seeing the cause's non-arising — where wisdom, the full comprehension of consciousness, settles.
- **No formation has a root; a name becomes a thing only through the name-factors.** We say "avijjā, avijjā" yet never meet ignorance; everything after it is understood, "a formation formed, so a mind arises, because of name-and-form" — but we never register that no idea has a condition. "Mountain" said alone conveys nothing, holding no meaning in itself, or no languages would be needed and a child would grasp it; so no formation has a root, and a condition — the name-factors of shape, colour and earth-element — is always needed, contact being just "thinking," which needs name-and-form. The concept "mountain" holds no meaning; its meaning sits on the shape, colour and hardness, which consciousness knots to it, and not seeing the knot we suppose they came "from the mountain," while "mountain" was understood only from them; a mere name sheds its name-ness and becomes "a thing that is there," its there-ness coming from the earth-element — the eye-seen colour and the "hardness felt by the body" view — as with an unknown word that means nothing until, once said, it registers as a thing.
- **"I see a mountain" is the feeling-perception knot, and the five become "me."** Once meaning is given, the mountain becomes a thing knotting with seeing — "I see a mountain" — which is feeling and perception knotted, the very knot the Buddha showed, so that the five clinging-aggregates form and "I" am there too: the mountain seen by me, the feeling mine, "I am feeling," the shape-colour-earth recognized by me, constructed by me, gathered-and-known by me — the five all become "me," the five clinging-aggregates being nothing separate but just this formed. And it forms all at once, simultaneously, not before-and-after, though we later retroject "there was a mountain I saw"; taking "mountain" and "seeing" separately has no meaning, and re-weaving that as "Dhamma" is one more consciousness that merely satisfies the mind.
- **The fault is in the forming, not the formed; understanding the aggregates is itself the fault.** The fault lies in the "forming," not the "formed," yet we hunt it in the formed — the horse bolting before the stable is shut — so grabbing the formed and examining its faults endlessly never catches the "forming," and understanding the five clinging-aggregates never shows the fault by the understood thing, for the understanding itself is the fault, which is why the Buddha calls formations-suffering "not something understood." We understand only after a construction; before it "mountain" cannot be understood, and seeing a formation as a formation no meaning can be given beyond — so formations-suffering is not cognizable by consciousness, and the very registering of "there is a seeing" is the suffering, not the seen thing. Re-thinking the built-false as false — "the mountain is a clinging-aggregate," "seeing is a conceiving" — is done to the *built* thing, mud-then-wash-then-mud, yielding no wise attention and no crossing, only another consciousness with a "core."
- **Wise attention is seeing the arising-place; realizing the cause's non-arising ends the cause-and-effect.** Wise attention is seeing the origin, the very arising-place, so that were it truly wise-attended attention could not arise; seeing the arising-place is seeing origination rightly, which is cessation realized, whereas we look only at the arisen. Realizing the cause's non-arising, no condition is met, and cause and effect both un-arisen, so to take an arisen cause-effect and say "it arose" is like the "no children" errand — the analysis itself is the wrong, for the Buddha says cause and effect never arose but arose through not-knowing, namely not knowing the cause is non-arisen. Seeing this, one asks whether a "cause-and-effect" talk comes or the cause's non-arising is realized; and "the mountain is impermanent" speaks only of the folly-built knot's impermanence, whereas the real impermanence is where the cause's non-arising is seen — "all formations are impermanent, when one sees with wisdom," which cannot be seen from the built formation, hence a realization; the impermanent nature never arose by condition, as an "illness" never arose, and "no" itself cannot be understood, existence being built from non-existence, so seeing both extremes one realizes "what I hold as perception is an absence of a never-arisen illness."
- **Wisdom is the full comprehension of consciousness; the constructing itself is suffering.** The cause never arose — that very place is impermanence, never within consciousness's range, where the extraordinary mindfulness must settle; settled there, the fault in the cause's arising shows, and seeing that fault is seeing the whole cause-and-effect, for realizing the cause's non-arising ends the cause-effect talk, since if the cause arose the fruit necessarily becomes real. So seeing the condition's non-arising is seeing impermanence, and one sees this nature can never be known by consciousness; settled there, "knowing at a place that cannot even be met" is itself suffering — formations-suffering — so impermanent and suffering, a persistence forming if the "knowing" becomes real, and that formed persistence is realized as non-persistence by the one who saw impermanence, for this is why there is no nibbāna apart from saṁsāra: comprehending the formed persistence, one realizes it *is* non-persistence, not a separate "non-persistence," so wisdom cannot be separated from consciousness — it is consciousness's own arising fully comprehended, wisdom being the full comprehension of consciousness. Thus the registering of "a seeing, a hearing" is suffering, the meeting-of-three-contact persistence appearing as saṁsāra, and one who sees that persistence has the wisdom "that very persistence is non-persistence," knowing origination and cessation at one place; seeing saṁsāra as saṁsāra, that vision itself is the wisdom that sees the three marks — impermanent, suffering, not-self.

## Summary

This dialogue holds that no formation has a root. We say "ignorance" yet never meet it, everything after it understood, "a formation formed, a mind arises, because of name-and-form" — but no idea has a condition. "Mountain" said alone conveys nothing, or no languages would be needed and a child would grasp it; so a condition, the name-factors of shape, colour and earth-element, is always needed, contact being "thinking," which needs name-and-form. The concept "mountain" holds no meaning, its meaning sitting on shape, colour and hardness knotted to it, which we suppose came "from the mountain" while "mountain" was understood only from them — a mere name becoming "a thing that is there" via the earth-element, as an unknown word means nothing until, once said, it registers as a thing. Once meaning is given, the mountain knots with seeing — "I see a mountain," feeling and perception knotted — and the five become "me": the mountain seen by me, the feeling mine, recognized, constructed and known by me, the five clinging-aggregates nothing separate but this formed, all at once, though we later retroject "there was a mountain I saw."

The fault is in the forming, not the formed, yet we hunt it in the formed — the horse bolting before the stable is shut — so understanding the aggregates never shows the fault, the understanding itself being the fault, which is why formations-suffering is "not something understood": before a construction "mountain" cannot be understood, and the very registering of "there is a seeing" is the suffering, not the seen thing, while re-thinking the built-false as false is mud-then-wash-then-mud, yielding no crossing. "The mountain is impermanent" speaks only of the built knot's impermanence, while the real impermanence is where the cause's non-arising is seen — "all formations impermanent, when one sees with wisdom" — which cannot be seen from the built formation, the impermanent nature never arising by condition, as an illness never arose, and "no" itself un-understandable, existence built from non-existence. That non-arising place is impermanence, never within consciousness's range, where wisdom must settle; settled there, "knowing at a place that cannot be met" is suffering — formations-suffering — a persistence forming if the "knowing" becomes real, realized as non-persistence by the one who saw impermanence, so that wisdom cannot be separated from consciousness but is consciousness's own arising fully comprehended; and seeing saṁsāra as saṁsāra, that vision itself is the wisdom that sees the three marks — impermanent, suffering, not-self.
