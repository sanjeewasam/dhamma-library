# සසර සහ නිවන (Saṃsāra and Nibbāna)

## Sutta referred to

No specific sutta is cited by name. The discussion references several well-known formulations: *tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso* (the coming-together of three is contact), *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā ti yadā paññāya passati* (when one sees with wisdom that all saṅkhāra are impermanent), and the *kullūpama* (raft simile). The *Sabbāsava Sutta* framework is implicit in the treatment of āsrava kṣaya. The discussion of *ākāra anussava ākāra parivitakka diṭṭhi vijjānaṃ kanti* references the criteria the Buddha gave for not accepting a teaching merely on those grounds.

## Main dhamma points

- **Tiṇṇaṃ saṅgati phasso = saṃsāra.** The coming-together of three (indriya, object, viññāṇa) as phassa is itself saṃsāra. Understanding this mechanism is the purpose of all dhamma terminology (nāma-rūpa, viññāṇa, vedanā, taṇhā, upādāna, bhava, jāti) — these terms exist to illuminate the mūlāva (root delusion), not as ends in themselves.

- **Medicine-disease analogy.** Dhamma is medicine for the disease of mūlāva. When the disease is cured, the medicine must also be let go. Holding onto the medicine (dhamma concepts) after the disease is gone is itself a new sickness. Both disease and cure disappear together — like a child who falls ill, receives medicine, recovers, and knows nothing of either illness or treatment. That unknowing wellness is the closest analogy to avabōdha.

- **Tērum gænīma (understanding) is itself saṅkhāra dukkha.** Every act of understanding constructs pañca upādānakkhandha. The "puṭuva" (chair) example: individual parts (legs, seat, armrests) seen separately don't produce "chair" — only saṅkhāra/saññā construct it. But even the statement "chair never arose" becomes another construction to hold onto. If paramāttha sacca is grasped, it becomes sakkāya.

- **The raft analogy deepened.** Dhamma is the raft; the raft belongs to the river. The eraser wears away with the letters it removes. Yōnisō manasikāra is the nature where manasikāra cannot arise — not a special kind of manasikāra. If yōnisō manasikāra becomes a manasikāra, it becomes another viññāṇa.

- **Every diṭṭhi trap exposed.** When someone sees that placing any word creates meaning and therefore avoids placing words and avoids investigation — that avoidance is itself a diṭṭhi. Knowing "every diṭṭhi is wrong" is itself a diṭṭhi (the Makkulī paribbājaka reference: "I don't approve of any diṭṭhi" — the Buddha responds that this non-approval is itself yet another diṭṭhi). Sammā diṭṭhi is the capacity to recognise that one falls into diṭṭhi from every angle.

- **"Pān" (bread) example.** The word "pān" instantly activates a nimitta — a Sri Lankan sees a loaf, an Indian hears "betel leaf." But one who has truly seen does not get "bread" stuck, nor "betel leaf" stuck — yet still knows "pān." The word is neither rejected nor does its meaning bind. Vibhūta saññā — distorting saññā to replace one meaning with another (e.g., "pān is really just four mahā dhātu" or "pān is paṭicca samuppanna") — is just the same yakkha in dhamma clothing.

- **Samudaya seen = nirodha realised.** Seeing the yōni (origin-point) itself reveals that what arises does so from an anutpāda (non-arisen) nature. Not knowing this anutpāda nature is precisely what the four noble truths call "not knowing samudaya." When samudaya is truly seen, one recognises that tērum gænīma (understanding) itself is dukkha — saṅkhāra dukkha.

- **Atīndriya (trans-sensory) nature of avabōdha.** When one reaches the point where no indriya — eye, ear, tongue, nose, mind — can take a tērum (meaning), that is atīndriya. The āyatana that develops gradually on the path culminates in the *chāyatana* (sixth sense-base as understood in ariya context). Āsrava kṣaya is gradual — one cannot be freed from "hearing" all at once — the four distinctions (between seeing, hearing, etc.) dissolve slowly through repeated seeing.

- **"Aṭa" (eight) — the centrepiece analogy.** When someone says "aṭa," the numeral 8 appears instantly. But "aṭa" is two Sinhala letters: අ (a) and ට (ṭa). The numeral 8 is neither contained in those two letters, nor does it exist independently of them. A child who doesn't know the script cannot produce the numeral from the sound. A foreigner hearing the same sound produces nothing. Therefore the sound itself carries no letter and no numeral — what was "understood" was only one's own saṅkalpa (mental construction). The mūlaya (root/origin) of "8" cannot be found. The rāmuva (frame) is self-constructed — we built the cage and sat inside it, then declared ourselves trapped.

- **Earth's rotation analogy.** We live on a rotating earth yet have never experienced rotation — not for a single degree. We cannot say "it rotates" from experience; we cannot say "it doesn't rotate" either, since we don't know what rotation feels like. This is identical to the jātyandha (born-blind) person who can neither affirm nor deny sight. Both extremes — "it exists" and "it doesn't exist" — are impossible to affirm. In that impossibility, tērum gænīma itself is seen as futile.

- **Saṅkhāra dukkha defined through impossibility.** The impossibility of understanding at a place where understanding fundamentally cannot occur — that is saṅkhāra dukkha. It must be seen pratyakṣa (directly), not merely conceptualised. When *sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā ti yadā paññāya passati* — when this is seen with paññā — no saṅkhāra can find footing in that seeing. Like a lotus leaf where water lands but cannot stick — not through effort, but because the prajñā is already established.

- **Saññā = bhāṣāvē vipāka.** Language is the vipāka (result) of saññā. Saññā has been distorted through language. Understanding this connection between saññā and language is itself an anumāna ñāṇa (inferential knowledge) that can be taken.

## Summary

This discussion, identified as the second in a series on "Saṃsāra and Nibbāna," unfolds across two main recordings. The first establishes the foundational paradox: dhamma terminology exists solely to reveal the mūlāva (root delusion), yet the very act of understanding that terminology reconstitutes the delusion it was meant to dissolve. The medicine-disease analogy runs through the entire first section — dhamma is medicine, mūlāva is disease, and genuine cure means both disappear together. The child who recovers from illness without ever knowing it was ill or that medicine was administered provides the closest experiential parallel to avabōdha: a state that cannot be described as "having understood" or "not having understood."

The discussion then systematically dismantles every position a practitioner might take. If one recognises that placing any word creates meaning and therefore generates diṭṭhi, one might try to avoid placing words altogether — but that avoidance is itself a diṭṭhi. If one recognises that every investigation produces a viññāṇa and therefore stops investigating, that recognition-and-stopping is yet another diṭṭhi. The Makkulī paribbājaka exchange is referenced: declaring oneself free of all diṭṭhi is the most insidious diṭṭhi of all. Sammā diṭṭhi, in this teaching, is not a correct view to hold but the living capacity to see diṭṭhi arising from every direction.

The "pān" (bread) example demonstrates how saññā operates. The word instantly activates a nimitta — but the nimitta comes entirely from within, not from the sound. A Sri Lankan hears "pān" and sees bread; an Indian hears "pān" and sees a betel leaf. The sound carries nothing. One who has truly seen this cannot force the nimitta to stick, but also does not reject or distort it. The teaching explicitly warns against vibhūta saññā — replacing "bread" with "four mahā dhātu" or "paṭicca samuppanna" — as this is merely the same yakkha (demon of delusion) dressed in dhamma language. The pāruva (raft) is let go only when one sees anutpāda — the non-arisen nature of what appears to have arisen.

The second recording develops the "aṭa" (eight) analogy at great length, and this becomes the discussion's most sustained demonstration. When "aṭa" is spoken, the numeral 8 appears instantly in the mind of anyone who knows Sinhala. But "aṭa" is composed of two letters — අ and ට — and the numeral 8 is found in neither letter. Yet it cannot exist without them. One cannot go to either extreme: saying "8 is contained in the two letters" is wrong; saying "8 exists independently of the two letters" is equally wrong. Both positions collapse. A child who doesn't know the script hears the sound but produces no numeral. A foreigner hears the same sound and produces nothing at all. What, then, was "understood"? Only one's own saṅkalpa — a mental construction with no mūlaya (root). The "frame" (rāmuva) is entirely self-built.

This is extended through the earth's rotation analogy. We live on a rotating planet but have never experienced rotation for a single moment. We cannot affirm rotation from experience; we cannot deny it either, since we have no experiential baseline for what rotation feels like. Like the jātyandha (born-blind) person who cannot affirm or deny sight because sight itself is unknown, we are in a condition where both "it is" and "it is not" are equally impossible. This is not agnosticism or suspension of judgment — it is the direct seeing that tērum gænīma (understanding/meaning-making) cannot be done at all. Not that it should not be done, but that it fundamentally cannot be done.

Saṅkhāra dukkha is defined precisely here: it is the activity of understanding at a place where understanding is constitutively impossible. The impossibility must be seen pratyakṣa (directly experienced), not merely conceptualised — because conceptualising "it's impossible to understand" is itself another understanding, another saṅkhāra. The lotus-leaf simile captures the result: when prajñā is established, thoughts and perceptions can still land, but they cannot stick — not through deliberate non-attachment, but because the nature of the surface itself prevents adhesion. Sabbe saṅkhārā aniccā ti yadā paññāya passati — no saṅkhāra can find footing in that seeing. The niṣphala bhāvaya (utter futility) of all meaning-making must be realised, and the "dōlanaya" (oscillation) within that futility is what the practice activates, gradually, until āsrava kṣaya is complete.
