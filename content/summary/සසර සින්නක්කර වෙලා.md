# සසර සින්නක්කර වෙලා (Saṃsāra Has Become Prescriptive)

## Sutta referred to

No single sutta is cited by name. The discussion draws on several well-known formulations: *cetanāhaṃ bhikkhave kammaṃ vadāmi* (volition, monks, is what I call kamma), *sabbe puthujjanā ummattakā* (all worldlings are mad), *māyūpamaṃ ca viññāṇaṃ* (consciousness is like a magic show), and the reference to *kilesa niddā* (defilement-sleep). The *kalyāṇa mitta saṃsevana* (association with good friends) is referenced near the close. *Sakkāya diṭṭhi* is mentioned as the core issue but noted as not yet discussed in full.

## Main dhamma points

- **Nibbāna is not separate from saṃsāra.** It is not a destination reached by escaping saṃsāra. It is present within this very experience, unseen only because of our lack of wisdom (nuvaṇa nætikama).

- **Sleep as analogy for avijjā.** During sleep, one does not know one is sleeping, does not feel the bed, does not notice the body's position, does not register eyes closing. Upon waking, one claims "I slept" — but sleep was never experienced. The claim is made from the waking state about something that was never known. This is structurally identical to how we construct all experience.

- **Waking life is equally unknowing.** While sitting, one does not notice breathing, blinking, limb movements, or posture — until someone points them out. When pointed out, one "feels" them. But this feeling arises only from thinking (hitīma), not from any prior experience. The difference between sleep and waking is negligible — both are states of not-knowing.

- **Kilesa niddā — defilement-sleep.** The Buddha says every being is in kilesa niddā. Even now, while apparently awake and aware, we are not actually aware of anything until we think about it. What we call awareness (sihiya) is just thinking prompted by reminders, not genuine knowing.

- **The dream-dog demonstration.** In a dream, a dog appears, barks, bites — one runs, screams, feels pain. Yet the eyes are closed. If seeing requires open eyes, how does one see in dreams? The dreamer is certain the dog is real — just as certain as the waking person is about waking-state dogs. Both certainties are identical in structure: the mind (hita) constructs the appearance, and the person believes the appearance comes from the sense organ. This proves that "rūpa are seen by the eye" is a diṭṭhi, not a fact.

- **The sinnakkara (prescriptive title) analogy.** In Sri Lankan law, occupying a property long enough earns a prescriptive deed (sinnakkara oppuva). Saṃsāra has done the same: the false habit of attributing perception to sense organs has been repeated across incalculable lifetimes until it has become a prescriptive truth — a falsehood so established it functions as fact. This is saṃsāra sinnakkara velā.

- **Cetanāhaṃ bhikkhave kammaṃ vadāmi.** Every time one thinks "there is a nose, there is breath," one creates kamma. The thought was never preceded by an experience — one constructs the experience from nothing, exactly as in a dream. The breath and nose "exist" only from the moment of thinking, not before.

- **The madness analogy.** A madman believes he is the president — his thinking feels correct to him. He cannot see his own madness. If cured, he cannot experience the cure either, because he never experienced the madness. Similarly, we cannot see our own delusion, and if avabōdha occurs, there is nothing new to "understand" — the delusion simply ceases. *Sabbe puthujjanā ummattakā* — all worldlings are mad.

- **The dog and its reflection.** A dog sees its own reflection in water and barks at it, trying to chase the "other dog" away. The problem is not a second dog existing — it is the error in the dog's seeing. Similarly, our problem is not that things exist and must be made to not-exist. The problem is entirely in the diṭṭhi — the wrong view that seeing happens through eyes, that objects are "out there."

- **Sammā diṭṭhi = correcting the error in seeing.** Nothing is gained and nothing is lost. No dog arrives and no dog departs. The delusion that a dog was there — the delusion that perception operates through sense organs contacting external objects — that delusion ceases. That cessation is nibbāna. It is not separate from saṃsāra; it is saṃsāra seen without the error.

- **Upādāna and future births.** Because we believe the eye sees, we cherish and protect the five sense faculties. That upādāna (clinging) is precisely what produces the next life with eyes, ears, nose — whether as human, animal, or peta. The cycle self-perpetuates through the error in view.

## Summary

This discussion, titled "Saṃsāra has become prescriptive" (sasara sinnakkara velā), uses the Sri Lankan legal concept of prescriptive title to frame the core problem: a falsehood repeated across immeasurable lifetimes has acquired the force of established truth. The specific falsehood is that perception operates through sense organs contacting external objects — that "the eye sees," "the ear hears," and so on.

The teaching begins with a firm correction: nibbāna is not a separate destination reached by escaping saṃsāra. It exists within this very experience, invisible only because of nondiscernment (avijjā). The sleep analogy establishes the method of investigation. During sleep, nothing is known — not the bed, not the body's position, not the closing of eyes, not the passage of time. Upon waking, one claims "I slept," but sleep itself was never experienced. The statement is made entirely from the waking state. This is the structure of all our knowing: we claim to know things we have never actually experienced.

The discussion then shows that the waking state is no different. While sitting in a chair, one does not notice breathing, blinking, posture, or limb movements until someone mentions them. When mentioned, awareness of them arises — but this "awareness" is merely thinking (hitīma), not experience. Before the thought, there was no experience of breath or nose. After the thought, one is certain these things "exist." But the certainty arose from thinking alone, exactly as a dream-dog arises from thinking alone. The teacher asks: what is the difference between dreaming and waking? The participant, pressed through the sequence, cannot find one.

The dream-dog analogy is developed at length. In a dream, a dog appears, barks, bites, causes fear and pain — yet the eyes are closed throughout. If sight requires open eyes, the dream should be impossible. But it occurs. This proves that the proposition "rūpa are seen by the eye" (rup pēnnē æhæṭa) is a saṃsāric habit, not a truth. The teacher describes this habit as "sinnakkara" — squatted on for so long across lifetimes that it has become a prescriptive deed, unchallengeable by ordinary means. The analogy to a legal prescriptive title (sinnakkara oppuva) is precise: just as long occupation of a house confers legal ownership regardless of original title, long repetition of the false view confers experiential certainty regardless of its falsity.

The madness analogy (ummattaka) drives the point further. A madman who believes he is the president cannot see his own madness — his thinking feels correct to him. All worldlings are in this condition: sabbe puthujjanā ummattakā. Critically, when madness is cured, the cure itself is not experienced either — the madman does not "understand" that he was mad. Similarly, avabōdha is not a new understanding added to the existing ones. It is the cessation of the wrong view, and that cessation leaves nothing new to be known.

The dog-and-reflection analogy provides the final frame. A dog barking at its own reflection in water is not confronting a real second dog. The problem is entirely in the dog's diṭṭhi — its way of seeing. Correcting the error does not make a dog disappear (no dog was ever there) and does not make the water disappear. It simply ends the barking. Sammā diṭṭhi is this correction. The teaching emphasises that there is nothing to gain (labbēnavakvat nǣ) and nothing to get rid of (næti karannavakvat nǣ): the error in view is the only problem, and its correction is nibbāna. Since the error perpetuates through upādāna — clinging to sense faculties as "mine" and "necessary" — its cessation also ends the mechanism that generates future births with sense faculties, whether as human, animal, or peta. The discussion closes noting that kalyāṇa mitta saṃsevana (association with a good teacher) is essential given how deeply the avijjā has been practised, and that the process is gradual — like finger-wear on a tool handle, imperceptible day to day.
