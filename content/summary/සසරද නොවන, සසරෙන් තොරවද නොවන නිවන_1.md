- Referred to, unattributed: *diṭṭha-suta-muta-viññāta* (the four — seen, heard, sensed, cognized — held as views); the **four postures** (*iriyāpatha*: standing, sitting, walking, lying); *nitya-saññā* (permanent-perception); *tiṇṇaṁ saṅgati phasso* (the meeting of three is contact); *avijjā* (ignorance); *anicca–dukkha–anatta* (the three marks); *nitya-sukha-atta* (permanent–pleasant–self) as the distortion; *samudaya / nirodha*; *nibbāna / nivīma* (extinguishing); *paññā-vimutti* (liberation by wisdom); formations as **un-constructed dhammas** (*asaṅkhata*); the nature as **absolute** (*nirapekṣa*). Similes: giving directions (Mītiyagoda → Kahava → Ambalangoda → Galle → Southern Province); "what is white? — like a crane" (the milk-and-crane exchange); a fire and its extinguishing; darkness needing light; all trees identical, so no "tall / short."

## Main dhamma points
- The first part arguing that the permanent-perception is never met, that our birth and its cause are un-knowable, that the meeting itself is impermanence and suffering, and that "extinguishing" can only be named because we are caught in "origination" — nibbāna being neither a thing extinguished nor saṁsāra.
- **The permanent-perception is never met; our birth and its cause are un-knowable.** Unable to understand the permanent-perception, we solidify "there is a permanent-perception" separately, its nature being un-knowability; so nothing is truly known — "I feel" never registers as a known feeling, yet we are never for a moment free of feeling, since in all four postures feeling is present while "feeling" as such registers nowhere. So the true permanent-perception is never met in life, and the real "birth" is not a womb-birth but the world registering through seen-heard-sensed-cognized, held as four views into which we are born; the cause of that birth is never met, for "I exist" registers only through seeing and the rest — in deep sleep there is neither "I exist" nor "I am dead" — so birth can never be understood by anyone, one being born through the four which are themselves un-understandable.
- **Everything is located only relative to another, with no root of its own.** Asked "where is Mītiyagoda?" one says "past Kahava"; asked "where is Kahava?" one names Ambalangoda, then Galle, then the Southern Province — each thing pointed to only through another, never in itself, as "what is white?" is answered "like a crane" and the crane by something else; so a formation has neither beginning nor end, an endless cycle. Not knowing the feeling, we knot it to a nose and a breath that are themselves un-located — as directions refer endlessly outward — and think we "know" when really we think from not-knowing, the world reading our thinking as skill while it is only the un-understanding that makes us think.
- **Impermanence is the not-knowing; the meeting itself is impermanence and suffering.** We cannot see a thing's impermanence, because a thing is met only when impermanence is unseen — the very not-knowing of "the meeting of a thing is impermanent" being why a thing seems met; so it is not knot together *because* the two exist, but because impermanence is unknown, and grabbing the knotted two one can never see impermanence in them. The nose-and-breath knot, once formed, cannot be denied by any thinking, showing how permanent they have become — a distortion built from not knowing impermanence; and the very making of "there is a nose and breath" is impermanent, so origination is itself a cessation, for what is met was met from an un-met place, and seeing this root — that feeling itself was never met — the "nose-and-breath met" story cannot stand, so the meeting itself is impermanence, and "knowing at a place that can never be met" is the suffering, the meeting itself being the suffering.
- **"Extinguishing" can only be named because we are caught in "origination"; one is freed even from nibbāna.** The meeting-of-three persistence looks a lovely "existence," yet wisdom settled there sees only a non-persistence — which cannot even be called "non-persistence," being given the labels "not-self, non-persistence" only because a distorted persistence formed and we carry a self-sense; were that self-sense never there, as there is no "extinguishing" without a fire ever kindled and no need of light where there is no darkness, so the talk of "cessation" and "nibbāna" exists only because we are caught in the talk of "origination." A mere "extinguishing" can be pointed nowhere and cannot even be named, the word given only because "this is burning"; we take "an existing thing extinguished," but at a place never existing, never met, never born, there is no "extinguished" — so finally there is nothing to understand, one being freed even from "extinguishing," and liberated even from wisdom, which only assists, so that "what is extinguished?" is irrelevant. Not knowing this un-met nature, we built "permanent-pleasant-self," and to escape those three the three words "impermanent, suffering, not-self" and a "cessation / nibbāna" must be brought — not that an existing nibbāna or a separate impermanence is shown, for grabbing those to look one never catches it, seeing only the fault in "permanent-pleasant-self," not any separate thing.

## Summary

This first part holds that the permanent-perception is never met. Unable to understand it, we solidify "there is a permanent-perception," its nature being un-knowability, so nothing is truly known — "I feel" never registers as a known feeling, yet we are never free of feeling, present in all four postures while "feeling" registers nowhere. The real birth is not a womb-birth but the world registering through seen-heard-sensed-cognized, held as four views we are born into, whose cause is never met, for "I exist" registers only through them — in deep sleep there is no "I exist" and no "I am dead" — so birth can never be understood, one being born through the four which are un-understandable. And everything is located only relative to another, with no root: "where is Mītiyagoda?" is answered by Kahava, Kahava by Ambalangoda, then Galle, then the province, endlessly, as "what is white?" is answered "like a crane"; so a formation has neither beginning nor end, and not knowing the feeling we knot it to a nose and breath themselves un-located, thinking we "know" when we only think from not-knowing.

Impermanence is that very not-knowing: a thing is met only when impermanence is unseen, so it is knot together not because the two exist but because impermanence is unknown, and grabbing the knotted two one never sees impermanence in them; the nose-and-breath knot, once formed, cannot be denied, a distortion built from not knowing impermanence, and the very making of "there is a nose and breath" is impermanent, so origination is itself cessation, what is met met from an un-met place — and seeing that feeling was never met, the meeting itself is impermanence, "knowing at a place that can never be met" being the suffering. The meeting-of-three persistence looks a lovely existence, yet wisdom sees only a non-persistence there, which cannot even be called "non-persistence," labelled "not-self" only because a distorted persistence formed and we carry a self-sense; were that never there, as there is no "extinguishing" without a fire kindled and no light needed where there is no darkness, the talk of "cessation" and "nibbāna" exists only because we are caught in "origination." A mere extinguishing can be pointed nowhere and not even named, the word given only because "this is burning"; at a place never existing, met or born there is no "extinguished," so finally nothing is to be understood, one being freed even from nibbāna and even from wisdom, which only assists, so "what is extinguished?" is irrelevant. Not knowing this un-met nature we built "permanent-pleasant-self," and to escape those three the words "impermanent, suffering, not-self" and a "nibbāna" must be brought — not that an existing nibbāna or a separate impermanence is shown, for grabbing those one never catches it, seeing only the fault in "permanent-pleasant-self."
