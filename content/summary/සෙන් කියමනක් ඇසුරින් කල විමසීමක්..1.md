## Main dhamma points
- A dialogue reading the Zen "mountain is a mountain / is not a mountain / is again a mountain" through the three stages of worldling, dhamma-view and arahatship — the difference lying in whether the mountain is taken as existing, as non-existing, or as a mere idea — and holding that only seeing the not-knowing (which requires consciousness to cease) shows the forming as a forming.
- **Stage one — the worldling meets the mountain as an existing thing.** For the ordinary worldling a mountain is met in its very "being-there," proved by all five sense-bases — grasp it, it is hard; touch water, it flows — so how could one deny it. But the mountain becomes "a thing" only through a set of marks (shape, sign, designation): the shape, the colour, the hardness, none of which belong to the perception "mountain" but are each a separate perception; not recognizing perception as perception (as a mirage), the earth-element becomes a real "hardness" and the water-element a real "wetness." So a mountain is made a mountain out of what-is-not-the-mountain — a form described by a bundle of name-factors, the form-body's designation-contact — and a felt sense arises that it is simply "a thing that is there."
- **Stage two — with basic knowledge the mountain is "not a mountain," taken as non-existent.** When a basic knowledge arises, water is no longer water but understood as "the water-element," the mountain no longer a mountain but "the earth-element" — freed from the first grip, yet now caught in the perceptions "earth" and "water," slipping from one perception to another. Here one says "these things are wrong," "this is impermanent," and falls to "there is no mountain, there are no mountains in the world," taking the very mountain once grasped as existing and now viewing it as non-existent — for whatever we take as existing, when we investigate the thing taken as existing it inevitably turns to "non-existence." So having grasped one extreme one slides to the other, and seeing both "it exists" and "it does not" as faults one circles into a third view: "the mountain neither exists nor does not exist" — which is itself just one more of the undeclared four ("exists / not / both / neither"), a box one falls into whenever origination is not rightly seen.
- **Stage three — the arahant meets the mountain again as a mountain, as a mere idea.** At arahatship the mountain is again met as a mountain, water as water — but now the recognition "mountain" is seen as only a recognition, the perception seen purely as perception, so that no colour, shape or hardness is found knotted within the idea "mountain," and no designation-contact of the form-body and no resistance-contact of the name-body occur; the formation is seen as a formation. Thus it is taken neither as an existing thing nor as a non-existent thing — the two extremes both avoided — the mountain met simply as an idea, whereas the worldling met it as existing (through the marks) and the second stage met it as non-existent. What the world meets is a mountain already conditioned into a five-clinging-aggregate, feeling and perception knotted, graspable and seeable; here there is no such construction, only perception met as perception.
- **Only seeing the not-knowing shows the forming; and that requires consciousness to cease.** To watch origination we take an already-become thing — a tree, a chair — but the formed thing can only ever show the second stage ("no such thing was really formed"); to see the *forming* one must see its root, its cause, its womb (*avijjā-paccayā saṅkhārā*), the very arising-place, for only seeing where it arises shows the arising to be a forming, and until then we see only "a thing that was formed." After a construction the mind understands "a tree, a chair," but what it was before the construction it cannot understand — that is the not-knowing, and the not-knowing is never within the range of consciousness, which by its nature only ever knows. "We know it is a tree, but we do not know *how* we know it is a tree — that itself is the not-knowing." So to see the not-knowing one must go beyond consciousness — consciousness must cease — and only when consciousness has ceased is the not-knowing seen, and only then does "this is a forming" show, and the mountain met as a mountain become a mere idea, taken neither as existing nor as non-existing.

## Summary

This first part reads the Zen saying "a mountain is a mountain; a mountain is not a mountain; a mountain is again a mountain" through three stages. For the ordinary worldling a mountain is met in its very being-there, proved by all five sense-bases; yet it becomes "a thing" only through a bundle of marks — shape, colour, hardness — none of which belong to the perception "mountain," each a separate perception, so that not recognizing perception as a mirage the earth-element becomes a real hardness and the water-element a real wetness. A mountain is thus made a mountain out of what is not the mountain, a form described by name-factors, the form-body's designation-contact, with a felt sense that it is simply "a thing that is there." When a basic knowledge arises the mountain is no longer a mountain but "the earth-element," water "the water-element" — freed from the first grip but now caught in those very perceptions, sliding from one to another, saying "these are wrong," "this is impermanent," falling to "there are no mountains in the world," taking the once-grasped mountain as non-existent; for whatever is taken as existing, once investigated, turns to non-existence, and seeing both extremes as faults one circles into "it neither exists nor does not exist," itself one more of the undeclared four into which one falls whenever origination is not rightly seen.

At arahatship the mountain is again met as a mountain, water as water, but now the recognition "mountain" is seen as only a recognition, perception seen purely as perception, no colour, shape or hardness found knotted within the idea, no designation-contact of the form-body and no resistance-contact of the name-body occurring — the formation seen as a formation, taken neither as an existing thing nor a non-existent one, met simply as an idea, whereas the worldling met it as existing through the marks and the second stage as non-existent. And to watch origination we take an already-become thing — a tree, a chair — but the formed thing can only show "no such thing was really formed"; to see the forming one must see its root, its cause, its womb, ignorance conditioning formations, the very arising-place, for only that shows the arising to be a forming. After a construction the mind understands "a tree," but what it was before the construction it cannot — that is the not-knowing, never within the range of consciousness, which only ever knows: "we know it is a tree, but not how we know it — that is the not-knowing." So to see the not-knowing consciousness must cease, and only then does "this is a forming" show and the mountain become a mere idea, taken neither as existing nor as non-existing.
