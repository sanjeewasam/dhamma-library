## Main dhamma points
- A dialogue holding that the five aggregates are "five non-formings," that feeling and the felt "nose-and-breath" arise together at one place so that origination and cessation are one, that "earlier not-felt, now felt" splits into a false past and present, and that seeing both together — beyond both "it applies" and "it does not" — is where ignorance-conditions-formations is truly grasped.
- **The five aggregates are "five non-formings"; the seeing itself is never within consciousness.** The five clinging-aggregates are the after-story, told once the mind and its object are already formed, and we take that formed thing and examine its form, feeling and perception; but the Buddha called these very five "five non-formings," five that were formed from not-knowing — so only if the not-knowing is seen first does it become plain that the five were formed from not-knowing, which is why the five clinging-aggregates cannot be understood by an ordinary mind. For "dependent on eye and forms, eye-consciousness arises," yet the arising of that seeing-consciousness is never itself within the range of consciousness; so on being told "a form," only "there is a form, there is an eye" registers, the knot never seen, and it is always the thing, never the seeing, that the mind meets.
- **Feeling and the felt "nose-and-breath" arise together; origination and cessation are one place.** Take feeling: while feeling is absent, no "there is a nose, there is a breath" ever registers, and nose and breath cannot even be spoken of apart from feeling; so exactly where feeling forms, right there "a nose and a breath exist" forms too — origination and cessation at one place, "this being, that is; this not being, that is not." But merely reciting the two formulas achieves nothing — the Buddha showed them to cross beyond the two, not to be repeated — and to escape the boxing-in one must see both together, which means seeing feeling as feeling, the conceit "it is felt" seen as false; then one can no longer go to "there is a felt thing" or "there is not," and that is seeing the true origination.
- **"Earlier not-felt, now felt" splits into a false past and present, arising from one place.** Seeing "before it was not felt, now it is felt" falls into two extremes, which is to fall into past and present — not-knowing read as the past, this moment's feeling taken as the present — so we strain to hold the mind in the present precisely because the mind runs to the past; but if the two are seen to have arisen from one and the same place, it was only a "sitting," a mere abiding, not-knowing and knowing met together here. From morning on there is no "I feel" registering and no "I do not feel" registering, both understood only from one place; and at the very instant attention arises as "feeling," "earlier not-felt" appears right here too — so what is "past" and what is "present"? Not-knowing cannot arise this moment, for we call this moment "feeling" precisely because not-knowing is gone; yet if both not-knowing and knowing arose here, then not-knowing was never met at all — an absence never experienced.
- **The absence of a never-met thing yields nothing; "feeling" itself is a mere point.** We speak as if "there is no not-knowing," which is the same as "feeling"; but the absence of a thing never met, never experienced, can yield nothing — it too is a non-gain — so with wisdom this present "feeling" is seen as itself a mere zero-point, not a gain, whereas the world gives not-knowing a standing, imagining it was experienced, caught in "there is a not-knowing." Seeing feeling by feeling, one goes neither to "earlier not-felt" nor to "now not-there" — but only if one sees how it arises; and to see the arising one must say "both do not apply," while not taking even "both do not apply" as a fact, for there is no "place where both do not apply." It is not a talk of two at all; that nature cannot be glossed even by the word "two," nor by "there are not two," or the not-knowing is again made into a knowing — in the not-knowing there is neither existence nor non-existence, neither feeling nor non-feeling, simply not known, which is exactly where we stand.
- **Seeing both together is the crossing, with no "middle"; "I neither halted nor strove."** Seeing origination rightly comes to "this cannot be seen by consciousness," and only seeing the two together crosses beyond them, with no "middle"; the worldly equanimity or "middle" is positing two sides and abstaining from both, but with this wisdom both efforts — going to the two and not going to the two — are wholly negated: no acceptance that "there are two," no striving "to abstain," neither settling nor straining, for both are the unnecessary pair, as the Buddha told a deity, "I neither halted nor strove." In that nature neither applies, no acceptance and no rejection, since by its very nature it is an impermanence, an un-met, an un-arisen; and one who has seen that un-arisen sees every forming as a forming that arises from a non-arisen place, so it becomes plain to him that these formations are built only from not knowing the not-knowing — there ignorance-conditions-formations is rightly grasped by wisdom, and there every arising view is seen as a view.
