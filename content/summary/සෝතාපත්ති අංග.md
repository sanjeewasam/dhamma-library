# සෝතාපත්ති අංග (Sotāpatti Aṅga — Factors of Stream-Entry)

## Sutta referred to

- Cetanāhaṃ bhikkhave kammaṃ vadāmi (AN 6.63 — "Intention, monks, is what I call kamma")
- Manasikarā sambhavō sabbē dhammā (AN 1.15 — "All dhammas have manasikāra as their origin")
- Khīṇaṃ purāṇaṃ navaṃ natthi sambhavaṃ (from the Ratana Sutta / arahant stock verse)
- Sabbe puthujjanā ummattakā (all worldlings are mad — attributed phrasing)
- Saccānulomika Sutta (referenced in closing discussion on past/present/future)
- The four sotāpatti aṅga are named: kalyāṇa mitta sēvanaya, saddharma śravaṇaya, yōnisō manasikāra, dhammānudhamma paṭipatti

## Main dhamma points

- The oil-lamp analogy is the centrepiece of the first recording: a lit lamp brought near an unlit one produces flame, yet that flame neither "comes from" the lit lamp nor arises "without" it — both extremes are wrong. This maps directly to avabōdha: understanding is paraprattya rahita (not produced by an external cause in the ordinary sense).

- Jvalanaya (combustion) does not reside in the oil or the wick; it arises at the point of contact. Likewise, when the Buddha delivers a single discourse and listeners attain different fruits (sotāpanna, sakadāgāmī, anāgāmī, arahant), this proves the dhamma does not "give" the attainment — the fruit arises at the individual's own point of contact with the teaching.

- Yōnisō manasikāra is not a technique to practise. It is the seeing of ayōnisō manasikāra — seeing that thinking arises where thinking is impossible. That seeing itself IS yōnisō manasikāra.

- Kriyā (action) analysis: examining "going" (yanavā) reveals that neither foot nor ground can be found within the cetanā of "going." The kriyā is śūnya from within — empty of any knowable content when properly investigated.

- Cetanāhaṃ bhikkhave kammaṃ vadāmi: the flower-picking example shows that the same physical kriyā (kicking or picking a flower) becomes puñña or pāpa depending on cetanā. The kriyā itself carries neither merit nor demerit.

- The tree analogy: a tree absorbs CO₂ and releases O₂ but has no cetanā about these processes, no viññāṇa. Therefore "awareness" (dænavat bhāvaya) is shown to be entirely irrelevant to the actual process of kriyā — a complete delusion layered on top.

- Sīlabbata parāmāsa: keeping precepts based on the dṛṣṭi of "I can do / I cannot do" is sīlabbata. When kriyā is truly seen as akarmanya (non-karmic), both doing and not-doing are transcended — the question of "keeping" or "breaking" precepts no longer applies.

- The speaking/lying analogy: the kriyā of speaking contains neither truth nor falsehood. Two people utter identical words ("a tree has fallen"); one "lies," the other tells "truth" — determined not by the kriyā of speaking but by an external referent (whether the tree actually fell). The label (boru/ætta) attached to the kriyā is itself the cetanā that generates kamma.

- Diṭṭha, suta, muta, viññāta — all four are maññanā (conceiving, distortion). Saṃsāra is constructed entirely from these four maññanā arising from not knowing kriyā as kriyā.

- Kriyā rendered akarmanya by paññā: an arahant eats, drinks, speaks, walks — but with no gaṭa (knot/entanglement). No labels of puñña or pāpa are generated because cetanā does not form around the kriyā. The same kriyā that traps the unknowing person in saṃsāra becomes the very bridge to nibbāna when avabōdha accompanies it.

- Khīṇaṃ purāṇaṃ navaṃ natthi sambhavaṃ: when kriyā is seen properly, old kamma (purāṇa) is destroyed at the point of contact with paññā (like husked rice that cannot germinate), no new kamma (nava) can form, and present-moment kamma ceases to accumulate — all three temporal directions are resolved simultaneously.

- The Aṅgulimāla principle: even a thousand killings are dissolved when kriyā becomes akarmanya through paññā, because the cetanā that labelled "killing" can no longer generate saṅkhāra. Like husked rice placed in mud — it will not sprout.

- Walking meditation question at the end: true practice is not observing foot-and-ground contact, but seeing how cetanā forms around the kriyā of walking. However, this "seeing" cannot be produced by deliberate thought — thinking about it is itself viññāṇa. The nature of paññā is that it cannot know itself as paññā, just as the tree cannot know its own breathing.

## Summary

This recording — comprising two substantial sessions — takes the four factors of stream-entry (sotāpatti aṅga) as its framework but spends most of its time on a deep, layered investigation of kriyā (action), cetanā (intention), and the mechanism by which saṃsāra is perpetuated through the unknowing labelling of action.

The first session opens with the oil-lamp analogy for kalyāṇa mitta sēvanaya. A lit lamp brought near an unlit one: the flame that appears on the second lamp neither "comes from" the first lamp nor arises independently of it. Both attributions collapse on examination. Mr. Lasantha uses this to map the nature of avabōdha — understanding cannot be said to come from the teacher (paraprattya rahita), yet it does not arise without the association. The analogy extends: combustion (jvalanaya) does not reside in the oil or the wick; it arises at the point of contact of conditions. This is demonstrated by the fact that the Buddha delivered a single discourse and different listeners attained different fruits — sotāpanna, sakadāgāmī, anāgāmī, arahant — from the same words. If the dhamma "gave" the attainment, everyone would attain the same level. The variation proves the fruit arises at each person's own point of contact.

Moving to yōnisō manasikāra, Mr. Lasantha rejects the common interpretation of it as a meditation technique or method of correct thinking. Instead, yōnisō manasikāra is defined as the seeing of ayōnisō — seeing that thinking arises where thinking is impossible. The moment one sees the impossibility of thought about kriyā, that seeing itself constitutes yōnisō manasikāra. This is supported by analysis of the kriyā of "going": when one examines the cetanā of walking, neither the foot nor the ground can be found within the intention to go. The kriyā is śūnya — empty of any graspable content. This empties all dhammas (manasikarā sambhavō sabbē dhammā), since every dhamma depends on manasikāra, and manasikāra itself is shown to be groundless.

The discussion distinguishes ārya samādhi from forced samādhi: the former arises naturally when kriyā is seen as it is, while the latter is manufactured through technique. The eraser analogy recurs — dhamma and avijjā consume each other, and both vanish together, leaving neither an eraser nor writing. Na āgatiṃ na gatiṃ — no coming, no going. Vidyā is meaningless without avidyā; neither has independent existence.

The second session deepens the analysis through cetanāhaṃ bhikkhave kammaṃ vadāmi. A person kicks a flower off a grave and picks it up — the kriyā (physical action) of kicking and picking is identical, but one generates pāpa (kicking sacred offerings) and the other puñña (reverently replacing them), depending entirely on the cetanā that accompanies it. The kriyā itself carries neither puñña nor pāpa. This leads to the analysis of sīlabbata: precepts kept on the basis of "I can do this / I should not do that" are grounded in the dṛṣṭi of doership. When kriyā is truly seen — when the impossibility of doing is understood — both doing and not-doing are transcended, and the question of keeping or breaking sīla no longer arises.

The tree analogy provides the most extended illustration: a tree absorbs carbon dioxide and releases oxygen, yet has no cetanā about these processes, no viññāṇa, no awareness that it is "doing" anything. The tree cannot think about its own breathing. This demonstrates that dænavat bhāvaya (awareness/knowingness) is entirely irrelevant to the actual process — a complete delusion layered on top of kriyā. When we try to explain kriyā through our interpretations, we are like someone trying to make a tree think about its breathing — an impossible project.

The speaking/lying example crystallises the point. Two people say the identical words — "a tree has fallen." One lies (no tree fell), the other tells the truth (a tree did fall). The kriyā of speaking is identical; the words are identical. The determination of puñña or pāpa comes not from the kriyā of speaking but from a completely external referent — whether the tree actually fell. This external referent is then knotted (gaṭa gahinavā) to the kriyā of speaking, and that knotting is cetanā, and that cetanā is kamma. The kriyā of speaking never contained truth or falsehood; we imposed labels from outside and then suffered the consequences of our own imposition.

Mr. Lasantha connects this to the four bases of conceiving: diṭṭha, suta, muta, viññāta are all maññanā — distortions arising from not knowing kriyā as kriyā. Saṃsāra is built entirely from these four maññanā. When kriyā is seen properly, maññanā dissolves, and with it puñña and pāpa are both abandoned (puñña-pāpa pahīnassa). An arahant continues to eat, drink, speak, and walk — the same kriyā as anyone else — but generates no entanglement because no labels are knotted to the action. The arahant's speaking has no category of truth or falsehood within it, because cetanā does not form around the kriyā.

The verse khīṇaṃ purāṇaṃ navaṃ natthi sambhavaṃ is then unpacked: when kriyā is seen by paññā, old kamma arriving in the present moment is "husked" — like rice that has been boiled and cannot germinate even if planted in mud. The present moment generates no new saṅkhāra, and the future is foreclosed. The Aṅgulimāla case is the extreme illustration: a thousand killings dissolved instantly when kriyā became akarmanya through paññā — the cetanā of "I killed" lost its generative power, like husked seed.

The recording closes with a question about walking meditation. The questioner asks whether one should observe how cetanā forms during walking. Mr. Lasantha affirms the direction but warns that this seeing cannot be produced by deliberate thought — thinking about it is itself viññāṇa, which is precisely the problem. The nature of paññā is that it cannot know itself as paññā, just as the tree cannot know its own processes. To understand is already to fail, because understanding builds viññāṇa. The point where understanding becomes impossible is exactly where paññā operates.
