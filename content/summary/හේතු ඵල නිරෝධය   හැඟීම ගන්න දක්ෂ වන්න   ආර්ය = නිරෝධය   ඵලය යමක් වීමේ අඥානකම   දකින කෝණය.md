# හේතු ඵල නිරෝධය — හැඟීම ගන්න දක්ෂ වන්න — ආර්ය = නිරෝධය — ඵලය යමක් වීමේ අඥානකම දකින කෝණය
# (Hetu-Phala Nirodha — Become Skilled at Grasping the Feeling — Ārya = Nirodha — The Angle of Seeing the Ignorance in Something Becoming a Result)

## Sutta referred to

- Cakkhuṃ ca paṭicca rūpe ca uppajjati cakkhu viññāṇaṃ (SN 35.93 / the standard paṭiccasamuppāda formulation for sense-contact)
- The eleven-fold classification of khandha: atīta, paccuppanna, anāgata, hīna, paṇīta, dūra, santika, oḷārika, sukhuma, ajjhatta, bahiddhā (from the Khandha Saṃyutta)
- Sō dhammaṃ passati sō maṃ passati (Vakkali Sutta, SN 22.87 — "He who sees the dhamma sees me")
- Dhammō have rakkhati dhammacāriṃ (Thag 4.10 — "The dhamma protects the dhamma-practitioner")
- The sixty-two miccā diṭṭhi (Brahmajāla Sutta, DN 1): eighteen śāśvata (eternalist) and forty-four ucchēda (annihilationist)
- Paccuppannaṃ referenced as a key term distinct from "present moment" (vattamāna)

## Main dhamma points

- The mother example: if everything arises in the mind, one should be able to manifest a deceased mother right here. One can think of her, but cannot see her as one sees a living person present. This disproves "everything is merely mind" — but equally, the external is not independent of the internal.

- Abhyantara (internal) and bāhira (external) cannot be separated. When saññā arises, both inside and outside arise together. Neither the position "everything is constructed in the mind" nor "everything comes from outside" is correct. The two are mutually dependent (anyōnya).

- Avijjā is so thoroughgoing that it has constructed both the physical (bhautika) and the internal. Both sides condition each other continuously.

- The eleven-fold classification (atīta through bahiddhā/ajjhatta) is one indivisible bundle. As humans, we perceive only the inside/outside split — the grossest layer. Arahants operate from paccuppanna — not "the present moment" in the ordinary sense, but the direct seeing of how things arise through paccaya (conditions).

- Eye surgery analogy: Western medicine cuts the eye from outside in. The Buddha "dissects" from inside out — without cutting anything. Avabōdha means understanding from within.

- Nirodha ārya sacca and udayavyaya ñāṇa: the one who has understood nirodha gains udayavyaya ñāṇa — not the reverse. Without nirodha, any attempt to understand arising leads to kamma-phalavāda (a result-first causal account) rather than true hetu-phala dhamma.

- The critical distinction between kamma-phalavāda and hetu-phala dhamma: kamma-phalavāda takes the result as real and then searches for causes that explain it; hetu-phala dhamma examines the supposed cause and asks whether the result actually resides within it.

- The milk-tea analogy: we name milk powder, sugar, tea leaves, and hot water as "causes" of milk tea — but only because we already know what milk tea is. We started from the result (phala) and worked backward. The Buddha's approach is the opposite: take the milk powder packet alone and try to find milk tea within it.

- The bed analogy: disassembling a bed reveals wood, nails, mattress — no "bed" was ever there. But this recognition (tibilam nǣ — "was never there") must arise even while looking at the intact bed, not only after dismantling. The bed "disintegrates" directly into avijjā, not into its physical components.

- Ātma saññā / sakkāya diṭṭhi: the cloth analogy — cloth never knows whether it has been torn or stitched. The sajīvī bhāvaya (aliveness) we project onto phenomena is the root of sakkāya diṭṭhi. All sixty-two miccā diṭṭhi (eighteen śāśvata, forty-four ucchēda) arise from this projection.

- Nirodha = ajīvī bhāvaya: not that something alive becomes dead, but the recognition that it was never alive — nohaṭagat svabhāvaya (the unarisen nature). This resolves all three times: was never there in the past, is not there now, cannot arise in the future.

- Prabhāsvara (luminous mind) = ajīvī bhāvaya — the inability to re-construct. When sajīvī bhāvaya (aliveness) arises from ajīvī bhāvaya, that is citta. Nirodha is the seeing that this arising never had substance.

- The resonance (anunāda) analogy: two tuning forks vibrating at the same frequency appear as two objects — the laboratory sees "two things with identical frequency," but wisdom sees "one appearing as two." This is the difference between anusōtagāmī (going with the stream — conventional analysis) and paṭisōtagāmī (going against the stream — wisdom).

- The driving school analogy: the school does not teach you driving — it shows you what you cannot do (the errors). You learn to drive yourself. Saddharma śravaṇa works the same way: it exposes miccā diṭṭhi (what is wrong); when enough errors are seen, one's own paññā reveals what is right. "He who sees the dhamma sees me" (sō dhammaṃ passati sō maṃ passati).

- Ārya = nirodha: the word ārya in each of the four noble truths refers to nirodha. That is why seeing any one truth reveals all four — because nirodha is present in each. Dukkha ārya sacca is not merely "the truth of suffering" — the ārya component points to nirodha. Without the nirodha dimension, what people call dukkha sacca is only the raw (amu) truth of suffering, seen through the lens of existence and non-existence.

## Summary

This recording is a single dense session that takes up the relationship between hetu-phala dhamma (the Buddha's teaching on causality) and nirodha, arguing that they are inseparable — one cannot understand true causality without first understanding cessation, and one cannot understand cessation through conventional causal analysis.

The discussion opens with a striking thought experiment. If everything arises in the mind, Mr. Lasantha asks, can you manifest your deceased mother here and now — not as a memory, but as a living presence the way you see the person sitting in front of you? You cannot. So the claim that "everything is mind" fails. But equally, the external world is not independent of the internal — when saññā arises, both inside and outside arise together. Neither idealism nor materialism survives examination. The two sides are mutually dependent (anyōnya), and avijjā is so thoroughgoing that it has constructed both the physical and the internal simultaneously.

This leads to the eleven-fold khandha classification. All eleven modes — past, present, future, inferior, superior, far, near, gross, subtle, external, internal — form one indivisible bundle. As ordinary humans, we perceive only the grossest split: inside versus outside. Arahants, by contrast, operate from paccuppanna — a term Mr. Lasantha carefully distinguishes from "the present moment" in the ordinary temporal sense. Paccuppanna is the direct seeing of conditioned arising (paccaya), prior to its division into temporal, spatial, or qualitative categories.

The eye surgery analogy illustrates the Buddha's method. Western ophthalmology cuts from outside in — removes the eyeball, dissects it, identifies the components. The Buddha "dissects" from inside out, without cutting anything at all. He describes the internal structure of seeing from within the seeing itself. Avabōdha is this inward understanding. And crucially, someone who has understood from inside out can then also explain what the external approach discovers — but the reverse does not hold.

The recording's central argument concerns the relationship between nirodha ārya sacca and udayavyaya ñāṇa (knowledge of arising and passing away). The conventional assumption is that one first understands arising and passing, and this leads to nirodha. Mr. Lasantha inverts this: the one who has understood nirodha is the one who can see how arising and non-arising actually work. Without nirodha, any attempt to understand arising produces only kamma-phalavāda — a result-first causal account. This is the difference between what the world calls "hetu-phala" and what the Buddha actually taught.

The distinction is developed through the milk-tea analogy. We say milk powder, sugar, tea leaves, and hot water are the "causes" of milk tea. But we named these as causes only because we already know what milk tea is — we started from the result (phala), accepted it as real, and then worked backward to identify contributing factors. This is kamma-phalavāda: result-first causality. The Buddha's approach is fundamentally different: take the milk powder packet and try to find milk tea within it. You cannot. The "cause" does not contain the "result." The entire causal chain that seemed so obvious was constructed by starting from the end and reading backward.

The bed analogy extends this. Asked "how was this bed constructed?", people answer: wood, nails, mattress — or rūpa, vedanā, saññā, saṅkhāra, viññāṇa — or paṭhavī, āpo, tejo, vāyo. Mr. Lasantha points out that all these answers already assume the bed exists and then explain it. They are kamma (actions/constructions), not hetu (causes). The true cause is avijjā — but saying "avijjā" as a label is itself another kamma. When one actually disassembles the bed, one finds wood, nails, mattress — no "bed" was ever there. But this recognition must arise even while looking at the intact bed, not only after physical dismantling. The bed must "disintegrate" directly into avijjā — meaning one sees that the bed-ness was constructed by avijjā, not by wood and nails.

The discussion then connects this to ātma saññā and sakkāya diṭṭhi through the cloth analogy. Cloth never knows whether it has been torn or stitched. The sajīvī bhāvaya (aliveness, animacy) that we project onto all phenomena — "the cloth exists," "the bed is there" — is the root of sakkāya diṭṭhi. From this single projection arise all sixty-two miccā diṭṭhi. The wise person neither affirms nor denies the existence of rūpa. They understand: if rūpa appears, it appears because of sajīvī bhāvaya (which arises from avijjā); if rūpa does not appear, it is because ajīvī bhāvaya (the unarisen nature) prevails. Neither ucchēda nor śāśvata can take hold.

Nirodha is defined as ajīvī bhāvaya — not that something alive becomes dead, but the recognition that it was never alive to begin with. Nohaṭagat svabhāvaya — the unarisen nature. This resolves all three times simultaneously: it was never there in the past, it is not there now, and it cannot arise in the future. Prabhāsvara (the luminous mind) is identified with this ajīvī nature — the state in which reconstruction is impossible. When sajīvī bhāvaya arises from ajīvī bhāvaya, that arising is citta. Nirodha is the seeing that this arising never had substance.

The resonance analogy provides a scientific parallel. Two tuning forks vibrating at the same frequency: the laboratory registers "two objects with identical vibration." Wisdom sees "one reality appearing as two." The conventional approach (anusōtagāmī) analyses the two forks; the wisdom approach (paṭisōtagāmī) sees through the apparent duality. Mr. Lasantha also notes that the ārya in each of the four noble truths is not merely an adjective — it refers to nirodha itself. That is why seeing any one truth reveals all four: nirodha is the common element in each.

The session closes with the driving school analogy. The school does not teach you to drive — it shows you what you cannot do. Saddharma śravaṇa works identically: it is an upaniśraya (supporting condition), not nibbāna itself. It exposes miccā diṭṭhi; when enough errors are seen, one's own paññā reveals the correct seeing. This is "he who sees the dhamma sees me" — the dhamma seen is one's own seeing, not a received teaching.
