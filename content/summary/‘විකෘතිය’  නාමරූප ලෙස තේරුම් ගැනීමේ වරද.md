## Main dhamma points
- A dialogue holding that existence is not built from things that exist but from the view solidified by seeing, that nothing has a place of its own, and that the "why do I collide if the bus is name-and-form" question is itself the error of taking a name-and-form distortion as real instead of seeing perception as perception.
- **Existence is not built from an existing thing.** We feel "because these things exist, they become existence," "because it is there, it appears"; but if existence arose from things-existing, then walking the road — where a hundred percent of the things on both sides really are there — everything would have to become existence, yet it does not: asked what was there, one recalls only three or four or five things "for sure," a light-post, a shop, though nothing elsewhere had been demolished and all of it was there. So existence is made not from a thing that is there but from the view taken from within an existence; we suppose existence is built from an existing thing, when it is built from thinking-upon-thinking, seeing itself solidified into a view.
- **Nothing has a place of its own; a location never comes from the thing.** Asked "where is the light-post?" one answers "where the light-post is," which the post itself can never give — one must borrow another thing, "just past the shop is the post"; "where is the pharmacy?" — "where the pharmacy is," with no findable address, as "a metre" is located only at "a metre" and never at Ambalangoda. A bus is located only "on the road," never found at Alutgama or Colombo, for those are only shop-boards; ask where the bus is and one answers with a board-name, ask where oneself is and again a board-name, so no question is ever actually answered — "I am on the bus, wherever I go I am on the bus, the bus has no place and I have no place." Everything is pointed to only through something else, and a place never issues from the thing itself.
- **Seeing solidifies "it was there," and that is existence.** Because "I saw it, for sure, with both eyes," a permanence forms — "a thing exists at that very spot" — unshakable once proved by seeing: from craving comes clinging, from clinging existence, the taking-as-existing knotting a knot through seeing, so the persistence forms because "it is right there, I saw it." And "it appeared" needs no added "it appeared to me," for the moment one says "appeared," a "me" is already established, and what appeared to me is precisely "a thing exists" — that itself is existence; so the question "do things exist or not" is never the cause of existence. Were existence built from things-existing, not even an arahant could live in the world, since things do appear as existing to him too; but one who has seen the view recognizes each thing at its place as perception-by-perception and walks the road without colliding — recognizing "space" as space and "vehicle" as vehicle, functioning without the knot "I saw it," the use ending right there and casting no forward copy, with the wisdom that no action is ever caught, for seeing that "seeing" is never met one never knots "a seen thing," knotting only to the four — seen, heard, sensed, cognized.
- **The name-and-form error: taking a distortion as name-and-form.** One asks, "if the bus is name-and-form, how would I die struck crossing the road?" — because we learn "name-and-form" as a separate thing and think "the bus is name-and-form," when name-and-form is the *way a perception is built*, a form designated by name-factors (shape, colour, earth-element), not the object itself. The recognition "bus" is only a form-perception; earth-element and colour are themselves perceptions, a mirage — colour is never actually cognized, hardness never the felt "feeling" — and not knowing the perception as perception the bus becomes a real existing bus, made permanent, running on a real road. Then we take "the Dhamma" as a separate piece and compare our built distortion against it — "the Buddha said these are name-and-form, so why am I struck?" — the question arising only from not knowing the distortion; had one seen the mirage as a mirage, perception as perception, the questions "is there an outside bus," "how am I struck," "if it is name-and-form I cannot be struck" would never arise, for the colliding itself is the mirage. We have fallen to a blind, foolish level where the bus is made a hundred percent real and both the "outside" and the "bus" are a distortion posited from not knowing perception; grasping that distortion we ask our own questions of it, when recognizing recognition as recognition from the very start dissolves every such question — no going to "do buses and roads exist," no asking "how am I struck crossing the road."

## Summary

This dialogue holds that existence is not built from things that exist. We feel "because these exist, they become existence," "because it is there, it appears"; but if existence arose from things-existing, then on the road — where all the things on both sides really are there — everything would become existence, yet asked what was there one recalls only three or four things for sure, a light-post, a shop, though all of it was there and nothing elsewhere was demolished. So existence is made not from an existing thing but from the view taken within an existence, built from thinking-upon-thinking, seeing itself solidified into a view. And nothing has a place of its own: "where is the light-post?" is answered "where the light-post is," which the post can never give, so one borrows another thing — "just past the shop"; "a metre" is located only at "a metre," a bus only "on the road," never at Alutgama or Colombo, for those are shop-boards, so asked where the bus is one gives a board-name, asked where oneself is again a board-name, and no question is ever answered — "I am on the bus, wherever I go, and the bus has no place and I have no place."

Because "I saw it, for sure, with both eyes," a permanence forms — "a thing exists at that spot," unshakable once proved by seeing: from craving clinging, from clinging existence, the taking-as-existing knotting through seeing, the persistence forming because "it is right there, I saw it"; and "it appeared" needs no "to me," for saying "appeared" already sets up a "me," and what appeared is "a thing exists" — that is existence, so "do things exist or not" is never its cause. Were existence built from things-existing, not even an arahant could live in the world; but one who has seen the view recognizes each thing at its place as perception-by-perception and walks the road without colliding, recognizing space as space and vehicle as vehicle, functioning without the knot "I saw it," the use ending right there and casting no forward copy. Hence the name-and-form error: asked "if the bus is name-and-form, how am I struck crossing the road?" one has learned "name-and-form" as a separate thing and thinks "the bus is name-and-form," when name-and-form is the way a perception is built — a form designated by name-factors of shape, colour and earth-element, themselves perceptions, a mirage — not the object; not knowing perception as perception the bus becomes a real permanent bus on a real road, and taking "the Dhamma" as a separate piece we compare our distortion against it and ask "so why am I struck?", the question arising only from not knowing the distortion. Had one seen the mirage as a mirage, the colliding itself being the mirage, no such question would arise — recognizing recognition as recognition from the start dissolves every one of them.
